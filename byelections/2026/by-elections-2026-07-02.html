Twelve by-elections on 2nd July 2026:

Peterhead South and Cruden

Aberdeenshire council, Scotland; caused by the death of Conservative councillor George Hall. Two weeks ago, the Conservatives scored an impressive parliamentary by-election gain in Scotland by gaining the Aberdeen South constituency from Labour. Last week, the voters of George St/Harbour ward in Aberdeen were called out for another by-election, in which the Scottish National Party gained an Aberdeen city council seat from the Lib Dems. This week we have the final entry in a trilogy of by-elections in north-east Scotland, this time with the Conservatives turning from attack to defence. Our venue is some miles north up the coast from Aberdeen. The town of Peterhead was established at the end of the 16th century - the Peter of the name comes from the dedication of the town's first church to St Peter - and its economy has always been based on its port, which according to a 2019 survey is the busiest fishing port in the whole of the UK. The South side of the town is newer, covering areas of housing such as Clerkhill, Meethill and Invernettie together with industrial areas and a prison complex. The Victorian-era Peterhead prison closed in 2013 and has been turned into a museum; it has been replaced by the adjacent HMP Grampian, which is a maximum-security prison housing some of Scotland's most notorious criminals. Scottish electoral law allows anyone incarcerated here for one year or less to vote by post in Holyrood or Scottish local elections at their previous address, but takeup of this option has been low: according to a Scottish Government report, only 38 eligble prisoners in Scotland (out of a total of around 600) were registered to vote at the 2021 Holyrood election. The two Peterhead wards both include substantial parts of the town's rural hinterland. In South ward this is represented by Cruden Bay, which was heavily promoted as a seaside resort back in the day: Bram Stoker was a regular visitor, and the early chapters of Dracula were written here during his holidays in the 1890s. The town still attracts visitors for its links golf course, whose sands hide a modern piece of infrastructure: Cruden Bay is where the crude oil from the Forties pipeline comes ashore. Peterhead South and Cruden ward was created in 2007 and took on its current boundaries in 2017, with three Aberdeenshire councillors. It has always been a strong SNP area, and in the 2007 and 2012 elections the Scottish National Party polled more than 50% of the first preferences and won two out of three seats: the other seat went in 2007 to the Lib Dems' Sam Coull, who was defeated in 2012 by independent candidate Tom Malone. The SNP vote fell in 2017 and they lost a seat to the Conservatives, and unaccountably the Nationalists didn't try to get that seat back in 2022. First preferences here last time were 46% to the SNP and 41% to the Conservatives; that would surely have delievered two SNP councillors but the party only one had one candidate, so the SNP surplus instead gave the final seat to the Lib Dems who were the only other party to stand. If we rerun the count for a single seat, then the SNP's 46-41 lead over the Conservatives on first preferences becomes 54-46 after transfers. Since then we have had elections to both Parliaments. In the July 2024 Westminster election Peterhead and its hinterland were transferred from the Banff and Buchan constituency to the new and cumbersomely-named seat of Aberdeenshire North and Moray East. When Rishi Sunak pressed the trigger on that election, the Banff and Buchan Conservative MP David Duguid was in hospital recovering from a spinal stroke. He was unceremoniously dumped by the Conservatives, who deselected him in favour of the Scottish Conservatives' then leader Douglas Ross; Ross then went on to lose Aberdeenshire North and Moray East to the SNP's Seamus Logan, a Northern Irishman from the Glens of Antrim who had come to the Commons late in life after retiring from a career in healthcare. Logan subsequently resigned his Aberdeenshire council seat, and the SNP lost the resulting November 2024 by-election in Fraserburgh and District ward to the Conservatives. For the recent Holyrood election the two parts of this ward were in different constituencies. The Cruden area is located within the Aberdeenshire East Holyrood seat, which returned First Minister Alex Salmond at its first election in 2011; in 2026 SNP MSP Gillian Martin, who sits in the Scottish Cabinet with the climate action and rural affairs portfolio, won a third term of office by narrowly holding off Conservative challenger Douglas Lumsden by 943 votes; Lumsden was re-elected to Holyrood on the regional list before he was elected to Westminster in the Aberdeen South by-election two weeks ago. There was an even closer result in the Banffshire and Buchan Coast seat, which includes Peterhead and was the number 1 Reform UK target in Scotland; in the end the turquoise tide came up just short, and SNP backbencher Karen Adam was re-elected for her second term with a majority of 364. The SNP have the withdrawal of the Greens from the constituency ballot to thank for those two seats: in the regional ballot Aberdeenshire East voted Conservative while Reform UK carried Banffshire and Buchan Coast. The 2022 Aberdeenshire council elections returned 26 Conservative councillors, 21 SNP, 14 Lib Dems and 9 independents. There were three by-elections in November 2024 which all resulted in Conservative gains from the SNP, but this gain for the Tory group has been more than wiped out by defection losses. The Aberdeenshire council website currently lists 20 Conservatives, 18 SNP councillors, 14 Lib Dems, 10 independents, 7 Reform UK councillors and this vacancy; the ruling coalition is made up of the Conservative and Lib Dem groups plus some of the independent councillors. One of the defectors had been the late councillor George Hall, who died in April at the age of 65. Hall, who was originally from Lisburn in Northern Ireland and had had a long police career before entering politics: in 2022 he was elected for his first and only term of office by winning the Conservative seat in Peterhead South and Cruden ward. However, Hall did not stay in the Conservative group for long: in 2023 he became an independent councillor. The Conservatives will be hoping to recover their by-election loss here, and they have selected Ross Gibb who is fighting his first election campaign. The SNP candidate is Angus Matheson, who lives in Cruden Bay and works in hospitality. Also standing are Tony Ware for the Lib Dems, Dave Bestwick for the Scottish Family Party, Reform UK's Andy Curwen who is drum major of the Gordon Highlanders Association pipes and drums and has run into trouble during the campaign over his social media, and two independent candidates: John Ross gives an address in the ward, while Ross Thomson is a high-profile former Conservative MP (Aberdeen South, 2017-19), MSP (North East Scotland, 2016-17) and Aberdeen city councillor (Hazlehead/Ashley/Queens Cross ward, 2012-17), who is apparently standing here as an independent after failing to get the Reform UK nomination. He completes an all-male ballot paper of seven candidates. Intending Conservative and independent voters should check carefully which Ross they are giving their first preference to. Horwich North

Bolton council, Greater Manchester; caused by the resignation of Horwich and Blackrod First councillor Ryan Bamforth. For our first English by-election of the week we are in the Greatest Town in the Known Universe - or, more accurately, one of its satellite towns. We're in Horwich (rhymes with Norwich), a town which clings to the south-western slopes of Winter Hill on the two roads between Bolton and Chorley - the old road over the bill, and the flatter but more circuitious Chorley New Road at a lower level. Horwich was not like the other towns which make up the modern Bolton metropolitan borough, which were economically based on textiles, coal mining, chemicals or some combination of those. Instead this was a railway town, with the major employer being the Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway which opened its locomotive works here in 1887. Many of the great transport engineers of the early twentieth century, including the London and North Eastern Railway's Nigel Gresley and the aviator Alliott Verdon-Roe (the A V Roe who gave his name to the Avro aircraft firm), worked at Horwich Works early in their careers. The works site, which is outside this ward, remains in industrial use today and has retained many of its original buildings. In 2004 a piece of public art was put in on a nearby roundabout, known as the "Ghost Train", to celebrate Horwich's railway heritage; this sculpture has fallen into disrepair over the last 20 years and is currently being refurbished. Horwich railway station itself closed in the 1960s, and its site is now a park within this ward. The industry continues up into the open space of Winter Hill, a large chunk of which has disappeared in the last couple of years as a result of an expansion of Montcliffe Quarry. To the west of here Horwich North ward extends northwards along the access road to the massive Winter Hill TV mast, which is just outside the ward boundary; some of the mast's support wires actually fly over this ward. The access road bypasses Winter Hill's subsidiary summit of the Two Lads, a pair of cairns which lie at 389 metres above sea level and are a stunning viewpoint. You'll often find me up here on a fine summer's evening taking in the extensive view: try to spot the Wrekin to the south, Snowdonia to the west, Scafell Pike to the north, and - over 90 miles away - the Isle of Man to the north-west, behind Blackpool Tower. (Not all of these are on the toposcope which has recently been installed.) The Two Lads is thought likely to be a prehistoric burial site, and the Woodland Trust - which owns the hill - have plans for this summer to demolish the cairns, do an archaeological dig and then rebuild the cairns in what should hopefully be a safer state. Work should start on this within the next couple of weeks. Famous former residents of Horwich North ward include Jenny Ryan of The Chase fame, who used to run a quiz league team out of this ward. Still here are the Crown Horwich quiz team anchored by their star playerx Derek Heyes, who has been blind since the age of 6 but didn't let that stop him from a successful teaching career. Derek can be heard here competing in the 2021 Brain of Britain semi-finals, and he also took money off Mark Labbett on The Chase. Not many people manage that. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8qLNm8oytuM At Parliamentary level Horwich is part of the Bolton West constituency, where the most popular Conservative MP in Greater Manchester Chris Green was chased down to defeat in 2024 despite favourable boundary changes. The Labour MP here is Phil Brickell, who had been tackling financial crime for NatWest before his election to the Commons; Brickell was also a Manchester city councillor before his election. Horwich still has some self-government in the form of a town council, but for most purposes the local authority is Bolton council whose elections in recent years have been a right old mess. In March 2018 I previewed a by-election in Farnworth ward which was contested by a new localist party called Farnworth and Kearsley First, and I remarked that "it's surprising in retrospect that nobody has previously tried to form a localist party in Bolton borough given its residents' reputation for insularity". Well, Farnworth and Kearsley First won that by-election and their performance inspired the formation of a whole series of localist parties in the satellite towns Bolton borough, of which the one to note here is Horwich and Blackrod First which started contesting elections in 2019 and found immediate success in Horwich and Blackrod. HBF have won every election in Horwich North ward since it was drawn up in 2023, and they were competitive in the predcessor ward of Horwich North East. Reform UK and another localist party called Bolton for Change had jointly run a full slate in the 2024 Bolton elections and got nowhere. Come 2026 Bolton for Change were nowhere to be seen and Reform UK got their first big chance here: they won nine of the 20 Bolton wards against three each for the Conservatives and the Greens, two for Labour, one for the Lib Dems, and two seats for Horwich and Blackrod First who held out against the turquoise tide that swept away all the other localists. Shares of the vote here in May were 39% for Horwich and Blackrod First, 26% for Reform UK, 12% for the Greens and 11% for Labour. The Labour minority adminstration in the Greatest Town continues with a reduced Labour group of 19 out of 60 seats, plus another vacancy; HBF (4) and the Lib Dems (5) supported the Labour candidate for leader of the council at the AGM in May, while the other opposition councillors are 10 each for Reform UK and the Conservatives, 3 Farnworth and Kearsley First, 2 remaining councillors in the Communities First group, and one councillor who has recently quit the Conservatives and gone independent. The Horwich and Blackrod First party hasn't remained united, and after May two councillors elected on their ticket were sitting as an independents within the Communities First group on the council. One of them was Ryan Bamforth, the council's Armed Forces champion until his resignation in May, who had first been elected here in 2023 but quit the party a year later and joined the Communities First group. Bamforth had topped the poll in the 2023 all-out election and won a four-year term, so the winner of this by-election to succeed him will need to seek re-election again next year. Communities First are not registered as a political party and they have no defending candidate. Horwich and Blackrod First will be looking to recover their defection loss with their candidate John Scoble, who sits on Horwich town council and runs a construction firm. Scoble gives an address on the street off Chorley Old Road called Bottom o' th' Moor, which is a postman's worst nightmare made real. Well done to anybody who can work out how the house numbers on Bottom o' th' Moor ended up in this state. Reform UK, who will become the official opposition on the council if they gain this by-election, have selected Wendy Parkinson. Standing for the Green Party is Nicola Salmon, a retired ex-teacher and teacher trainer. Labour have reselected Graham Dawson, who will be hoping to improve on his fourth-place finish here two months ago. Also standing are Raymond Ndokwo for the Conservatives, independent candidate Peter Hopkinson who stood in Astley Bridge ward in May and got noewhere, and the Lib Dems' Scott Turner-Preece. Weaver and Cuddington

Cheshire West and Chester coumcil; caused by the resignation of Conservative councillor Phil Rimmer. Our first Conservative defence of this week is in the Cheshire countryside to the west of Northwich, running from the Weaver Navigation to the east into the sandstone hills to the west. In the valley we find the town of Weaverham, which is this ward's largest centre of population, while Cuddington is further up the hill; other villages here include Sandiway (which shares a parish council with Cuddington), Norley and Acton Bridge which is the only crossing of the River Weaver here. Sandiway is the location of a curious relic of the former Vale Royal abbey, the Round Tower Lodge which now stands incongruously between the two carriageways of the A556 road. The ward has excellent commuting opportunities in many directions. Cuddington and Sandiway are at the crossing of the A49 Warrington-Whitchurch and A556 Chester-Northwich roads, Cuddington railway station lies on the Mid-Cheshire line between Chester and Manchester via Northwich, and Acton Bridge railway station lies on the West Coast main line and is served by local trains between Crewe and Liverpool. This has attracted a commuter demographic to go with the wealthy Cheshire set. The country house of Delamere Manor within the ward (available for weddings, etc) was once home to the Teke That singer Gary Barlow, while the Manchester City footballer Phil Foden is reportedly an elector here. Presumably he'll be able to vote in person, given that he didn't make the England World Cup squad. One suspects that the real housewives of Cheshire have voted Conservative in recent elections. Weaver and Cuddington is in the Eddisbury part of the Cheshire South and Eddisbury constituency, which was one of the few seats in the North West to return a Conservative MP in 2024: she is former Richmond upon Thames councillor Aphra Brandreth, daughter of Gyles Brandreth. The last local elections were back in 2023, when Weaver and Cuddington ward split its three seats. Top of the poll was independent councillor Gillian Edwards, who got 42% of the vote and polled almost twice as many votes as anybody else. The race for the ward's other two seats was unusually clos, with the Conservative slate eventually beating Labour by 22-21; Phil Rimmer, who won the third and final seat, had a majority of 64 votes. This places Weaver and Cuddington within the minority on Cheshire West and Chester council, which is run by Labour. Phil Rimmer stood down in May due to changes in his professional and personal circumstances, so the Conservatives are defending this by-election. Their defending candidate is Patrick Melody, who will hope that he is in tune with the electorate. We have an independent candidacy from Gabrielle Goad, who is standing in her first election. Labour have reselected Bob Cernik, who is the mayor of Northwich and holds the title of emeritus professor of materials engineering at the University of Manchester; Cernik was a councillor for Northwich Winnington and Castle ward from 2019 to 2023 and he has previously served for six years on Cuddington and Sandiway parish council, but his 2023 result here was the latest in a long series of previous failed local election campaigns in this ward. Also on the ballot are Elizabeth Jewkes who is not cheap jewellery from Argos but is the Lib Dem candidate, and Reform UK's Adam Wordsworth who was the losing Conservative candidate here in both 2019 and 2023. It's likely that Wordsworth lost out on those occasions because his surname is at the wrong end of the alphabet - but will that be a disadvantage this time? Malinslee and Dawley Bank

Telford and Wrekin council, Shropshire; caused by the resignation of Labour councillor Shaun Davies.

Our next poll is in the centre of a New Town, as we visit the Architectural Heaven Circa 1970 that is the Telford Shopping Centre. Opened in 1973, this is the largest shopping mall in Shropshire. It's orbited by a series of commercial and leisure buildings, including the main offices of Telford and Wrekin council together with the Telford International Centre which is a regular stop on the World Snooker tour. The whole complex has easy access to the M54 motorway and is also served by Telford Central railway station, on the line between Wolverhampton and Shrewsbury. The New Town of Telford took its name from the great civil engineer of the 18th and 19th centuries. Originally the New Town was going to be called Dawley after one of the pre-existing towns in the area, which was a little to the south-west of the modern town centre; as the above poem from Betjeman might suggest, Dawley was the birthplace of the first Channel swimmer Captain Matthew Webb. In between Dawley and Telford is the New Town estate of Malinslee, with Dawley Bank lying to the west of it. The Malinslee and Dawley Bank ward also takes in a large chunk of the open green space that is Telford Town Park. The ward is strongly working-class and has high levels of social housing, reflecting its New Town nature. Malinslee and Dawley Bank ward was created in 2015 and took on its current boundaries in 2023. The outgoing councillor Shaun Davies had first been elected for the predecessor Malinslee ward in 2011, and in 2016 he took over from his ward colleauge Kuldip Singh Sahota as leader of the Labour-controlled Telford and Wrekin council. Davies was just 30 years old, which at the time made him England's youngest council leader. In 2022 Davies became leader of the Labour group on the Local Government Association, and then he continued his political rise in the 2024 general election by being elected as the Labour MP for Telford, gaining the seat from its controversial previous Conservative MP Lucy Allan who had stood down at the election. Following his election to the Commons Davies stood down from the council leadership, but he stayed on as a Telford and Wrekin councillor until May 2026 when he resigned from the council in order to take up a post as a government whip. Davies isn't the only former Malinslee and Dawley Bank councillor who has gone to Westminster: his former ward colleague Kuldip Singh Sahota was elevated to the Lords in 2022. Lord Sahota stood down from Telford and Wrekin council in 2023, when the new Labour slate of Shaun Davies and his wife Elise won both of the ward's seats by the emphatic margin of 75-18 over the Conservatives. Whether this is Reform UK-proof remains to be seen. Defending Shaun Davies's seat for Labour in this by-election is Ben Carter, a factory worker: he is the mayor of Great Dawley parish, which includes the Malinsee part of this ward. The Conservatives have selected Thomas Hoof, who is an IT director for a multinational recycling company and has served on a number of parishes in the Telford area (but not on either of parishes covering this ward). Also on the ballot paper in Malinslee and Dawley Bank are Patrick Doddy for the Lib Dems, construction worker and martial arts enthusiast Doug Halsall for Reform UK, and Jazz Holmes for the Green Party. Glantwymyn;
Llanyre with Nantmel; and
Yscir with Honddu Isaf and Llanddew

Powys council, Mid Wales; caused respectively by the resignations of councillors Elwyn Vaughan of Plaid Cymru, Claire Johnson-Wood and Iain McIntosh. Our series of by-elections to replace Welsh councillors who have been elected to Senedd Cymru now resumes. Unlike Shaun Davies in Telford, Welsh members of either Parliament are required to resign any council seats which they hold under Welsh local government law. We have four such polls today, three of which occur in the three Welsh counties which merged to create the modern county of Powys in 1974. All of the Powys wards up for election today are rural wards which cover large areas of mid-Wales. The northernmost of those counties, and the largest by population, was Montgomeryshire to the north. Here we find Glantwymyn ward, which surrounds the town of Machynlleth on three sides and sprawls over the lower part of the Dovey valley which marks the traditional boundary between North and South Wales. Glantwymyn is the Welsh-language name for the village of Cemmaes Road, whose English name comes from a now-closed railway station on the Cambrian railway line. The only railway station within the ward now is Dovey Junction, which is in a very isolated location to the west of Machynlleth (the nearest road is over half a mile away) and exists solely to allow passengers to change between the Cambrian's Aberystwyth and Pwllheli branches. In the north of the ward we find the Centre for Alternative Technology, an eco centre which has been demonstrating practical solutions for a sustainable future since 1973; unfortunately, it is no longer open to walk-up visitors. Although Machynlleth town is a separate Powys ward, it hosts the largest of this ward's three polling station. Radnorshire had the smallest population of any old Welsh county, and its upland and hilly nature was not well-suited to agriculture meaning that the local squires were chronically hard-up. As an old local poem goes:
Alas, alas poor Radnorshire!
Never a park, nor never a deer
Never a man with five hundred a year
Save Richard Fowler of Abbey Cwm Hir
The Fowlers were large landowners based in the village of Abbeycwmhir, which is a tiny vilage off the beaten track in the middle of nowhere. It was probably busier in the 12th century when construction work began on Cwmhir Abbey, which was intended to be the largest abbey in Wales; only the nave was ever completed, but even that was longer than several cathedral naves. The abbey is the final resting place of the body (but not the head) of the last native Prince of Wales Llywelyn ap Gruffudd, who was killed in an ambush in 1282. Abbeycwmhir is one of several scattered villages in the Llanyre with Nantmel ward, which covers countryside to the east and north of Rhayader and to the west of Llandrindod Wells. Despite the order of the names the largest centre of opulation is Nantmel, on the A44 main road from Rhayader to Leominster; Llanyre lies to the west of Llandrindod Wells, while the ward also takes in the St Harmon community to the north of Rhayader. Within the ward boundary is the former Roman fort at Castell Collen, to the west of Llandrindod. The Romans were also present in Breconshire at Cicucium, a fort to the west of modern-day Brecon located at the confluence of the River Usk and the River Yscir. Like Castell Collen, Cicucium was contructed around AD 75; it was originally garrisonned by 500 cavalrymen from modern-day Spain, but what they thought of the Welsh weather is not recorded. The military still have a presence in Breconshire, but today they concentrate on the Sennybridge training area which covers a large area of upland to the north. Ruins from a later era can be found at Llanddew to the north of Brecon, where there was a mediaeval palace belonging to the bishops of St Davids. In the late 12th century this palace was home to the priest and historian Gerald of Wales, who was archdeacon of Brecon at the time. Gerald's series of books on Ireland and Wales, and his polemic on Henry II and the Angevin dynasty De principis instructione ("Instruction for a Ruler"), form valuable sources for the mediaeval historian of today. Llanddew village hall is one of the six polling stations in Yscir with Honddu Isaf and Llanddew ward. The Honddu Isaf community, as the name suggests, covers the lower Honddu valley to the north of Brecon; its polling station is in the village of Lower Chapel. The largest polling station here, with 392 electors on the roll, is in the village of Cradoc to the north-west of Brecon which lies in the shadow of the Iron Age hillfort at Pen-y-crug. Cradoc is one of a number of villages on the southern fringes of this ward which lie wholly or partly within the Bannau Brycheiniog national park. Following the 2024 Westminster boundary changes and the 2026 Senedd electoral reform, there is no longer any constituency wholly within Powys. Montgomeryshire is combined for Westminster purposes with southern parts of the Wrexham council area in the Montgomeryshire and Glyndŵr constituency, and its Labour MP Steve Witherden - whose family were involved in the founding of the Centre for Alternative Techology - will have had his electoral base in the Glyndŵr part of that seat. In the 2026 Senedd election Montgomeryshire was the Maldwyn part of the Gwynedd Maldwyn constituency, which takes in most of the Gwynedd council area to the west; this seat gave 45% of the vote and 4 seats to Plaid Cymru, and 27% of the vote and 2 seats to Reform UK. Elected in fourth place on the Plaid list for Gwynedd Maldwyn was Elwyn Vaughan, who had served since 2017 as the Powys councillor for Glantwymyn ward which, unusually for Powys, is a predomninantly Welsh-speaking area. Vaughan had won his seat in 2017 rather narrowly, defeating a Conservative candidate by 55-45. He was re-elected for a second term unopposed in 2022, but has been involved in contested elections since nonetheless: Vaughan was the Plaid candidate for Montgomeryshire in the 2021 Senedd election and for Montgomeryshire and Glyndŵr in 2024. Radnorshire hasn't had a single MP of its own since 1918, when its small population meant it was merged with Breconshire to form the Brecon and Radnorshire parliamentary seat. This was expanded in 2024 by the addition of the Swansea valley to create a Brecon, Radnor and Cwm Tawe constituency which is the seat of Wales' only Lib Dem MP, David Chadwick. The Swansea valley wards of that seat are administered by Neath Port Talbot council, and the Senedd electoral reform of 2026 merged it with the Neath and Swansea East constituency to create a 6-seat electoral area of Brycheiniog Tawe Nedd. There was no room for Radnorshire (Maesyfed) in that seat name, but the use of proportional representation means that there is room for all of the areas in this diverse constituency to be represented. In May Reform UK were top of the poll in Brycheiniog Tawe Nedd with 33% and they won 3 out of 6 seats; Plaid Cymru polled 29% and won 2 seats; the Liberal Democrats polled 12% and won 1 seat, with the party's Welsh leader Jane Dodds becoming their only MS; and Labour polled 9% and won nothing. Elected in second place on the Reform UK list for Brycheiniog Tawe Nedd was Iain McIntosh, a carpet and flooring shop owner from Brecon who featured in this column in June 2017 when he won a by-election in Yscir ward. That by-election had a rather unusual cause: nobody had been nominated as a candidate there in the May 2017 Powys council election. McIntosh was then in the Conservatives, and he was re-elected on their ticket in 2022 in the expanded ward of Yscir with Honddu Isaf and Llanddew by a 59-41 margin over the Lib Dems. He had subsequently defected to Reform UK. Following his election to the Senedd, McIntosh has closed his carpet and flooring shop and passed the business on to one of his former employees. Due to its location in Radnorshire Llanyre with Nantmel ward is part of the Brycheiniog Tawe Nedd constituency, but its former councillor Claire Johnson-Wood now finds herself as an MS representing Gwynedd Maldwyn after being elected in second place on the Reform UK list there. Johnson-Wood was first elected to Powys council in 2017 as a Conservative councillor for the former Llanyre ward, but in 2020 she defected to the Abolish the Welsh Assembly Party: in the 2021 Senedd election Johnson-Wood was top of the Abolish list for Mid and West Wales and also stood in the Brecon and Radnorshire constituency. In 2022 she was re-elected to Powys council as an independent candidate for Llanyre with Nantmel, defeating a Lib Dem candidate by the narrow margin of 54-46, before subsequently joining Reform UK in 2025. We have no repeat of Yscir 2017 this time and all three Powys by-elections have attracted a decent field. In Yscir with Honddu Isaf and Llanddew, Reform UK will hoping that their defection gain in Iain McIntosh will be succeeded by a by-election gain for his wife, Dawn McIntosh; in 2022 she was a Conservative candidate for Brecon East ward. The Conservatives' Mark Burton comes here straight from the last Powys council by-election in May, where he finished in fourth place in Llandrindod South ward. Standing for the Lib Dems is Colin Millichap, who farms sheep and cows in the Yscirfawr valley and is a former chairman of the local NFU branch.
