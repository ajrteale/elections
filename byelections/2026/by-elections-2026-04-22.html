"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

One by-election on Wednesday 22nd April 2026:

Barton and Winton

Salford council, Greater Manchester; caused by the death of Labour councillor David Lancaster.

We are just over two weeks away from the May 2026 local elections, when one-third of Salford city council will be up for election. Which makes it rather strange that Andrew's Previews is turning up now for a by-election in Barton upon Irwell, whose electors will now be called out to vote twice in the space of three weeks. Normally vacancies in thirds councils which arise in February, like this one, would be held over until the ordinary election in May; but either the local parties misjudged the timing or somebody really believes they can win this and doesn't want to wait. Either way, the timing now means extra time and expense for everybody involved.

Anyway, this gives Andrew's Previews an excuse to visit a part of Greater Manchester which is less than a mile away from the bright lights of the Trafford Centre, but still gets a bit overlooked. Which is a shame, because Barton upon Irwell is an area with an interesting story to tell. It was the first place east of Warrington where the Irwell could be forded, and a road bridge was built in the 1670s to replace the ford. This bridge was then demolished in 1745 to deny it to the Jacobite army, and a new Barton Bridge of three stone arches was built once the danger had passed.

The River Irwell here has been navigable for centuries, which presented a problem for the builders of the Duke of Bridgewater's Canal - which was intended to link the Duke's coalmines at Worsley, north of the river, with central Manchester south of the river. An initial plan for a flight of locks down to the river at Barton was rejected in favour of a stone aqueduct to take the canal over the Irwell - something which had never been done before over a navigable river. The Barton Aqueduct - deliberately designed to the same specifications as the existing road bridge of three arches just downstream - was opened to traffic in July 1761 and it quickly became one of the engineering wonders of its day. It's seen here in a 1793 watercolour by G F Yates.

All of this was swept away in the 1890s, when the lower course of the Irwell was turned into the Manchester Ship Canal to allow ocean-going vessels to reach the big city inland. The old stone bridges at Barton were too small to permit such ships, and they had to go. The replacement for the stone aqueduct is unique in the world: the Bridgewater Canal is now carried on a swinging aqueduct, which can be closed to narrowboat traffic and turn through 90 degrees when ships need to pass on the lower waterway.

The Barton Swing Aqueduct and the adjacent Barton Road Swing Bridge were both completed in 1894, and they are both controlled from a tower located on an island in the middle of the canal. The bridges are listed at Grade II* and have been considered for designation as a World Heritage Site, but the area immediately around them has been somewhat neglected and needs a fair bit of sprucing up for tourism to be a realistic proposition.

Once the canal had been completed, the Barton Road Swing Bridge - which became part of the A575 road between Stretford and Bolton - was the only toll-free crossing of the Manchester Ship Canal between Manchester and Warrington. It was also the only way in from the north to the massive Trafford Park industrial estate, and the level of both road and ship traffic led to chronic congestion. By the 1950s 9,600 vehicles were crossing the bridge every day, while it closed to traffic to allow ships to pass an average of 20 times a day. Relief eventually came in 1960 with the opening of the Barton High Level Bridge, which now carries the M60 motorway, and the Barton Road Swing Bridge was downgraded to a B-road (the B5211) for pedestrians and local traffic. Which there is still quite a lot of, particularly when the M60 is having one of its moments.

The B5211 runs north from Barton, initially on the west bank of the Bridgewater Canal, to reach Winton. This is a residential area hemmed in by motorways to the east and north and by the Liverpool and Manchester railway line to the south. Tucked away behind the housing to the west is Winton Park, which has recently had some money spent on it and is a very pleasant place to spend a spring afternoon.

Barton upon Irwell was once the centre of an extensive civil parish which took in Eccles, Irlam and Cadishead: the parish also extended south of the river to include Davyhulme and much of Trafford Park. This was reflected in 1894 by the creation of Barton upon Irwell Rural District, which administered the parishes of Barton Moss, Davyhulme, Flixton and (somewhat detached from the rest) Clifton until it was split up in the 1930s. Like many Rural Districts it did not administer the area it was actually named after: Barton upon Irwell itself had been incorporated into Eccles when that town became a borough in 1892. Eccles then became part of the City of Salford in the 1974 reorganisation.

Barton and Winton were two separate electoral wards of Salford until they were combined by a boundary review in 2021: doing this freed up space for a new ward to be created to cover the Salford Quays area, whose population is growing fast. The development of the BBC complex at MediaCityUK in Salford Quays also led to rather significant demographic change in Eccles, particularly in the Monton area where large family houses were available for the sort of money that would buy you a shoebox in London. Monton and its trendy wine bars are part of Salford's Eccles ward, and Barton and Winton ward as a whole remains rather more down-to-earth, but this ward's boundary does run up right to the edges of Monton and of Eccles town centre. The large Akcros Chemicals site, which cuts Eccles town centre off from the Ship Canal, lies at the eastern end of this ward.

Elections in Barton and Winton have dominated by the Labour Party for many years. This area has had Labour MPs continuously since 1945: the current representative is Michael Wheeler, a backbencher who is in his first term as the member for Worsley and Eccles. Wheeler cut his political teeth on Salford city council, where he represented Eccles ward from 2011 to 2021.

Salford council has had a Labour majority continuously since reorganisation in 1974, with political leadership provided by the elected mayor Paul Dennett who won his third term of office in 2024. Dennett is also Salford's representative on the Greater Manchester Combined Authority, whose members are Andy Burnham and the ten borough council leaders.

Both Mayor Dennett and Michael Wheeler MP weren't born when the late councillor David Lancaster began a political career which has literally reshaped the city of Salford. When he died in February at the age of 81, Lancaster was thought to have been England's longest-serving local councillor: he was first elected as far back as 1965, when he joined Eccles borough council as a 21-year-old, and he had represented Winton continuously since then for an extraordinary career of over 60 years in local government. After initially working in housing, Lancaster went into politics full-time in 1999 and in 2003 he became deputy leader of Salford council. He was a founder member of the Lowry Centre Trust and was one of the people who successfully lured the BBC to Salford Quays, creating the demographic and economic change already mentioned. In the 2016 New Year Honours Lancaster was appointed MBE for services to the community in Salford, and for charitable services to elderly people through the Salfordian Hotel Trust.

David Lancaster won his final term of office in 2023, so the winner of this by-election will need to seek re-election in May 2027. His ward last went to the polls in May 2024, when Labour held Barton and Winton with a 62-15 lead over the Green Party.

Defending this Wednesday by-election for Labour is Catherine Goodyer, a caseworker who is standing for election for the first time. Another name who is new to our ballot papers is the Green Party's Jack Groom, a trade union rep who works in the heritage sector. The Conservatives have selected Holly Muldoon, Antony Duke is standing for the Lib Dems, Kirsty Downie is an independent candidate endorsed by Your Party, and Reform UK have recruited one-time drag queen Michael Felse who has a fairly long electoral record on the radical right: Felse was the English Democrats candidate in the 2012 Salford mayoral election, and he was most recently seen on a ballot paper as part of the UKIP list for north-west England in the 2019 European elections. If you want an indication of what Downie and Duke think of their chances of winning this by-election, well, they are standing here again in the ordinary Salford council election in 15 days' time.
