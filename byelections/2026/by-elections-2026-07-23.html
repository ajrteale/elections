Grays Riverside; and
Grays Town

Thurrock council, Essex; caused respectively by the resignations of Reform UK councillors Joe Soper and Marc Stanley. We now come to two by-elections on the north bank of the Thames Estuary, to replace Reform UK councillors who submitted their resignations just two weeks after being elected to Thurrock council in May. Joe Soper and Marc Stanley both cited personal reasons for their resignations, with Soper indicating that he was focusing on helping a family member through serious illness while Stanley's resignation statement indicated that his personal circumstances had changed. Soper and Stanley were elected for the two wards in Grays, a town which lies on the north bank of the Thames estuary a few miles downstream from the Dartford crossing. This town was once known as Grays Thurrock, with "Grays" coming from the late 12th-century Norman landowners the de Grai family and "Thurrock" being derived from an Old English word for "bilge", which would have been a reference to the marshy land on the Thames riverside. Over time the name "Thurrock" came to refer to the wider area, and the full name of "Grays Thurrock" town has fallen into disuse. The bilge area on the riverside has been drained and turned into housing, which forms the majority of the Grays Riverside ward. This ward also extends to the north of the London, Tilbury and Southend railway line to take in an oil depot run by Goff Petroleum, which supplies heating oil to East Anglia and the South East, and the quieter surroundings of the Chafford Gorges nature park. This covers a series of disused chalk quarries, which are of interest to scientists both for their wildlife and for their geology. Grays Riverside ward also includes the main offices of Thurrock council, next to Grays railway station. To the north of the station is Grays Town ward, which covers the town centre and housing to the east and north. This is centred on St Thomas of Canterbury primary school - for those who remember my rant about the name "Thomas à Becket" last week, this is an acceptable name for Becket - and also includes the secondary school of Hathaway Academy, named after the adjacent Hathaway Road. Hathaway Academy's former pupils include Russell Brand, whose first acting role was in a school production of Bugsy Malone here. Grays is recognised by the ONS as part of the London Travel to Work Area, and its demographics look far more like a London ward than an Essex ward. In the 2021 census Grays Riverside ward, which then had slightly different boundaries, was in the top 10 wards in the Eastern region for black residents (15.3%) and in the top 100 wards across England and Wales for residents born in the 2004 EU eastern expansion states (9.9%) or in Romania or Bulgaria (7.9%). The ward is economically dominated by the transport and storage sector in the Port of London, which employs 12.5% of the workforce. Grays Thurrock, the predecessor ward to the present Grays Town, had demographics which were similar but not quite so extreme. The present ward boundaries only came in for the May 2026 local elections, in which both Grays wards were part of the very large Reform UK majority on Thurrock council - although only narrowly so. Reform UK won the three seats in Grays Riverside with 33% of the vote, against 29% for the Green Party and 26% for Labour: the final Reform UK councillor had a majority over the Greens of just 17 votes. Grays Town ward was more of a two-party fight, with Reform UK's winning score being 35% against 32% for Labour - whose slate included the defeated council leader Lynn Worrall - and 16% for the Greens; here the Reform UK majority for their third-placed councillor, Marc Stanley, was 55 votes. Both predecessor wards had consistently voted Labour in the period 2004-24, often with large majorities. The Commissioners are still in place following Thurrock council's recent bankruptcy, so any political change which the new Reform UK leadership might be able to put through could be limited. The 2026 Thurrock results will have been noted with some concern by Thurrock's Labour MP Jen Craft, who gained the seat from the Conservatives in 2024. Craft, who was educated at Grays Convent high school within Grays Town ward, had previously given up a civil service career in order to become a full-time carer for one of her children who has Down's syndrome. She has ongoing health challenges herself, having been diagnosed with OCD and bipolar disorder when she was in her 20s. Reform UK's defending candidate in Grays Town ward is John Pearson, who was one of only four unsuccessful candidates for the party in May when he contested West Thurrock and South Stifford ward. He is looking to hold off Labour's Amin Hoque, who stood in May in Aveley ward. The Green candidate here is Rhea Nathan. Also standing here are Joglur Rahman for the Conservatives, Simon Lancaster for the Lib Dems and independent candidate Neil Speight, a former independent Thurrock councillor who is trying to get straight onto the council after losing his seat in Stanford-le-Hope in May. In Grays Riverside ward the defending Reform UK candidate Jason Mitchell has hit the headlines during this campaign for dubious social media posts from some years ago: this didn't come out in the run-up to May's election when Mitchell was the runner-up in Chafford Hundred West ward. The Greens have changed candidate to Pauline Weir, who wants to hold the Reform UK administration to account if she is elected. Labour have selected Jo Potter, who was an unsuccessful candidate in May for Chadwell St Mary ward. Grays Riverside has a shorter candidate list, with the Conservatives' Srikanth Panjala completing a ballot paper with four options.

Cuxton, Halling and Riverside

Medway council, Kent; caused by the death of Conservative councillor Phil Filmer. We'll finish for the week in a Kentish ward - but not a Ward of Kent, because that would be on the eastern side of the River Medway. It's on the western side of the Medway valley that we find the vllages of Cuxton and Halling, which lie to the west of the Medway towns. This is the area where the navigable Medway estuary punches through the chalk of the North Downs, and large parts of this ward are within the North Downs National Landscape. Halling's economy was tradtionally based on chalk quarrying and a cement factory. The major quarry here has now been flooded to create St Andrews Lake, a centre for watersports. Further down the estuary is the village of Cuxton, whose name has been shortened over the centuries from its original form of Cucklestone. Here the parish church - dedicated to St Michael and All Angels - briefly had as its rector in 1610 one William Laud, who would go on to become Archbishop of Canterbury in 1633. Laud was a key ally of Charles I and he had introduced controversial religious and liturgical reforms, which made him a marked man when the English Civil War broke out shortly afterwards: in 1640 Laud was arrested and imprisoned in the Tower of London. Following an inconclusive impeachment trial in 1644, which clearly demonstrated that Laud was innocent of the charges laid against him, Parliament passed an Act of Attainder under whose terms Laud was executed in January 1645. Attainder was a concept of English common law under which traitors or other people fell foul of the King and/or Parliament could be executed and their heirs disinherited. Many prominent figures have had Acts of Attainder passed on them over the years, including King Richard III (posthumously, after his death at Bosworth) and a number of other leading figures in the Wars of the Roses, Henry VIII's fifth wife Queen Catherine Howard, Guy Fawkes and his fellow Gunpowder Plot conspirators, and the Duke of Monmouth for leading the 1685 Monmouth Rebellion. The last person to be attainted by Parliament was Lord Edward Fitzgerald in 1798, for leading a rebellion in Ireland; Fitzgerald had already died in the rebellion, so the effect of the Act was to confiscate his estate. The process was last attempted in 1820, after Queen Caroline had been accused of adultery: a Pains and Penalties Bill (a milder version of the same idea, which did not involve the death penalty) was narrowly passed by the Lords, but Lord Liverpool's government then withdrew the Bill after realising that the Commons would likely vote it down. The penalties of attainder, forfeiture and corruption of blood for treason were finally abolished by the Forfeiture Act 1870, which still has the effect of banning persons convicted of treason from holding public office or voting in the UK. Cuxton and Halling makes the top 60 wards in England and Wales for residents employed in the construction sector (15.4%), which is perhaps approrpriate given that many of the major communication links to and from Kent pass through Cuxton. Cuxton railway station is on the Medway Valley line, which is one of the few Southeastern routes that does not radiate from London: passengers from Cuxton or Halling stations heading to the capital will need to change trains at Strood. Climbing up the hillside above the village is the Chatham and Dover main line towards London, which has no station here. And crossing both of those railway lines and the river at right-angles are the eight traffic lanes of the M2 motorway and the two tracks of High Speed One, which thunder high above the river on the three Medway Viaducts. The original M2 viaduct, which opened in 1963 at a cost of £2.5 million, now carries only the eastbound carriageway of the motorway: the westbound viaduct and the railway viaduct both date from 2003, when the current UK rail speed record of 208 miles per hour was set here by a specially-modified Eurostar train. The two road bridges relieved the old Rochester Bridge carrying the A2 between Rochester and Strood, which even in the 1950s was generating traffic jame notorious enough that they were mentioned by Ian Fleming in a James Bond novel. Until 2023 the parishes of Cuxton and Halling formed the only single-member ward of Medway council, which covers the five Medway Towns plus a number of parishes to the west for which Strood - the only Medway Town west of the river - is the most convenient urban centre. Boundary changes for the 2023 election created the present ward by adding the Waterside area of southern Strood and a second councillor. The previous Cuxton and Halling ward had been safely Conservative and on topline figures from 2023 the expanded ward looked like it had continued in that vein with 44% for the Conservatives, 32% for the Greens and 21% for Labour; but much of the Conservative lead was a personal vote for the former Cuxton and Halling ward councillor Matt Fearn and not all of it transferred to his running-mate Phil Filmer. Filmer ended up winning the second seat just 44 votes ahead of the lead Green candidate. Cuxton, Halling and Riverside ward is politically in the minority on Medway council, which is run by Labour. Labour also hold the local parliamentary seat of Rochester snd Strood, which was gained from the Conservatives in 2024 by Australian-born Lauren Edwards who featured in this column when she won a Medway council by-election in December 2021. At that time Edwards was working as a financial regular at the Bank of England; she also had extensive experience as a union rep. The Conservatives will be defending this by-election to replace Phil Filmer, who had only represented Cuxton, Halling and Riverside ward since 2023 but whose council service went back a long time before that. He was first elected in 1991 to Rochester-upon-Medway city council, serving as a councillor for All Saints ward until 1995 when he lost re-election in a different ward. In 2000 Filmer was elected to Medway council for All Saints ward, which was based on the rural Isle of Grain, and in 2003 he transferred to the successor Peninsula ward which he represented for 20 years. For most of that time Filmer sat in the Medway council cabinet with the frontline services portfolio; from 2021 to 2023 he was also Senior Warden of the Rochester Bridge Trust, a charity which has maintained Rochester Bridge since the 14th century. He died in May at the age of 74. Defending this seat for the Conservatives is Richard Thorne, who is the chair of Halling parish council; Thorne is also seeking to return to Medway council, where he represented Strood South ward from 2019 to 2023. The Green Party have selected Trish Marchant, a healthcare engineer who gives an address in Gillingham on the far side of the Medway Towns; Marchant's most recent election campaign was in a Medway council by-election in Gillingham South ward in February last year, and she was also the Greens' parliamentary candidate for Gillingham and Rainham back in 2010. Standing for Labour is Iain Childs, who contested Rainham South East ward in 2023. Also on the ballot are Ronald Gillett for the Lib Dems and Reform UK's William Burvill, who is chair of the party's Rochester and Strood and will be hoping to join two previous Reform UK by-election winners on Medway council.
