Tottington

Bury council, Greater Manchester; caused by the resignation of Luis McBriar. Today is Maundy Thursday, which for many years was a dies non for the purposes of the electoral timetable: no polls could be scheduled for Maundy Thursday, and it didn't count towards the calculation of deadlines for close of nominations, postal vote applications and so on. This rule was abolished by the Electoral Administration Act 2006, and by-elections have been held on this date since 2009, but Maundy Thursday is still an unpopular time for local by-elections: apart from the fact that it lies immediately before a four-day bank holiday weekend, it normally falls in the run-up to the main local elections in May and most local parties want to keep their powder dry for that event. Which makes it rather curious that Bury council have been asked to organise a by-election for today in Tottington ward, when the ward will be up for another seat in just five weeks' time. Located on relatively high ground to the north-west of Bury, Tottington is one of the six towns which merged in 1974 to create the Metropolitan Borough of Bury. It's quite a middle-class area, but Tottington still has the history of a classic Pennine textile town, and there were cotton mills going here before 1800. John Gorton, who had bought Tottington Hall in 1770, was from a family which had made its fortune in textiles; and the money brought in by the Gorton family from the Tottington and Kirklees mills and various bleachworks paid for a number of Georgian buildings which still stand in the town today. Tottington Hall, which was extensively remodelled in the early 19th century, subsequently served as the home of Tottington urban district council from 1918 to 1974 and is now the town's library; St Anne's church dates from 1799. One effect of this relatively early industrialisation is that Tottington claims to have one of the UK's oldest continuously-performing brass bands. The Tottington Public Band goes back to 1835 and they are still very much in business more than 190 years on. Here they are in action on Whit Friday 2010, performing at the Mossley contest. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6LeSCXzD2Jg The modern Tottington electoral ward takes in two other villages. To the south is Walshaw, which is dominated by the massive Christ Church: this building, which dates from 1888, was built as a memorial to a Manchester textile manufacturer called Jesse Haworth. Walshaw is currently separated by open fields (although this might not remain the case for much longer) from some housing on the western edge of Bury proper around Elton High School. This school has recently been comprehensively rebuilt and its most famous former pupils - the England football and netball stars Gary, Phil and Tracey Neville - might not recognise it now. It's a polling station for today's by-election. (I am informed by my brother-in-law, who is a teacher at Elton, that the kids here don't break up for Easter until today.) The third and smallest village in Tottington ward is Affetside, which lies right on the top of the hill at an altitude just short of 1,000 feet. The fact that the only road through Affetside is arrow-straight and called Watling Street is a big clue as to its origin: it was originally the Roman road from Manchester to Ribchester. This road was the boundary between Bury and Bolton boroughs, which left Affetside divided between the two districts, until a boundary change in 1991 placed Affetside entirely within Bury. The summit of Watling Street is marked by the stone stump of Affetside Cross, which is probably of mediaeval origin, and by the adjacent Millennium Green which occupies the site of a former bus turning circle. Nearby is the Pack Horse pub, which has been here since 1443: your columnist can report that it serves excellent food and beer and has views for miles around. The Pack Horse also houses a rather grisly relic: on display here is the skull of George Whowell, who executed the Earl of Derby in nearby Bolton in 1651. In 1882 the railway came to Tottington with the opening of a single-track branch line from Bury to Holcombe Brook. This was electrified with overhead lines at the early date of 1913 at the expense of the railway manufacturers Dick, Kerr and Company, who were considering tendering for an electric railway contract in Brazil and used the Holcombe Brook branch as a testbed for the equipment and rolling stock. The overhead power system proved to have a short life, and in 1918 the railway through Tottingto was re-electrified as an extension of the third-rail system then used on the Manchester to Bury line. The Holcombe Brook branch line closed in the 1960s and it has been turned into a footpath known as the Kirklees Trail. The railway passes next to Tottington St John's cricket club, where I once spent a bizarre afternoon in 2009 watching cricket while playing music. The bizarre bit was that this was Boxing Day, and although the artificial wicket had been cleared the rest of the field was under several inches of snow.
10 overs in and the comedy moment of the day arrived. Having already been pelted with snowballs on his way to the crease, former Totts boy Cotters was at the wicket. Just when it couldn't be any more idyllic with the snow, the blue skies and the festive ambience, the band started to play their first festive carol ... and within 15 seconds they were ducking for cover as Cotters mashed the ball directly at them. Much hilarity ensued!

This tower, located above the village of Holcombe, is a memorial to the Conservative prime minister Sir Robert Peel who was born in nearby Bury to a family of wealthy textile manufacturers. It's a local tradition to climb up to the tower on Good Friday, which is tomorrow. Another local son who ended up in the Commons was Sir Edward Holden, who was born in a Tottington pub in 1848 and rose through the ranks of the banking industry to become managing director and then chairman of the Midland Bank from 1898 until his death in 1919 - by which point the Midland was one of the largest banks in the world. (It's now part of the HSBC empire.) Holden also served one term as a Liberal MP, and he would have become Chancellor of the Exchequer in 1908 had David Lloyd George not threatened to resign. Appropriately, Holden's constituency was Heywood - whose boundaries then included his Tottington birthplace. Since 1918 Tottington has been linked with Bury for parliamentary purposes, and it is currently included within the Bury North constituency. This seat has voted for the government at every election since its creation in 1983 with the exception of a Labour win in 2017, when James Frith - who had lost in 2015 by just 378 votes - defeated the previous Conservative MP David Nuttall. Frith then lost his seat to the Conservatives' James Daly in 2019 by 105 votes, the second-closest margin of any seat in that election, but he won the rematch convincingly in 2024. Tottington ward in general is a middle-class area of Bury which is normally a Conservative banker. In this century Labour have only won the ward once, in 2012; their councillor was then forced to resign in 2015 after pleading guilty to making indecent images of children, and the Conservatives got the seat back in the resulting by-election. However, the Conservatives did lose one seat here in 2022, when the current ward boundaries were introduced, after long-serving Conservative councillor Yvonne Wright sought re-election as an independent candidate and topped the poll. Her seat is up for re-election in May. This by-election is to replace Luis McBriar, who has served for Tottington ward since 2021 and was most recently re-elected in May 2024: on that occasion he polled 46% of the vote against 29% for Labour and 25% for a Bury Independents candidate. McBriar resigned from the council because he has taken up a new job which is politically restricted. Luis McBriar was elected as a Conservative candidate, but in July 2025 the Conservative group on Bury council split down the middle as the result of a vicious internal dispute. The Tory group leader Russell Bernstein, his deputy Jo Lancaster, and two other councillors including McBriar walked off to form a new council group called "Together for Bury". That split left the Conservatives in third place on the council: the latest composition has 32 Labour councillors against 7 for the localist party Radcliffe First, 5 remaining Conservatives, 3 Together for Bury councillors plus this vacancy, 2 defectors to Reform UK (one elected for Radcliffe First, the other for the Conservatives) and independent Yvonne Wright. Together for Bury have registered with the Electoral Commission as a political party, and their defending candidate in this by-election is retired policeman Greg Keeley: he is a former Conservative councillor for Tottington ward, serving from the 2015 by-election until he retired in 2021. The Conservatives will want their seat back and they have selected Emma Lee, who lives in Walshaw and runs a "paint your own pottery" business. The Labour candidate is Drew Bell, who is an educational professional. The Bury Independents haven't returned after their third-place finish in 2024, so completing the ballot paper are Chloe King for the Greens and project manager George Martin for Reform UK. Hareholme and Waterfoot

Rossendale council, Lancashire; caused by the resignation of Labour councillor Thomas Belli. Our next poll this week is not far at all from Tottington. We've now come down off the hill into the Irwell valley and the strange landscape of the Forest of Rossendale, where high ground is punctuated by steep-sided valleys along which all lines of communication run. The vista might look like the South Wales valleys, but the industry is different: the road to Waterfoot is lined with former shoe factories, which now serve as distribution points for footwear made elsewhere. Waterfoot lies at the meeting point of two valleys, where the Whitewell Brook (flowing south) merges with the River Irwell (flowing east to west). Not far to the east of here the Irwell valley narrows into the short but deep Thrutch gorge, whose bottom is barely wide enough for the B*cup-Rawtenstall road to pass alongside the river. When the railway was extended to B*cup in the early 1850s, the railway engineers had to bore three tunnels along the southern side of the gorge. The railway line through Waterfoot closed down in the 1960s, but in recent years the two shorter tunnels have been reopened for the benefit of pedestrians and cyclists. Perhaps the next job would be to do something about Trickett's Arcade on the main road junction, a set of Victorian shop buildings with a glass canopy outside which has been lying empty for decades. The Hareholme part of the ward name refers to the eastern part of the town of Rawtenstall (pronounced Rotten-stall). This is now the end of the railway line, but the railway here is not run primarily for the benefit of commuters: this is the northern terminus of the East Lancashire Railway, which runs heritage trains here from Bury and Heywood for the tourist market. In recent years Rawtenstall town centre has been spruced up a bit for the benefit of tourists, and on your columnist's recent visit I found an open square full of open-air dining and street food stalls. In March. Round the corner, Mr Fitzpatrick's has been in business here since 1890 and claims to be the UK's last surviving temperance bar. Its menu combines the traditional favourites like dandelion and burdock with a selection of milkshakes and other non-alcoholic drinks to suit the modern-day palate. Not far away is Rawtenstall's shiny new bus station, which serves the commuter market with express buses straight down the motorway to Manchester: these are branded as the Witch Way, because they originate in Pendle and Burrnley. Famous people from this corner of Rozzendale include the Dean of Westminster David Hoyle, who was born in Waterfoot in 1957 and officiated at the funeral of Elizabeth II in 2022; the supermodel Agyness Deyn who is from Cloughfold in between Rawtenstall and Waterfoot; and the light music composer Ernest Tomlinson who attended B*cup and Rawtenstall Grammar School in Waterfoot and served for five years as musical director of the Rossendale Male Voice Choir - a post he took over from his father. Tomlinson helped to ensure the survival of his genre by establishing the Library of Light Orchestral Music, which for many years was housed by him in a Lancashire barn; it's now in the Victoria Hall in Bolton. One of the buildings on the main road in Waterfoot is now occupied by the constituency office of the local MP Andy Macnae, who defeated the Liz Truss-era cabinet minister Sir Jake Berry in 2024. Macnae is a former mountaineer who ran an adventure sports company before his election to Parliament. He represents Rossendale and Darwen, a constituency which sprawls across the moorland to the north of Greater Manchester: this seat has appalling internal communications and has only managed to survive through multiple boundary reviews because all the alternatives to it are worse. Like Bury North in the previous section, Rossendale and Darwen is a near-bellwether: it has voted for the government in every election since its creation in 1983 with the exception of a Labour win in 1992. This marginal theme extends to Rossendale council, which has historically been one to watch because most of its wards are both marginal and politically and demographically similar to one another: when one party wins, it tends to win big. Mind, the former Hareholme ward was a bit of an exception to this: it voted Labour at every Rossendale election between 2002 and 2023 with the exception of Conservative wins in 2004 (reversed in a by-election the following year) and 2008. Andy Macnae was one of the councillors for the old Hareholme ward from 2016 to 2021. Boundary changes for the 2024 election added Waterfoot from the former Whitewell ward, which had a Green Party presence: but Labour still won the new ward comfortably, with topline figures of 47% for the Labour slate, 29% for the Greens and 24% for the Conservatives. This places Hareholme and Waterfoot within the Labour majority on Rossendale council, as the 2024 elections returned 20 Labour councillors against 5 Conservatives, 3 Greens and 2 councillors for the Whitworth localist group Community First. At this point Rossendale council moved from the thirds cycle to all-out elections, so there are no polls due here in May. One of the Community First Rossendale councillors died last year and Reform UK won the resulting by-election. This built on an excellent performance by RUK in last year's Lancashire county elections, when they won four of Rossendale's five county divisions on the way to taking over control of County Hall in Preston. The Reform UK total included the Mid-Rossendale division which covers Hareholme and Waterfoot ward, and which the party gained from Labour by 14 percentage points. In both the 2021 and 2017 county elections Mid-Rossendale elections had been photofinishes in straight fights between Labour and the Conservatives: Labour won in 2021 by 60 votes, the Conservatives in 2017 by 8 votes. Reform UK will be looking for another gain in this by-election, which would give them group status on Rossendale council. Wigmore

Luton council, Bedfordshire; caused by the disqualification of Liberal Democrat councillor Alan Skepelhorn. Our two other wards up for election today are both in areas which are associated with holidays. Indeed, the Wigmore ward of Luton is full of holidaymakers right now, although it's probably fair to say that this is not their ultimate destination. Wigmore ward covers privately-developed 1970s housing at the eastern end of Luton's built-up area, together with the terminal, apron and runways of Luton Airport. The airport and its associated industries dominate the ward's economy: for example easyjet (who occupy a hangar painted in corporate orange) has its head office in Wigmore ward. TUI Airways are also based here in an office block called Wigmore House, but one wonders how much longer this will be the case for given that TUI have recently transferred all their Luton-based aircraft to Gatwick. The airport itself is owned by Luton council which used to fund a number of services from its dividend - until the Covid-19 pandemic led to the airport's dividend disappearing virtually overnight, forcing the council to seek a massive bailout from central government. Luton is the UK's fifth-busiest airport after Heathrow, Gatwick, Manchester and Stansted: in 2024 it handled 16.7 million passengers, down from 18.2 million in 2019, with its busiest route wafting passengers from paradise to Bucharest. The airport and Wigmore ward are part of the Luton South and South Bedfordshire parliamentary seat, which (under this name and its previous name of Luton South) has been a safe Labour constituency since 1997; only in 2010 did the Labour majority here fall below 10 percentage points. Its MP since 2019 has been Labour backbencher Rachel Hopkins, who is the second generation of her family to represent the town: her father Kelvin Hopkins was the Labour MP for Luton North from 1997 to 2019. Rachel Hopkins has previously sat on Luton council, which has had a Labour majority since 1997. The opposition to Labour here at local level mainly comes from the Liberal Democrats, and Wigmore ward is squarely in the opposition camp when it comes to council elections. The 2023 election in this ward returned one of the strongest Lib Dem scores in the country, with the party polling 83% in a straight fight with the Labour slate. One of the Wigmore ward councillors, Claire Gallagher, resigned the following year after finding herself unable to properly devote time to both her young family and her democratic duties. The resulting by-election, with a wider choice of candidates, was held by the Lib Dems with 55% of the vote against 15% for an independent and 11% for the Conservatives (Andrew's Previews 2024, page ??). The winning Lib Dem candidate Adrees Latif, who was born in Azad Kashmir and came to the UK at the age of eight, was one of the main players in uncovering one of the most notorious scandals of recent years: from 2001 to 2018 Latif was the subpostmaster at Caddington post office to the south-west of Luton, and he was one of the key witnesses in the case of Bates v Post Office whose evidence persuaded the Court that the Post Office's Horizon computer system wasn't working properly. Latif was restored to the Lib Dem whip last month, after the local party had suspended him while they investigated a now-withdrawn complaint which he subsequently described as "malicious". This means that the Luton Lib Dems are now only one councillor down in Wigmore ward, after Alan Skepelhorn was disqualified in February for failing to attend any council meetings in six months. His period of absence had run from 15th July 2025 to 20th January this year, when Skepelhorn attended a full council meeting after Luton council's democratic services team had failed to notice his disqualification. He had previously been absent from the council for more than six months after contracting a serious case of COVID-19 during the second wage in January 2021, but on that occasion the council voted to grant him leave of absence. Skepelhorn was a long-serving councillor who had been first elected to Luton council in 2003 for Round Green ward; after losing his seat there in 2011, he returned in April 2013 by winning a by-election in Wigmore ward which he had represented since then. Fremington

North Devon council; caused by the death of independent councillor Neil Denton. Our final poll of the week is in that traditional holiday area of the West Country. Fremington is a large village on the south bank of the Taw estuary, a few miles to the west of Barnstaple. The village has a small quay on the river, and that made it sufficiently important to send members to Parliament for a time during the fourteenth century. Fremington's main exports were pottery and power. The pottery came from Fremington lying on a large deposit of Ice Age boulder clay: an unusual geological feature for Devon, which is far south enough to have mostly escaped glaciation during the last Ice Age. The power originally came from a small coal-fired power station in the nearly village of Yelland, one of only a handful of power stations in the South West; the coal to supply it was ferried across the Bristol Channel from the South Wales coalfields until they closed down. Electricity from Fremington is now generated more sustainably from the Horsacott solar farm, which came online in 2013 and has recently been extensively refurbished. The military were also important here. The Royal Marines airfield at Chivenor is just across the water, and until 2009 Fremington was home to an Army camp which was used during the Second World War as a US Army hospital, rehabilitating casualties from D-Day. But this is now gone; the Army camp has been built on and commuting to Barnstaple is now the main economic driver for the area. Fremington is part of the North Devon constituency and the North Devon local government district, which both have the same boundaries and are both run by the Liberal Democrats. Indeed, the 2024 election saw the council leader Ian Roome, a Yorkshireman by birth who had first come to North Devon when the RAF posted him to Chivenor, join the green benches by gaining the North Devon constituency from the Conservatives. The Liberal Democrats had previously gained overall control of North Devon council in 2023 after four years of running a minority administration. The 2023 election returned 22 Lib Dem councillors against 10 independents, 7 Conservatives and 3 Greens. The Lib Dem caucus has since increased to 24 councillors, as a result of a by-election gain from the Conservatives last year in the neighbouring Instow ward (Andrew's Previews 2025, forthcoming) and the recruitment to the party's ranks of Frank Biederman. Biederman has dominated local elections in Fremington since he gained his seat from the Conservatives as an independent candidate in 2011, and at the last North Devon council elections 72% of Fremington ward's voters gave him one of their two votes. On that occasion all of the top four places in Fremington ward went to independent candidates, with topline figures giving the independents a 72-12 win over the Conservatives. Biederman has also been the Devon county councillor for the larger division of Fremington Rural since 2013, and he was easily re-elected there last year under his new Lib Dem colours. Frank Biederman has had several independent ward colleagues during his 15 years on North Devon council. He was first elected alongside Joanne Bell, who resigned almost immediately and was replaced in an August 2011 by-election by Chris Turner. Turner didn't seek re-election in 2015 and his seat went to Tony Wood, who died three years later and was replaced in a June 2018 by-election by Jayne Mackie (Andrew's Previews 2018, page 230). Mackie then stod down in 2023 and her seat went to Neil Denton, who passed away in January as the result of a brain tumour. Denton was well-known in Fremington as a carpenter, tradesman and firefighter who was heavily involved in the lcoal community. The third Fremington by-election in 15 years will result in a change of council composition, because there is no new independent candidate to replace Denton. We have a free-for-all! Partisan candidates did poorly here in 2023, so vote share changes in this by-election are going to be wild even by the standards of recent months. The Conservatives have selected Sue Kingdom who is the chair of Fremington parish council (which is larger than this ward and also takes in the Barnstaple suburb of Bickington); in the 2023 North Devon elections she contested Mortehoe ward. Labour have reselected Douglas McLynn who finished sixth out of seven candidates here in 2023. Starting from last place are the Liberal Democrats, who have recruited as their candidate former ward councillor Jayne Mackie who won the 2018 by-election here. The Green Party's Jules Credgington is fighting his first election campaign, while Reform UK's Derek Sargent has been a regular candidate in recent years in the Torridge area: Sargent was the UKIP candidate for Torridge and West Devon in the 2015 general election, and he previously appeared in this column as the UKIP candidate for a December 2017 by-election to Torridge council in Westward Ho! ward (Andrew's Previews 2017, page ??).
