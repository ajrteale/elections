Dowlais and Pant

Merthyr Tydfil council, Glamorgan; caused by the resignation of David Hughes. We now travel east from the Llanelli area to reach an area which is synonymous with iron and steelmaking. In 1759 nine business partners established the Dowlais ironworks on a hillside to the east of Merthyr Tydfil, a location where the presence of all the required raw materials for ironworking - iron ore, limestone, and coal to power the furnaces - outweighed the disadvantages of the sloping site. John Josiah Guest became the majority owner in 1815 and rode the Industrial Revolution to build up one of the largest ironworks in the world: Dowlais won an enormous amout of work off the back of supplying the tracks for the Stockton and Darlington Railway in 1821, and by 1845 the site had 18 blast furnaces in operation and employed more than 7,000 men. Following Sir John Josiah's death control of the business passed to his widow Lady Charlotte Guest, a fascinating figure in her own right who had introduced welfare schemes for the workforce and went on to publish the first single-volume collection of the Mabinogion in Welsh and English, and to the works manager G T Clark who revitalised the Dowlais works by licensing the Bessemer process for steel production and developing the Goat Mill, at the time the most powerful rolling mill in the world. The Guest family eventually sold their interest in the Dowlais ironworks in 1899 to industrialist Arthur Keen, who merged it with the Midlands metalbashing firm of Nettlefolds three years later to form Guest, Kenn and Nettlefolds. Today GKN is still in business as a large British multinational company in the aerospace and automotive sectors - neither of which existed back in 1759, of course. In 2023 the firm's parent company spun off GKN's automotive and powder metallurgy business into a new firm, which took the name Dowlais Group after the the Dowlais Ironworks of all those years ago. Dowlais was listed on the London stock exchange, and it was a member of the FTSE 250 until it was taken over earlier this year by an American firm. Meanwhile, on the eastern slopes above Merthyr not much is left of the Dowlais ironworks, which finally went cold in 1987. These days the main factories in Dowlais are in the food sector, with OP Chocolates and the St Merryn meat-packing plant being major employers. Mining and quarrying is also still a feature of the ward: part of the large Ffos-y-fran opencast coalmine is within the ward boundary, as are a number of quarries on the southern slopes of the Brecon Beacons. The A465 Heads of the Valleys Road links all this to the outside world, and long-running works to rebuild the road junction at Dowlais Top were completed last year. There is also some tourism here: a small part of the ward is within the Bannau Brycheiniog national park boundary, and the village of Pant is the southern terminus of the narrow-guage Brecon Mountain Railway. Sir John Josiah Guest was the first MP for Merthyr Tydfil, which was enfranchised by the First Reform Act in 1832 and increased to two MPs by the Second Reform in 1868. One of them was the first Labour Party leader Keir Hardie, who served the town from 1900 until his death in 1915. The Merthyr Tydfil parliamentary borough of 1832-1918 was larger than the present council area, as it also included Aberdare and Mountain Ash in the Cynon valley to the west; in fact, its boundaries were not that dissimilar to the Merthyr Tydfil and Aberdare seat created by the 2024 redistribution, although that seat only has one MP. He is Labour backbencher Gerald Jones, who was first elected in 2015 for the predecessor seat of Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney. Jones defeated the previous Cynon Valley MP Beth Winter for the 2024 Labour selection here. Winter subsequently quit the Labour Party, and after some involvement with Your Party she was an independent candidate in the 2026 Senedd election in Pontypridd Cynon Merthyr, polling 3.2% which was nowhere near enough to win a seat. Plaid Cymru topped the poll in this constituency in May, polling 39% and winning 3 seats; Reform UK won 31% and 2 seats, and Labour scraped into the final seat with 13% of the vote. Not that this has much relevance in the context of elections to Merthyr Tydfil council, which have closely fought for many years between Labour and independent candidates. The town's ward boundaries haven't seen any change since the 1980s, and the only effect of the 2022 boundary review here was to rename two wards and to remove a councillor from three wards which were oversized: Dowlais ward was affected by both of these changes, as Pant was added to the ward name and its representation was cut from four councillors to three. The cut in the council size from 33 councillors to 30 might not have been a wise choice, as was demonstrated when the 2022 Merthyr elections returned a tie with 15 Labour and 15 independent councillors; the present administration is a coalition of Labour and some of the independents. The renamed Dowlais and Pant returned three independent councillors in 2022 by the wide margin of 64-28 over Labour. One of them was David Hughes, who was re-elected for his second term of office having gained his seat from Labour in 2017. In 2025 Hughes joined Reform UK, and last month he was elected to the Senedd in second place on the Reform UK list for Pontypridd Cynon Merthyr. Reform UK will want to convert their defection gain into a by-election gain here, and they have selected Terry Donegan. One new independent candidate has come forward: Nick Meaney is a former Army military intelligence veteran who now works in cybersecurity and property development. Labour have selected Dean Barrett, who gives an address in the ward. Also standing are James Cushen for Plaid Cymru, Janeen Davies for the Greens and Roxanne Richardson for the Conservatives. Dawlish South West

Teignbridge council, Devon; caused by the death of Liberal Democrat councillor Mike James. Moving into England, the summer holiday season is nearly upon us and that means boom time for the south-west. The Devon town of Dawlish will expect to get a fair chunk of that business thanks to its location on the English Riviera coast, around 12 miles south of Exeter, and on the Great Western railway line which takes a spectacular course along the seafront: overlooking the beach here, tunnelling through the cliffs between Dawlish and Teignmouth there. The railway alignment here is the outcome of one of Isambard Kingdom Brunel's more harebrained schemes, in which trains between Exeter and Newton Abbot were to be drawn by atmospheric pressure from a vacuum pipe between the tracks: this did operate for a period in 1847-48, but was quickly abandoned due to technical problems. The atmospheric scheme required level track, which forced a course close to sea level: this has proven to be vulnerable to the elements, and part of the line at Dawlish was washed away by a storm in 2014. The constant battle here against coastal erosion has raised questions over the long-term sustainability of the line through Dawlish, which is the only railway connection to Plymouth and Cornwall. Dawlish South West ward covers the southern half of the town together with the parish of Ashcombe to the west. It has returned the Liberal Democrat slate at both elections since the current boundaries were introduced in 2019, but this masks some turmoil in the ranks: the ruling Lib Dem group on the council had split in the 2019-23 term, and outgoing Lib Dem coucillor for this ward John Petherick sought re-election in 2023 as an independent candidate without success. Shares of the vote here in the 2023 Teignbridge council election were 39% for the Lib Dems, 27% for independents and 23% for the Conservatives. Following that election Martin Wrigley became leader of the council, and he kept that position until he was elected to Westminster in 2023 as the Lib Dem MP for the local seat of Newton Abbot. Wrigley has kept his Teignbridge council seat but he stood down last year from Devon county council, where he had represented Dawlish since 2021; the Lib Dems held that seat narrowly against a challenge from Reform UK. The Liberal Democrats also hold the Exminster and Haldon county division, which covers the Ashcombe part of this ward. This by-election will replace Lib Dem councillor Mike James, who died in April at the age of 89. Despite his advanced age James was in his first term as a Teignbridge councillor, but he did have previous local government experience as a Torbay councillor from 2003 to 2007. He had been a footballer in his youth with Cardiff City, and it was his work as a football coach that initially brought him to Devon. Goldsmid

Brighton and Hove council, East Sussex; caused by the resignation of Labour councillor Jackie O'Quinn. We stay on the south coast for our next poll, which is in the town of Hove Actually. It's here that we find the Goldsmid ward, whose name is a reference to a prominent Jewish family who owned and developed much of Hove in the 19th century. The Goldsmids had made their fortune in stockbroking and London finance, and the first of them to be associated with Hove was Isaac Goldsmid who was a director of the London, Brighton and South Coast Railway. Isaac was the first Jew to receive a UK hereditary title - he was made a baronet in 1843 - and he was one of the key figures in the eventual lifting of disabilites on British Jews in the mid-19th century. Several of his descendants went on to serve as MPs. In 1830 Sir Isaac Goldsmid had bought the Wick Estate, 250 acres of land in Hove and nearby Preston, and he had a large house built for the use of him and his family called Wick Hall, to the north of Brunswick Square. The modern-day Wick Hall is a block of flats close to St Ann's Well Gardens, which were originally laid out by the Goldsmids around a mineral spring and are now in municipal ownership. This is the largest open space in the modern Goldsmid ward, with second place going to the County Ground which has been the home of Sussex county cricket club since 1872. Also here is Hove railway station, a junction station on the West Coastway line, which makes Goldsmid ward commutable to London and it does have something of an educated commuter demographic. This area is in the parliamentary seat of Hove and Portslade, whose MP is a prominent figure. Peter Kyle, who gained what was then called the Hove constituency from the Conservatives in 2015 against the national trend, has sat in Cabinet throughout the Starmer administration: he currently holds the posts of business and trade secretary and President of the Board of Trade. The city of Brighton and Hove was gained by Labour in the 2023 local elections, after a long period of no overall control where sizeable Conservative, Green and Labour groups were unable to make sufficient headway against each other to get an overall majority. Goldsmid ward was closely fought between the Conservatives and Labour until a July 2009 by-election was gained by the Greens, after which it turned into a fight between Labour and the Greens. So the big Labour win here in 2023 - 47% for Labour, 27% for the Greens, 10% for the Conservatives - is a bit out of kilter with previous results, although ward boundary changes for the 2023 election complicate this. This by-election is to replace Labour councillor Jackie O'Quinn, a retired teacher who was first elected here in 2015 and served as mayor of Brighton and Hove in 2023-24. O'Quinn, who had left the Labour Party earlier this year, stood down from the council at the start of May to focus on family, friends and other interests. Defending this seat for Labour is Philip Berman, a former TV producer who now makes and edits podcasts. In the meantime, the electors of the George St/Harbour ward of Aberdeen get to go to the polls all over again this week, for an Aberdeen city council by-election which was already in the diary when Stephen Flynn resigned. For those who are wondering why this by-election couldn't have been held last week on the undercard of the Westminster by-election, which would have saved a lot of time and money for everyone involved, the answer is that Westminster and Scottish council by-elections are very different. Scottish council by-elections use a different electoral system, with transferable voting compared to Westminster's single X; they have a wider franchise, with 16- and 17-year-olds and a more extensive range of foreign nationals eligible to vote; and, crucially, they are not subject to Voter ID which the present Scottish government considers to be pointless at best. Let's look at this from the point of view of the voter. If you turn up at a polling station which is running a Westmineter election and a Scottish local election at the same time, and you're only eligible to vote in one of them, then your name will be marked on the register to indicate that and the poll staff will only give you a ballot paper for the election you're eligible to vote in. This is not rocket science, it happens all the time where elections with different franchises are combined and it's easy for everyone to understand, including the voter involved. From the voter's point of view, the different electoral systems are basically a case of following the instructions on the ballot paper, and if you mess it up and vote using the wrong system then that won't make your vote invalid. If you vote with preferences in a Westminster election then your ballot will be counted as a vote for your first preference; if you vote with an X in a Scottish local election then that will be treated as a first preference and won't transfer to any other candidate. So far, so good. Now let's throw voter ID into the mix, and say that you turn up to the polling station without acceptable photo ID. The presiding officer would be entitled to give you the Scottish ballot paper but would have to refuse to give you the Westminster ballot paper, a decision which you could quite reasonably see as ridiculous and which would only lead to constant arguments breaking out between the voters and the poll staff. The best case scenario is that nobody gets hurt. The Electoral Management Board for Scotland, a group of returning officers and electoral registration officers which co-ordinates the administration of Holyrood and Scottish local elections, have taken the view that we shouldn't be putting our election staff in that position, and they have a strong recommendation not to combine polls which require voter ID with those that don't. Similar guidance has gone out to returning officers in Wales.
