Denbighshire council, Wales; caused respectively by the resignation of Plaid Cymru councillor Huw Elfed Williams and by the death of Labour councillor Jeanette Chamberlain-Jones. This is one of the biggest weeks Andrew's Previews has ever covered, with 23 standalone local elections and by-elections taking place in all three countries of Great Britain. There are 24 seats up for election, with the defences being 7 for Labour, 4 each for the Greens, the Lib Dems and Reform UK, 2 each for Plaid Cymru and independent councillors, and one for the Conservatives - or at least, that's what happened when these seats were last contested. In a few cases defections will confuse the issue a bit. Five of today's elections take place in Wales, with four of these being to replace councillors who had to stand down after being elected to Senedd Cymru last month. The exception is Rhyl South ward, where the outgoing councillor Jeanette Chamberlain-Jones died in March so this by-election has taken some time to organise. Chamberlain-Jones had continuous service on Denbighshire council since her first election in 2004, and her civic positions including serving as the council chairman in 2012-13 and as mayor of Rhyl. She represented Rhyl South ward, which you will not be surprised to find covers the southern fringes of Rhyl town along the A525 road towards Rhuddlan. This is a very different area from Llanrhaeadr-yng-Nghinmeirch ward, a name which refers to a church (llan) by a waterfall (rhaeadr) in the Cinmeirch area. The village of Llanrhaeadr lies further up the Conwy valley and the A525 road, between Denbigh and Ruthin; the ward extends into the high ground to the west to take in the communities of Cyffylliog and Nantglyn, together with large chunks of the Clocaenog Forest and the northern part of the Llyn Brenig reservoir which supplies Liverpool with drinking water. The forest is one of the last holdouts of the red squirrel in Wales, while Llyn Brenig is a favourite nesting location for ospreys. Treorchy

Rhondda Cynon Taf council, Glamorgan; caused by the resignation of Plaid Cymru councillor Sera Evans. Our last Welsh by-election today lies in the Rhondda valley. Treorchy was one of the many towns in the Valleys of South Wales created by the coal mining industry: the first colliery here, at Abergorki, opened for business in 1859, and by 1875 the street pattern of the town was well advanced. But all of Treorchy's pits had closed by the end of the 1970s, and these days retail is the town's largest economic sector: in 2020 Treorchy was named UK High Street of the Year, with the judges noting the town's large number of independent shops, so the local businesses must be doing something right. Treorchy and Ynyswen railway stations are on the recently-electrified Rhondda branch of the Valley Lines network, while the village of Cwmparc lies on a side valley leading up to the mountain pass at Bwlch y Clawdd. The Bwlch summit is one of only two road links between the Rhondda and Ogmore valley, which were combined for Parliamentary purposes in 2024 to elect a single MP. This is Labour's Sir Chris Bryant, a junior trade minister who had represented the predecessor seat of Rhondda since 2001. In the Senedd Treorchy is part of the Afan Ogwr Rhondda constituency, which in 2026 gave 37% of the vote and 3 seats to Plaid Cymru, 34% and 2 seats to Reform UK, and 17% and 1 seat to Labour: the Labour MS Huw Irranca-Davies is now the Llywydd or presiding officer of the Senedd, while the outgoing Labour MS for Rhondda Buffy Williams was ranked second on the Labour list and lost her seat. Top of the Plaid Cymru list here in May was Sera Evans, who is originally from Cwmparc and was a long-serving councillor for Treorchy: she was first elected here in 2008, when she was 26 years old. In elections to Rhondda Cynon Taf council Treorchy has leaned towards Plaid Cymru, but at the most recent poll in 2022 - at which a boundary review cut Treorchy ward down from three councillors to two - the ward gave 55% to Labour and 39% to Plaid Cymru. Much of the Labour lead was a personal vote for their main candidate Bob Harris, and Sera Evans was able to hold Treorchy's other seat for Plaid. Evans is off to the Senedd and the defending Plaid Cymru candidate is Richard Clarke, who came to prominence in the area last year for leading the campaign to remove a gate which had closed off public access to Cwmparc forest - a row which inevitably became known as Gategate. The Labour candidate is David Morris, who previously contested this ward in 2017. Also standing are Cerys Walker for the Conservatives, Daniel Buck for the Greens and Craig Ford - who was a Conservative candidate in the last two RCT elections - for Reform UK. Goldsmid

Brighton and Hove council, East Sussex; caused by the resignation of Labour councillor Jackie O'Quinn. We stay on the south coast for our next poll, which is in the town of Hove Actually. It's here that we find the Goldsmid ward, whose name is a reference to a prominent Jewish family who owned and developed much of Hove in the 19th century. The Goldsmids had made their fortune in stockbroking and London finance, and the first of them to be associated with Hove was Isaac Goldsmid who was a director of the London, Brighton and South Coast Railway. Isaac was the first Jew to receive a UK hereditary title - he was made a baronet in 1843 - and he was one of the key figures in the eventual lifting of disabilites on British Jews in the mid-19th century. Several of his descendants went on to serve as MPs. In 1830 Sir Isaac Goldsmid had bought the Wick Estate, 250 acres of land in Hove and nearby Preston, and he had a large house built for the use of him and his family called Wick Hall, to the north of Brunswick Square. The modern-day Wick Hall is a block of flats close to St Ann's Well Gardens, which were originally laid out by the Goldsmids around a mineral spring and are now in municipal ownership. This is the largest open space in the modern Goldsmid ward, with second place going to the County Ground which has been the home of Sussex county cricket club since 1872. Also here is Hove railway station, a junction station on the West Coastway line, which makes Goldsmid ward commutable to London and it does have something of an educated commuter demographic. This area is in the parliamentary seat of Hove and Portslade Actually, whose MP is a prominent figure. Peter Kyle, who gained the Hove constituency from the Conservatives in 2015 against the national trend, has sat in Cabinet throughout the Starmer administration: he currently holds the posts of business and trade secretary and President of the Board of Trade. The city of Brighton and Hove was gained by Labour in the 2023 local elections, after a long period of no overall control where sizeable Conservative, Green and Labour groups were unable to make sufficient headway against each other to get an overall majority. Goldsmid ward was closely fought between the Conservatives and Labour until a July 2009 by-election was gained by the Greens, after which it turned into a fight between Labour and the Greens. So the big Labour win here in 2023 - 47% for Labour, 27% for the Greens, 10% for the Conservatives - is a bit out of kilter with previous results, although ward boundary changes for the 2023 election complicate this. This by-election is to replace Labour councillor Jackie O'Quinn, a retired teacher who was first elected here in 2015 and served as mayor of Brighton and Hove in 2023-24. O'Quinn, who had left the Labour Party earlier this year, stood down from the council at the start of May to focus on family, friends and other interests. Defending this seat for Labour is Philip Berman, a former TV producer who now makes and edits podcasts. The Greens have already gained two Brighton and Hove by-elections in this term, including in the neighbouring Brunswick and Adelaide ward two years ago, and they will be hoping for more with their candidate Nadia Barton Ahmad who is coming to the end of her degree in global development at the University of York. Younger still is the Conservatives' Louis Bird, an 18-year-old who is currently taking his A-levels at BHASVIC, the Brighton, Hove and Sussex Sixth Form College. We also have candidacies from Kim Leyland-Walker for the Lib Dems, Glenn Kelly for the Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition ad Luke Willmoth for Reform UK. The last in our series of by-elections caused by London Green councillors being returned while ineligible comes in Haringey, where we have two crucial polls taking place. One is to replace that disqualified Green, Jayon Henriques. The reason for Henriques' ineligibility has not been disclosed, but the rumour this column heard is that a previous criminal conviction is involved: persons who have been sentenced to three months or more in prison within the previous five years, including suspended sentences, are barred from being councillors. The other by-election is to fill a seat vacated by Labour's Hasret Bozdorgan who handed in her resignation within two weeks of her election for personal reasons which apparently transpired after polling day. These by-elections are crucial. The 2026 Haringey council election saw Labour lose control of the borough, and the Green party finished one seat short of a majority on 28 seats. Labour won 21 and the Lib Dems 8. Both of the wards up for election today were closely fought in May, returning two Labour councillors and one Green each. If the Greens win both of today's Haringey by-elections, that will give them a council majority. The Green defence is in Northumberland Park ward which lies in the Lea valley on the eastern edge of the borough. The ward is centred on Northumberland Park railway station, which is served by local trains on the Lea Valley line between Stratford and Meridian Water. There are no Tube stations here but London Underground have a presence nonetheless: the depot for Victoria Line trains is at Northumberland Park, although there is no station here. Northumberland Park ward's western boundary is the High Road in Tottenham, and the ward includes the Tottenham Hotspur Stadium which is England's third-largest football stadium: as well as Spurs, this is the home of the UK's NFL American football games and it will host five games at the forthcoming Euro 2028 tournament. At the far end of White Hart Lane we reach Woodside ward, which is centred on Woodside Park and covers the northern part of Wood Green. There is no wood here so do not be fooled by the ward name, but there is quite a lot of green space in various gardens and recreation grounds. Municipal buildings here include Haringey civic centre, which was built for the old Wood Green borough council in the late 1950s and is Grade II listed for its Scandinavian-influenced design. Wood Green underground station, on the Piccadilly line, lies on the ward's southern boundary. In the 2021 census Woodside ward made the top 20 wards in England and Wales for the White Other ethnic group (34.0%), and was in the top 100 for residents born in Bulgaria or Romania (8.1%), in Malta or Cyprus (2.1%) or in non-EU European states (9.1%). Northumberland Park also scores in the top 100 for residents born in those countries, but it is multicultural in a different way: it is in the top 20 wards for Black population (36.4%) and makes the top 100 for residents born in Africa (13.6%), the Americas and Caribbean (9.4%) or in British overseas territories (0.3%). Northumberland Park also ranks 3rd of all the wards in England and Wales for residents working in the administrative and support service sector (12.9%). The ward has high levels of owner-occupation, and easy access not just to the railway line into Liverpool but also to the M62 motorway. Since the 2024 general election Widnes has been in a constituency entirely north of the River Mersey, with the name of Widnes and Halewood. This and the predecessor Halton constituency have been represented since 1997 by long-serving Labour backbencher Derek Twigg, who had no trouble being re-elected in 2024 for his eighth term of office. Halton council was one of those districts where Reform UK ran rampant in May, but where Labour had done so well in the previous two years that their council majority was secure. Of the 19 Halton seats up for election this year, Reform UK won 16, the Lib Dems held one and just two seats stayed with Labour: that gave an overall council of 32 Labour, 16 Reform UK, 3 Lib Dems and 3 independents. One of the seats which Labour held onto was Farnworth, which was an open seat going into this year's election after the outgoing Labour councillor Val Hill died shortly before the end of her term. But the final Labour majority here against the turquiose tide was just one vote, with new Labour councillor Luke Williams defeating the Reform UK candidate by 837 votes to 836. In percentage terms Labour and Reform UK both scored 36%, with the Greens third on 14%. The previous three results here had been straight fights betwen the Conservatives and Labour, with very one-sided results in Labour's favour. We now have a second vacancy in Farnworth ward this year, after Aimee Skinner handed in her notice just after the May election for personal reasons. Skinner had represented this ward since the all-out election in 2021, when she was still known by her previous surname of Teeling, and she was re-elected for a second term in 2023. Accordingly, the winner of this by-election will need to seek re-election next year. Trying to defend this seat for Labour will be Louise Nolan, who is trying to get back onto Halton council after losing her seat in the neighbouring Halton View ward in May. Nolan had previously represented that ward since 2018, originally being elected under her previous surname of Whitley. Reform UK have changed their candidate to Christopher Charlton-Kingsley, who is a travel agent. The Green Party candidate is Kiel Oldfield, who stood in May in Highfield ward. Also on the ballot are Phil Harper for the Conservatives and Dan Clarke, fresh from his deposit-losing performance in last week's Makerfield by-election, for the Libertarian Party.
