"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

Five by-elections on 12th March 2026:

Penrith South

Westmorland and Furness council, Cumbria; caused by the resignation of Liberal Democrat councillor Virginia Taylor.

Those readers of Andrew’s Previews who align politically towards the Liberal Democrats might enjoy this week’s edition. The Lib Dems are defending three of the five seats up for election, and they hold a seat in one of the other wards which poll today.

We'll start this week's Lib Dem special in Penrith, a town at which the major communication links from England to Scotland collide: the M6 and West Coast Main Line on their descent from Shap Summit towards Carlisle cross the A66 on its descent from Bowes and Scotch Corner. All of these roads and railway lines are subject to major work: the A66 towards Scotch Corner is being dualled, while a railway viaduct over the M6 just south of Penrith has recently been replaced in a process which closed the railway for a week and closed the motorway for two weekends.

The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle records that on 12th July 927 a number of British monarchs gathered at Eamont Bridge, just south of Penrith at the point where the modern A6 road crosses the River Eamont. Æthelstan, king of the Anglo-Saxons, had recently conquered the Viking kingdom of York; at Eamont Bridge he met his fellow kings Constantine II of Scotland, Owain of Strathclyde, Ealdred I of Bamburgh and the Welsh rulers Hywel Dda and Owain of Gwent, who recognised Æthelstan as the first king of England. Before this point, England didn't exist as a political unit; after this point the River Eamont was the boundary between England and the Kingdom of Strathclyde.

The creation of England in 927 did not lead to lasting peace with its northern neighbours, and for centuries afterwards this corner of the north found itself in the debatable lands between England and Scotland. The Domesday survey of 1086 didn't cover Penrith: this area was then considered to be part of Scotland, which by this point had conquered the Kingdom of Strathclyde. It wasn't until 1092 that William Rufus took Cumberland for England, but for centuries afterwards the growing town of Penrith was on the front line between England and Scotland.

The ruins of Penrith Castle, which was built in the fifteenth century to protect the town from Scottish raids, bear witness to those uncertain times. They lie next to Penrith railway station, which is the main railhead for the North Lakes, and the castle ruins were actually owned by the railway for a time before Penrith urban district council turned them into gardens. Not far away is the town centre, but fans of Withnail and I may be disappointed to find out that none of Penrith town centre made it into the film; the building which claimed to be the Penrith Tea Rooms is in fact in Milton Keynes. Nonetheless Penrith South makes the top 100 wards in England and Wales for residents employed in the accommodation and food service sector (13.0%), showing the importance of tourism to the local economy.

There is certainly a lot for tourists in this area. Modern-day Penrith's population of just over 17,000 makes it the largest settlement for 20 miles in any direction. Until Cumbria's local government was reorganised in 2023, it was the centre of the Eden local government district which covered a large and very sparsely-populated area between the eastern Lake District to the west, the Howgill Fells to the south and the High Pennines to the east.

The Eden district made geographical and administrative sense, but its population - at around 50,000 - was considered to be too low. Accordingly the local authority here now is a geographical monstrosity called Westmorland and Furness council. Neither of these names are a good fit for Penrith, which was a Cumberland town up until 1974 but was left out of the modern-day Cumberland council. Penrith's local government is now administered from Kendal, nearly 30 miles to the south down the A6 over Shap Fell.

The modern-day parliamentary boundaries still respect the old Westmorland/Cumberland or England/Scotland divide of the River Eamont. Penrith has been named on the Parliamentary map continuously since 1885, and it has nearly always had Conservative MPs since that time. Prominent former MPs for Penrith include James Lowther, who was Speaker of the House of Commons for 16 years between 1905 and 1921; William Whitelaw, who was deputy leader of the Conservative Party for 16 years between 1975 and 1991; and Rory Stewart, who was in Cabinet for slightly less than three months in 2019 before finishing in fifth place in that year's Tory leadership election, and then being thrown out of the Conservative party over Brexit.

Stewart's former constituency of Penrith and the Border was broken up in the 2024 boundary changes, and the new seat of Penrith and Solway was effectively a heavily redrawn and much more rural successor to the former Workington constituency. Workington's Tory MP Mark Jenkinson must have thought he'd hit the Boundary Commission jackpot, particularly after he had defeated the Penrith and the Border MP Neil Hudson for the Conservative nomination in Penrith and Solway. But Hudson eventually managed to find an alternative berth in Epping Forest, whereas Jenkinson went down to defeat in one of the most surprising results of the 2024 election: Penrith and Solway voted for the Labour candidate Markus Campbell-Savours, who successfully turned his local government powerbase in Keswick into becoming the first-ever Labour MP for Penrith. Campbell-Savours lost the Labour whip last year for voting against plans to make agricultural land subject to inheritance tax, a big issue in one of the most agricultural of all present Labour constituencies, but he had the whip restored last week.

Despite this Parliamentary history, Westmorland and Furness council has a Liberal Democrat majority, with the Lib Dems' strength in Westmorland compensating for their lack of support in Barrow-in-Furness. The Lib Dem majority was also built on a strong performance in Eden district, which included winning all four council seats for the two Penrith wards. Penrith South ward covers the town centre, the industrial estates in the west of the town, the Cumbria police headquarters at Carleton Hall, and the auction mart next to the M6/A66 junction. In 2022 the ward gave 45% of the vote to the Liberal Democrats, 25% to the Conservatives and 19% to an independent slate; the new councillors fully took up their roles when Eden council was abolished in April 2023.

Top of the poll here in 2022, beating her running-mate from higher up the alphabet, was the Lib Dems' Virginia Taylor who had been the last leader of Eden council before its abolition. Taylor then joined the Westmorland and Furness council cabinet, but she stepped down from that role in 2025 for health reasons. She then resigned from Westmorland and Furness council in January.

Unusually, we have here a by-election where none of the four candidates has previously stood for election at principal council level. Defending for the Liberal Democrats is Barbara Jayson, who is a Penrith town councillor and the current deputy mayor of Penrith. By profession Jayson is a nurse and health visitor; she has spent a large amount of her career working abroad, and in the 2004 Birthday Honours she was appointed MBE "for services to the local community, especially mothers and children, and to the British community in Indonesia". The Conservatives have turned to the next generation by selecting Maiki Tolmie, who is studying for his A-levels at the Queen Elizabeth Grammar School in Penrith. Also standing are Emilia Hoffman for the Greens and Michael Houston of Reform UK, who was an unsuccessful candidate in a Penrith town council by-election in November. This time there is no independent candidate, so a fair number of votes are up for grabs here.

Aigburth

Liverpool council, Merseyside; caused by the resignation of Liberal Democrat councillor Dave Antrobus.

Now here's a nasty little quiz question for you which has caught a few people out over the years: on which Liverpool ground did Geoff Hurst fail to score? Well, the answer to that is not Anfield or Goodison. In football Hurst was a legendary striker for England and West Ham who became the first person to score a hat-trick in a World Cup final; but he was one of those talented people who could have turned their hand to any sport, and Hurst was a talented cricketer before his football commitments took over. In fact, he was good enough to make one first-class appearance for Essex, who included him in their XI to play Lancashire at Aigburth, the home of Liverpool cricket club, in May and June 1962. Hurst took one catch in Lancashire's first innings but failed to contribute with the bat to a 28-run win for Essex; he was 0 not out in the Essex first innings and was bowled for a duck in the second innings.

So the answer to that question is Aigburth, which is a middle-class southern suburb of Liverpool. The ward is centred on the Aigburth Road which is a major artery going south from the city centre, towards Garston, Speke and Widnes. Also here is the open space of Otterspool Park, which runs down to the Mersey riverfront at Otterspool Promenade; the park has been here since 1950 on a landscaped site which was previously a dumping ground for household waste and spoil from the Birkenhead Tunnel construction. Aigburth railway station, on the Merseyrail electric network, connects the area to Liverpool city centre.

The present Aigburth ward was created in 2023 from territory which was previously in Mossley Hill ward. In the 21st century Mossley Hill had voted Lib Dem until 2010 and Labour thereafter: the formation of the Conservative-Lib Dem coalition had a devastating effect on the previously sizeable Lib Dem local vote in Liverpool. It took until 2021 for the Lib Dems to get Mossley Hill back, but when the ward was broken up in 2023 its successors all clearly voted Lib Dem: Aigburth had a Lib Dem lead of 50-32 over Labour. That puts Aigburth in the minority on Liverpool city council, which gave Labour 61 out of 99 seats in 2023.

Finishing last here in 2023 was the Conservatives' David Jeffery. Jeffery is a senior lecturer in British politics at Liverpool University, who has put together an archive of local election results in Merseyside since 1945 - your columnist helped him with getting some of that data together. Based on that research, Jeffery wrote a well-received book in 2023 called Whatever Happened to Tory Liverpool?, charting the success, decline and irrelevance of the Conservative Party since the Second World War in the second city of Lancashire. In the postwar years Aigburth was a centre of Conservative success in Liverpool, an extremely safe ward where the party regularly racked up 4,000, 5,000 or more votes; today we're in the irrelevance phase, with Jeffery's score in Aigburth three years ago being just 39 votes or 2.3% of the total. The Conservatives lost their last seat on Liverpool council in 1998, and on this evidence a comeback does not look to be on the cards any time soon.

We can also see this from the 2024 general election, for which boundary changes transferred Aigburth from the Liverpool Riverside constituency into Liverpool Wavertree. This seat is very strongly in Labour hands with the Greens second; in 2024 the Conservative candidate, war correspondent and journalist Charlotte Eagar, finished in fifth place and lost her deposit. The Labour MP for Wavertree is Paula Barker, who had been the North West regional convenor of Unison before she was elected to Parliament in 2019; Barker took that job over from one Angela Rayner.

This by-election is to replace the Lib Dems' Dave Antrobus, who was first elected for the old Aigburth ward in 1996; following boundary changes, he transferred to Mossley Hill ward in 2004 before retiring at the 2008 election. Antrobus returned to the council in 2023 at the first election for the present Aigburth ward. He is now 80 years old, and he tendered his resignation in January on health grounds.

Defending this seat for the Liberal Democrats is Dave Thomas, who is chair of the Mossley Hill residents' association and has previously managed a recruitment agency. Labour's Jim Davies stood in 2023 in the neighbouring Sefton Park ward. Also on an all-male ballot are Paul Ruddick for the Greens, Chris Hall for the Conservatives, independent candidate Craig Carden who is standing on a localist ticket, and Reform UK's Harry Gallimore-King who has been on quite the political journey: Gallimore-King has previously stood for Liverpool council without success for both the Conservatives and the Green Party.

Sleaford Westholme

North Kesteven council, Lincolnshire; caused by the death of Lincolnshire Independents councillor Ann Mear.

We now come to the first of two former county towns which appear in this week's Previews. From 1889 to 1974 Sleaford was the county town of the Parts of Kesteven, the south-western of the three subdivisions of Lincolnshire; Kesteven county council's offices were here from 1925, although throughout that council's existence its meetings were held alternately in Sleaford and Grantham. The old county offices are now home to North Kesteven district council.

Sleaford goes back to Roman times, and it became a major agricultural centre in the eighteenth century with the canalising of the River Slea to create the Sleaford Navigation. The railways came here in 1857, and Sleaford is still a railway junction, with lines from Peterborough to Lincoln and from Grantham to Skegness meeting here. Within a sharp bend of the line to Lincoln we find Westholme ward, which covers housing in the north-west of the town together with St George's Academy, which is Sleaford town's comprehensive secondary school and sixth form.

The school incorporates Westholme House, a Victorian mansion built in the French Gothic style for Thomas Parry. Parry was a partner in an architectural firm in the town, Kirk and Parry, and he also had three short spells as a Liberal MP for Boston - achieving the dubious distinction of being unseated by two separate election petitions for bribery, following corrupt elections in Boston in 1865 and 1874. On the second occasion Parry's majority was 351 but the Election Court found that 877 voters had received gifts of coal from Parry's agents with intent to influence their votes; the Court eventually struck out 353 of the 1,347 votes cast for Parry, keeping the scrutiny going for just long enough to award the seat to his Conservative opponent.

Modern-day Sleaford has been the centre of its own constituency, called Sleaford and North Hykeham, since 1997. This has been held since a 2016 by-election by Caroline Johnson, who enjoyed one of the safest Conservative seats in the land in 2017 and 2019 but saw her seat fall to marginal status in 2024. Johnson, who had a medical career before entering the Commons and continues to do part-time work as a consultant paediatrician, is now on the Conservative frontbench as a shadow health minister.

The Sleaford and North Hykeham constituency has similar boundaries to North Kesteven council, which was an against-the-trend Conservative gain in the 2023 local elections; the Conservatives won 25 seats against 11 for the Lincolnshire Independents, who are a longstanding localist group led by county councillor Marianne Overton, 5 other independent councillors and 2 Labour. North Kesteven had returned a majority of independent or Lincolnshire Independent councillors in 2019, but the Tories eventually did a deal with enough of the independents to retain control.

The last time that North Kesteven had returned Labour councillors was back in 2003, and on that occasion Sleaford Westholme was in their column. However, the Labour councillor Brian Watson successfully sought re-election as an independent candidate in 2007. He resigned from the council on health grounds in 2014, after completing his year as mayor of Sleaford, and the resulting by-election was won by Lincolnshire Independents candidate Steve Fields. Fields retired at the 2019 election at which Sleaford Westholme was a straight fight between two independent candidates, with Anthony Brand narrowly defeating Heather Lorimer by 239 votes to 204. The present ward boundaries were introduced in 2023, when Brand lost his seat to the Lincolnshire Independents' Ann Mear by just one vote, 151 to 150. In percentage terms the Lincolnshire Independents and Brand both polled 29%, Labour had 23% and independent Heather Lorimer had 18%.

Sleaford Westholme ward is part of the Sleaford division of Lincolnshire county council, which a very easy Reform UK gain from the Conservatives in last year's county elections. Reform UK have proved that they still have momentum in Sleaford by winning a town council by-election in a different ward last week.

So this could be a difficult defence for the Lincolnshire Independents after their councillor Ann Mear, who was a former solicitor, died just before Christmas at the age of 71. Their defending candidate is Dave Darmon, who is the current deputy mayor of Sleaford and has previously worked in retail as a store manager; in last year's county elections Darmon contested Sleaford Rural division, and he previously appeared in this column in a March 2024 North Kesteven by-election in Heckington Rural ward (Andrew's Previews 2024, page 146). Anthony Brand, the independent councillor who lost his seat here so narrowly in 2023, is trying to get back; another independent on the ballot is Alex Tugwell. Labour, who were the only political party to stand here last time, haven't returned; but nonetheless there is a wider choice for the electorate this time with candidates from Reform UK (Sandy Buchanan), the Lib Dems (Susan Hislop), the Greens (Ian Leach) and the Conservatives (Mark Webster).

Abingdon Abbey Northcourt

Vale of White Horse council, Oxfordshire; caused by the resignation of Green Party councillor Cheryl Briggs.

We now come to the Thames valley and to the town of Abingdon-on-Thames, which has a long history. Abingdon Abbey was founded at some point in the late 7th century and became powerful thanks to grants from successive kings of Wessex, until the Danes trashed it in the reign of Alfred the Great. By the time of the Norman conquest Abingdon Abbey had recovered its fortunes, and the Domesday survey of 1086 recorded it as a major landowner; local tradition has it that William the Conqueror had sent his son, later Henry I, for some education here. The Abbey occupied a site next to the Thames, close to the modern Abingdon bridge which has been here since the 15th century in some form and is still the only river crossing here. Nothing remains of the abbey church today, but some of the associated monastic buildings do survive in the town centre; this includes parts of the Guildhall, which is now the headquarters of Abingdon-on-Thames town council.

The dissolution of Abingdon Abbey in 1538 led to economic decline, but the town's fortunes picked up in 1556 when Mary I granted Abingdon a borough charter. This enabled the town to elect one member of Parliament - which was quite unusual, as boroughs in the pre-Reform Act days normally had two MPs each. The right to vote in Abingdon was vested in residents paying the local tax of scot and lot, providing a large electorate which no patron was ever strong enough to control. Abingdon town kept its MP up until the Third Reform in 1885.

Shortly after receiving its borough charter, Abingdon became the county town of Berkshire. One remaining vestige of this is the County Hall, which lies on the south side of the Market Place and is now run as a museum; this building dates from the 1680s, and it was built on pillars to allow a market to take place in the sheltered space underneath the building. But Abingdon lost its county town status to Reading in 1869 as a result of the town failing to embrace the new technology of the railways: Reading was and still is a major railway junction, while Abingdon was served only by a now-closed branch line to the country halt of Radley. In 1974 Abingdon left Berkshire altogether, as reorganisation transferred the part of the county north of the Ridgeway into Oxfordshire; most of the transferred area became the Vale of White Horse local government district, which is based in Abingdon within this ward.

This boundary change reflected economic reality. Modern-day Abingdon is effectively a commuter town for Oxford to the north as well as serving the modern day science complex around Culham and Harwell to the south. In the 2021 census just over half of Abbey Northcourt ward's adult residents were educated to degree level, and just under half were in professional or managerial occupations. Abbey Northcourt ward covers the town centre area plus the Northcourt area just to the north, where we find the Fitzharrys secondary school (which educated the TV presenter Kate Garraway) and the Abingdon and Witney college.

Since 1983 the linkage between Abingdon and Oxford has been extended to Parliamentary level thanks to the presence of the Oxford West and Abingdon constituency, which combines the town with Oxford city centre and the countryside in between. This seat has alternated between the Conservatives and the Liberal Democrats over the last 43 years, with its previous MPs including the Conservatives' John Patten (who served in Cabinet as education secretary in the Major government) and the Lib Dems' Evan Harris (who represented the seat from 1997 to 2010 and was on the party's frontbench for most of that period). Since 2017 the MP here has been the Lib Dems' Layla Moran, who holds the powerful Commons post of chair of the health and social care committee.

The Lib Dems are also in the ascendancy here at local government level. In fact, the Conservatives were completely wiped of Vale of White Horse district at the 2023 local elections which returned 34 Lib Dem and 4 Green Party councillors. The Lib Dems have since successfully defended three by-elections, but they face a stern test later this month in the very marginal Stanford ward; this column will be back in Oxfordshire to discuss that in due course. The Tories did manage to keep a foothold in the Vale of White Horse district at the 2025 Oxfordshire county elections, but the Abingdon East and Abingdon North county divisions which cover this ward both elected Lib Dem candidates with over 50% of the vote.

The 2023 Vale election could have been even more emphatic for the Lib Dems, because they did not field a full slate of candidates as part of an electoral pact with the Green Party. All four of the district's Green councillors were elected without Lib Dem opposition, including Cheryl Briggs who has represented Abingdon Abbey Northcourt ward since 2019 alongside Lib Dem councillor Helen Pighills. Topline voting figures here in 2023 were 40% for the Lib Dems, 35% for the Greens and 14% for the Conservatives, but this is distorted by the fact that the Lib Dem and Green candidates were effectively a joint slate.

This by-election is a Green Party defence to replace Cheryl Briggs, a former youth worker who also served on the town council and was mayor of Abingdon-on-Thames in 2021-22. Briggs tendered her resignation from both the town and district councils in January.

The defending Green candidate is Aidan Reilly, an engineer who works in the nuclear fusion industry; he has been a frequent Green candidate in the Abingdon area in recent years, most recently standing for the county council in 2025 in Abingdon East which covers the town-centre part of this ward. It would appear that the electoral pact has broken down, because Reilly faces Lib Dem competition from Caleb Pell who was recently returned to his native Abingdon after graduating in economics from the University of York. While he was a student, Pell sat on Heslington parish council (which covers the York university campus) and he was an unsuccessful Lib Dem candidate for York council in 2023. In the unusual position for a P of top of the ballot paper is the Conservatives' Chris Palmer, who previously sat on Vale of White Horse council for Abingdon Fitzharris ward until 2019 and unsuccessfully tried to get back there in 2023. Another all-male candidate list is completed by Stephen Webb for Labour and Luis Prado for Reform UK.

The Beeches

Cotswold council, Gloucestershire; caused by the resignation of Liberal Democrat councillor Nigel Robbins.

We'll finish for the week in the Gloucestershire town of Cirencester. The Beeches ward covers the Beeches housing estate on the eastern edge of the town, running along the London Road and the Burford Road as far as the A417/A419 bypass. Travellers on the main roads from the north, the A417 Ermin Street or the A429 Fosse Way, will enter Cirencester town here. The ward boundaries include the Cirencester Kingshill secondary school, which is celebrating its 50th anniversary this year: it was opened in 1976 by Princess Anne.

In the 2021 census around a quarter of The Beeches ward's housing stock was socially rented, amounting to just under 300 households. There were a further 50 households in shared ownership (4.4% of the ward) which is normally an indicator of recent housebuilding. Cotswold district has a number of wards which have significant amounts of housing in shared ownership, particularly New Mills ward which borders this ward to the south.

The Beeches ward was created for the 2015 local elections and all of its three previous contests have been won by Nigel Robbins for the Lib Dems, putting the ward within the large Lib Dem majority on Cotswold council. This majority received a boost in January when the Liberal Democrats gained a by-election from the Green Party in the very different environment of The Rissingtons ward; judging from the scores then, it appears that the Greens made no effort to defend that seat. The Beeches last polled in May 2023, when the Lib Dem lead here was 56-34 over the Conservatives.

The Liberal Democrats now represent the Beeches estate at all levels of government. Cirencester is part of the South Cotswolds parliamentary seat which straddles the boundary between Gloucestershire and Wiltshire; this seat was won in 2024 by the Lib Dems' Roz Savage, who had previously been best known as an ocean rower. The Beeches ward is part of the Cirencester North with Ermin division of Gloucestershire county council, which was an easy Lib Dem hold in last year's county elections.

This division was known as Cirencester Beeches until it was expanded in boundary changes last year, and the outgoing district councillor Nigel Robbins represented it on the county council from 2013 to 2017. Robbins spent his career in education, and he was the principal of Cirencester College for 20 years before entering politics; he has served this ward on Cotswold council since 2015 and also sits on Cirencester town council. He is now in his 80s and he has chosen to retire from the district council.

Defending this seat for the Liberal Democrats is Paul Evans, who was elected to Cirencester town council in a by-election for a different ward last year. He is a first-time election candidate at this level as is the Conservatives' Ben Shaw, who lives in the ward. Also standing the Green Party's Bob Irving and Labour's Joshua Littler-Jennings, both of whom are regular unsuccessful candidates in Cotswold district, and Reform UK's Mackenzie Vaughan; she is a qualified youth worker.
