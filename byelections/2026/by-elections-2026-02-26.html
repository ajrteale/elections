"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

Two by-elections on 26th February 2026, one of which is more important than the other:

Gorton and Denton

House of Commons; caused by the resignation of Labour MP Andrew Gwynne.

Andrew's Previews is built on talking about by-elections to our local government, which happen at the rate of about five or six a week, on average. These are not evenly distributed through the year, and February is a slow period thanks to the winter weather. Parliamentary by-elections, on the other hand, are much more uncommon: over the last fifteen years there have been sixty polls to fill casual vacancies in the House of Commons, which is an average of one every three months. But we are still in the early years of a Parliament which followed a general election where there was a massive turnover in the Commons membership: this process tends to make the average MP younger, healthier, and less experienced - and thus less prone to scandal. We can see this in a dropoff in the number of Parliamentary by-elections over the last two years: today is only the second House of Commons by-election of the current Parliament.

This poll falls in the Gorton and Denton constituency, which covers two places which many people would be hard-placed to find on a map. These are two towns in Greater Manchester, linked together by the A57 road - the Hyde Road - which goes east from Manchester city centre towards Sheffield. Denton is part of the Metropolitan Borough of Tameside, whereas Gorton has been part of the city of Manchester since 1909 and lies just three miles south-east of the city centre.

Gorton has traditionally been a heavily industrial area, but it was less dependent on the textile industry than some other towns within the orbit of Manchester. Traditionally it was a railway centre, with major locomotive factories here both for the Great Central Railway and for Beyer, Peacock and Company. The latter firm opened here in 1854 and was in operation in Gorton for more than a century, with around 8,000 locomotives coming out of the Gorton Foundry in that time to work on railways all over the world.

All this industry led to quite a lot of slum housing in Gorton, much of which was been cleared in the twentieth century leading to major depopulation in this area. Regeneration work continues to go on, with Gorton Market moving to a new building in 2008 next a large new Tesco supermarket on the Hyde Road. Not far away from here is the Oasis Academy, but don't go here expecting to learn how to sing like Noel and Liam: it's a primary school.

Perhaps Gorton's most famous feature is Gorton Monastery, which is not an ancient building. This was constructed between 1866 and 1872 as a Franciscan friary, and it is seen as the Gothic Revival architectural masterpiece of Edward Pugin - the son of the Houses of Parliament architect Augustus Pugin. The monastery church is beautifully decorated inside, with its unusual north-south orientation enabling natural light to illuminate the high altar via the length of the nave.

The Franciscans moved out of Gorton Monastery in 1989. They sold the site to property developers, who had a scheme to convert the building into flats. The new owners got as far as getting rid of most of the fixtures and fittings: the organ went for scrap, while a series of 12 statues of Franciscan saints which had overlooked the nave were removed. (The statues eventually ended up on a Sotheby's catalogue listed as garden ornaments: following a tip-off, the Saints were bought by Manchester city council and they are now back in their original positions.) But the developers quickly went bust and the building just as quickly became derelict, to the extent that in 1997 Gorton Monastery was placed on the World Monuments Fund's watchlist of the world's 100 most endangered sites. The building was then bought from the property developers' receivers for £1 by local campaigners, who have extensively restored the Monastery thanks to European and Lottery funding. It is now a space for events and for the local community, and your columnist has played music in Gorton Monastery in the past.

Denton, which lies a few miles to the east of here, had a rather different industrial base. There was a colliery here (which didn't survive the General Strike), but Denton's economy was traditionally based on the manufacture of felt hats. This was so important to the town that in the 1921 census over 40% of Denton's workforce was either working in the hat factories or in related occupations such as the Oldham Batteries factory - which, despite its name, maintained a sideline making hatting machinery. Denton Town Hall, which was originally opened in 1889 as a library and now has that function again, still stands as a proud reminder of the town's independence.

It has to be said that the rest of Denton town centre is not a pretty sight. Much of it was obliterated in the late 1970s by the M67 motorway, the only part of a grand motorway scheme to connect Manchester to Sheffield that was ever built. Somebody in the Ministry of Transport with a dark sense of humour called it the Denton Relief Road, despite the fact that it relieves the town centre of traffic by ploughing straight through the middle of it.

The M67 motorway ends just before the M60 junction at Denton Island, which has clear indications that this road was meant to be part of a larger scheme. The motorway traffic is carried on what were intended to be slip roads either side of what locals call the "ski jump". Winter Olympians should not be fooled: the ski-jump was going to be the eastern abutment of a viaduct which would have taken M67 traffic over Denton Island and into Manchester city centre along a similarly destructive line through Gorton. The motorway through Gorton was never built, but its plans did accelerate slum clearance.

Directly north of the ski jump we find Denton railway station, which once had four platforms. The two platforms which remain are reasonably well-kept, with planters which will look better when we're not in the depths of winter.

But there are no facilities here: and, more to the point, there are almost no trains and almost no passengers. Denton station is on a line between Stockport and Stalybridge, avoiding Manchester, which was built by the London and North Western Railway to connect its main line from Manchester to London with the transpennine route to Huddersfield via Standedge Tunnel. This route still sees a fair amount of freight traffic, but it lost its point for passenger use when the fast transpennine trains were rerouted to Manchester Piccadilly in 1989 enabling connections to be made there. Denton station's passenger service was all but withdrawn in 1992, and its current service consists of two trains per week: one to Stockport, the other to Stalybridge, both of which run on Saturday mornings only. In 2023-24 Denton was the UK's least-used railway station, with just 54 entries and exits being recorded. The only reason it is still open is that running this minimal service is cheaper than going through the legal process for closure. Perhaps as an indicator of distress, the sign directing pedestrians to the Denton station platform has been made with the National Rail double-arrow symbol upside down.

There are more convenient railway stations in the Gorton and Denton constituency, although a number of these have no Sunday service. This includes the station at Belle Vue, a name which for Mancunians of a certain age will bring to mind memories of a good day out. From 1836 the Belle Vue Zoological Gardens had developed into a major entertainment centre. As well as the zoo - which was the third-largest in the UK - there was also an amusement park, a greyhound track, a speedway stadium and the UK's largest exhibition hall outside London, the Kings Hall. The whole complex attracted up to two million visitors every year. Among many other things, Belle Vue was the original home of the British Open brass band championships, which were held here from 1853 to 1981 (this competition now takes place at Symphony Hall in Birmingham). The Hallé Orchestra were also regular visitors to the Kings Hall, which hosted the occasional political meeting as well. In 1924 David Lloyd George addressed a Liberal Party rally of over 50,000 here by the newfangled technology of loudspeakers; the British Union of Fascists held their own rally here in 1934; while in 1968 the TUC chose Belle Vue for its centenary celebrations.

In the 1870s Belle Vue zoo bought an elephant called Maharajah from a circus in Edinburgh. The zoo owner had intended to bring the elephant to Manchester by train, but this plan fell through after Maharajah destroyed the intended railway compartment. So the elephant and his trainer made the journey from Edinburgh on foot, which took them ten days. Legend has it that they were stopped on the journey at a tollgate by a jobsworth turnpike official, who got into an argument with the trainer on what the appropriate charge would be given that there was no entry for "elephant" on the schedule of tolls: Maharajah's solution to this problem was to wrap his trunk around the tollgate and lift it clean off its hinges. The elephant's skeleton was preserved after his death, and Maharajah is now in the collection of Manchester Museum.

Almost none of the old Belle Vue survives today. The zoo was the first to go, closing in 1977 due to mounting losses; the amusement park's final summer was 1980, and in 1982 the land was sold off. Initially the Kings Hall was spared, but it closed in 1987 following the opening of the G-Mex exhibition centre (now Manchester Central) in the city centre: its site is now a car auction mart. Last to go was the greyhound track, which last held a meeting in 2020; its site has since been redeveloped for housing accessed by the new thoroughfare of Scenic Street. Don't complain that I don't give you scenic photos.

But there is still sport going on here. The Belle Vue Aces speedway team have moved further along Kirkmanshulme Lane to the National Speedway Stadium, which opened in 2016 on a site previously used for hockey pitches. This was where the hockey events were held for the 2002 Commonwealth Games, and a pylon from those games still lurks at the side of Kirkmanshulme Lane. Next to this is the National Basketball Performance Centre.

The housebuilders are also at work on what used to be Mellands Park in southern Gorton. This lies next to Nico Ditch, an earthwork of mysterious origin which goes back into the Anglo-Saxon era and which once ran from Audenshaw to Stretford. Its primary purpose appears to have been as a boundary marker, and the Nico Ditch once marked the southern boundary of Gorton; the present Manchester-Tameside boundary between Gorton and Denton is clearly derived from its course. Little of Nico Ditch survives today, but the stretch along the south side of the former Mellands Park has been repurposed as a drainage ditch.

The Nico Ditch probably isn't as old as the main road through Longsight and Levenshulme, which is marked on old Ordnance Survey maps as a Roman road. This would have gone to Buxton, as indeed the modern A6 road does. Like Gorton, Levenshulme was an urban district of its own until 1909. Its old town hall still stands on the Stockport Road, but Manchester council sold the building off in 1990 and it is now used as an antiques store. On my visit I came out of there with a copy of the 1919 edition of Whitaker's Almanack, which has been helpful in putting this preview together. The foyer of the Levenshulme Antiques Village still has a mosaic floor decorated with the initials of Levenshulme Urban District Council.

Modern-day Levenshulme and Longsight are very different in their demographic character from Gorton and (particularly) Denton, in that they are predominantly non-white and heavily Muslim. Both Levenshulme and Longsight wards make the top 100 wards in England and Wales for residents born in the Middle East and Asia, for Islam and for residents who are not in work because they are looking after home or family. Longsight ward is additionally in the top 100 for residents who have never worked or are long-term unemployed, for those working in administrative or support service roles and for part-time employment. The Stockport Road through Longsight and Levenshulme is lined with shops and restaurants which wouldn't look out of place in multicultural London - many of which have put Green Party or Labour posters in the window for the occasion.

This multicultural feeling extends to Levenshulme railway station, where the platform signs give the station name in English, Urdu, Irish Gaelic and British Sign Language. This non-standard signage extends to the street outside the station building, which is officially unnamed: some wag has put up a street sign saying "THE STREET WITH NO NAME".

The final piece of the Gorton and Denton puzzle is Burnage ward, which mostly consists of a large suburban council estate along the A34 Kingsway: the King named here is George V, and the road was laid out in 1928 as an early urban dual carriageway. It originally had a tram track along the central reservation. This was the area where the Gallagher brothers grew up, and their local connection is marked by a mural on the side of Sifters record shop - which once got a namecheck in an Oasis song.

As can be seen Gorton and Denton is a complicated area. It has a complicated parliamentary history to match, partly because only one corner of it was within the Manchester city boundary when single-member constituencies became the norm at the Third Reform in 1885. That area was Longsight, which was part of the parish of Rusholme until it was annexed by Manchester in that year, and Longsight was included within the original Manchester South constituency. Levenshulme and the Burnage area (which would become part of the big city by the time the First World War broke out) were at this point within the Stretford division of Lancashire, while there was also a Gorton division of Lancashire which combined Gorton with Denton.

For the first ten years of its existence the Gorton constituency was dominated by the Liberals. Appropriately enough its first MP was the Liberal Richard Peacock, who had perhaps done more than anyone else for the nineteenth-century development of Gorton and Openshaw. Peacock had made his career as a railway engineer. He started in 1838 as an 18-year-old locomotive superintendent for the Leeds and Selby Railway; he founded the Manchester, Sheffield and Lincolnshire Railway's locomotive works at Gorton in the 1840s; and he then became the Peacock in Beyer, Peacock and Company which, as already noted, supplied railway locomotives to the UK and the world from their foundry in Gorton.

Richard Peacock is still in Gorton today, lying in eternal rest in his mausoleum at the Brookfield Unitarian Church on the Hyde Road - a church which he had commissioned in the late 1860s. He was still the MP for Gorton at the time of his death in 1889, and the resulting Gorton by-election of 22nd March 1889 returned another Liberal MP very much in Peacock's image. William Mather was from the Mather family that owned the Salford Iron Works, and he was a senior partner (from 1892, chairman) of the engineering firm Mather and Platt which was a major employer in the Newton Heath area. (The Mather and Platt company is still going today: now trading as M & P Engineering, they have a factory in Trafford Park making equipment for the food processing industry.) William Mather had been elected in 1885 as the first MP for Salford South, but he had lost that seat the following year; the Gorton by-election was a quick return to the Commons for him. He defeated the Conservative candidate Ernest Hatch by an increased majority of 846 votes; that majority reduced to 222 at the 1892 election.

William Mather didn't seek re-election in Gorton in 1895, and the Liberals gave up their seat by standing down in favour of a former Liberal who was now in the Independent Labour Party. This was the last of several unsuccessful election campaigns before his 1898 death for Richard Pankhurst, a barrister who was a strong supporter of women's rights. Although he never made it into the Commons, Pankhurst's legacy was one of the first major pieces of equality legislation: he had drafted the bill which became the Married Women's Property Act 1882, giving wives (rather than their husbands) control over their own property and earnings. Presumably his own wife approved of this: Richard was the husband of Emmeline Pankhurst, who was already prominent in the women's suffrage movement, and he was the father of her three daughters Christabel, Sylvia and Adela, who were all aged between 15 and 10 at this point. Their suffragette careers came later.

Richard Pankhurst was rather soundly defeated in the 1895 election by the Conservatives' Ernest Hatch, who won at the third attempt to become the first Conservative MP for Gorton. Hatch was a wine merchant who had recently gone into business with the former first-class cricketer James Mansfield, forming Hatch, Mansfield and Company. He was re-elected rather more narrowly in the khaki election of 1900, defeating a joint Liberal-Labour candidate by 520 votes.

Ernest Hatch crossed the floor to the Liberals in 1905, disagreeing with the Conservatives over free trade. The Gorton Liberals were not interested in adopting him as their candidate in the 1906 general election, and he retired. The Conservatives failed to recover their defection loss and they were thrashed in the election by former Manchester city councillor John Hodge, standing for the Labour Representation Committee. Hodge was a trade union leader from the Scottish iron industry, having been the founding secretary of the British Steel Smelters' Association in 1885: he had also been elected as a Manchester councillor in 1898.

After holding his seat rather more narrowly in both 1910 elections, John Hodge made it into government under the Lloyd George coalition of December 1916 as the first Minister of Labour, with a seat in Cabinet. He was a strong supporter of the Great War, and despite his trade unionism Hodge broke a boilermakers' strike during his tenure by threatening the strikers with the Defence of the Realm Act. In 1917 he was reshuffled to become Minister for Pensions.

In the meantime, as stated, Levenshulme and Burnage were part of the Stretford constituency which had very different boundaries to the modern seat of Stretford and Urmston: it also took in areas like Chorlton-cum-Hardy, Withington, Didsbury, Heaton Mersey and Reddish, all of which would soon be annexed by Manchester or Stockport. As such this was a suburban constituency whose electorate saw huge growth during this period: from 11,140 at the 1885 election to 27,629 in December 1910.

The first MP for Stretford was the Liberal William Agnew, who had previously represented the South-East Lancashire constituency since 1880. Agnew was one of the Sons in Thomas Agnew and Sons, the London fine art dealership which during this period became the go-to firm for sales of Old Masters. He defeated John Maclure of the Conservatives by 4,866 votes to 4,676, a majority of 190.

Maclure got his revenge the following year, defeating Agnew by 739 votes. As well as being one of the prime movers of Conservatism in the Manchester area at the time, Maclure was a businessman in the insurance industry and he had also been involved in the relief efforts during the 1860s Lancashire cotton famine. In 1892 he increased his majority over Agnew to 1,345, and nobody bothered to oppose his re-election in 1895. He was made a baronet in 1898.

Sir John Maclure died in 1901, aged 65, and he is commemorated by a stained-glass window in Manchester Cathedral; helpfully in this regard, his brother was the Dean of Manchester. The resulting Stretford by-election of 26th February 1901 - 125 years ago today - was held for the Conservatives by another prominent Anglican, Charles Cripps. The father of the Labour politician Sir Stafford Cripps, Charles was a barrister who had previously served in the 1895 parliament as Conservative MP for Stroud; he had lost his seat in 1900, so this was a quick return to the green benches for him. During his tenure as MP for Stretford Cripps was appointed as attorney-general to the Prince of Wales, the future George V (he had served the future Edward VII in the same capacity) and he became Vicar-General of Canterbury, concentrating on church affairs. He later served as MP for Wycombe from 1910 and was promoted to the House of Lords in 1914 as Lord Parmoor, serving on the Lords' judicial committee despite having never been a judge. Parmoor eventually ended up in the Labour Party, and he was Leader of the Lords in both Macdonald governments.

All this lay in the future in 1906, when the Liberal landslide swept Charles Cripps away as MP for Stretford. The new Liberal MP for the seat was Harry Nuttall, an import and export merchant who was president of the Manchester Chamber of Commerce in 1905, and who had strong support from the 5,000 Manchester freeholders who were eligible to vote in the Stretford county constituency. Nuttall had got nowhere against John Maclure in 1900, but he pulled off a swing of nearly 18% to unseat Cripps with a majority of 2,824. He was re-elected in both 1910 elections, although his majority fell to 876 in December 1910. Arthur Samuel, who was the defeated Conservative candidate here in both 1910 polls and later had a prominent government career between the wars, polled 10,467 votes in December - a score which would have won nearly any other constituency.

Meanwhile Longsight was part of the Manchester South constituency, which covered south Manchester as that city existed in 1885 - plus some clearly-associated parts like the Belle Vue area, which at this point was a detached part of Newton Heath parish known as Kirkmanshulme. There was also a small detached part of Gorton parish between Belle Vue and Longsight which was included in the seat. As well as Longsight, the Manchester South constituency took in Rusholme, Moss Side - which in those days was a fashionable middle-class suburb - and the Chorlton-on-Medlock area which then, as now, was dominated by what would become Manchester University.

Manchester South was a marginal seat whose electorate strongly grew during this period. Its first MP was associated with the University. Sir Henry Roscoe, elected here in 1885 on the Liberal ticket, was a noted chemist who had done a lot of work to advance the scientific understanding of the metal vanadium and its compounds. He had also extensively collaborated with the German scientist Robert Bunsen, whose eponymous gas burner you might remember from your school chemistry lessons: together, Roscoe and Bunsen are thought to have taken the first flash photograph in 1864, using a magnesium wire as the light source. Roscoe's political career burned rather longer than that: he represented Manchester South for ten years, and lost his seat in 1895 by only 78 votes.

The Liberal Unionist MP for Manchester South from 1895 was genuine royalty. John George Edward Henry Douglas Sutherland Campbell, Marquess of Lorne, was not altogether happily married to Queen Victoria's daughter Princess Louise. He was also the heir to the dukedom of Argyll, and he had previously served as MP for Argyllshire for ten years from 1868 until he left these shores in 1878 to become Governor-General of Canada. Lorne left his mark on Canada, where a large number of places and other things are named after him: there is still a Canadian infantry regiment in existence called the Lorne Scots. However, he made little impact in Parliament, and the London World went so far as to label him as "a non-entity in the House of Commons, and a non-entity without".

In 1900 John Campbell succeeded to his father's titles and entered the Lords as the 9th Duke of Argyll. For the resulting first Manchester South by-election of 25th May 1900 the Liberal Unionists selected another scion of the upper class. This by-election marked the entry into national politics of William Peel, who would later become the first Earl Peel and serve in a number of Cabinet positions in the 1920s. He was a grandson of the prime minister Sir Robert Peel and son of Arthur Peel, who had been Speaker of the Commons from 1884 to 1895. William Peel had recently been elected to the London County Council as a Municipal Reform member for Woolwich in a February 1900 by-election. In the Manchester South parliamentary by-election three months later Peel pulled off an 11% swing towards the government, increasing the Liberal Unionist majority from 78 votes to 2,039 - a harbinger of the good Conservative performance in the "khaki" 1900 general election later that year, when Peel was re-elected with a further swing in his favour. His Liberal opponent in the by-election was Leif Jones, who was associated with the temperance movement and would later serve as a Liberal MP for four non-consecutive spells in three different constituencies.

In 1906 William Peel left our story to seek re-election in Harrow, which he lost. Manchester South's electorate had grown by 2,500 since 1900, and this may have been helpful in enabling the Liberals to gain the seat on a swing of 25%, turning a Liberal deficit of 43-57 into a lead of 68-32. The new Liberal MP Arthur Haworth was a businessman from the Manchester textile industry, and in 1909 he became chairman of the Royal Exchange. Haworth was re-elected in both 1910 elections, winning the December 1910 election unopposed - we'll come back to that in a moment. In 1911 he was made a baronet.

Sir Arthur Haworth was then appointed to a government position in 1912 as a Lord Commissioner of the Treasury. In those days joining the ministerial ladder was seen as accepting an office of profit under the Crown, meaning that Haworth's Parliamentary seat was vacated and he would have to seek re-election from his constituents. Ministerial by-elections were often a formality and normally left uncontested, but they were nonetheless a risk. The list of MPs who lost their seats in ministerial by-elections is a short one, but it does include a famous name: Winston Churchill had been unseated in 1908 in Manchester North West after he was appointed as President of the Board of Trade.

The Conservatives' Philip Glazebrook had unfinished business from the December 1910 election in which he was supposed to have been the Conservative candidate for Manchester South - but he had turned up to Manchester town hall to submit his nomination six minutes after the deadline, as a result of an error by his agent. For the second Manchester South by-election of 5th March 1912 Glazebrook made no such mistake, and he put up a strong campaign on the issues of Irish home rule, National Insurance and women's suffrage. He pulled off an 11-point swing to win the by-election by 579 votes, and Sir Arthur Haworth's ministerial career was over before it had begun. Before the decade was out, the Lloyd George Liberal government had taken the first steps towards abolishing ministerial by-elections - but this came too late for someone who we will meet later in this preview.

Philip Glazebrook was a Manchester businessman and he was also an officer in the Cheshire Yeomanry. This meant that he went to war when the First World War broke out two years later. Major Glazebrook spent the war in Egypt and then Palestine, where he won the DSO in February 1918 and was then killed in action at Bireh, in what is now the West Bank, on 7th March 1918. He is buried at the British military cemetery in Jerusalem. The resulting third Manchester South by-election of 22nd March 1918 returned the Conservatives' Robert Stoker, chairman of the Manchester Liners shipping line, who was returned unopposed as the last MP for Manchester South thanks to the wartime political truce.

The 1918 redistribution divorced Gorton from Denton for the first time, as by now Gorton (along with Levenshulme, and Burnage) was part of the City of Manchester. The expanded city was granted ten MPs, up from six on the previous boundaries, and three of its constituencies contained substantial parts of the modern Gorton and Denton. One of these was Manchester Gorton itself, which roughly corresponded to the modern Gorton and Abbey Hey ward plus the Openshaw half of the modern Clapton and Openshaw ward. The fact that one-and-a-half modern Manchester wards were enough to be a constituency in 1918 says something about how much this area has depopulated over the last century.

The interwar Manchester Gorton constituency was an industrial area which proved to be a safe Labour seat in practice, except for the disaster of 1931. The previous Gorton MP John Hodge stayed on until 1923, when he successfully passed his seat on to Labour's Joseph Compton. Compton lost his seat to the Conservatives' Eric Bailey in 1931, but got it back in 1935; Bailey is still the last Conservative MP to represent Gorton proper.

Joseph Compton died in January 1937 at the early age of 55. The resulting first Manchester Gorton by-election of 18th February 1937 pitted the Conservatives' Alexander Spearman, a stockbroker, against a political veteran. William Wedgwood Benn had first been elected to Parliament back in 1906 as a Liberal MP for the St George's division of Tower Hamlets. He transferred to Leith at the 1918 general election, but in 1927 Benn quit both the seat and the Liberal Party in order to join Labour. Benn returned to Parliament the following year as a Labour MP by winning the Aberdeen North by-election, and he served in Cabinet during the second Macdonald government as India secretary; but he had lost his seat in the 1931 landslide. Gorton proved to the final berth of Benn's long and winding Commons career, as he won the 1937 by-election by 4,758 votes with a slight swing to Labour.

Five years later Benn was promoted to the House of Lords as the first Viscount Stansgate - a decision which would later give his son Tony Benn some trouble. The resulting second Manchester Gorton by-election of 11th March 1942 was held under the wartime political truce, and nobody opposed the Labour candidate William Oldfield who had previously been Benn's election agent. Oldfield was a long-serving Manchester city councillor who worked in the cotton mills and had strong links to the cotton spinners' unions. He was very easily re-elected in 1945.

During the interwar years Levenshulme, Longsight and Belle Vue formed the majority of the new Manchester Rusholme constituency. All of these were respectable middle-class suburbs in the first half of the 20th century, and Rusholme voted accordingly: Rusholme in its first incarnation was a Conservative seat in all but two elections. Its first contest in December 1918 was won easily by the Conservatives' Robert Stoker, who had won the main predecessor seat of Manchester South in an unopposed by-election earlier in the year. His opponents included one of Parliament's first female candidates: the Labour nomination in Rusholme went to the suffragist Emmeline Pethick-Lawrence, who finished in third place and narrowly saved her deposit.

Stoker died in 1919 at the age of 60, following a long illness. The resulting first Manchester Rusholme by-election of 7th October 1919 saw Labour nominate Robert Dunstan, a doctor who had served in the Royal Army Medical Corps during the war; the Liberals put up William Pringle, a former MP for North West Lanarkshire who was trying to get back in to Parliament after losing his seat the previous year; and the Conservative candidate was John Thorpe, a barrister who is probably now best known as the father of the Liberal leader Jeremy Thorpe. Thorpe was elected easily, with a strong swing to Labour putting Dunstan in second place.

But Labour couldn't make this performance stick, and when Thorpe lost his seat in 1923 it was not to Labour candidate and Communist William Paul: it was to the Liberals' Charles Masterman. This proved to be the final act of an accident-prone Parliamentary career for Masterman, who was first elected in 1906 as the Liberal MP for West Ham North. He had been unseated in 1911 by the Election Court for overspending on his December 1910 re-election campaign; Masterman's election agent accepted responsibility for this, admitting to the Court that he had suffered a breakdown during the campaign.

Masterman quickly got back onto the green benches by winning a by-election in Bethnal Green South West, and he just as quickly found himself on the frontline of politics trying to get the Liberal government's social and welfare reform measures through Parliament. This included the National Insurance Act 1911, which introduced National Insurance and its contributions to the UK workforce for the first time. Masterman had been responsible fur securing the passage of Part I of the 1911 Act, relating to health insurance. But this made him enemies, and matters came to a head in 1914 when Masterman joined Asquith's cabinet as Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster.

Readers who have been paying attention will have guessed what's coming next: yes, it's our old friend the ministerial by-election again. Masterman was taken on the February 1914 Bethnal Green South West by-election by the Conservatives' Sir Matthew Wilson, by Horatio Bottomley's right-wing magazine John Bull, by the suffragette movement (even though he was in favour of women's suffrage) and by the Northcliffe press. It all proved too much for Masterman who lost to Wilson by 24 votes, with an Independent Labour spoiler candidate polling 316. The government inserted Masterman into a further by-election in Ipswich in May 1914, which he also lost leaving him with no option but to resign from the Cabinet. Instead Masterman spent most of the First World War trying to influence the general public as head of the War Propoganda Bureau at Wellington House. It took until 1923 for Masterman to get back in Parliament as MP for Manchester Rusholme, for what proved to be a short and relatively uninfluential final term.

Charles Masterman was soundly defeated in the 1924 general election by Boyd Merriman, who returned Manchester Rusholme to its previous status of a safe Conservative seat. Merriman was a prominent barrister whose legal career blossomed during this period. He became Solicitor General in 1928, receiving the customary knighthood, and again in 1932, and he then left the Commons in 1933 to become president of the Probate, Divorce and Admiralty Division of the High Court.

The resulting second Manchester Rusholme by-election of 21st November 1933 was one of the many Labour parliamentary campaigns of the Reverend George Woods, a Unitarian minister who contested an impressive range of constituencies over a long career - we will meet him again later. Woods got a large swing to Labour, but the Conservatives' Edmund Radford still won comfortably enough. Radford was a chartered accountant who had previously been the Conservative MP for Salford South in the 1924-29 Parliament; Rusholme proved to be a safer berth for him.

Edmund Radford died in May 1944 at the age of 63. The resulting third Manchester Rusholme by-election of 8th July 1944 was held under the wartime political truce but was nonetheless contested. In fact the Conservatives' Frederick Cundiff, a local businessman, faced a strong challenge from the Common Wealth candidate Harold Blomerley which he only fought off by 1,760 votes. This was a harbinger of things to come.

Cundiff served in Rusholme for less than a year before he lost his seat in the 1945 Attlee landslide. Manchester Rusholme turned in one of the closest results of that election, with Labour's Lester Hutchinson defeating Cundiff by 15,408 votes to 15,398, a majority of ten. Hutchinson was a Communist sympathiser who ended up being expelled from the Labour Party in 1949 for opposing the North Atlantic Treaty. He then joined the Labour Independent Group in Parliament, which consisted of him and four other fellow travellers including Konni Zilliacus, of whom more later.

While all this was going on in Rusholme, the Burnage area was part of the Manchester Withington constituency which also covered areas like Didsbury, Whalley Range and Chorlton-cum-Hardy. This was a similar seat to Rusholme in terms of its middle-class professional demographic, and as in Rusholme the Conservatives only lost two elections here in the period 1918-50. The Withington seat also saw strong population growth, and by 1945 its electorate was over twice that of the other Manchester constituencies.

The first Conservative MP for Withington was Rei Alfred Deakin Carter, a bank manager and alderman of Manchester city council who served only one term in the Commons. His retirement in 1922 set the scene for four contests here in the 1920s between Conservative Thomas Watts and Liberal candidate Ernest Simon. Watts won the 1922 and 1924 elections; Simon, an industrialist who in 1921--22 became at the time the youngest Lord Mayor of Manchester, won in 1923 (by 11,678 votes to 11,008, a majority of 670) and 1929 (by 1,885 votes). Thomas Watts got a knighthood for his political service, but of the two Ernest Simon was the one who had the most lasting impact on society: as well as being one of the first investors in the New Statesman, he was prominent in Manchester's slum clearance efforts. To that end, in 1926 Simon bought the stately home and estate of Wythenshawe Hall and donated it to the city for new housing. He was elevated to the Lords in 1947 as Lord Simon of Wythenshawe, and chaired the BBC Governors for five years.

Simon didn't seek re-election in Withington in 1931 (he instead contested Penryn and Falmouth, without success) and Edward Fleming regained the seat for the Conservatives with a large majority which wasn't seriously threatened thereafter.

The final piece of the puzzle here in the 1918-50 period was Denton, which was still an independent urban district. The 1918 redistribution dissolved the union between Denton and Gorton and created a new constituency called Mossley, which as well as the town of that name wrapped around the northern side of Ashton-under-Lyne to take in Lees, Failsworth, Droylsden, Audenshaw and Denton. Effectively, this seat was everything in this particular corner of Lancashire which didn't fit into Manchester to the west, Ashton-under-Lyne to the south-east or Oldham to the north.

Throughout its existence the Mossley constituency was dominated by the figure of Austin Hopkinson. An industrialist from an academic family (his father had been vice-chancellor of Manchester University, and served as an MP for the Combined English Universities), Hopkinson had fought in the Boer War and the Great War, in between inventing a revolutionary coal-cutting machine. He had joined Audenshaw urban district council in 1917, and then got his leg-up into Parliament in October 1918 after Oswald Cawley, the Liberal MP for the Prestwich seat which at that time covered most of the small towns north and east of Manchester, was killed in action in Palestine. (Cawley had himself been elected only in January 1918, replacing his father Sir Frederick Cawley who had been elevated to the peerage.) Hopkinson won the resulting Prestwich by-election without a contest, and he started his unconventional parliamentary career as he meant to go on by taking his seat in the Commons while wearing his Royal Dragoon Guards uniform.

The Prestwich constituency was broken up shortly afterwards by the 1918 redistribution, and Hopkinson decided to seek re-election in the new Mossley constituency which covered his local political powerbase in Audenshaw. He won easily with the Government's coupon and that set him up for a long parliamentary career as essentially an independent MP. Hopkinson lost Mossley only twice: in 1929, to Labour; and at his last election in 1945 when he lost his deposit and Labour won convincingly.

The first time Hopkinson lost in 1929 it was to Labour's Herbert Gibson, who worked in local government and was heavily involved in the co-operative movement. Gibson enjoyed a majority of 5,029 votes in 1929 but lost the rematch to Hopkinson in 1931 by 1,430 votes with the Conservatives close behind in third. A second rematch between Gibson and Hopkinson in 1935 saw Hopkinson's majority increase to 2,170.

Austen Hopkinson's political career was finally ended in the 1945 Attlee landslide. This is where Labour's Reverend George Woods comes back into the story. In the 1935-45 Parliament Woods had been the MP for Finsbury in London, but he sought re-election here rather than in the capital.

The 1950 redistribution transferred Mossley town into the Ashton-under-Lyne constituency and Lees into the new seat of Oldham East, with the remaining four towns in the Mossley seat constituting the new and short-lived Droylsden constituency. The Revd Woods sought re-election here in 1950, winning by 4,136 votes. He then died in July 1951 at the age of 65, and his seat was vacant going into the general election that October. The Labour party selected William Williams, a Welshman who had lost his seat the previous year in the Middlesex constituency of Heston and Isleworth; Williams had represented that seat since 1945 after previously being assistant secretary of the Post Office Workers' Union. He won Droylsden with a reduced majority of 1,870.

Meanwhile, the 1950 redistribution had cut the number of MPs for Manchester - which by now had expanded to include Wythenshawe - from ten to nine. One of the seats which disappeared was Manchester Rusholme, taking Labour Independent Group MP Leslie Hutchinson with it: he decided to seek re-election by taking on Clement Attlee as the Labour Independent Group candidate for Walthamstow West, and he finished last with 2% of the vote. Longsight and Belle Vue were transferred out of Hutchinson's seat into the Manchester Ardwick constituency, which also covered a very industrial/slum area to the south-east of the city centre. Ardwick was an open seat taken over in this election by Labour's Leslie Lever, whose brother Harold was already a Manchester Labour MP. The Levers were a family of solicitors and philanthropists who did a lot of work for the poor, which gave Leslie - who was Lord Mayor of Manchester in 1957-58 - a strong personal vote in the area. This was despite the fact that slum clearance saw his constituency's electorate fall sharply during his 20 years in office.

Slum clearance wasn't a problem for Manchester Withington, which was severely oversized and needed to be cut down at the 1950 election. Didsbury went into the the new Manchester Wythenshawe constituency while Chorlton-cum-Hardy and Whalley Range were transferred to Manchester Moss Side, leaving the Withington seat consisting only of Withington, Burnage and Rusholme (transferred in from the abolished Manchester Rusholme). Withington's previous MP Edward Fleming decided to transfer to Manchester Moss Side, which had been flipped from a Labour seat into a safe Conservative seat by the transfer of Chorlton; but as it happened he died six days before polling day and the 1950 election in Moss Side had to be postponed in consequence.

That meant that 1950 Withington election saw a return to the Commons for the Conservatives' Frederick Cundiff, following his defeat in Rusholme in 1945, but this proved to be short-lived as Cundiff then stood down at the 1951 election. He was replaced by Sir Robert Cary, who himself made a return to the Commons after serving for Eccles in the 1935-45 Parliament. Cary, who had served in both World Wars, got onto the lowest rungs of the Conservative frontbench during his political career but never any further up the greasy pole than that.

For the final piece of the jigsaw, the Manchester Gorton constituency was expanded southwards in 1950 to take in Levenshulme. The Conservative candidate for this seat in 1950 was Agatha Christie's nephew James Watts, but there was no mystery as to the winner in this seat: Labour's William Oldfield was re-elected in both 1950 and 1951 without fuss.

There was further boundary upheaval here in 1955, when the Boundary Commission drew two seats which crossed the city of Manchester's eastern boundary. One of these was more or less a recreation of the old Gorton constituency including Denton, but now with the name Manchester Gorton, as the Droylsden constituency disappeared after just five years and Denton - this time along with Audenshaw - was transferred back into what was by now called the Manchester Gorton constituency. To make room for this, Levenshulme was moved into Manchester Withington and Rusholme transferred from Withington to Ardwick.

The previous Gorton MP William Oldfield stood down and Droylsden's MP William Williams chose to seek re-election in Manchester Openshaw, so Gorton was an open seat. At this point the curious figure of Konni Zilliacus comes properly into the story. Born in Japan to a Finnish father and an American mother, Zilliacus had worked for the League of Nations between the wars, and after becoming Labour MP for Gateshead in the Attlee landslide he planted himself firmly on the party's left wing, to the point where he was thrown out of Labour in 1949 for his pro-Soviet views and ended up in the Labour Independent Group. By 1952 Labour and Zilliacus had patched up their quarrel and he was selected for the redrawn Manchester Gorton seat. The 1955 boundary changes, together with some distrust of Zilliacus, meant that he beat the Conservatives by just 269 votes.

In that context Zilliacus did well to hold the seat against the Macmillan landslide of 1959, increasing his majority to 857. The UK electorate in general might never have had it so good in 1959, but the Lancashire cotton industry was going through a hard patch at the time and Manchester Gorton was one of several constituencies in south-east Lancashire which swung to Labour in 1959 against the national trend. The seat then became safe for Labour in the two 1960s elections.

Konni Zilliacus died in 1967. The resulting third Manchester Gorton by-election of 2nd November 1967 was a tricky defence for Labour at a time of unpopularity for the Wilson government, and the Conservatives took the seat seriously with a young candidate who had serious name recognition. Winston Churchill, the grandson of the wartime prime minister of the same name, was at this point a young Times journalist who had distinguished himself with his reporting on the Six-Day War five months previously. He was up against Labour candidate Kenneth Marks, a headteacher, NUT figure and Denton urban district councillor. Churchill pulled off a swing to the Conservatives of over 9%, but it wasn't enough and Marks won the Manchester Gorton by-election with a majority of 557. Marks wasn't seriously challenged after that, and he served until 1983 when the Gorton seat was heavily redrawn.

Levenshulme and Rusholme were now in the constituency of the Conservatives' Sir Robert Cary who went on to represent Withington for 23 years, seeing off three future Labour MPs in the process. Robert Sheldon (Ashton-under-Lyne, 1964-2001) lost here in 1959 by a distance, future Cabinet minister David Clark (Colne Valley 1970-Feb 1974, South Shields 1979-2001) lost in 1966 by just 647 votes, and Michael Noble (Rossendale Oct 1974-1979) saw Cary increase his majority to 3,489 at the 1970 election.

The transfer of Rusholme into Manchester Ardwick for the 1955 election tilted the constituency towards the political right, and Leslie Lever had to work hard for his re-election that year, but he wasn't seriously challenged after that as urban decay started to take its toll. Lever retired in 1970 and successfully passed a safe Labour seat on to journalist and satirist Gerald Kaufman, who wasn't remotely affected by further boundary changes in February 1974 which transferred Levenshulme from Manchester Withington into Manchester Ardwick.

Those boundary changes coincided with Sir Robert Cary's retirement as Conservative MP for Withington. For the February 1974 election the Withington Conservatives selected Fred Silvester who had previous Commons experience: Silvester had won the Walthamstow West by-election in 1967, but he then lost that seat at the following general election in 1970. Again Withington was a safer berth, particularly with the return of Didsbury in the 1974 boundary changes. Silvester won by 4,413 votes over Labour in a contest where all three parties polled over 10,000 votes, and he was appointed as an opposition whip - in which role he was depicted in James Graham's 2012 play This House.

Gorton and Denton went through their second divorce at constituency level in 1983, when the parliamentary boundaries caught up with the new English local government structure. This area was no longer on the edge of Lancashire, hard up against the borders with Cheshire and with Stockport county borough which had left Denton in a corner of the map. Now Denton was part of the Tameside metropolitan borough alongside other old Lancashire towns like Ashton-under-Lyne and former Cheshire towns like Hyde, Dukinfield and Stalybridge; and the Denton/Stockport boundary was no longer a county line. So, when the Boundary Commission found that Stockport and Tameside boroughs had awkwardly-sized populations entitling them to 3½ and 2½ MPs, it was OK to create a constituency crossing their border to give 6 seats to the combined area. That crossborder seat was called Denton and Reddish, and we shall come to it in a moment. Meanwhile Gorton was added to the Manchester Ardwick constituency, which needed a new name because Ardwick itself had been transferred into Manchester Central: thus the Manchester Gorton name was retained, even through Denton and Reddish was the true successor of that seat. These boundaries more or less survived further boundary reviews in 1997 and 2010.

None of this affected Burnage, which remained part of the Manchester Withington constituency all the way through to 2024. But by the 1980s the demographic and social changes which would ultimately transform Withington from an urban professional Conservative stronghold into the urban professional left-wing stronghold we know today were well under way. Despite the return of Chorlton to the seat at the 1983 redistribution, Fred Silvester might well have lost his seat to Labour that year had it not been for a strong performance by the SDP candidate Bernard Lever, who had name recognition as the son of Leslie Lever. Lever junior finished third with 26%, Labour's Frances Done was second with 34%, and Silvester was re-elected for his fourth term with 39%.

That was the last hurrah for Conservatism in Withington. Fred Silvester lost his seat in 1987 to Keith Bradley, who became the first Labour MP for the constituency. Bradley was a Manchester city councillor and secretary of the Stockport Community Health Council at the time of his first election, which he won by a majority of 3,391.

Ten years later, Keith Bradley's majority was over 19,000 votes and he had made it onto the rungs of the ministerial ladder, peaking as Deputy Chief Whip. The Conservatives fell to third place behind the Lib Dems in 2001, but a majority of 11,524 for Labour didn't seem cause for concern.

Wrong. Keith Bradley lost his seat in 2005 to the Liberal Democrats' John Leech on a swing of 17%, the largest swing in any seat that year. The Labour vote may have been split in a rather unusual way, as Keith Bradley appeared on the ballot paper below the Conservative candidate Karen Bradley (who was later elected as MP for Staffordshire Moorlands, was a cabinet minister throughout the Theresa May administration and now chairs the Commons home affairs select committee). Leech, who was the deputy leader of the Liberal Democrat group on Manchester city council, won with a majority of 667 votes. He was re-elected with an increased majority of 1,850 over Labour in the 2010 Withington election, a contest in which all three major party candidates eventually became MPs: the Tories' Chris Green represented Bolton West from 2015 to 2024, while Labour's Lucy Powell won the Manchester Central by-election in 2012. Powell, of course, is now deputy leader of the Labour Party.

John Leech was an opponent of the Conservative-Lib Dem coalition that resulted from the 2010 election, and - not surprisingly given his student-heavy electorate - he voted against increasing tuition fees. Not that that helped Leech in the end: the 2015 election saw a swing of 17% against him to restore the status quo ante for Labour.

The Labour MP for Withington since 2015 has been Jeff Smith, who at the time of his election was a Manchester city councillor and a former event manager and DJ. Smith's initial majority was 14,874 votes, and two subsequent attempts by John Leech to get his seat back saw the Lib Dem vote fall further. In December 2019 the third round of Smith v Leech finished with a 67-15 win for Jeff Smith, a majority of 27,905 votes. Smith leaves our story in 2024, when Burnage was transferred out of his seat.

Meanwhile Manchester Gorton's MP Kenneth Marks retired in 1983, and his seat was taken over by the previous Manchester Ardwick MP Gerald Kaufman who leveraged the "longest suicide note in history" into a majority of 9,965 votes in that year. He was never seriously challenged afterwards. Kaufman had never risen above junior ministerial level before the Labour defeat in 1979, but he spent the Kinnock years as one of Labour's most senior frontbenchers: he was shadow home secretary in 1983-87, followed by five years as shadow foreign secretary in 1987-92. In 1992 Kaufman became chair of what we now call the Commons culture, media and sport committee, serving in that role for 13 years. He was knighted in 2004. And, following his election in 2015 for his 12th term of office, Kaufman became the Father of the House as the first to have been sworn in of four survivors from the 1970 general election. The other three were Ken Clarke, Michael Meacher - who died later that year - and Dennis Skinner.

Sir Gerald Kaufman died in February 2017 at the age of 86, as the oldest member of the Commons - a title which then went to Skinner, while Clarke took over as Father of the House. Fittingly given his support for the restoration of Gorton Monastery, his memorial service was held there. His death would have triggered the fourth Manchester Gorton by-election, which was called to take place on 4th May 2017 as the eleventh by-election of the 2015 Parliament. Eleven candidates were nominated.

Then, on 18th April, the prime minister Theresa May announced that she would seek an early general election to take place on 8th June 2017. The timings involved meant that by the time the Manchester Gorton by-election had concluded Parliament would have been dissolved, rendering the poll a dead letter. In order to call the poll off the House of Commons had to resolve to issue a writ of supersedeas to the returning officer, in line with a precedent from 1923, because there is no statutory way to cancel an election that is already in progress.

The nominations from the cancelled Manchester Gorton by-election were not carried forward to the general election, but nine of the eleven candidates from the by-election did stand again including George Galloway, who finished in third place as an independent candidate. However, the winner with a massive 76% of the vote was Labour's Afzal Khan, who was born in Pakistan and came to the UK at the age of 11. Khan was a solicitor who had served on Manchester city council from 2000 to 2015, including a year as Lord Mayor in 2004-05, before being elected in 2014 as a Labour MEP for north-west England. He continued to serve Manchester Gorton until the seat was abolished in 2024, then transferred to the new Manchester Rusholme constituency.

The final piece of this complicated historical puzzle is the 1983-2024 seat of Denton and Reddish, a safe Labour seat which had only two MPs - both of them called Andrew - in its 41-year history. The first of these was Andrew Bennett, who had previously represented Reddish as part of the marginal Stockport North constituency for nine years. Bennett was a definite winner from the 1983 edition of Boundary Commission roulette, taking over a safe seat for as long as he wanted it, and for much of the 1980s he was a Labour frontbencher focusing on education and science. Boundary changes for the 1997 election, which transferred Brinnington out of the seat and Dukinfield in, made not a jot of difference to his majority. Bennett eventually called time on his parliamentary career at the 2005 election, when he retired after 31 years in office.

When Andrew Bennett started his career on the green benches, his successor hadn't been born. Andrew Gwynne, the son of the late darts commentator John Gwynne, first came to prominence in 1996 when he was elected as a Labour councillor for the Denton West ward of Tameside council, aged slightly less than 21 years and 11 months. At the time he was reported to have been the country's youngest councillor, and the council experience must surely have helped with his degree in politics and contemporary history at Salford, from where he graduated in 1998. In 2005 Gwynne became, aged 30, the youngest member of the Labour benches, easily defeating the Conservative candidate and former Olympic rower Alex Story.

Despite his 20 years on the green benches, Gwynne's frontbench career was modest. His peak position on the greasy pole came in the Corbyn years, when Gwynne spent three years in the Shadow Cabinet with the communities and local government portfolio. Instead Gwynne mostly served the Labour Party behind he scenes: he was the party's co-national campaign co-ordinator, alongside Ian Lavery, for the 2017 and 2019 general elections.

Gwynne's Denton and Reddish seat was broken up in 2024, and he followed the Denton part of the seat to became the first MP for Gorton and Denton, polling 51% of the vote to 14% for Reform UK and 13% for the Green Party; in integer terms, that gave Gwynne a majority of 13,413 votes over Reform UK. He immediately joined the government as a junior health minister.

And all was fine until the existence of "Trigger Me Timbers" came to light in February 2025. This was a bad-taste WhatsApp group in which Gwynne and friends mocked all sorts of targets ranging from their own constituents to the Labour deputy leader Angela Rayner. It was definitely the sort of behaviour which does not look clever in the cold light of day, and once the Labour Party realised what its members had been doing it suspended all of its elected representatives who were members of the group. This amounted to Gwynne, nine Tameside councillors (mostly from the three Denton wards, and including Gwynne's wife), two Stockport councillors and former Tameside councillor Oliver Ryan, who had been elected in 2024 as the Labour MP for Burrnley. Gwynne also lost his job as a junior health minister as a result of all this.

Over the subsequent twelve months all of the Labour councillors and MPs who were suspended over Trigger Me Timbers have since been readmitted to the whip, or quit the party, or (in some cases) both, except for Andrew Gwynne. After almost a year in the political wilderness Gwynne eventually resigned from the Commons in January after receiving medical advice not to return to work. He has reportedly reached a settlement with the Commons authorities for early retirement, which would allow him to claim an ill-health pension straight away.

Andrew Gwynne leaves behind what would be, in normal times, a plum Labour seat which the party should expect to defend without breaking much sweat. The last non-Labour MP for any part of this seat was the Lib Dems' John Leech who was defeated in 2015; the last Conservative MP for any part of this seat was defeated in 1987; and Gorton itself has an unbroken line of Labour MPs going all the way back to 1935.

The Labour party is similarly dominant in local government across the constituency. Manchester city council has had a Labour majority continuously since 1971, Tameside council continuously since 1979. The Conservatives haven't won any of the wards in this constituency for decades, and until Labour lost Longsight ward to the Worker's Party in 2024 they held a full slate of councillors here.

If we add up the votes cast in May 2024 in the four Manchester wards within this seat and the three Denton wards, as a best fit for the constituency boundary, we get 54% for Labour, 17% for the Workers Party (nearly all of which came out of Longsight and Levenshulme) and 15% for the Greens. Labour and the Greens were the only parties to stand candidates in all seven wards, and Denton North East was a straight fight between them.

Andrew Gwynne's resignation brought to a head months of tiresome speculation as to whether Andy Burnham, the popular elected mayor of Greater Manchester, would go for the Labour nomination in the resulting by-election. Burnham would undoubtedly have been a strong candidate. His three crushing mayoral wins have demonstrated that he can reach parts of Greater Manchester which other Labour politicians can't: Burnham carried every single ward in Greater Manchester in the 2021 mayoral election, and all but one ward in 2024. He has spent his ten years as mayor literally reshaping the streets of the county, with the creation of the locally-controlled yellow and black-liveried Bee Network of buses with their £2 maximum fare. Andy Burnham did in the end apply for the Gorton and Denton nomination, but he was rejected by the Labour Party's national executive committee on the grounds that he would have to resign as mayor to seek election as an MP: the Greater Manchester mayoralty has police and crime commissioner powers, and MPs can't be PCCs at the same time. A county-wide mayoral by-election was seen as too much of a risk.

Instead the Labour nomination has gone to Angeliki Stogia, who has been a Manchester city councillor since 2012. She represents Whalley Range ward, which is part of the Rusholme constituency. Stogia, who was born in Greece but has lived in Manchester for decades and is a naturalised UK citizen, works for the planning consultancy firm Arup: they have seconded her to Network Rail to work on the ongoing Transpennine Route Upgrade. Before she joined Arup, Stogia sat for four years in Manchester council's cabinet with the environment, planning and transport portfolio. Stogia has Burnham's full support in this campaign, and the Manchester wards in this seat have plenty of posters around painting her as the unity candidate.

Second here in July 2024 were Reform UK. Their candidate is Matt Goodwin, a political commentator whose name and opinions will be familiar to some of the electorate thanks to his regular show on the GB News channel. Goodwin is a former academic, who wrote books on the radical right such as the 2015 work Revolt on the Right before he joined the ranks of the radical right himself.

Goodwin's co-author on Revolt on the Right, Rob Ford, has taken a different professional route by staying in academia to become one of the most respected psephologists working in the UK today. Ford is a professor of politics at Manchester University who has his own Substack at swingometer.substack.com which comes strongly recommended. He has blogged extensively on this by-election - and not without consequences for himself, as we shall come to in a moment. In his initial post on the by-election Ford had this to say on his former colleague:

As many Swingometer readers will know, Matthew and I have a history - we worked together a lot as young postdoctoral researchers in Manchester, culminating in a well received book on the rise of UKIP - “Revolt on the Right.” More recently, I have criticised Matthew’s misrepresentation of evidence and statistics and his use of irresponsible and polarising rhetoric on difficult issues. I have not worked with him professionally or been in contact with him socially in a long time. While I can’t erase our personal history, I will do my best to assess his candidacy objectively - I leave it to readers to decide whether I am successful.

Ford went on to identify one strength of Goodwin as a candidate, namely his high media profile, and two weaknesses: that he is a first-time candidate and that he has, "to put it mildly, a message discipline issue". But possibly the biggest problem which Reform UK have had with message discipline in this by-election didn't relate to words coming out of Goodwin's mouth. Early in the campaign the party sent out thousands of handwritten-style letters to electors, which were credited to a named local pensioner but which lacked the required legal imprint showing that they were material from the Goodwin campaign. The printers have apparently accepted that they were at fault for this.

Yesterday Goodwin's election agent - who is legally responsible for this material - appeared before the High Court and was granted relief from this breach of electoral law, with Mr Justice Butcher accepting - in the words of section 167 of the Representation of the People Act 1983 - that the lack of an imprint "arose from inadvertence or from accidental miscalculation or from some other reasonable cause of a like nature, and in any case did not arise from any want of good faith". None of the other candidates opposed this application - they will be well aware that this could just as easily have happened to them. The effect of relief under section 167 is a get of jail card. It isn't a get out of jail free card given the legal costs involved, but it would probably be less expensive and certainly less hassle than being prosecuted.

Third place in Gorton and Denton two years ago went to the Green Party. There had been some speculation that their new leader Zack Polanski might go for the candidacy - he is originally from Salford - but the party's nomination has ultimately gone to Hannah Spencer. Spencer is the leader of the Green Party group on Trafford council, where she represents Hale Central ward which is far more middle-class than anything to be found in Gorton and Denton. By trade she is a plumber and gas engineer.

The Greens clearly fancy their chances in this by-election and they are fighting it hard, with Green stakeboards all over the seat's Manchester wards and Green posters appearing in the windows of many businesses in Levenshulme and Longsight. So it's a bit of a shame that the Greens' leaflet composers decided that some deeply misleading quotes from Professor Rob Ford's Substack were just the ticket to demonstrate that they were in the race to beat Labour here. Ford extracted and accepted an apology from the Greens for this, and then understandably blew his top when a further Green leaflet subsequently went out with the same out-of-context quotes on it.

Two other parties saved their respective deposits here in 2024. Fourth place went to the Workers' Party on 10.3%, which led to some speculation as to whether they would field George Galloway or their Longsight councillor Shahbaz Sarwar. As it turned out, the answer to that question was "neither": there is no Worker's Party candidate in this by-election and that 10.3% of the vote is up for grabs. Fifth here last time were the Conservatives on 7.9%; their candidate Charlotte Cadden comes to politics from a 30-year career as a police officer. The Liberal Democrats, who finished in sixth and last place in 2024 with a deposit-losing 3.8%, have gone for Jackie Pearcey who has a long history in this seat: Pearcey served multiple terms as a councillor for the former Gorton North ward until 2012, and she has stood four times for Parliament in the former Manchester Gorton constituency, in 1997, 2001, 2017 and 2019. Pearcey is the only candidate to explicitly give an address in the constituency.

Five new candidates have also turned up at the electoral services office in Manchester town hall before the deadline and successfully submitted their nomination papers. Hoping to bring home the electoral bacon is the Official Monster Raving Loony Party's Sir Oink A-Lot, who is described as "the whole hog" and has reportedly had a crackling campaign. His position at the top of the ballot paper certainly proves that pigs might fly. Nick Buckley MBE, who was the Reform UK candidate for the 2021 Greater Manchester mayoral election and then finished in third place as an independent candidate for the 2024 mayoral election, has the nomination of Advance UK here: Buckley, who is originally from Longsight, founded a charity for the homeless called the Mancunian Way, and (as his name on the ballot paper suggests) he was appointed MBE in the 2020 New Year Honours for services to young people and to the community in Greater Manchester. In the 2024 mayoral election Buckley was the only candidate other than Andy Burnham to carry a ward in Greater Manchester, via the slightly bizarre route of picking up the Gaza independent vote in Oldham Werneth. The Libertarian Party have selected Dan Clarke, who was previously the Liberal Party candidate for Runcorn and Helsby in the 2024 general election and the 2025 by-election. Standing for the Social Democratic Party is Sebastian Moore, who was last seen on a ballot paper in the 2024 general election when he contested Manchester Central. Rejoin EU's candidate Joseph O'Meachair, who stood in Preston in the 2024 general election, has some experience in answering the hard questions: under the name Joseph Meagher, he was captain of the Manchester University team that won University Challenge in 2006. And last on the ballot paper is Hugo Wils, who works in an aerospace factory and is the candidate of the Communist League: Wils has four previous candidacies for Manchester or Tameside council under his belt, and he is yet to score 100 votes. In total.

Constituency polling in the UK has a poor track record, and this is a particularly difficult constituency to poll well thanks to its demographic makeup. The Manchester component of Gorton and Denton includes large proportions of young adults and Muslims, neither of which are groups which respond very well to opinion pollsters with their pesky questions.

These difficulties are illustrated with the first published poll for this by-election from Find Out Now, which was conducted from 25th to 27th January and had topline figures of 36% for Reform UK, 33% for Labour and 21% for the Greens. This poll has been heavily criticised both for its small sample size - with just 143 responses there are very large error bars here - and for its methodology, which is based on market research questions to participants in a literal postcode lottery. This is not a polling method which is going to reach large numbers of Muslims, given that gambling is seen by Islam as haram.

We have since had two published constituency polls with larger, and hopefully more balanced, samples. Omnisis were in the field from 13th to 19th February with a sample of 452: topline figures there were 33% for the Greens, 29% for Reform UK and 26% for Labour. Meanwhile Opinium for the Byline Times and Forward Democracy took from 16th to 24th February to collect 401 responses, finding a tie for first place between Labour and the Greens on 28% with Reform UK just one point behind on 27%. All of these figures are within the margin of error of each other. There were rumours that Survation were also trying to get a sample of Gorton and Denton voters together, but if they succeeded in that task then the results of their work have not been published.

Some other supposed constituency voting figures have been circulating on leaflets, which range from extrapolations from national polling through selected canvass returns to numbers made up from whole cloth. Since I've had a pop at both Reform UK and the Greens in this piece for misleading leaflets, it's only fair to highlight this very dubious piece of data visualisation from the Labour campaign. Quite where the underlying figures come from I have no idea; and as for the style, well, I'm reminded of some maps I used to draw of Scottish PR local election results in this format for the Local Elections Archive Project. I eventually stopped doing that after receiving feedback that one particular map looked like "a tropical fish on acid".

The limited evidence from reputable polling, taken together with the campaign dynamics, does suggest that this is a three-way race and a case could be made that any of the Greens, Labour and Reform UK have a good chance of winning. Labour have the track record in all parts of this seat; the Greens are hoping to tap into the current zeigeist and capitalise on Labour's poor national polling; while Reform UK will expect to do well in Denton which is much whiter than the Manchester wards but is only a third of the seat's electorate. Really it's all to play for, so there's no excuse for Gorton and Denton's electors not to go down to their local polling station today and cast their vote for their preferred candidate. You might not end up voting for the winner, but you will be most welcome nonetheless.

This column likes to highlight pubs which serve as polling stations, so a limited mention is due to the Sun in September, in Burnage. This pub isn't a polling station itself, but they have allowed a temporary polling station to be parked in their car park for the benefit of democracy.

Shirley

Southampton council, Hampshire; caused by the resignation of Liberal Democrat councillor George Percival.

We have just one by-election on the undercard of today's Parliamentary Special, and it's in Shirley, a north-western suburb of Southampton. Shirley's old town centre lies on the main road going north-west from Southampton city centre towards Romsey; the Shirley ward extends north and east from here to take in Southampton Common and the large Southampton General Hospital. In 1980 a boy called Rishi Sunak was born at that hospital, and the pharmacy which Sunak's mother ran for many years lies just outside the boundary of Shirley ward. The presence of Southampton General Hospital means that Shirley ward is within the top 25 wards in England and Wales for those working in the health and social work sector (26.8% of the workforce).

Shirley was once, very briefly, the centre of its own local government unit: the Shirley and Fremantle Urban District was created in 1894, and then abolished just a year later when its area was annexed by Southampton. The modern Shirley ward of Southampton council took on its current boundaries in 2002, and it has trended towards the left over the last 22 years: Shirley ward consistently voted Conservative up to 2010, then changed to consistently voting Labour from 2011. In May 2024 Labour held the ward with 40% of the vote against 25% for the Conservatives (an unusually poor score) and 20% for the Liberal Democrats (an unusually good score).

Since 1950 Southampton has been divided into two parliamentary seats which are named after rivers: the Itchen seat is eastern Southampton, while the Test constituency covers the western half of the city including Shirley. Test has been in Labour hands since 1997 when it was gained by Alan Whitehead, who had previously been leader of Southampton council and had made three previous failed attempts on the seat. Whitehead retired at the 2024 general election and successfully passed his seat on to another former Southampton council leader. Satvir Kaur had represented Shirley ward on the council since 2011, and she became leader of the opposition Labour group in 2021 and then leader of the council when Labour took control of Southampton in 2022. Kaur was the first Sikh woman to lead a council in the UK.

Following her election to Westminster in July 2024, Satvir Kaur resigned her seat on Southampton council. The resulting Shorley ward by-election in October 2024 saw an upset, with the Liberal Democrats' George Percival coming from third place to gain the seat from Labour. Percival topped the poll with 39%, while the Conservatives and Labour finished on 24% each; the defending Labour candidate Victoria Ugwoeme was pushed into third place by six votes.

The 2024 by-election winner George Percival served for just fifteen months on Southampton council before he tendered his resignation in January. He has been promoted at work, and his new role - which presumably pays more than being a councillor - is based outside Southampton.

So, the voters of Shirley ward are being called out for their second by-election of this term. Defending for the Lib Dems is Chris Shank, a Canadian by birth who has lived in Southampton for 12 years. Shank works for the University of Southampton and he is a local scout leader; in the 2024 Southampton elections he contested the neighbouring Freemantle ward. The Conservatives have changed candidate to Andy Palmer, a retired Metropolitan Police officer who ran an accommodation business after leaving the force. The Labour candidate James Clark appears to be standing for election for the first time. Also on the ballot are Joanne Clements for the Greens, former Southampton Labour councillor Andrew Pope who was an independent candidate in the 2024 by-election and now has the nomination of the Independent Network, David Cowley for the Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition, and Paul Chance for Reform UK.
