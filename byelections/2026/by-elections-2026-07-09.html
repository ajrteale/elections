Regent's Park

Camden council, London; caused by the disqualification of Green Party councillor Mohammed Abu Naser. Today's other London by-election is also a Green Party defence, in one of the few Zone 1 wards which contains a significant amount of green space. Regent's Park is one of the Royal Parks of central London, and administratively it is split between the City of Westminster and the London Borough of Camden which both have wards called Regent's Park. The Regent's Park ward of Camden covers the eastern fringe of the park itself, while its southern boundary is Euston Road. Suffield Park

North Norfolk council; caused by the resignation of Liberal Democrat councillor Emma Tooke. Our East Anglian by-election this week is in the self-styed Gem of the Norfolk Coast, the seaside resort of Cromer. Suffield Park ward is the eastern third of Cromer, covering the East Cliff, the lighthouse, the town's secondary school Cromer Academy and the local hospital. The present lighthouse dates from 1833 and was originaly half a mile from the cliff-edge; as a result of coastal erosion, it's a lot closer now. The ward name is a reference to the Lords Suffield, who owned a significant amount of land in Cromer back in the day including the stately home of Cromer Hall, which is not in this ward. We should note in particular here Edward Harbord, the third Lord Suffield, who was prominent in the campaign to abolish slavery and piloted the Slavery Abolition Act 1833 through the Lords. His son Charles Harbord, the fifth Lord, was a close friend of Edward VII and briefly held government office under Gladstone in 1886 as Master of the Buckhounds. Now there's a government job you don't get any more. There's no government job of any kind for the current MP for Cromer, who is a Liberal Democrat: he is Steff Aquarone, who gained the North Norfolk constituency from the Conservatives in 2024. This seat has similar boundaries to North Norfolk council, which is based in Cromer and has had a Lib Dem majority since 2019; the leader of the district council is Tim Adams, who was also re-elected two months ago as the Norfolk county councillor for Cromer. North Norfolk had previously been under Conservative leadership from 2011 to 2019, and its list of former leaders during that period includes a notorious figure. Keith Johnson, who represented the neighbouring Cromer Town ward, killed his wife in December 2012 before turning the gun on himself. Since 2019 Suffield Park ward had been represented by Emma Tooke, who was elected in 2019 and re-elected in 2023 under her previous surname of Spagnola. Tooke, who is a former mayor of Cromer, had a reputation in the town as the "loo lady" for her campaigning for disabled public toilets in Norfolk; she has two autistic sons who benefitted from their installation. She has relocated to Poland for family reasons, and the North Norfolk Lib Dems called the resulting by-election for today. This turned out to be a bad move, because within hours of that timescale being confirmed the Norfolk Police and Crime Commissioner resigned, prompting a county-wide by-election which - thanks to the ridiculously inflexible timetable for PCC by-elections - will take place in seven days' time. So the voters of Suffield Park ward will get to do this all over again next week. Defending this week's by-election here for the Lib Dems is Tyler Wragg, a former pupil of Cromer Academy who is only 19 or 20 but is already fighting his second election campaign: in May he was a Norfolk county council candidate in Stalham division. The Conservatives have selected John Lee, a fisherman who is the son of the late John Lee who was a councillor for this ward from 2007 to 2019: that John Lee had been leader of North Norfolk council when he lost his seat to Tooke in 2019. Also on an all-male ballot are David Rusell for Labour, Paul Sutcliff for the Greens and Reform UK's Benjamin Miles, who was a rather distant runner-up to the Lib Dems in the Cromer county division two months ago. Baruc

Vale of Glamorgan council; Glamorgan; caused by the resignation of Plaid Cymru councillor Mark Hooper. We now travel south from Cardiff to meet Gavin and Stacey at the seaside resort of Barry Island. Depending which source you consult, this takes its name either from a Welsh word for a hill or from St Baruc, an obscure Welsh saint of the sixth century who lived here as a hermit. Baruc clearly gives his name to this electoral ward. Barry Island used to be a genuine island in the Severn estuary, but in the late 19th century it was joined to the Welsh mainland by the development of Barry Docks: this was a project of the Barry Railway Company to export coal from the South Wales coalfields, bypassing the congested dock complex in Cardiff. The north side of Barry Island was an industrial area dominated by the docks and by extensive railway yards; the sandy south side of the island became a tourist and entertainment complex, which in time included a Butlin's holiday camp. Once coal exports declined much of the area around Barry Docks became Woodham Brothers' scrapyard, to which railway wagons and steam locomotives were sent for disposal. But the withdrawal of steam by British Railways meant that Woodham's ended up with more work than it could handle. The scrappers mostly left the locomotives alone because the wagons were easier to cut up and took up more space in the yard, and ultimately 217 of the 297 steam locomatives which had been sent to Barry were sold to preservationists by Woodham's at their scrap metal value. Many of these were restored to working order, including the experimental British Railways locomotive Duke of Glouester which is still pulling charter trains on the main line today, and the Great Western Railway's Olton Hall which has been seen by millions at the head of the Hogwarts Express in the Harry Potter films. The furmer Butlins camp and the Waterfront area around the dock have now been extensively redeveloped for housing, which has led to strong population growth in this century in Baruc ward which covers the whole of Barry Island together with the Knap and the area around Barry railway station on the mainland. The area's rail link to Cardiff has clearly made the Waterfront an attractive commuter area. Baruc ward was increased from two councillors to three in 2022, but this was not enough to keep pace with demand. A special boundary review has recently taken place for Vale of Glamorgan council, as a result of which Baruc ward will be split into two for the 2027 Welsh local elections: the dockland part of its area will become the basis for a new Waterfront ward, with two councillors of its own, while the rump Baruc ward still retains three councillors. Barry is the largest town in the Vale of Glamorgan district, which has been under no overall political control since 2012. The 2022 elections here returned 25 Labour councillors against 13 Conservatives, 8 for Plaid Cymru, 4 independents and 4 seats for a Llantwit Major localist party, who are the junior partners to Labour in the ruling coalition. In a by-election last year Reform UK gained a seat in Illtyd ward elsewhere in Barry, but the by-election winner has since jumped ship to Restore Britain. The district is slightly larger than the Vale of Glamorgan Westminster seat, which has been closely fought between Labour and the Conservatives: the Vale has voted for the government at every election since its creation in 1983 with the exception of a Labour win at 1989 by-election. The current Labour MP Kanishka Narayan, who was born in India, came to Cardiff at the age of 12 and is one of the many people who have moved house to the Barry Waterfront in recent years: he is a junior minister responsible for artificial intelligence and online safety. Narayan became Wales' first ethnic minority MP when he gained his seat from the Conservatives in 2024. For Senedd purposes the Vale is joined with Bridgend in the Pen-y-bont Bro Morgannwg constituency, which returns six MSs. In May Plaid Cymru were top of the poll here, polling 34% of the vote and winning two seats, Reform UK had 30% and also won two seats, and the remaining two seats were split between the Conservatives on 15% and Labour on 12%; the Conservative MS is the party's former Welsh leader Andrew R T Davies. Top of the Plaid Cymru list here in May was Mark Hooper, who defeated sitting MS Luke Fletcher (who was ranked 3rd and lost his seat at the election) for the number 1 slot. Hooper had previously served since 2022 as a Vale of Glamorgan councillor for Baruc ward, which is Plaid's strongest ward in the district at local election time. In the 2022 election Plaid won 36% of the vote here to 25% for Labour, 20% for the Conservatives and 18% for the Greens, with Hooper taking the third seat created by the boundary review to join long-serving ward councillors Nic Hodges and Steffan Williams. With Hooper off to Cardiff Bay Plaid Cymru have a by-election to defend here. Their candidate is Barry town councillor Shirley Hodges, who is married to ward coucnillor Nic Hodges and runs the Victorian Barry Experience on social media. The Labour candidate is Trevor Durham, who is a chartered financial planner. Standing for the Conservatives is first-time election candidate Chelsea Cottle. The Greens' Lynden Mack will be hoping to improve on his last-place finish in this ward in 2022; he has since been the party's Westminster candidate for Vale of Glamorgan in 2024. And joining the fray after sitting out the 2022 election here are the Lib Dems, with Gabriela Ferguson, and Reform UK who have selected Sarah Roderick. Pembroke Dock: Market

Pembrokeshire council, West Wales; caused by the death of independent councillor Brian Hall. We now travel into West Wales to reach the Cleddau estuary, which effectively divides the county of Pembrokeshire into two halves. This is a centre of the oil and gas industry with a large number of refineries and LNG storage facilities surrounding the town of Milford Haven, but not all sea movements here are to do with fossil fuels. There's also some passenger travel, as Irish Ferries run two sailings a day between Rosslare and the Welsh port of Pembroke Dock. The dockyard itself is the main feature of Pembroke Dock: Market ward, which is the western of the five wards covering the town of Pembroke Dock. This is a nineteenth-century town spawned by the dockyard, which was established by the Admiralty in 1814. The Navy moved out in 1926, but after a short period the Air Force moved in and RAF Pembroke Dock became the world's largest station for flying boats until the military finally turned the docks over to civilian use in 1956. Hangovers from the Navy and RAF eras include a pair of flying boat hangars, one of which housed a full-scale Millennium Falcon during the filming of The Empire Strikes Back, and the Victorian-era Defensible Barracks which housed Pembroke Dockyard's Royal Marines contingent. Also here are the small South Pembrokeshire Hospital and the Garrison Chapel, which has been rebuilt using EU funding and turned into a heritage centre. In the 2021 census Pembroke Dock: Market had 10.4% of adults working in lower supervisory and technical occupatios, which was the highest figure for any ward in Wales and was exceeded in England only by four wards dominated by Sellafield or by the quarrying industry. It also made the top 20 wards in England and Wales for residents with level 2 qualifications (5+ GCSEs or equivalent), and was in the top 100 for residents working in mining or quarrying at 1.3% of adults in work, although this only amounted to 11 people. Pembroke Dock is the third-largest town in Pembrokeshire after Milford Haven and Haverfordwest, having overtaken the older and neighbouring town of Pembroke. It is one of the major towns in the Mid and South Pembrokeshire parliamentary seat, which roughly corresponds to the "Little England Beyond Wales" where the culture and language has for centuries more English than Welsh. This seat has been represented since 2024 by Labour backbencher Henry Tufnell. In the Senedd Pembrokeshire is paired with Ceredigion to form the Ceredigion Penfro constituency whic split its six seats three ways in May. Plaid Cymru were top of the poll with 36% and won 3 seats; Reform UK polled 26% and won two seats; and the Conservatives won one seat with 17%. The Labour slate polled just 7% vote and their lead candidate, the outgoing First Minister Eluned Morgan, was nowhere near holding her seat. Not that this has much relevance for local elections in Pembrokeshire, whose local elections are dominated by independent candidates and where there is a serious problem with unopposed elections: at the last Pembrokeshire council elections in 2022, 25 of the 60 seats were filled without a single vote being cast. This included Brian Hall, who had been an independent councillor for Pembroke Dock: Market ward for 30 years since he won a by-election in 1996. Hall last faced a contested election in 2017 when he defeated another independent candidate, Hayley Wood, by 58-42. In 2008 he had seen off a challenge from a high-profile Labour candidate in Christine Gwyther, who had previously represented Carmarthen West and South Pembrokeshire from 1999 to 2007 in what was then called the Welsh Assembly. Brian Hall died in April at the age of 78. There is a lot of interest in the by-election to replace Hall, with seven candidates being nominated - that's six more than in the last local election here. Three of them are independents, and I'll take them in reverse alphabetical order. Hayley Wood, who is a local food back trustee and a miscarriage of justice advocate, was the losing candidate here in 2017; Claire Francis-Boswell also stresses her record of community work in the town; and Paul Dowson previously had a controversial term as an independent councillor for Pembroke Dock: Central ward from 2017 to 2022, which included a run as a UKIP candidate in the 2021 Senedd election. After Dowson lost his seat in 2022, he was disqualified from public office for three years by the Adjucation Panel for Wales - the Welsh councillors' ombudsman and custodians of the Welsh Councillors Behaving Badly file - for falsely claiming that a fellow councillor had distributed child pornography, falsely suggesting that a member of the public had a criminal record, misrepresenting the sex education school curriculum, and fabricating evidence to the Panel to suggest that another member of the public was a sex offender. Since 2022 Dowson has also pleaded guilty to possessing amphetamines and he has been prosecuted for working as a pub doorman without a liance. With his disqualification now expired, Dowson was an independent candidate in the 2026 Senedd election for Ceredigion Penfro, polling 88 votes and finishing in 44th and last place; he is standing here as an independent affiliated with Restore Britain. We also have four candidates who have been nominated more conventionally by political parties: Lee Herring is standing for the Lib Dems, Ryan Morgan for Reform UK, Chloe Richards for Plaid Cymru and Jamie Street for the Conservatives.
