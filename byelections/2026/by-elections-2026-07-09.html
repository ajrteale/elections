Lambeth council, London; caused respectively by the resignations of Green Party councillors Joanna Eaves and Saiqa Ali. We'll start in a London borough which took rather a long time to settle its administration following May's local elections. The Labour Party lost control of Lambeth council two months ago and fell into second place on the council: the Green Party won 29 seats in the election, Labour fell to 26 and the Lib Dems ended up with eight seats and the balance of power. A Green Party mayor was installed - Paul Valentine, who had won a by-election in Herne Hill and Loughborough Junction ward last year - but the AGM was delayed while the Green and Lib Dem groups attempted to set up an old-fashioned committee system to run the council. Eventually this was found to be legally impossible, and a minority Green Party cabinet was installed in June to run the council. Perhaps it says something that one of Cllr Valentine's mayoral charities for 2026-27 is the Bureau of Silly Ideas. Lambeth was by no means the last council to finish coalition-building this year. In fact there are still two councils which have yet to even agree on a leader following May's elections, Kirklees and Oldham. Oldham council is deeply split between the Labour group of 18, the Reform UK group of 16 and a 20-strong alliance of Oldham Group (mostly Asian independents), Lib Dem and Conservative councillors; none of these blocs is anywhere near the 31 votes required for a majority, and nobody appears to be prepared to do a deal with anyone else. The failure of Kirklees to elect a leader is more curious, because the Reform UK and Conservative groups there have a majority between them and similar arithmetic in neighbouring Bradford quickly led to a Reform UK minority administration being appointed. Oldham and Kirklees have both had multiple previous failed attempts to elect a new leader over the last two months, and they will both be trying again on 15th July. The Green minority administration in Lambeth might well be in some trouble before then, because they already have two by-elections to defend as a result of newly-elected councillors handing in their notices. If Labour gain both of these seats, they will take over from the Greens as the largest party on Lambeth council. And one of the by-elections is in a very marginal ward. Clapham Park split its three seats in May, with the Labour slate polling 36% of the vote and winning two seats; the Green Party polling 32% and winning one seat; and the Lib Dems finishing best of the rest on 10%. The Green Party councillor Joanna Eaves, who defeated the third Labour candidate Louie Somerville-Sutherland by 37 votes, stood down within two weeks of her election on health grounds. Those who come to Clapham Park ward looking for open space will be disappointed: apart from the small Agnes Riley Gardens, the ward is entirely built up with flats and houses either side of the South Circular Road. Brixton Hill, the A23 towards Croydon, is the ward's eastern boundary; the La Retraite Catholic girls' school is at the western end. Clapham Park is in the top 100 wards for residents born in the Americas and the Caribbean (9.9%) or in Africa (11.8%), for black (27.1%) and mixed-race (8.1%) residents, and for residents working in administrative or support services (9.3%). The vast majority of Clapham Park ward is included in the Clapham and Brixton Hill parliamentary seat represented by Labour backbencher Bell Ribeiro-Addy, who was first elected in 2019 for the predecessor seat of Streatham. That constituency was broken up in the 2024 boundary review, and Streatham itself is now part of the Streatham and Croydon North constituency represented by Steve Reed, who currently sits in Cabinet as the Housing, Communities and Local Government secretary. The latter constituency includes Streatham St Leonard's ward, located a mile or two south along the A23 from Clapham Park. This ward is the core of Streatham, which is named after the road: the A23 was originally a Roman road running from Londinium to a port on the south coast. St Leonard's church has been located next to the road since Saxon times, although the oldest part of the present-day structure is the tower which dates from the Tudor era. The ward has rail links to central London from Streatham Hill, Streatham and Streatham Common railway stations. Streatham was an attractive middle-class suburb of south London for the most of the 20th century, and it elected Conservative MPs into the Thatcher years and Conservative local councillors unto the 1990s. John Bercow, the future Speaker of the House of Commons, was a Lambeth councillor for St Leonard's ward for one term from 1986 to 1990. But by the 1990s urban decay had set in: the present-day Streatham St Leonard's ward has very similar demographics to Clapham Park. Its politics, however, remain significantly different. This is one of London's longest-standing Green wards, with the Green Party's Scott Ainslie - who was an MEP for London from 2019 to 2020 - having been a councillor here continuously since 2014. From 2018 to 2022 one of his ward colleagues was Jonathan Bartley, who was co-leader of the Green Party of England and Wales for most of that period. The Greens haven't had it all their own way in this ward, and in 2022 Labour gained one of the ward's seats with their candidate Martin Abrams benefitting from being at the top of the alphabetical ballot paper. Ironically, Abrams subsequently defected from Labour to the Greens and he is now the leader of Lambeth council, having been re-elected in a different ward in May. So this meant that the Green Party needed to gain a seat from Labour in Streatham St Leonard's ward this year. In retrospect, their selection of Saiqi Ali was not a good one. The Labour campaign quickly drew attention to Ali's social media, which turned out to have all sorts of nasty anti-Semitic stuff on it. The police then got involved, and a week before polling day Ali was one of two Lambeth Green candidates who were arrested by the Met on suspicion of stirring up racial hatred. By this point it was far too late for the Greens to take her off the ballot paper. When polling day came, the Green Party won all three seats in Streatham St Leonard's ward with 47% of the vote, against 23% for Labour and 15% for the Liberal Democrats. Ali finished in third place a very long way behind her running-mates Scott Ainslie and Alice Weavers, but she was still elected with a majority of 353 votes over Labour. Saiqa Ali was not admitted to the Green group on Lambeth council, and she quickly handed in her notice. That unforced selection error means that the Greens have a by-election to defend in Streatham St Leonard's ward. This time they have gone for Dario Goodwin, an author who is a fairly recent recruit to the party from Labour; he was an unsuccessful candidate in May in Brixton Acre Lane ward. Labour have reselected Jade Albás, who was runner-up here two months ago. Baruc

Vale of Glamorgan council; Glamorgan; caused by the resignation of Plaid Cymru councillor Mark Hooper. We now travel south from Cardiff to meet Gavin and Stacey at the seaside resort of Barry Island. Depending which source you consult, this takes its name either from a Welsh word for a hill or from St Baruc, an obscure Welsh saint of the sixth century who lived here as a hermit. Baruc clearly gives his name to this electoral ward. Barry Island used to be a genuine island in the Severn estuary, but in the late 19th century it was joined to the Welsh mainland by the development of Barry Docks: this was a project of the Barry Railway Company to export coal from the South Wales coalfields, bypassing the congested dock complex in Cardiff. The north side of Barry Island was an industrial area dominated by the docks and by extensive railway yards; the sandy south side of the island became a tourist and entertainment complex, which in time included a Butlin's holiday camp. Once coal exports declined much of the area around Barry Docks became Woodham Brothers' scrapyard, to which railway wagons and steam locomotives were sent for disposal. But the withdrawal of steam by British Railways meant that Woodham's ended up with more work than it could handle. The scrappers mostly left the locomotives alone because the wagons were easier to cut up and took up more space in the yard, and ultimately 217 of the 297 steam locomatives which had been sent to Barry were sold to preservationists by Woodham's at their scrap metal value. Many of these were restored to working order, including the experimental British Railways locomotive Duke of Glouester which is still pulling charter trains on the main line today, and the Great Western Railway's Olton Hall which has been seen by millions at the head of the Hogwarts Express in the Harry Potter films. The furmer Butlins camp and the Waterfront area around the dock have now been extensively redeveloped for housing, which has led to strong population growth in this century in Baruc ward which covers the whole of Barry Island together with the Knap and the area around Barry railway station on the mainland. The area's rail link to Cardiff has clearly made the Waterfront an attractive commuter area. Baruc ward was increased from two councillors to three in 2022, but this was not enough to keep pace with demand. A special boundary review has recently taken place for Vale of Glamorgan council, as a result of which Baruc ward will be split into two for the 2027 Welsh local elections: the dockland part of its area will become the basis for a new Waterfront ward, with two councillors of its own, while the rump Baruc ward still retains three councillors. Barry is the largest town in the Vale of Glamorgan district, which has been under no overall political control since 2012. The 2022 elections here returned 25 Labour councillors against 13 Conservatives, 8 for Plaid Cymru, 4 independents and 4 seats for a Llantwit Major localist party, who are the junior partners to Labour in the ruling coalition. In a by-election last year Reform UK gained a seat in Illtyd ward elsewhere in Barry, but the by-election winner has since jumped ship to Restore Britain. The district is slightly larger than the Vale of Glamorgan Westminster seat, which has been closely fought between Labour and the Conservatives: the Vale has voted for the government at every election since its creation in 1983 with the exception of a Labour win at 1989 by-election. The current Labour MP Kanishka Narayan, who was born in India, came to Cardiff at the age of 12 and is one of the many people who have moved house to the Barry Waterfront in recent years: he is a junior minister responsible for artificial intelligence and online safety. Narayan became Wales' first ethnic minority MP when he gained his seat from the Conservatives in 2024. For Senedd purposes the Vale is joined with Bridgend in the Pen-y-bont Bro Morgannwg constituency, which returns six MSs. In May Plaid Cymru were top of the poll here, polling 34% of the vote and winning two seats, Reform UK had 30% and also won two seats, and the remaining two seats were split between the Conservatives on 15% and Labour on 12%; the Conservative MS is the party's former Welsh leader Andrew R T Davies. Pembroke Dock: Market

Pembrokeshire council, West Wales; caused by the death of independent councillor Brian Hall. We now travel into West Wales to reach the Cleddau estuary, which effectively divides the county of Pembrokeshire into two halves. This is a centre of the oil and gas industry with a large number of refineries and LNG storage facilities surrounding the town of Milford Haven, but not all sea movements here are to do with fossil fuels. There's also some passenger travel, as Irish Ferries run two sailings a day between Rosslare and the Welsh port of Pembroke Dock. The dockyard itself is the main feature of Pembroke Dock: Market ward, which is the western of the five wards covering the town of Pembroke Dock. This is a nineteenth-century town spawned by the dockyard, which was established by the Admiralty in 1814. The Navy moved out in 1926, but after a short period the Air Force moved in and RAF Pembroke Dock became the world's largest station for flying boats until the military finally turned the docks over to civilian use in 1956. Hangovers from the Navy and RAF eras include a pair of flying boat hangars, one of which housed a full-scale Millennium Falcon during the filming of The Empire Strikes Back, and the Victorian-era Defensible Barracks which was built as housing for Pembroke Dockyard's Royal Marines contingent. Also here are the small South Pembrokeshire Hospital and the Garrison Chapel, which has been rebuilt using EU funding and turned into a heritage centre. In the 2021 census Pembroke Dock: Market had 10.4% of adults working in lower supervisory and technical occupatios, which was the highest figure for any ward in Wales and was exceeded in England only by four wards dominated by Sellafield or by the quarrying industry. It also made the top 20 wards in England and Wales for residents with level 2 qualifications (5+ GCSEs or equivalent), and was in the top 100 for residents working in mining or quarrying at 1.3% of adults in work, although this only amounted to 11 people. Pembroke Dock is the third-largest town in Pembrokeshire after Milford Haven and Haverfordwest, having overtaken the older and neighbouring town of Pembroke. It is one of the major towns in the Mid and South Pembrokeshire parliamentary seat, which roughly corresponds to the "Little England Beyond Wales" where the culture and language has for centuries more English than Welsh. This seat has been represented since 2024 by Labour backbencher Henry Tufnell. In the Senedd Pembrokeshire is paired with Ceredigion to form the Ceredigion Penfro constituency whic split its six seats three ways in May. The Labour slate polled just 7% vote and their lead candidate, the outgoing First Minister Eluned Morgan, was nowhere near holding her seat. Not that this has much relevance for local elections in Pembrokeshire, whose local elections are dominated by independent candidates and where there is a serious problem with unopposed elections: at the last Pembrokeshire council elections in 2022, 25 of the 60 seats were filled without a single vote being cast. This included Brian Hall, who had been an independent councillor for Pembroke Dock: Market ward for 30 years since he won a by-election in 1996. Hall last faced a contested election in 2017 when he defeated another independent candidate, Hayley Wood, by 58-42. In 2008 he had seen off a challenge from a high-profile Labour candidate in Christine Gwyther, who had previously represented Carmarthen West and South Pembrokeshire from 1999 to 2007 in what was then called the Welsh Assembly. Brian Hall died in April at the age of 78. There is a lot of interest in the by-election to replace Hall, with seven candidates being nominated - that's six more than in the last local election here. Three of them are independents, and I'll take them in reverse alphabetical order. Flintshire council, North Wales; caused respectively by the death of Liberal Democrat councillor Hilary McGuill and by the resignation of independent councillor Bernie Attridge. Today's surfeit of Welsh council by-elections concludes in the north-east of the county, with two polls to rather different wards of Flintshire council. Up in the Clwydian hills we find the ward of Argoed and New Brighton, which covers a pair of villages located to the west of Buckley and to the east of the former county town of Mold. (Flintshire council quit the County Hall in Mold last year and is now based in Ewloe.) The name Argoed refers to the community council which has the same boundaries as the ward, but the largest centre of population is the village of Mynydd Isa on the Mold-Buckley road; New Brighton lies just to the north of here. Rather different in character is the town of Connah's Quay, which is actually the largest settlement in Flintshire by population but goes to a lot of effort to hide its light under a bushel. Although Connah's Quay lies on the North Wales Coast railway line, there is no railway station here. The Quay is not recognised as a town by the Royal Mail, which identifies every address in the local CH5 postcode district as part of the "Deeside" post town. The area was sufficiently under English control in the 11th century to be listed in the Domesday Book, but that was under its previous name of "Wepre". The Connah of the modern name was James Connah, an eighteenth-century industrialist, and the Quay was created by the Industrial Revolution to replace the port of Chester further up the Dee, which had silted up. But the quay at Connah's Quay has itself succumbed to the silt deposited here by the River Dee. Indeed, the Dee Estuary itself now contains a lot of reclaimed land and saltmarsh. The England-Wales border and the other administrative boundaries here were clearly drawn before the estuary silted up and they now make no sense whatsoever on the ground, and one effect of this is that Connah's Quay Central ward crosses over what is now the channel of the Dee to take a narrow slice through the Deeside Industrial Park on the far side of the river. The ward boundary cuts through the Tata steeldworks and the Shotton Paper mill at an acute angle, and runs up to take in the Sealand rifle range next to the English border. And Connah's Quay Central is not alone in this: there are nine other Flintshire wards which extend out into the estuary and saltmarsh in this bizarre manner, including two of the other three Connah's Quay wards. No, I don't understand it either. The Deeside Indsutrial Park is a major employer in Connah's Quay and clearly shows up in the town's census return. Connah's Quay Central is in the top 20 wards in England and Wales for residents employed in manufacturing (22.6%) and in the top 100 for routine occupations (25.0%). It is also in the top 10 wards in Wales for residents who born in Bulgaria or Romania (4.0%), adults who have Level 1 or entry qualifications (1-4 GCSEs or equivalent, 12.8%) or adults who have Apprenticeship qualifications (8.2%). Argoed and New Brighton ward makes the top 100 wards in England and Wales for intermediate occupations (16.1%) and has high levels of owner-occupation. These wards are in Labour-held Westminster seats. Argoed and New Brighton ward is part of the Clwyd East constituency represented by Labour backbencher Becky Gittins, while Connah's Quay is the largest town in the Alyn and Deeside constituency which has returned government whip Sir Mark Tami to Parliament continuously since 2001. Alyn and Deeside was the only constituency in North Wales which remained in Labour hands at the 2019 general election, and in May's Senedd election the Fflint Wrecsam constituency which it is part of was the only costituency to return a Labour MS: that was Ken Skates, who has taken over as the leader of Welsh Labour on an interim basis after Eluned Morgan lost her seat in the election. Argoed and New Brighton was included within the Clwyd Senedd constituency, which gave 32% and three seats to Reform UK, 28% and two seats to Plaid, 20% and one seat to the Conservatives whose Welsh leader Darren Millar was elected here, and 10% to Labour who missed out. Flintshire has been under no overall control since 2008 with large Labour and independent groups who have never been quite able to knock each other out. Bernie Attridge had also fallen foul of the Standards Board for Wales during his career, and he was suspended as a councillor for four months in 2024 for sending sexualised messages to a vulnerable constituent. Argoed and New Brighton ward was created in 2022 by the merger of two previous single-member wards. High Fell

Gateshead council, Tyne and Wear; caused by the resignation of Reform UK councillor Danielle Cavanagh. Up in the North East of England, High Fell ward is aptly-named: it occupies high ground on the southern edge of Gateshead, around the summits of Sheriff Hill (167 metres) and Black Hill (151 metres). Sheriff Hill overlooks the Queen Elizabeth Hospital, which is the main general hospital for Gateshead and nearby towns; further along the Old Durham Road which links this ward together is the Cardinal Hume Catholic School, named after the late-20th century Archbishop of Westminster. Basil Hume was originally a Geordie. High Fell ward makes the top 100 wards in England and Wales for social renting, at 46.2% of households, and it has a strongly working-class demographic profile. In other words, this is exactly the sort of Northern ward where Reform UK ran riot this year after decades of Labour representation - and Gateshead council got new ward boundaries this year, allowing Reform UK to win an overall majority on the council straight away. The 2026 Gateshead elections returned 38 Reform UK councillors against 13 Lib Dems, 12 Labour and 3 Greens. Labour had never failed to poll an absolute majority of the vote in High Fell ward in the period 2004-24, but second-place finishes for UKIP in 2014 and 2015 had demonstrated the potential for the radical right here: and in 2026 High Fell elected the Reform UK slate with 45% of the vote against 29% for Labour and 14% for the Greens. The local Labour MP Mark Ferguson, a former editor of the website LabourList who was elected in 2024 for the Gateshead Central and Whickham constituency, must be looking at these results with some concern. The ruling Reform UK group on Gateshead council have immediately found themselves one woman down, after High Fell ward councillor Danielle Cavanagh stood down just ten days after her election. She cited rapidly-expanding business commitments for her resignation. Cavanagh was elected in second place here in May, so the winner of this by-election will take over a two-year term ending in 2028. Defending this seat for Reform UK is Lindsay Atkinson, who stood in May in Whickham South and Sunniside ward; he is the chair of the party's Gateshead Central and Whickham branch, which appears to have bullied one of their Gateshead council cabinet members into not taking their special responsibility allowance for this year in order to offset the cost to the council of this by-election. The Labour candidate is Kathryn Walker, a retired financial services professional who is seeking to get straight back onto the council after losing her seat in May: Walker previously represented this ward from 2023 to 2026. Standing for the Greens is Lee Turner, who was an unsuccessful candidate in May in Windy Nook and Whitehills ward. Also on the High Fell ballot are Jonathan Aibi for the Lib Dems, Elaine Brunskill for the Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition (who actually finished second here back in 2012), and former Northumberland councillor Barry Flux for the Conservatives.
