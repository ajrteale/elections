"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

Four by-elections on 26th March 2026:

Stanford

Vale of White Horse council, Oxfordshire; caused by the resignation of Liberal Democrat councillor Sue Caul.

We have four polls today, two of which are in districts in southern England which this column has already visited once this month. Both of those contents are for wards lying on the northern slopes of chalk hills, and the first of these is home to a major tourist attraction whose origins go back into the mists of time.

The Uffington White Horse has been overlooking its eponymous Vale since some time in the 2nd or 1st millennium BC, making it far older than its many modern-day imitators on other chalk slopes around the country. But the White Horse is just one element of a prehistoric landscape. It was located close to a Bronze and Iron Age hillfort now known as Uffington Castle, which occupies the highest point in Oxfordshire at an altitude of 261 metres. The hillfort was handily located on the Ridgeway, a trackway of similarly ancient origins which ran from Salisbury Plain along the ridge of the Berkshire Downs and the Chilterns.

In the modern day the hilltops here are uninhabited, and the people here now live in the less exposed Vale of the White Horse. This is the valley of the River Ock, which flows east to join the Thames at Abingdon. On this river we find Stanford in the Vale, a village of around 2,300 souls which is named after a "stone ford" of the Ock. Stanford was the first home of the poet and entertainer Pam Ayres, who was born here in 1947; Christopher Wordsworth, a nephew of the poet William Wordsworth, was vicar of Stanford in the Vale in the 1850s before ascending the Church of England's greasy pole to end up as bishop of Lincoln. There are five other parishes in Stanford ward, including Uffington, but Stanford in the Vale is by far the largest of them.

Stanford lies at the western end of the Didcot and Wantage constituency, which was won at its first election in 2024 by the Liberal Democrat candidate Olly Glover. Glover is a former railwayman who is now the Lib Dems' transport spokesman. The Liberal Democrat success here followed up on a spectacular performance at the 2023 Vale of White Horse council elections, when the entire Lib Dem slate of 34 candidates was elected; the other four seats on the council went to the Green Party as part of an electoral pact, but the Lib Dems have since gained one of these seats at a by-election in Abingdon Abbey Northcourt ward two weeks ago.

The closest the Conservatives came to holding any of their seats was in Stanford ward, where the Lib Dem candidate Sue Caul defeated the Conservatives' Nathan Boyd by just ten votes, 546 to 536. In percentage terms that was 44-43, with the Greens being the only other party to stand. Caul submitted her resignation from the council in February, but her seat had previously been placed on your columnist's radar before then. In 2025 Sue Caul was temporarily taken off the list of councillors on the Vale of White Horse council website, which prompted me to add her council's 2023 election results to the Local Elections Archive Project, but on that occasion her removal turned out to be a website error and the by-election klaxon was a false alarm. These things happen sometimes.

The Lib Dems' defence of their most marginal Vale of White Horse ward might not be helped by locally underwhelming results in last year's Oxfordshire county elections. Stanford in the Vale and the three parishes around it are part of the Kingston and Stanford county division, which voted Conservative in 2025 with the Lib Dems not far behind. Meanwhile Uffington and the neighbouring parish of Woolstone are located in the Shrivenham county division, which was narrowly won by the Green Party last year thanks to an even split of the right-wing vote between the Conservatives and Reform UK.

So holding this seat could be a difficult task for the defending Lib Dem candidate Adrian Bettridge, a former Royal Air Force officer who has a military OBE from the 2012 Birthday Honours. Bettridge is a former head of Armed Forces Remuneration, and he now works as a bursar at a girls' boarding school in Banbury. The Conservatives' Lee Evans is the county councillor for the Kingston and Stanford division which covers most of this ward, and he is also a historian of his party who co-hosts Since Attlee and Churchill, a podcast on the subject of postwar British politics. Additionally Evans has a previous Parliamentary campaign under his belt: in the 2024 general election he was the Conservative candidate for Ellesmere Port and Bromborough. Also standing are Jacob Yung for the Green Party, Labour's Rob Blundell who failed to get into double figures when he stood in a by-election for the neighbouring Ridgeway ward last year and polled 8 votes, and Reform UK's Sarah von Simson who was a creditable third in the 2025 county elections in Shrivenham.

Halstead, Knockholt and Badgers Mount

Sevenoaks council, Kent; caused by the death of Conservative councillor Gary Williamson.

Our first Conservative defence of the week comes in one of the few genuinely rural areas inside the M25 motorway, as we consider three villages which are located in the North Downs landscape between Orpington to the north and Sevenoaks to the south. Badgers Mount is on the old main road between the two towns, with Halstead and Knockholt in the countryside to the west.

All three of these villages now lie just outside the Greater London boundary, but Knockholt was included within the original version of Greater London created in 1965: before that reorganisation, the village had been part of Orpington urban district since the 1930s. This particular extension of the Metropolis didn't last very long: Knockholt was removed from the London Borough of Bromley in 1969 to become a parish within Sevenoaks rural district. Halstead had been a parish within that district since the 1890s, while Badgers Mount parish is of much more recent vintage: it was created in 2015 as a split from Shoreham parish.

Halstead parish includes part of the large and sprawling site of Fort Halstead, which had originally been built in 1892 to defend London against any invasion from the south. From the 1930s Fort Halstead became a centre for defence research: a number of rockets and projectiles were developed here before the Second World War, while after the war the fort became home to "High Explosive Research", the secretive project which designed the UK's first atomic bomb. There was also civilian work undertaken at Fort Halstead, including analysis of major terrorist events: the plane fuselage from Lockerbie and the bus blown up in the 7th July 2005 London bombings were brought here for analysis. After many years of forensic, ballistics and other research, the Defence Science and Technology Laboratory finally left Fort Halstead in 2022; demolition work started earlier this year, with the site slated for new homes.

Knockholt railway station, which is served by commuter trains between London and Sevenoaks, lies just outside the ward boundary. This station is actually closer to Badgers Mount and Halstead than it is to Knockholt village, which is around 3 miles away. Knockholt station had originally been opened in 1876 with the name "Halstead for Knockholt", but Halstead was dropped from the name in 1900: this was partly to avoid confusion with the Essex town of that name, and partly at the instigation of the South Eastern and Chatham Railway's deputy chairman Alfred Smithers who lived in Knockholt village; a stained-glass window in St Katharine's Church in Knockholt is a memorial to Smithers and his wife. In 1918 Sir Alfred Smithers was elected as the first MP for the Chislehurst constituency, which then included Knockholt; he served one term as a Conservative MP, while his son Sir Waldron Smithers went on to represent his father's seat for 21 years from 1924 to 1945.

Since 1974 all three villages in this ward have been part of the Sevenoaks local government district and the Sevenoaks parliamentary constituency. The parliamentary seat has returned Conservative MPs continuously for more than a century. Since 2019 it has been represented by Laura Trott, who served in Cabinet from 2023 to 2024 as chief secretary to the Treasury; she is now in the shadow cabinet as the Conservatives' education spokeswoman.

Every election to the modern Sevenoaks council has resulted in a Conservative majority with the sole exception of 1995, but the Tories lost control here last year following a split in the local party. Nine of the 33 Conservative councillors elected here in 2023 are now in the breakaway "West Kent Independent" group, while two more have walked off to join Reform UK. Three weeks ago the Conservatives gained a by-election following the death of an independent councillor in Hextable, but they are still short of a majority: the latest composition has 22 Conservatives plus this vacancy against 14 Lib Dems, 9 West Kent Independents, 4 Greens, 2 Reform UK and an independent.

Halstead, Knockholt and Badgers Mount ward is part of the Sevenoaks West division of Kent county council, which was one of only five Kent seats which the Tories held in last year's county elections. So this is still an area which the Conservatives represent at all levels of government, but that will be tested in this by-election to replace long-serving councillor Gary Williamson following his death in late December. Williamson, who ran a chain of butcher's shops in the area, had sat on Sevenoaks council continuously since 1999, and he had topped the poll in four of the five elections to Halstead, Knockholt and Badgers Mount ward since the current boundaries were introduced: the exception was 2015, when there was no poll because the Conservative candidates for this ward were unopposed. The 2023 local elections here were a straight fight between the Conservatives and the Lib Dems, with Williamson and his running-mate John Grint (who has served this ward since 2007) winning by 65-35.

There will be more choice for the electors in this by-election. Defending for the Conservatives is Tony Marshall, who is the vice-chair of Halstead parish council; Marshall is also a former vice-president of the British Sub-Aqua Club, so he might appreciate this dive into his local area's history and politics. Another parish councillor on the ballot is the Lib Dem candidate Stephen Maines, a retired civil servant who sits on Knockholt parish council. George Pender, a former Conservative councillor for Ash and New Ash Green ward who lost his seat to the Green Party in 2023, is hoping to make a political comeback here with the Reform UK nomination; he completes an all-male ballot alongside the Green Party's Robert Royston, who stood here in last year's county elections.

Axholme Central; and
Brumby

North Lincolnshire council; caused respectively by the deaths of Conservative councillor David Robinson and Labour councillor Susan Armitage.

The last occasion that this column covered polls in North Lincolnshire was in June 2024, just before the last general election. On that occasion there were two seats up for election, a Labour seat in the Brumby ward of Scunthorpe and a Conservative seat on the rural Isle of Axholme, both of which were to replace councillors who had recently died (Andrew's Previews 2024, page 295). Nearly two years on, we are back in the district for exactly the same reason.

Brumby ward is actually appearing in Andrew's Previews for the third time, having previously had a by-election in June 2017 - on the undercard of that year's general election - as well as June 2024. It's a very working-class corner of Scunthorpe, making the top 50 wards in England and Wales for residents employed in manufacturing (19.8%) and the top 40 wards for adults with 1-5 GCSEs or equivalent (14.7%). Back in 2017 I mistakenly stated that this is housing in a flat and nondescript landscape (Andrew's Previews 2017, page 152), but in fact Brumby ward, which covers housing on the western edge of Scunthorpe, lies close to the bottom of a fairly steep slope running down to the Trent floodplain.

On the other side of the Trent we find the Isle of Axholme, which is not a true island: the Isle name is used here in the old sense of slightly raised ground surrounded by marshland and floodplain. Originally Axholme lay between the Trent to the east, the Idle to the south and the Don to the north and west, but significant land drainage works in the seventeenth century saw the Don (which was once a tributary of the Trent) diverted well away from this area to flow into the Yorkshire Ouse at Goole.

The Axholme Central ward is based on the market town of Epworth, which is seen as the spiritual home of Methodism because it was the birthplace of John and Charles Wesley. Their father, Samuel Wesley, was the rector of St Andrew's church here from 1697 until his death in 1735. During this period the Wesleys' home burned down in 1709 and was rebuilt in the Queen Anne style; the Old Rectory, as it is now known, is owned by the Methodist Church and run by them as a museum. The Old Rectory is reputedly haunted, with poltergeist activity recorded here by the Wesley family in the winter of 1716-17.

Also here is the parish of Belton, which takes in a large number of hamlets to the south of the M180 motorway. These include Sandtoft, where there is a museum with the UK's largest collection of preserved trolleybuses. This museum occupies part of the site of the former RAF Sandtoft, which is now a minor airfield mainly used for flying instruction.

The Isle of Axholme is the only significant part of Lincolnshire to lie west of the River Trent, but its parliamentary history has been complicated by the fact that in 1974 it was transferred from Lincolnshire into the short-lived and unloved county of Humberside. Because of this, since 1983 it has been included in constituencies with parts of what were once Yorkshire. In its current form this is the Doncaster East and the Isle of Axholme constituency, which voted for the Labour candidate Lee Pitcher in 2024. Appropriately enough for someone with that surname, Pitcher had previously worked in the water industry.

This result must have been rather galling for the defeated Conservative candidate Nick Fletcher, who had previously been the Conservative MP for Don Valley and who lost his seat despite very favourable boundary changes. The Isle of Axholme has traditionally been a strongly Conservative area in elections to North Lincolnshire council, and Axholme Central's local election results are typical of that. In 2023 the Conservative slate defeated Labour here by 68-32 in a straight fight; and the Tories have held both of the ward's seats since an independent councillor stood down in 2011.

Until 2019 one of the councillors for Axholme Central was the then North Lincolnshire council leader Liz Redfern, who is now in the House of Lords as Baroness Redfern. Her successor as council leader, Rob Waltham, has now been in post for over nine years. Waltham's Conservative group must be doing something right, because the 2023 elections to North Lincolnshire council saw no change from the 2019 seat totals: the Conservatives continued to lead Labour here by 27-16 while many other Tory councils across the country were falling like ninepins.

Baroness Redfern is not the only former North Lincolnshire council leader currently serving in Parliament. The 2024 general election also saw a return to the Commons for Labour MP Sir Nic Dakin, who recovered the Scunthorpe seat which he had lost to the Conservatives in 2019. Dakin is now a government whip with the title of Vice-Chamberlain of the Household, which means he is the designated MP held "hostage" in Buckingham Palace during the State Opening of Parliament. One of the official jobs of the Vice-Chamberlain of the Household is to send daily emails to the King reporting on what's going on in the Commons; Elizabeth II reportedly had a particular interest in hearing gossip and insider news.

Dakin's Scunthorpe constituency is much more urban and industrial than the seats around it, and Brumby ward is politically typical of the town. In all previous contests this century it has been safely Labour, with the Labour slate winning all three seats in 2023 thanks to a 71-29 margin over the Conservatives in another straight fight. Councillor Stephen Swift then died after being suddenly taken ill while giving a speech at the council's 2024 budget meeting, and Labour held the resulting by-election - a month before the 2024 general election - with a reduced margin of 61-29 following interventions from the Greens and Lib Dems.

The voters of Brumby ward are now being called out for their second by-election of this term to replace Sue Armitage, who had served for Brumby ward since 2007 and was mayor of North Lincolnshire in 2009-10. Armitage passed away in mid-December, so this by-election has taken quite some time to organise. In the meantime the Conservatives have also lost a long-serving councillor in David Robinson, who lived in Epworth for all his life and had represented Axholme Central ward since 2011. Robinson died in January following a long period of ill-health.

Defending Axholme Central ward for the Conservatives is Caroline Finch, an Epworth town councillor who runs a fencing firm. Labour have selected Matthew Rawcliffe, who gives an address some distance away in the town of Kirton in Lindsey; he has recently graduated in psychology from Huddersfield University. Alex Ellis is the Green candidate, Reform UK have selected Gerrad Farmer who was an unsuccessful candidate for Doncaster council last year, and the Lib Dems have nominated Michael Shaw.

In Brumby ward the defending Labour candidate is Thomas Smith, who comes from the old Labour Party stereotype: Smith is a steelworker who is secretary of the Scunthorpe steelworks branch of the Community union, and he has been a prominent figure in recent years' negotiations on the steelworks' future. The Conservatives have selected Joanne Saunby, who is trying to get back onto North Lincolnshire council: Saunby won a May 2021 by-election in the town's former Ashby ward (Andrew's Previews 2021, pages 92 and 184), but she lost re-election in the redrawn Ashby Lakeside ward two years later. Sam Hewson stands for the Greens, Alan Kelly is the Lib Dem nominee, and the Reform UK candidate is retired teacher Ellen Dew.
