One by-election on 30th April 2026:

Tenbury

Malvern Hills council, Worcestershire; caused by the resignation of Conservative councillor John Willmott. There is one week to go to the 2026 English local elections - but not all of England has local elections this year. Many rural parts of England miss out on the fun, such as the Malvern Hills district which snakes along the western edge of Worcestershire. It's here that we find the last by-election of the 2025-26 local government year - and, in three weeks' time, we will be elsewhere in this district to cover the first by-election of the 2026-27 local government year. The small town of Tenbury Wells lies in an area of the beautiful Welsh Marches which is administratively not well-served. It is in the north-western corner of Worcestershire, in a salient between Shropshire to the north and Herefordshire to the south. This is probably a reflection of the topography here: Tenbury is in the valley of the River Teme, which rises in mid-Wales near Newtown and flows east through Knighton and Ludlow to eventually merge with the Severn just south of Worcester. However, Worcester is not the most convenient town for local residents: the nearest larger towns and railheads are Ludlow in Shropshire and Leominster in Herefordshire, while the main road past Tenbury runs east-west between Leominster and Kidderminster. The Heath local government reform took the view that the Herefordshire/Worcestershire county boundary was perhaps not the sanest, and it also took the view that Herefordshire was too small to be a viable county on its own. The 1974 big bang placed Tenbury Wells within the county of Hereford and Worcester and within the district of Leominster. But this proved to be unpopular, and Hereford and Worcester were demerged in the 1990s. As a knock-on effect of this, Tenbury was transferred to the Malvern Hills district in exchange for the Ledbury area, which returned to Herefordshire. The modern town of Tenbury Wells expanded in the Victorian era thanks to the discovery of mineral springs here in 1839. The 1862 Pump Rooms, which are now home to Tenbury town council and host the main polling station for today's by-election, are a striking building in what is sometimes described as a "Chinese Gothic" style. The Pump Rooms' architect James Cranston is also responsible for the Round Market, which dates from 1858 and still serves as the town's market building. Tenbury Wells might look like something out of a picture postcard, but its future is somewhat uncertain. The town centre is built on low-lying ground next to the River Teme and the Kyre Brook, and it has suffered from serious flooding on an almost annual basis in recent years. After paying out four times in the five years from 2019 to 2024 for flood damage to businesses and properties, the insurers have had enough and Tenbury Wells is now uninsurable against flood risk. The next flood, whenever it arrives, will likely bankrupt the town. Despite this, Tenbury has failed to qualify for a recent round of governemnt funding for flood defences. On my research visit, I parked outside a shop on the main street called "Nice Things", which - this being a Sunday evening - was shut. Apparently we can't have nice things. Malvern Hills council has been under independent leadership since the Conservatives lost control here in 2019. Tenbury ward re-elected its two Conservative councillors without a contest in that year. In 2021 Conservative councillor Tony Penn resigned on age grounds - he was then 87, and he was one of a fast-dwindlng number of people who had first-hand experience of the Coventry Blitz in November 1940 - and the resulting by-election was won by independent candidate Lesley Bruton, who was then the town council clerk. Bruton was re-elected at the top of the poll in the 2023 election for a Tenbury ward which had been expanded in boundary changes; on that occasion she had the nomination of the Malvern Hills Independents party. The Conservatives held the other seat, with new face (John) Andrew Willmott easily defeating his running-mate from higher up the alphabetical ballot paper. Topline vote shares were 42% for the Malvern Hills Independents, 37% for the Conservative slate, and 20% for the Greens who were the only other party to stand. Overall in the 2023 Malvern Hills elections independent candidates won 11 seats, the Conservatives and the Green Party seven each, and the Liberal Democrats and the Malvern Hills Independents three each. This area most recently went to the polls in the 2025 Worcestershire county council election, when the Tenbury division - which is larger than this ward - narrowly became the only Malvern Hills division to vote for Reform UK. In the 2024 general election the Conservatives' Dame Harriett Baldwin was comfortably re-elected for her fifth term as MP for the local seat of West Worcestershire; Baldwin had been chair of the Treasury select committee going into that election, and she is now a shadow business minister. She is not related to the former Conservative prime minister Stanley Baldwin, who represented Tenbury Wells - which was then part of the Bewdley constituency - for most of the first half of the 20th century. In due course Dame Harriett Baldwin may well be taking an interest in the Malvern Hills Bill, which is currently at committee stage in the House of Lords. This private bill is being promoted by the Malvern Hills Conservators, a body which owns much of the Malvern Hills and has a legal duty to keep them unenclosed and unbuilt on as open spaces for the recreation and enjoyment of the public. According to its long title, the purpose of the Malvern Hills Bill is "to repeal and re-enact certain enactments relating to the Malvern Hills Conservators and the management of the Malvern Hills, to reconstitute and rename those Conservators as the Malvern Hills Trust and to confer further powers on the Malvern Hills Trust, to make further provision in relation to the Malvern Hills, and for other purposes". Tenbury ward's Conservative councillor Andrew Willmott had been nominated by Malvern Hills council as a trustee of the Malvern Hills Conservators. He resigned from the council, and thus from the trusteeship, on 12th March. No reason was publicly given for Willmott's resignation, but it may be significant that the Lords committee on the Malvern Hills Bill had heard evidence that day from Jeremy Owenson, the deputy leader of Malvern Hills council and leader of the Conservative group, who was making objections to the Bill. Defending the resulting Tenbury by-election for the Conservatives is Carl Fordington, who was their defeated candidate for the county council last year. There is no Malvern Hills Independent candidate this time so a lot of votes are up for grabs. The Green Party have nominated Sam Lewis, who is in his mid-20s and is standing for election for the first time. Also on the ballot are first-time candidate Duane Hubbard (or "Lard" to his friends) for Reform UK and the Lib Dems' Jed Marson, who will be hoping for the more than the 32 votes he scored at the last Tenbury by-election in 2021.
