Two by-elections on 16th April 2026:

Narborough and Whetstone

Leicestershire county council; caused by the resignation of Reform UK councillor Andrew Thorp. Both of today's local by-elections are to replace Reform UK councillors who were elected in last year's local elections, and who have now stepped down shortly before the end of their first year in office. We'll start in Leicestershire by travelling south-west of the city, to a pair of large villages which lie slightly beyond the city limits. The village of Narborough lies on the Fosse Way, which was once one of the major thoroughfares of Roman Britain but has now been superseded by the modern M1 and M69 motorways. The Fosse Way through Narborough is now partly a B-road and partly a footpath. To the south of the Roman line is the old village centre next to the River Soar, and the village's railway station on the line from Leicester to Birmingham; to the north-west is the Pastures housing estate and the large Carlton Park office complex. This was once the headquarters of the Alliance and Leicester building society, until it ran into difficulties during the Great Financial Crisis and was taken over by Santander; that bank still maintains offices and a data centre here. In 2021 the Narborough and Littlethorpe ward of Blaby council, which was smaller then than the present ward of that name, ranked third of the wards in the East Midlands for residents employed in the financial and insurance sector (5.8% of the workforce). Over the M1 motorway to the east we find the northern part of Whetstone, which grew strongly in population after 1941 when Frank Whittle's jet engine factory was established here. This factory ended up in the hands of the engineering giants English Electric and later GEC, and branched out from aero engines into manufacturing turbines for nuclear power stations, and also into computing with the development of the English Electric KDF9 computer in 1964. This was in the days when computers took up entire rooms: the KDF9 weighed over 5 tonnnes and only 29 of them were ever built, mostly for universities and technical colleges. But one offshoot of this work which persists today was the development of the Whetstone benchmark, originally based on the performance of English Electric's Whetstone ALGOL 60 compiler, which is still used to masure the perforamnce of computers. One of the originators of the Whetstone benchmark, computer scientist Brian Wichmann, was for many years afterwards involved in the Electoral Reform Society. Electoral reform has yet to come to this corner of Leicestershire, and Blaby council - named after Whetstone's eastern twin village - is still elected by the first-past-the-post system. The 2023 Blaby elections returned a narrow Conservative majority, which has since disappeared after one of their councillors became independent; this included a Conservative clean sweep in the Narborough and Littlethorpe, and North Whetstone wards which closely correspond to this division. Despite the name Blaby council is based in Narborough, and its council chamber is a polling station for this by-election. Also in the Conservative column is the local Parliamentary seat, which used to be called Blaby until the Boundary Commission renamed it in 2010 on the grounds that nobody knew where Blaby is. The South Leicestershire constituency has been represented since 2015 by Conservative MP Alberto Costa, who holds the powerful Commons position of chair of the standards committee. Costa has come to public attention for campaigning against parole for Colin Pitchfork, a former resident of Littlethorpe in this ward who murdered two children in Narborough and nearby Enderby in the 1980s and was the first person to be convicted of murder on the strength of DNA evidence. Pitchfork's 28-year minimum term has now expired and he was released in 2021, but he was then recalled to prison within three months for breaching his licence conditions; a subsequent decision to grant parole to Pitchfork in 2023 was then overturned following opposition from Costa and an intervention from the Lord Chancellor. Narborough and Whetstone division has a Conservative MP and a full set of Conservative district councillors, but in last year's Leicestershire county elections it was gained by Reform UK. Andrew Thorp because the local county councillor with 42% of the vote, to 24% for the defendng Conservatives and 15% for the Green Party. Reform UK are the largest party on Leicestershire county council, but they are short of a majority and run the county as a minority administration: the 2025 election returned 25 RUK councillors, 15 Conservatives, 11 Lib Dems, 2 Labour, a Green and an independent. One of the Reform UK councillors has since walked off to join Restore Britain, a breakaway party associated with the Great Yarmouth MP Rupert Lowe. Andrew Thorp stepped down from the county council in February, nine months after his election. Initially no reason for this was given, leading the opposition Conservative group to speculate that Thorp's poor attendance record had been a factor in his deperture. Thorp has since released a statement denying that, instead citing health issues for his resignation. Defending the resulting by-election for Reform UK is Dee North, who is a health and safety manager. North is standing for election for the first time, but in 2021 she was co-opted to Whetstone parish council - and then quickly fell off that council for failing to attend meetings. The Conservatives candidate is Les Phllimore, a Whetstone parish councillor who represents Cosby and South Whetstone ward on Blaby council; Phillimore is seeking to get back onto the county council after losing his seat in the neighbouring Cosby and Countesthorpe division last year. The Green Party have reselected Mike Jelfs, who stood here in the 2025 and 2021 county elections and finished in third place on both occasions: Jelfs was also the Green parliamentary candidate for South Leicestershire in 2024. Also returning from the 2025 election are Labour's Lisa Pendery-Hunt (who is the chair of Whetstone parish council) and the Lib Dems' Ande Savage (who was elected to Blaby council in a December 2023 in Blaby and Glen Parva ward), while a sixth party has intervened in this election: Martin Garfoot is nominated by Advance UK, the Reform UK splinter group associated with its former deputy leader Ben Habib. Cramlington South West

Northumberland council; caused by the resignation of Reform UK councillor Shaun Knowles. Our second by-election of the week comes in a corner of the North East which has seen massive ongoing housing development over the last few years. Mind, massive housing development is nothing new in Cramlington, which is a quasi-New Town from the 1960s and 1970s located about ten miles to the north of Newcastle upon Tyne. I say quasi-New Town beacuse Cramlington was never officially designated as a New Town and never had the development corporation provided for by the New Town Acts: instead, all the housing and industrial sites were built by private firms in co-operation with Northumberland county council. The Beaconhill estate, 1970s housing to the west of the town centre, dates from this era. Just to the north of the estate is Cramlington railway station, which is served by local trains on the East Coast main line between Newcastle and Morpeth. In more recent years the housebuilders' sights have been set on the open land to the west and south of the Beaconhill eatate, an area with good road links to the A1/A19 junction at Seaton Burn. And the houses are piling up in this area thick and fast. Construction work started in 2023 on two new estates called West Meadows and Foxton Mill, which are now partially occupied but very much not finished yet. Until 2025 all of this new development and several other new estates were within the Cramlington West ward of Northumberland, which had become severely oversized as a result. On the 2022 electoral register Cramlington West had 5,532 electors, which was already 47% over the Northumberland average, and this was forecast to increase to 7,352 electors (85% over the average) by 2028. This was enough on its own to trigger a boundary review for Northumberland council, and for the 2025 local election the Local Government Boundary Commission essentially divided Cramlington West into two new wards called Cramlington North West and South West. South West ward was deliberately drawn with a very low electorate to allow room for future population growth: on the 2022 register the new ward had just 1,834 electors, which was 50% of the average size for a Northumberland ward, but by May 2025 this had already increased to 2,629 and the notice of poll for this by-election, eleven months later, shows a further increase to 2,941 electors. The average Northumberland ward has an enrolment of around 3,700 to 3,800, so there's still some way to go. The newness of Cramlington South West's electorate makes it difficult to tell much from the 2021 census or from previous results, but for what it's worth the old Cramlington West ward had voted Conservative at the three Northumberland elections from 2013 to 2021. Cramlington joined the Parliamentary map in 2024 with the creation of the Cramlington and Killingworth constituency, which easily returned Labour candidate Emma Foody for her first term of office with Reform UK in a rather distant second place. Previously the town had been part of the Blyth Valley constituency, which had voted Conservative in 2019 after a long hiotory of Labour representation. Northumberland is the only North East council run by the Conservatives, who have had had (normally) minority control since 2017. In 2025 this was one of the few areas of England where the Conservatives generally held their own against the Reform UK challenge: the election here returned 26 Conservative councillors against 23 for Reform UK, 8 Labour, 7 independents, 3 Lib Dems and 2 Greens, and the Tories retained the council leadership with independent, Lib Dem and Green support. The Reform UK caucus is now down to 19: one of their councillors has crossed the floor to the Conservatives, two others have gone independent, and Cramlington South West ward councillor Shaun Knowles tendered his resignation at the end of February. Knowles, who like Andrew Thorp in Leicestershire had been criticised for poor attendance at council meetings and for failing to engage with his electors, stepped down from the council on health grounds: he is undergoing treatment for kidney cancer. Knowles was the only Reform UK councillor in Cramlington, as the town's six other seats split five to the Conservatives and one to an indepdnent last year. His winning score last year of 274 votes was the lowest vote total for any Northumverland councillor, which is largely a reflection of Cramlington South West ward's unusually low electorate. In percentage terms Reform UK won the ward in 2025 with 39% of the vote , against 29% for Labour and 25% for the Conservatives. Defending this seat for Reform UK is Bridget Byrne, a pub landlady who fought the neighbouring Cramlington North West ward last year. The Labour candidate is Lynne Robinson who was an also an unsuccessful Northumberland council candidate in 2025: on that occasion she stood in Cramlington Village ward. The Conservatives have reselected Neil Graham, who already represents this ward on Cramlington town council: he was the Conservative candidate for this ward twelve months ago and will be hoping to improve on his third-place finish from then. Also on the ballot are Nick Cott for the Lib Dems, independent candidate Dawn Furness who used to be a regular Green Party parliamentary candidate before she fell out with the Greens over transgneder issues, and official Green candidate Alex Phimister.
