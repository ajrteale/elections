"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

Five by-elections on 5th March 2026:

Thrupp

Stroud council, Gloucestershire; caused by the resignation of Green Party councillor Rebecca Adam.

It's the first Thursday of a new month and a new season. The main electoral event this spring will of course be the devolved and local elections on 7th May, but Andrew's Previews does have some by-elections to bring you before then. There are fifteen local polls in the diary for March 2026, and five them are taking place today.

The dust is, of course, still settling from last week's Parliamentary Special in which the Green Party gained the seat of Gorton and Denton from Labour on a large swing. It was a landmark result in many ways, not least as the Greens had never come anywhere near winning a Parliamentary by-election in the past. Their previous best result was in Somerton and Frome 2023, when the party just managed to break 10% of the vote.

Despite this, the Greens have been slowly chipping away at local government level for some decades now. The first place where they amounted to a serious electoral force was the Gloucestershire town of Stroud, where John Marjoram became the first Green Party district councillor in 1986. Marjoram, who died last year, lived long enough to see his party take control of Stroud council: the 2024 elections returned 22 Green councillors against 20 Labour, 7 Conservatives and 2 Lib Dems, and the Green Party run the district as a minority. They have since gone on to gain two by-elections from Labour last year.

One of those by-election gains followed the resignation of the town's former Labour MP David Drew, after he had fallen out with his party. The Stroud constituency has trended towards the left over recent decades, voting for the government at every election since 1979 with the exception of 2017 when Drew - who had previously held the seat from 1997 to 2010 - won his final term of office. The Labour nomination for Stroud in 2024 instead went to Simon Opher, a GP, who defeated the previous Conservative MP Siobhan Baillie very easily.

The Thrupp electoral ward is based on the parish of Brimscombe and Thrupp, which lies south-east of Stroud town in the steep-sided Frome valley. The valley is transited by the Golden Valley railway line from Swindon to Gloucester, and by the disused Thames and Severn Canal which once connected Stroud to the River Thames at Lechlade. The canal was wide enough for Thames barges but could not accommodate Severn trows; the latter boats could get up the River Frome as far as Brimscombe, which became an important port for the transfer of cargos. Brimscombe was also a boatbuilding centre: in the early 20th century the firm of Abdela and Mitchell built a number of paddle steamers here, which ended up plying their trade on rivers as far away as the Nile and the Amazon. Other villages in this ward, including Thrupp itself, were dominated by textile mills in a way more often seen in the Pennines.

Thrupp ward has been solidly in the Green column throughout this century, and in 2024 Green councillor Rebecca Aldam was re-elected for her second term of office by the wide margin of 64-18 over Labour. The Greens polled a similar score in last year's Gloucestershire county elections across the Minchinhampton division, which includes this ward and has been in Green hands since 2017. Minchinhampton's county councillor Chloe Turner is also the leader of Stroud council.

Rebecca Aldam stood down from Stroud council in January due to changes in her professional and family circumstances. Defending this seat for the Greens is Helen Elliott-Boult, who is the vice-chair of Rodborough parish council which covers western parts of this ward; in 2024 she was an independent candidate for Rodborough ward. The Labour candidate Mick Fealty, who is a Stroud town councillor, may be better known to political obsessives as the founding editor of the long-running Northern Irish blog and political discussion website Slugger O'Toole, which he still runs. Also on the ballot are Anthony Pilkington for the Conservatives, Lucian Aeris for UKIP, Raffy Chiswick for the Lib Dems and Michael Rees - the county councillor for Cam Valley division - for Reform UK.

Hextable

Sevenoaks council, Kent; caused by the death of independent councillor Darren Kitchener.

Two of this week's polls are to replace independent councillors, one in Kent and the other in Essex. The Kent poll takes place in one of the few genuine villages inside the M25, located just to the north of the town of Swanley.

Arthur Mee was a journalist of the early 20th century who wrote a number of works for children including the eight-volume Children's Encyclopaedia of 1908-10 and the weekly Children's Newspaper, which ran from 1919 to 1963. Mee also wrote and edited The King's England, a topographical history of England which began publication in 1936 and eventually ran to 43 volumes. But Hextable, where Mee lived for some years, defeated his historical research: he is reputed to have once said that "Hextable has no history!"

As if in an attempt to disprove that statement, the Hextable Heritage Society have helpfully put together a six-page pamphlet describing the history of the village. It has to be said that this is not a settlement of particularly long standing, and there was very little in Hextable before a number of villas were built in the 19th century for well-to-do Victorian gentlemen who wanted to live in the countryside within striking distance of London.

Which brings us to Sir Edward Reed, who owned Hextable House until 1889. Reed had a long political career, serving as a Liberal MP for first the Pembroke Boroughs and then the Cardiff Boroughs from 1874 to 1895 and again from 1900 to 1906; but he had made his name as a naval architect and as Chief Constructor of the Royal Navy from 1863 to 1870. Reed had some success with his warship designs in that period, but probably his best-known ship was a notorious failure.

The SS Bessemer had been launched in 1874 to Reed's design as the brainchild of the steelmaking inventor Sir Henry Bessemer, who suffered horribly from seasickness. Bessemer's idea was to develop a paddle steamer whose passenger saloon would be kept level, whatever the state of the sea, by suspending it on gimbals inside the ship's hull. In practice this swinging saloon made the Bessemer extremely difficult to handle, and the ship's only public voyage in 1875 ended with her demolishing part of the pier at Calais harbour. The Bessemer was scrapped in 1879, but the rather luxurious passenger cabin was saved: Reed had it transported to Hextable House, where he used it as a billiard room. In 1889 Reed sold Hextable House to a businessman, who turned it and the surrounding land into Swanley Horticultural College; the billiard room then became a lecture theatre. Unfortunately, the cabin of the Bessemer no longer exists today: it received a direct hit from a German bomb in 1944.

Following the Second World War Hextable became overshadowed by the growth of nearby Swanley, which is the most downmarket part of Sevenoaks district thanks to the presence of large amounts of London overspill housing. Perhaps because of this, the village declared independence from Swanley in 2008 to become a separate parish of its own.

One of the prime movers behind the creation of Hextable parish was the late Darren Kitchener, who had been part of Hextable parish council since its formation and had been chairman of that council for many years. By day and night Kitchener worked on the railways, latterly in a health and safety role; when off duty Kitchener was clearly passionate about Hextable and its history, chairing the Hextable Heritage Society.

Unlike Swanley, which is the only part of Sevenoaks district capable of returning Labour councillors, Hextable ward has been fought over between Conservatives and independents in this century. The current ward boundaries dated from 2003, when Hextable was won by an "Independent Team" slate who were not opposed by the Conservatives and appear to have subsequently joined them. Darren Kitchener was first elected here in 2015 as an independent candidate, gaining his seat from the Conservatives.

In 2019 Kitchener stood for re-election at the head of a "Hextable Independents" slate which won both seats in Hextable ward easily. By 2023 the Hextable Independents' party registration had been allowed to lapse, and Kitchener and his running-mate Christine Hudson were easily re-elected as independent candidates. Kitchener polled 80% of the vote, with only a third independent and the Conservatives providing token opposition.

This result puts Hextable ward in the minority on Sevenoaks council, which safely retained its Conservative majority even amid the carnage of 2023; the Liberal Democrats, whose vote is concentrated in Sevenoaks town, are the main opposition. On the other hand Hextable is part of the Reform UK majority on Kent county council, where it is part of the Swanley county division.

Darren Kitchener died in mid-November at the early age of 54, from complications while he was preparing for surgery for a heart condition. The resulting by-election has taken quite a long time to organise, but we are here now.

One new independent candidate has come forward: Jackie Griffiths is a trained nurse and midwife and a former Sevenoaks councillor, who represented Swanley St Mary's ward on the Labour ticket from 2019 until she lost her seat in 2023. Griffiths was also the Labour candidate for Swanley in last year's county elections, but she is standing in this by-election on a generic localist independent ticket. The Conservatives' Lee Allen, who is standing for election for the first time, is a former racing driver who now runs a bus and truck repair company. Reform UK's Daniel Kersten, who will be hoping for a better return than the 83 votes he polled in Sevenoaks Northern ward in 2023, completes the ballot paper along with the Lib Dems' Ashley Wassall and the Greens' Oliver Young.

Coggeshall

Braintree council, Essex; caused by the resignation of independent councillor Thomas Walsh.

Our second independent defence of the week lies north of the Thames, in rural Essex. The village of Coggeshall lies some distance to the north-east of Chelmsford, on the Roman Road of Stane Street which runs west from Colchester: it was at this point that Stane Street crossed another path which forded the River Blackwater here.

Coggeshall's traditional industry was textiles, but in mediaeval times it was also a minor religious centre. During the years of the Anarchy, King Stephen and his queen Matilda (not to be confused with the Empress Matilda) founded an abbey here in 1140. This was the last English abbey established by the Savigniac monastic order, before it merged with the Cistercians a few years later. The Abbey's gatehouse chapel still stands and it is the oldest surviving brick building in England to be built after the Romans left. Meanwhile Coggeshall's woollen trade provided the money for an extremely oversized church in the town, dedicated to St Peter ad Vincula; the present-day church dates from the 15th century.

The current Coggeshall ward also covers the parishes of Bradwell and Stisted, which cover the countryside between Coggeshall and Braintree. Boundary changes for the 2024 general election united the ward within the Witham parliamentary seat, which has been represented since its creation in 2010 by the Conservatives' Dame Priti Patel. Patel, who is the only co-author of Britannia Unchained still in the Commons, served as Home Secretary throughout the Johnson administration; despite that experience of a Great Office of State, she then finished in sixth and last place in the 2024 Conservative leadership election. The eventual winner, fellow Essex MP Kemi Badenoch, subsequently brought Patel back to the frontbench as shadow foreign secretary.

At local government level Coggeshall is part of the Braintree district, which has a slim Conservative majority that is not at stake in by-election. The 2023 Braintree election returned 26 Conservative councillors against 9 Labour, 7 independents, 4 Greens and 3 councillors for the Halstead Residents Association.

The Greens and independent councillors in Braintree have an electoral pact and their councillors have formed a joint group on the council. Coggeshall ward is on the independent side of the pact, as it was gained from the Conservatives by two independent councillors in 2019 following a local controversy over a proposed waste incinerator. One of the independent councillors moved out of the town and tendered his resignation in 2022, and the resulting by-election returned another independent candidate. Coggeshall's two independent councillors, Thomas Walsh and Dennis Abram, were then re-elected in 2023 with a 54-30 lead over the Conservatives.

At county level we see the other side of the Green-Independent pact. Coggeshall town is part of the Braintree Eastern county division, which was safe Green in the 2021 Essex county elections. In 2021 one of the ward's three parishes, Bradwell, was included in the Witham Northern division which the Conservatives gained from the Green Party by 39 votes; but boundary changes for the next Essex county council election in two months' time will unify Coggeshall ward within Braintree Eastern division.

Independent councillor Tom Walsh, who topped the poll here in 2023, stepped down in January for personal and family reasons. Defending the seat for the Independent and Green group is independent candidate Colin Riches, who has been a regular candidate in Braintree town in recent Essex and Braintree elections: Riches had the Labour nomination for Braintree Town division in the 2017 county elections, but his campaigns since then have been as an independent. Standing for the Conservatives is India Jayatillake, who is a former parish councillor in Kelvedon and contested Kelvedon and Feering ward in the 2023 Braintree elections. Also on the ballot are Sarah Neal for Labour, Barry Fleet for the Lib Dems and Nathan Robins for Reform UK.

Spital

Tamworth council, Staffordshire; caused by the death of Labour councillor Marion Couchman.

This week's Labour defence comes in another ward which was covered in this column back in 2022. We return to the Spital ward of Tamworth, which covers the Perry Crofts area to the north of the town centre and to the north-west of the railway station. The ward also covers a rural fringe to the north-east of the town, where we find a brand-new attraction which wasn't here four years ago: this is the National Fairground Museum, which relocated here from Devon in 2025 and has all the fairground rides you might remember from when you were younger. This museum is linked with the Statfold Barn Railway, a series of narrow-gauge railways which straddle the ward, district and county boundary.

Demographically this area is much of a muchness, like most of Tamworth. Indeed, Tamworth is quite unusual in that its ten wards are all demographically and politically similar to each other, so when one party wins the town it tends to win big.

The present Tamworth council is made up of councillors elected in 2022, 2023 and 2024. The latter two of these were Labour wave elections, with Labour defeating the Conservatives 8-2 in 2023 and 9-1 in May 2024; that's enough on its own for a majority of the 30 council seats. In between those two elections we had the 2023 Tamworth parliamentary by-election, a famous win for the Labour candidate Sarah Edwards who gained the seat from the Conservatives on a 24-point swing. Edwards was re-elected in July 2024 on top of a rather narrow three-way split with 35% of the vote: second place on 32% went to Conservative MP Eddie Hughes who attempted to do the chicken run from Walsall North, while Reform UK's Ian Cooper was third on 25%.

Reform UK built on that performance to sweep all five of Tamworth's county divisions at the 2025 Staffordshire county elections, with Cooper himself being elected in Perrycrofts division which covers Spital ward. Again Cooper's winning score was only 35%; the outgoing Conservative councillor polled 24%, an independent had 23% and Labour scored 14%; the Reform UK majorities in the other four Tamworth divisions were much higher. Boundary changes mean that these results are not comparable to previous years.

There is no such difficulty in the case of Spital ward, which has had unchanged boundaries since 2002. All of its contests up to May 2022 were won by the Conservatives, including a by-election in February 2022, but Labour narrowly gained the ward in the 2023 and 2024 elections. The May 2024 result saw Labour's Marion Couchman win with 40% of the vote against 38% for the Conservatives and 22% for an independent candidate; her numerical majority was 33 votes.

Tamworth was one of the 30 councils which applied for postponement of their May 2026 local elections, were granted that postponment, and then found their elections back on following a government U-turn. Reform UK had brought a legal challenge to the postponements, and it appears that the government threw in the towel after being advised that they couldn't defend the case.

The postponement list certainly had some bizarre decisions. For example, several Essex districts were granted postponements despite the fact that Essex county council wanted their elections to go ahead, while here in Staffordshire Cannock Chase and Tamworth were granted postponements but Newcastle-under-Lyme wanted to go ahead with their election. But this is an area of local governemnt law which you play around with at your peril, as readers with longer memories might recall from 2010. The May 2010 elections to Exeter and Norwich councils had been cancelled by ministerial order as part of a plan to turn those councils into unitary authorities; but this was challenged in the High Court by Devon and Norfolk county councils. The legal challenge succeeded on the grounds that the statutory consultation required had been inadequate, and the High Court quashed the orders (Devon County Council v Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government [2010] EWHC 1456 (Admin)). By this point we were in June 2010, and the new Coalition government had abandoned the unitary plans for Exeter and Norwich. In a subsequent judgment Mr Justice Ouseley ruled that because the postponement order had been quashed, the Exeter and Norwich councillors whose terms had been extended from May 2010 were now out of office; the effect was that one-third of each council suddenly became vacant. Extraordinary elections had to be held in September 2010 to bring the councils back up to their full strength. The consequences of something going wrong like that for 30 councils at once don't bear thinking about.

So we are going to have an election for Tamworth council election in May. As noted above the 2024 election result here was 9 Labour and 1 Conservative, 2023 was 8 Labour and 2 Conservatives, while 2022 was 7 Conservatives, 2 Labour and 1 independent which should add up to 19 Labour, 10 Conservatives and 1 independent. Given that 16 seats are required for a majority and Labour are only defending two seats in May, continued Labour control of Tamworth in 2026-27 should be guaranteed.

But of course, things in local government are never that simple. The independent councillor elected in 2022 has recently handed in his notice and his seat will remain vacant going into May's election. Meanwhile the Conservative group has suffered a massive split, with six of the Conservative councillors who are due for re-election in May walking off to become independents (including their remaining Spital ward councillor). Labour have also lost councillors to defection, with two Labour councillors becoming independent (including their remaining Spital ward councillor) and one crossing the floor to join the Greens.

And then we have the Spital ward by-election to consider. This is to replace Labour councillor Marion Couchman following her death in January. Couchman was first elected to Tamworth council all the way back in 1997 and she was mayor of the borough in 2003-04, but Labour's weakness in Tamworth for much of this century meant that she had long breaks in service. Couchman lost her seat in the town-centre Castle ward in 2006, and subsequently served one term for Belgrave ward in 2012-16 before returning to the council for the final time in Spital ward in 2024. Her death leaves 15 Labour councillors in Tamworth, so if Labour lose this by-election and then draw a blank in May the council will fall into No Overall Control.

Unexpectedly high stakes, then, for the defending Labour candidate Steve Holland who is fighting his first election campaign. The Conservative candidate Brett Beetham, who works full-time for the NHS, has local government experience as a former Warwickshire county councillor (until 2025) and Nuneaton and Bedworth councillor (until 2024); he is making his third attempt to get elected in Tamworth. This time there is no independent candidate so a lot of votes are up for grabs. The Spital ballot paper is completed by Stephen Andrews for the Green Party and Reform UK's Wayne Luca, who is the county councillor for Watling division in the south of the town.

Murton

Durham council; caused by the resignation of Reform UK councillor David Cumming.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lRN1QHBC12E

In the opening scene of the film Alien 3, Sigourney Weaver crashlands onto the industrial coastline of the planet Fiorina 161, a prison planet described as a "mineral ore refinery". Which isn't too far from the truth. The beach seen in the film here is Blast Beach on the Durham coast, which still bears the scars from over a century of coalmining and metal refining: the blast furnaces on the clifftop simply dumped their slag onto the beach, and even after a major cleanup operation involving the National Trust most of the stones on the beach are red due to their high iron content. Pools of standing water on Blast Beach quickly develop a red tinge, as iron leaches out of the stones into the water.

Blast Beach lies in the parish of Hawthorn, one of three parishes to the south of Seaham which make up the modern Murton ward. Murton village was the creation of the South Hetton Coal Company, which sank a pit here in 1838 that was still going into the 1990s and which employed 1,000 people at its peak. The old spoil heaps of Murton Colliery are now covered by Dalton Park, which lies next to the main A19 road and is described as the North East's largest outlet shopping centre. The ward's other parish is South Hetton, another pit village which lies on the road from Easington to Houghton-le-Spring.

South Hetton is possibly still best known as the location of the so-called "one-armed bandit murder" in 1967 of Angus Sibbet, who worked for a firm which supplied fruit machines to working men's clubs. Sibbet's murder was one of the inspirations for the 1971 film Get Carter. A century earlier Murton and South Hetton were the stomping ground of serial killer Mary Ann Cotton, who went to the gallows in 1873 for poisoning her son Charles with arsenic. That was the only murder which Cotton was convicted of, but she is thought to be responsible for around 21 deaths: her mother, all four of her husbands, a lover and 11 of her 13 children had all died suddenly from stomach fevers consistent with arsenic poisoning. Cotton's father had died in 1842 by falling down a mine shaft at Murton Colliery; his body was returned to the family in a sack stamped with the words "Property of the South Hetton Coal Company".

The polling station in South Hetton is located in the Robin Todd Centre, which is named after a long-serving former Labour councillor for that village who died in 2015. Labour held the resulting August 2015 by-election in the former Shotton and South Hetton ward, but then that ward started trending away from them and in 2021 Shotton and South Hetton returned two independent councillors. By contrast Murton ward, which until 2025 consisted solely of Murton parish, remained safely in the Labour column up to 2021. Both of Murton ward's Labour councillors stood for re-election in the expanded ward in 2025, but they were swept away in the Reform UK landslide of that year: in the only previous contest on these boundaries the Reform UK slate won with 44% of the vote, against 33% for Labour and 14% for an independent slate. This and other results in the area will have been noted with some concern by Labour backbencher Grahame Morris, who has represented the Easington parliamentary seat since 2010.

Reform UK won 65 seats on Durham council out of a possible 98 in 2025. They had to defend two by-elections last year after one councillor was found to be disqualified and a second resigned: one of those by-elections was lost to the Liberal Democrats, while Reform UK successfully defended the other seat in Easington and Shotton ward which lies immediately to the south of Murton ward. The party will now have to repeat that trick here after Reform UK councillor David Cumming tendered his resignation just before Christmas: he is now working overseas.

Defending this seat for Reform UK is Theo Bell, who is described as a local lad. Labour have reselected Julie Griffiths, who was a councillor for the former Murton ward from 2021 until she lost her seat last year. This time there is no independent on the ballot, so the candidate list is completed by Dorothy Luckhurst for the Conservatives, Neil Thompson for the Lib Dems and Isaac Short for the Green Party.
