"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

There are eighteen elections taking place on 18th June 2026, of which four (for a total of seven seats) are polls which were postponed from 7th May after a candidate died during the election campaign; eleven are ordinary by-elections, some of which deal with matters arising from May's local elections; and three are Parliamentary Specials, one of which might have an impact far larger than the usual filling of a casual vacancy. But we're not going to start with that one. In fulfilment of Andrew's Previews' promise to cover all the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order, let's go north of the Border:

Aberdeen South

House of Commons; caused by the resignation of Scottish National Party MP Stephen Flynn.

In 1969, British Petroleum were drilling for gas in the North Sea, about 135 miles east of Aberdeen. Gas was already being exploited in British and Dutch waters of the North Sea to the south, and BP wanted to test a theory that the gas field extended further north. Their geologists were astonished when the well struck oil.

This original discovery is now the Montrose oil field, and it is still going strong. Over the next few years many more oilfields were discovered under the harsh waters of the North Sea - Forties, Brent, Piper and so on - and the oil shocks of 1973 and 1979 made it commercially viable to develop them. To this day the Brent oilfield gives its name to the Brent Crude price for North Sea oil, although Brent itself is now exhausted and buying a barrel of Brent Crude will instead give you a blend of crude oil from other British and Norwegian fields.

North Sea oilfields in British waters were developed by private enterprise, and their extraction turned the old city of Aberdeen into an economic boom town, the Oil Capital of Europe. Aberdeen has the largest and busiest seaport in north-east Scotland, and its airport is the main base for helicopters serving the oil rigs.

But Aberdeen goes back a long way. The Port of Aberdeen has been here since at least 1136 when David I gave the city a royal charter, and Aberdeen had a floushing fishing and shipbuilding industry for centuries. Aberdeen harbour is still the embarkation point for the main ferry service to Shetland, with overnight sailings departing every evening.

The nearby city centre is filled with 19th and 20th century buildings in grey granite, and Marischal College is the second-largest granite building in the world after the Escorial Palace in Madrid. The building is now occupied by the city council, but Marischal College was originally home to a university founded in 1593: it merged with King's College in 1860 to form the modern Aberdeen University.

Aberdeen's second, more modern university is Robert Gordon which obtained its university status in 1992: its main campus is a few miles up the river Dee at Garthdee. The eponymous Robert Gordon had graduated from Marischal College before making his fortune as a merchant in the Baltic city of Danzig (modern-day Gdańsk), and on his death in 1731 he left his fortune to found a private school in the city from which the university eventually spun off. Two current MPs were pupils of the original Robert Gordon's College, including the former Labour Party chairman Dame Anneliese Dodds and the present Aberdeen North SNP MP Kirsty Blackman.

The city of Aberdeen was promoted from one MP to two by the Third Reform Act in 1885, and the Aberdeen South constituency has existed ever since then. It has been subject to frequent boundary changes over the years, and only the areas of Ferryhill, Broomhill, Ashley and Seafield have been in the Aberdeen South constituency continuously since 1885. One major factor in boundary-drawing here over the decades has been the River Dee, which was the boundary between Aberdeenshire and Kincardineshire. The Torry district, despite being not far to the south of Aberdeen city centre, was once in a different county.

So, as originally drawn, the Aberdeen South constituency of 1885 to 1950 (with minor boundary changes in 1918) lay entirely to the north of the Dee. It was centred on the city's West End, with the eastern boundary being the arrow-straight George Street. In other words, this was Aberdeen's middle-class seat - and the comparison to Aberdeen North, which has only voted Conservative once in its similarly long history (in 1935) is stark. Some of what are now the built-up western suburbs of the city such as Hazlehead, Airyhall and Garthdee were still open space at this time, and they had not yet been incorporated into Aberdeen: these areas were part of the seat of West Aberdeenshire until 1918 and Central Aberdeenshire thereafter. In 1885 the Kincardineshire parts of the present Aberdeen South - Kincorth, Torry and Cove - were part of the Kincardineshire constituency; in 1918 Torry was transferred to Aberdeen North, while Kincorth and Cove moved into a new seat of Kincardine and Aberdeenshire West.

In this form Aberdeen South was a safe Liberal seat from 1885 to 1918, including in the 1886 general election when the seat was uncontested. For most of this period its MP was James Bryce, a noted scholar of history and law who held the Regius Chair of Civil Law at Oxford University from 1870 to 1893; in 1876 he was one of the first people since Noah to reach the summit of Mount Ararat. He had entered politics in 1880 as a Liberal MP for the Tower Hamlets constituency, transferring here in 1885 as a result of the boundary changes.

In 1892 Bryce joined Gladstone's Cabinet as Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster, which under the rules of the time meant that he had to seek re-election to the House of Commons in a by-election: at the resulting first Aberdeen South by-election of 23rd August 1892 he was returned unopposed. Bryce subsequently served as president of the Board of Trade from 1894 until the Liberal government was defeated in 1895; when the Liberals returned to power under Campbell-Bannerman in 1905, Bryce rejoined the Cabinet as chief secretary for Ireland.

James Bryce resigned from the Commons in 1907 to become British ambassador to the USA, with the British government of the time feeling that a high-profile political figure was needed to deal with the Yanks rather than the usual senior diplomat - a course of action which has been tried again in recent years with a rather less satisfactory outcome. The resulting second Aberdeen South by-election of 20th February 1907 was a much closer affair than previous elections here thanks to the intervention of the future TUC general secretary Fred Bramley as the candidate of the Aberdeen Labour Representation Committee. The Conservatives' Ronald McNeill, a barrister who had previously edited the London evening newspaper St James's Gazette and was also contributing to the Encyclopædia Britannica at the time, eventually lost by 367 votes to the new Liberal candidate George Esslemont. A rematch in January 1910 saw Esslemont increase his majority to 2,316; McNeill's subsequent government career (he was Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster from 1927 to 1929) came from seats elsewhere.

George Esslemont resigned from the Commons in 1917, a few months before his early death at the age of 57. The resulting third Aberdeen South by-election of 3rd April 1917 was not contested by the Conservatives thanks to the wartime electoral truce, but that didn't stop the Liberals bickering over their candidate. They eventually settled on Sir John Fleming, a timber businessman who had been Lord Provost of Aberdeen in 1898-1902 and was the first holder of that title to act as Lord-Lieutenant of Aberdeen when the city was made a county of itself. Sir John was a younger brother of Robert Fleming, who went into merchant banking and became an extremely wealthy man, and he was a great-uncle of the author Ian Fleming. He won the by-election easily against competition from two independent candidates: second place went to James Watson, a chemistry professor at Anderson's College in Glasgow who had previously been associated with the Independent Labour Party, while the women's suffrage campaigner Frederick Pethick-Lawrence (who was very briefly Leader of the Opposition during the Second World War coalition government) performed very badly on a platform of "peace by negotiation".

With peace won instead through the surrender of Germany, the 1918 general election marked a step-change in Aberdeen South's politics. Sir John Fleming stood for re-election, but he didn't get the coalition government's coupon which instead went to the Unionist candidate Frederick Thomson. Thomson was a barrister from the Leith family which had founded the Ben Line shipping company, which is still in operation today as a shipping agency based in Singapore; he had fought in the First World War in Egypt and in Salonika, where he had been severely wounded. He defeated Fleming by over 7,000 votes, with independent James Watson standing again and saving his deposit.

Frederick Thomson went on to successfully navigate the turbulent political years of the 1920s as the Unionist MP for what quickly became a safe seat, particularly after Labour took over second place in Aberdeen South in 1922. In April 1923 he was appointed as Solicitor General for Scotland and got the title of KC to go with it, but from 1924 onwards he was a Conservative whip. In 1929 Thomson was created a baronet.

Sir Frederick Thomson, 1st Baronet, died in 1935 at the age of 59. For the resulting fourth Aberdeen South by-election of 21st May 1935 the Unionists selected his son Sir Douglas Thomson, 2nd Baronet, as their defending candidate, and he romped to victory by almost a 2 to 1 margin over Labour's Joseph Duncan who was secretary of the Scottish Farm Servants' Union. Douglas Thomson had previously rowed for Oxford in the 1926 and 1927 Boat Races, finishing on the losing side on both occasions.

The Unionist majority over Labour here was cut to 1,816 votes by the Attlee landslide of 1945, so when Sir Douglas Thomson resigned the following year the fifth Aberdeen South by-election, scheduled for 26th November 1946, proved to be a close contest. The Labour challenger was Arthur Irvine, a barrister who had made several previous attempts to get into Parliament: he would eventually win a by-election in Liverpool the following year and serve as Solicitor General in the second Wilson government. The Unionist defence was led by Aberdeen South's first female candidate. Priscilla Grant was only 31 but she was already a widow after her first husband, Grenadier Guards officer Sir Arthur Grant, had been killed in action in 1944. Lady Grant had stood in Aberdeen North in the 1945 general election and got nowhere; but Aberdeen South proved to be a much easier proposition, and Grant defeated Irvine by 3,839 votes.

In 1948 Priscilla Grant remarried. Her second husband was Johnnie Buchan, who was the son of the author John Buchan and had inherited the title of Lord Tweedsmuir - and a seat in the House of Lords - from him. Lady Tweedsmuir went on to have a long parliamentary career, which included winning what was dubbed the "Battle of the Housewives" in 1955: in a rare all-female candidate list, that year her Labour opponent was a young Judith Hart. Her tenure in Aberdeen South also took in an expansion of the seat in 1950, when boundary changes brought Torry, Kincorth and some new western suburbs of Aberdeen into the seat while moving most of the city centre into Aberdeen North.

It took one of the titans of Scottish political history to finally lever Priscilla Buchan out of office. Donald Dewar, who ended his career as the inaugural First Minister of Scotland from 1999 until his death the following year, had first contested Aberdeen South in the 1964 general election just after he had finished his law degree at Glasgow University. He cut Lady Tweedsmuir's majority to 3,898 votes on that occasion, and Dewar - then aged 29 - finished the job in the 1966 Wilson landslide to become the first Labour MP for Aberdeen South with a majority of 1,799.

This small majority proved to be a false start for Donald Dewar's parliamentary career: he held Aberdeen South for only one term and didn't get back into the Commons until 1978. In the 1970 general election Aberdeen South reverted to the Conservatives, with Iain Sproat defeating Dewar by 1,089 votes. Sproat would go on to win four terms of office here, often on knife-edge majorities: his re-elections in October 1974 and 1979 (when the Labour candidate was future Greenock MP Norman Godman) were both with under 1,000 votes.

The 1983 redistribution saw major changes to the Aberdeen South constituency. In came the city centre and Pittodrie from Aberdeen North; out went the suburbs of Kincorth and Garthdee, which were transferred into a rural seat called Kincardine and Deeside. The BBC/ITN estimates were that this reduced the Conservative majority in Aberdeen South from 772 votes to 541. Iain Sproat decided to do the chicken run and he sought re-election in Roxburgh and Berwickshire, which proved to be a disastrous decision for him: not only did Sproat lose in his new seat, but Aberdeen South returned the new Conservative candidate. Gerry Malone was elected to Parliament at his fifth attempt with an increased Conservative majority of 3,581 votes thanks to an even vote split between Labour and the SDP. Malone's four previous failed candidates had including losing the 1982 Glasgow Hillhead by-election to Roy Jenkins. (Sproat, meanwhile, did not return to the Commons until 1992 when he was elected for Harwich.)

Gerry Malone was appointed to the Whips office in 1986 towards the end of what proved to be his only term as MP for Aberdeen South. In 1987 he lost his seat to Labour by 1,198 votes, and he subsequently turned up as MP for Winchester from 1992 to 1997. Malone lost his seat in Winchester in May 1997 by just two votes, went to the Election Court which voided the result for uncertainty and ordered a re-run, and he then lost the resulting Winchester by-election in November 1997 by 21,556 votes. The electorate tend not to like sore losers.

Malone's successor as MP for Aberdeen South was Labour's Frank Doran, a solicitor who only served one term in the seat after his election in 1987. In 1992 Aberdeen South was the only seat (excluding by-election regains) which the Conservatives gained from Labour against the national trend, with former teacher Raymond Robertson defeating Doran by 1,517 votes.

The 1997 redistribution reflected the oil boom and population growth in Aberdeen by granting three seats to the city for the first time. The new seat was Aberdeen Central, which took the city centre and the West End from Aberdeen South; it was projected to be a Labour seat, and Frank Doran successfully returned to the Commons there. In compensation Aberdeen South regained Garthdee and also extended up the Dee valley to take in the villages of Milltimber and Peterculter from the Kincardine and Deeside seat. That seat had been gained by the Liberal Democrats' Nicol Stephen in a 1991 by-election before the Tories took it back in 1992, and partly because of this the Rallings and Thrasher estimates had the Lib Dems in second place in the redrawn Aberdeen South. Which is what happened in 1997 - except they were in second place behind Labour. The outgoing Conservative MP Raymond Robertson polled 26% and finished in third place; Nicol Stephen placed second with 28%, and the winner with 35% of the vote was Labour's Anne Begg.

As in many other places across the UK, 1997 was a result which broke the mould in this seat. Dame Anne Begg, as she became in 2011, went on to win four terms of office as MP for Aberdeen South, becoming by far the longest-serving of the seat's three Labour MPs to date. And Begg didn't let her physical disability stop her from pursuing elected politics or her previous teaching career. She suffers from Gaucher's disease, a genetic degenerative condition, and she has been a wheelchair user since 1984. We can see from elections to the Scottish Parliament that there was nothing inevitable about Begg's wins: the Aberdeen South constituency there was represented by the Lib Dems' Nicol Stephen from 1999 to 2011.

The establishment of the Scottish Parliament led to a redistribution of Scotland's Westminster seats in 2005. Before devolution Scotland had been over-represented in the House of Commons, and the 2005 boundary changes brought the size of its constituencies into line with those in England by cutting the number of Scottish MPs from 72 to 59. This led to the abolition of the Aberdeen Central constituency after only eight years, and some of its territory returned to Aberdeen South.

It took the SNP landslide of 2015 to sweep Dame Anne Begg away. She was defeated in 2015 by the Scottish National Party's Callum McCaig, who jumped from fourth place at the previous election to win the seat by over 7,000 votes. McCaig had started his political career early, being elected as an Aberdeen city councillor for Kincorth/Loirston ward in 2007 and then taking over as leader of the city council in 2011. He was 26 at the time.

McCaig resigned his seat on Aberdeen council after the election, and the resulting by-election was held by the SNP's Stephen Flynn - who I described at the time as "McCaig's parliamentary assistant" - with a massive 61% of the first-preference vote. But if Callum McCaig thought he was in for a long political career, he was wrong. The snap general election in June 2017 saw the Conservatives make gains in Scotland, and one of them was Aberdeen South. McCaig lost his seat to the Conservatives' Ross Thomson by 4,752 votes. He is now the SNP's chief executive, trying to pick up the shattered pieces left behind by the embezzlement conviction of the party's long-serving former chief executive Peter Murrell.

Ross Thomson had also started his political career early. He first stood for election at the age of 19 as the Conservative candidate for Coatbridge and Chryston in the 2007 Holyrood elections, and in 2012 he was elected to Aberdeen city council as a councillor for Hazlehead/Ashley/Queens Cross ward. In 2016 Thomson was elected to the Scottish Parliament as a regional MSP for the North East region, and he stood down from Aberdeen council at the May 2017 election. He then resigned his Holyrood seat shortly afterwards upon being elected to Westminster.

During his time in Holyrood Ross Thomson was one of the few MSPs who supported a Leave vote in the 2016 EU membership referendum (which was ten years ago next week). He was a strong supporter of Brexit in a Parliament which was dominated by that single issue, and he supported Boris Johnson in the 2019 leadership election. But if Ross Thomson thought he was in for a long political career, he was wrong. His time at Westminster ended with his constituency party refusing to support him when allegations of sexual misconduct in Parliament's Strangers Bar were made: this effectively forced Thomson into a last-minute retirement when the 2019 general election was called. Ross Thomson subsequently joined Reform UK and there was some speculation that he might be their candidate in this by-election, but the latest news is that he has flounced out of that party after failing to get their nomination for an Aberdeenshire council by-election in Peterhead next month.

The beneficiary of Ross Thomson's fall from grace in December 2019 was the SNP's Stephen Flynn, who as previously stated had been office manager for and had taken over the Aberdeen council seat vacated by Callum McCaig. He defeated the replacement Conservative candidate Douglas Lumsden, the co-leader of Aberdeen council, by 3,990 votes. Flynn then resigned his council seat in Kincorth/Nigg/Cove ward and a by-election to replace him was scheduled for May 2020, but this had to be called off as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic: his seat eventually remained vacant until November 2020, when the SNP easily held the rescheduled by-election.

Stephen Flynn took over in 2022 as leader of the SNP's Westminster group, giving him the speaking rights afforded to the leader of the third-largest party in the Commons. In July 2024 Flynn lost those rights to the Lib Dem leader Sir Ed Davey but was still easily re-elected in his own seat, with a redrawn Aberdeen South constituency - now again including the city centre and Pittodrie - giving him 33% of the vote to 25% for Labour and 24% for the Conservatives. This was a numerical majority of 3,758 votes over Labour.

The relationship between Westminster and Holyrood constituencies was decoupled by the 2005 Westminster redistribution, and the former Aberdeen South Holyrood seat has taken a different turn. As stated above, it was held by the Lib Dems' Nicol Stephen from 1999 until he retired in 2011. In that year the Holyrood seat was redrawn with the new name of Aberdeen South and North Kincardine, and it has had SNP representation since then. Maureen Watt (who is the daughter and mother of former SNP MPs) took the seat over in 2011 after five years as a regional MSP for North East Scotland. She passed it on in 2021 to Audrey Nicoll, who had won a November 2019 Aberdeen council by-election in Torry/Ferryhill ward; I described Nicoll then as "a part-time lecturer and retired police officer". Her husband Alex is leader of the SNP group on Aberdeen council.

Nicoll stood down at last month's Holyrood election and the SNP nomination in a renamed seat of Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine went to then-MP Stephen Flynn. Flynn was duly elected but fairly narrowly so, polling 34% against 31% for the former Scottish Conservative deputy leader Liam Kerr and 18% for Reform UK's Duncan Massey; Kerr and Massey were both returned as regional MSPs for North East Scotland, at the top of their respective party lists. Under the terms of the Scotland Act 1998 as amended, once Stephen Flynn was elected to Holyrood he was obliged to resign his Westminster seat within 49 days or be disqualified from the Scottish Parliament.

The pro-independence majority in Holyrood is not a reflection of the votes which were cast last month, and is entirely due to the SNP's overhang in the constituency seats. Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine is one seat where the Scottish Greens' withdrawal from the constituency vote might have made a crucial difference, because the Conservatives carried that seat in the Scottish Parliament regional vote with 26%; the SNP had 25%, Reform UK 19% and Labour 9%. The 2,671 votes or 8% which went to the Greens in the regional vote is comfortably more than Stephen Flynn's constituency vote majority of 1,244.

Mind, the Conservatives probably shouldn't look on that result from Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine and conclude that they are about to win this Westminster by-election. This will be a tougher assignment for them. The Westminster seat does not include the North Kincardine ward of Aberdeenshire, where the Conservatives are strong, and it takes in large parts of the Aberdeen Central Holyrood seat where the Tories were fifth in the regional vote this year.

The last local elections in Scotland were back in May 2022. If we add together the votes cast then for the six wards wholly in this constituency, we see that the SNP topped the poll with 29% of the first preferences, the Conservatives polled 23%, the Lib Dems and Labour scored 16% each, and 10% of the votes were cast for independent candidates. This translated into eight SNP councillors, five Conservatives, four Labour, three Lib Dems and two independents. The Aberdeen South constituency also includes part of Midstocket/Rosemount ward, which the SNP carried in 2022. There is currently one vacant seat in the city-centre George St/Harbour ward where the Lib Dem councillor has resigned, and the Aberdeen returning officer had already scheduled a by-election to fill that seat for 25th June before Stephen Flynn's resignation came through. Westminster elections and Scottish local elections can't be combined because of the voter ID rules which apply to Westminster elections, so the electors of George St/Harbour ward will have to come back and do this all over again next week. And I'm going to have to write something different about the place.

Before then we have a Westminster by-election to deal with. Defending for the SNP from the bottom of the alphabetical ballot paper is Richard Thomson, who is hoping to return to elected politics after losing his Westminster seat in 2024. Thomson had represented the Gordon constituency in the 2019-24 Parliament, narrowly losing the successor seat of Gordon and Buchan to the Conservatives in 2024; before then he was an Aberdeenshire councillor from 2012 to 2020, representing Ellon and District ward and serving as leader of Aberdeenshire council from 2015 to 2017. Since 2024 Thomson had been working in public affairs. He had applied without success for the SNP nomination for Angus North and Mearns in the recent Holyrood election, but maybe this will be a consolation prize?

Starting in second place and hoping for more is the Labour candidate Nurul Ali, who is an Aberdeen city councillor for Bridge of Don ward (which is not part of this constituency). Ali was the Labour candidate for Gordon and Buchan in the 2024 Westminster election.

The Conservatives' Douglas Lumsden is straight back on the campaign trail after securing re-election to the Scottish Parliament in May. Lumsden was previously the Conservative candidate for this seat in 2019; in 2021 he was elected as a North East Scotland regional MSP, and he retained that seat last month after narrowly missing out on a constituency seat in Aberdeenshire East.

Two other parties saved their deposits here in 2024. Reform UK, who finished fourth here in 2024 with 6.9%, have selected Jo Hart: she stood in May in the Aberdeenshire West constituency and was in fourth place on the Reform UK list for North East Scotland. The Lib Dems' Mel Sullivan is also straight back on the campaign trail after her fourth-place finish in Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine last month, which is better than the fifth-place 6.3% the party managed in 2024. Sullivan, who was also ranked fourth on the Lib Dem list for North East Scotland, is an Aberdeenshire councillor for North Kincardine ward which borders this constituency. Of the three parties or candidates who lost their deposit here in 2024, one is back for another go: the Scottish Greens' Jorg Shelton-Eckstein is hoping to improve on his party's 3.5% and sixth place from two years ago. And one new party has entered the fray: David Ballantine, who gives an address in Edinburgh and was the Brexit Party candidate for Edinburgh South West in 2019, is standing on the ticket of the Alliance for Democracy and Freedom. He completes a ballot paper of seven candidates.

The list of polling stations for this by-election includes St Mary's Cathedral (Roman Catholic), St Andrew's Cathedral (Scottish Episcopal Church) and, for those whose religion is football, Pittodrie Stadium where electors from the Pittodrie North polling district are invited to cast their vote in the Sir Alex Ferguson Lounge. The returning officer has successfully managed to avoid this by-election clashing with Scotland's World Cup games, and once the polls close the challenge will be on to see whether we can get a result earlier in the night than Haiti v Scotland last weekend. This is a Westminster by-election, so all voters are reminded to bring photo ID with them to the polling station.

Arbroath and Broughty Ferry

House of Commons; caused by the resignation of Scottish National Party MP Stephen Gethins.

Our second Scottish National Party defence of the week occurs in the ground zero for Scottish nationalism. The date is 6th April 1320, the scene is the abbey at Aberbrothock on the east coast of Scotland. King Robert the Bruce is still fighting the English invaders under Edward II, and he needs to keep his friends outwith Scotland on side. Which is a problem, because the world's most powerful political figure is an septuagenarian who wants the war to end and is prepared to make dire threats to the leader of the smaller country in order to bring peace about. (Nothing like that could happen today, of course.) And 76-year-old Pope John XXII has recently made good on that threat by excommunicating King Robert.

In response to this, the Scottish Parliament met at Aberbrothock and put together three letters to be sent to the Pope in Avignon: one from Robert himself, one from the bishops, and one from the nobles. The last letter has survived. Composed (probably) by Abbot Bernard of Kilwinning and written in Latin, as was normal for learned people of the day, the Declaration of Arbroath made the case that Scotland was a proud, independent kingdom which occupied not only the literal but also the moral high ground. It asserted that the English had committed atrocities and their attacks on Scotland were unjust. There was also a hint that Scotland, if supported, might help out the Holy Father in a war which he wanted to wage against Muslim states in the Middle East. (Nothing like that could happen today, of course.)

And there needed to be a defence of King Robert's previous behaviour in coming to the Scottish throne by deposing King John Balliol and then murdering John Comyn III, so the Declaration included text to the effect that the independence of Scotland was the prerogative of the Scottish people and that Balliol had broken his contract with them by failing to defend the country against the English. This was probably not intended as a statement of popular sovereignty, which did not exist in the mediaeval period when European politics was a literal game of thrones. We can't be sure what influence this argument had on John XXII, but the Declaration of Arbroath as a whole found favour: Robert the Bruce's excommunication was lifted.

Arbroath Abbey now lies in ruins, but the town itself continues to thrive. Arbroath - or Aberbrothock, as it was once known - has been a royal burgh since 1599. The former Town House on the High Street which was home to the burgh council still has legal significance: by law, genuine Arbroath Smokies can only be produced within an 8-kilometre radius of the Town House. Smokies, which originated in the tiny fishing village of Auchmithie to the east of Arbroath, are salted haddock cooked over a very hot and smoky fire, and there are several firms in the harbour area which still produce this local delicacy. The town's economy was traditionally based on the harbour, together with textiles: there were a number of jute mills here, and Arbroath was once a major manufacturer of sailcloth.

Jute, together with jam and journalism, were the traditional industries of the city of Dundee further along the coast. The profits from the jute mills accrued to a number of "jute barons", who showed off their wealth by building massive houses in the fishing village of Broughty Ferry not far to the east of Dundee. By the turn of the 20th century, Broughty Ferry ranked among the richest suburbs in Europe. It became an independent burgh in 1864, and maintained that status until it was annexed by Dundee in 1913. Despite the name, the ferry (which connected the town with Tayport, on the Fife side of the Firth of Tay) no longer runs: its business was taken long ago by the Tay Bridges to the west.

In between Arbroath and Broughty Ferry we find this constituency's third town. Carnoustie is another old burgh based on the textile industry, but there is another side to its economy: tourism, particularly that generated by the sport of golf. Carnoustie Golf Links is one of the courses on the Open Championship rota and it has hosted the tournament eight times, most recently in 2018 when Francesco Molinari was the winner. The course is noted for its difficulty, and the events of the 1999 Open Championship - when the French golfer Jean van de Velde notoriously blew a three-shot lead on the final hole, and subsequently lost a playoff to local favourite Paul Lawrie - still live long in the memory.

Golf is not yet on the Commonwealth Games programme, but Carnoustie was nonetheless involved in the 2014 Glasgow Commonwealth Games in which it hosted the shooting events. These were held at the Barry Buddon training area, a Ministry of Defence rifle range which occupies most of the extensive system of sand dunes between Carnoustie and Monifieth. The military also have a presence at Arbroath, which hosts the Condor training area for the Royal Marines.

All of these towns are linked together by the A92 dual carriageway and by the railway line from Dundee to Aberdeen. Arbroath is the main railhead for the area, with express trains to England as well as the usual ScotRail services; the Aberdeen portion of the Highland Sleeper to and from London calls both there and at Carnoustie. Broughty Ferry is recognised by ScotRail as Scotland's oldest railway station still in operation, having opened in October 1838. Many of the intermediate stops are sparsely served, and Golf Street and Barry Links stations (which lie to the west of Carnoustie) see just three trains a day and regularly feature near the top of the annual list of the UK's least-used railway stations.

The towns and the large rural hinterland within the Arbroath and Broughty Ferry constituency were all within the traditional county of Angus, which takes its name from an 8th-century king of the Picts. But the county of Angus wasn't always called that. The name dates in law from the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1947, which consolidated Scottish local government law of the time; the county council had previously resolved to use the name Angus in 1928. Before then, the county of Angus - and the parliamentary seat based on it, which went back at least as far the Union of 1707 - was known as Forfarshire. The First Reform Act of 1832 took the Forfarshire towns of Arbroath, Brechin, Forfar and Montrose out of the Forfarshire seat: those four burghs, plus the Kincardineshire burgh of Inverbervie, then formed the disconnected Montrose Burghs constituency.

The Montrose Burghs was a strongly Liberal constituency almost throughout its existence, and the Conservatives rarely even contested it. MPs of some strain of Liberalism held the seat continously from 1859 to 1930. In fact, in the 1885 general election - held on a reformed and expanded franchise - the Liberal vote here was so strong that the top two candidates in the final reckoning were a Liberal and an Independent Liberal. The official Liberal John Shiress Will, a barrister, was the winner with a shade under 50% of the vote, and he was easily re-elected in 1886, 1892 and 1895 against opposition from the Liberal Unionists.

In 1896 John Shiress Will resigned his seat in the House of Commons to allow a return to the Commons for John Morley, a former Cabinet minister who had served in the third and fourth Gladstone administrations as Chief Secretary for Ireland until he lost his seat in Newcastle upon Tyne at the 1895 general election. Morley increased the Liberal majority at the resulting Montrose Burghs by-election of 22nd February 1895, and in time he was back on the Cabinet frontline: when the Liberals returned to power in 1905 he became India secretary. In between, Morley was one of the first 12 people appointed to the Order of Merit on its creation in 1902. The Order of Merit is, of course, unique in the British honours system in that it is awarded for merit.

When Asquith came to power in 1908, he kept Morley in post as India secretary but elevated him to the House of Lords, as the first and only Viscount Morley of Blackburn. The resulting Montrose Burghs by-election of 12th May 1908 was won by Robert Harcourt, the son of the former Liberal Party leader Sir William Harcourt, who had previously worked in the Foreign Office and in journalism; second place in that by-election went to an Independent Labour Party candidate.

The more rural Forfarshire seat - which at this time took in Carnoustie and the fast-growing Broughty Ferry - was also Liberal-inclined during the period 1885 to 1918. It had been held since an 1872 by-election by James Barclay, a large landowner and farmer in the county who was living proof of the old adage that where there's muck there's brass: he was a manure merchant. He won his fourth term of office very easily in 1885, but when the Liberal Party split a year later over the issue of Irish home rule Barclay joined the breakaway Liberal Unionists. He was narrowly re-elected on that ticket in 1886 with a majority of 407 votes, before losing his seat back to the official Liberals by 866 votes in 1892.

The winning Liberal candidate on that occasion was John Rigby, a noted lawyer who was making a return to the Commons after some years away: he had represented Wisbech from 1885 to 1886. Gladstone immediately appointed Rigby to the frontbench as Solicitor General for England and Wales, with a knighthood to go with it: this meant he had to seek re-election to the Commons, and nobody opposed him in the resulting Forfarshire by-election of 24th August 1892. Sir John Rigby's legal career then blossomed: in 1894 he was promoted to Attorney General, and later that year he resigned from the Commons to become a Court of Appeal judge.

The resulting Forfarshire by-election of 17th November 1894 saw a poor choice of candidate from the defending Liberals, who selected London-based stockbroker Henry Robson over a well-known local candidate. This created an opening for the Conservative candidate Charles Ramsay, who was the youngest son of the 12th Earl of Dalhousie and had inherited a large amount of land in the county. In an upset result, Ramsay gained the seat with a majority of 286 votes; the large agricultural labourers' vote in Forfarshire was thought to have been decisive in this result.

For the 1895 general election the Forfarshire Liberals reversed their mistake and selected the well-known local candidate who had been passed over for the 1894 by-election. Martin White, who defeated Ramsay by 441 votes, was a Dundee businessman whose wealth enabled him to pursue his interests. As well as politics, White was a skilled player of the pipe organ; he funded academic work in the relatively new discipline of sociology; and he installed Scotland's first domestic hydropower plant to power his baronial castle at Balruddery, north-west of Dundee.

White resigned his seat in the Commons in November 1896 in rather murky circumstances. Officially, he fell out with his constituency party after a story falsely reporting his resignation had appeared in the Dundee Advertiser. The backstory was that he had been having an affair for some time with a woman called Helen Grant and had got her pregnant, and she was suing him for breaching a promise to marry her. They eventually settled the case out of court without the public or the press noticing.

The resulting Forfarshire by-election of 30th January 1897 provided a route back into the Commons for John Sinclair, a former Army officer who had served in the Gordon relief expedition in Sudan in the 1880s. Sinclair had previously been the Liberal MP for Dunbartonshire from 1892 until he lost his seat in 1895, and he had been a London county councillor before that. In the 1897 Forfarshire by-election he defeated the Conservatives' Charles Ramsay by 458 votes, with no swing at all from the 1895 result. Ramsay then went off to the Yukon Territory to look for gold in the Klondike gold rush, presumably without success: he was back for another go in the 1900 general election, but narrowly lost to Sinclair again.

John Sinclair had worked closely in Parliament with the Liberal leader Sir Henry Campbell-Bannerman, and when the Liberals came to power in 1905 Campbell-Bannerman appointed him to the Cabinet as Scottish Secretary. Sinclair used this position to push through a number of reforms, including allowing women to stand in Scottish local government elections for the first time: in November 1907 Lavinia Malcolm became Scotland's first female councillor in Dollar, Clackmannanshire.

In 1909 Sinclair was promoted to the House of Lords, taking the hereditary title of Lord Pentland; he then left these shores in 1912 to become Governor of Madras. The resulting Forfarshire by-election of 27th February 1909 pitted two lawyers against each other, with the Conservatives' Robert Blackburn (who would go on to become a Court of Session judge) being easily defeated by the new Liberal candidate James Falconer. Falconer went on to be easily re-elected in both 1910 elections.

Once the First World War was over the the Forfarshire constituency was reduced in size at the 1918 general election, reflecting that Broughty Ferry had by now been annexed by Dundee. This might have been important in the Unionist gain of Forfarshire that year by William Shaw, a stockbroker who had served in the Royal Army Service Corps during the war; there were also rumours that Shaw had received the coalition government's coupon, although this was not in fact the case.

James Falconer got his seat back in 1922 and round 3 of Falconer v Shaw was also a Liberal win in 1923. But when Falconer lost his seat for the second time in 1924 that pretty much ended the Liberal challenge in Forfarshire. The 1924 election here was a big win for the Unionist candidate Sir Harry Hope, a farmer and agriculturalist who had previously sat for Buteshire from January 1910 to 1918 and then for West Stirlingshire from 1918 to 1922. Hope retired at the 1931 election and William Shaw then returned as the MP for Forfarshire, winning in 1931 and 1935 with large majorities.

William Shaw finally retired from the Commons in 1945 and passed the Forfarshire seat on to the new Unionist candidate Simon Ramsay, who had served in the Black Watch during the Second World War: he fought in the Allied invasion of Sicily and won the Military Cross. Ramsay spent much of the 1945-50 Parliament as a Conservative whip. He retired from the Commons in 1950, shortly before he inherited the title of Earl of Dalhousie from his brother.

Meanwhile the Dundee constituency, which included Broughty Ferry from 1918, was a two-seat borough during this period. In 1918 it easily re-elected its incumbent MPs, Labour's Alexander Wilkie and the National Liberal candidate Winston Churchill. Yes, that Winston Churchill: at this point he was Minister of Munitions and he was in the National Liberal faction associated with the Prime Minister David Lloyd George. Churchill had received the Coalition government's coupon, and both Churchill and Wilkie were endorsed by the Dundee branch of the Unionist Party which had stood down in their favour. In the 1918-22 Parliament Churchill returned to the Cabinet, first as War and Air Secretary, then as Colonial Secretary from 1921.

Winston Churchill leaves our story in 1922, when the Dundee constituency returned a unique combination of candidates. Top of the poll was Edwin Scrymgeour, the only MP ever elected for the single-issue Scottish Prohibition Party; while the Labour seat was retained by E D Morel, a journalist who had spent most of his career exposing human rights abuses in the Belgian Congo. Churchill finished in fourth place, behind his National Liberal running-mate David Macdonald, and lost his seat. Morel suffered a fatal heart attack in 1924, and the resulting Dundee by-election of 22nd December 1924 was held for Labour by former MP Tom Johnston who had lost his seat in West Stirlingshire at the previous general election. In 1929 Johnston went back to West Stirlingshire, and Dundee's Labour seat was retained by solicitor and Edinburgh councillor Michael Marcus.

Marcus and Edwin Scrymgeour were both ejected by the voters of Dundee in the 1931 National Government landslide. Again Dundee split its two seats, but this time between the Liberals and the Unionists. The Liberal MP was a very young Dingle Foot, who had recently been called to the bar and who joined his father Isaac Foot (re-elected for Bodmin) in the Commons; the Unionist MP was Florence Horsburgh MBE (as she was then) who became Dundee's first ever female politician, as no women had ever been elected to the city council before then. Horsburgh subsequently became the first MP ever to be interviewed for television, in 1936.

The unusual double act of Foot and Horsburgh represented Dundee until 1945, when they lost their seats to Labour who won both of Dundee's seats in that year for the first time. Top of the poll here in 1945 was Thomas Cook, a Lanarkshire man who had got into politics via the co-operative movement; his running-mate was John Strachey, a journalist who had previously served as MP for Birmingham Aston in the 1929-31 Parliament and who had been closely associated at that time with Oswald Mosley, before Mosley went fascist. Strachey had spent much of the war in the RAF as a public relations officer, and he immediately joined the government ranks as under-secretary of state for war; in 1946 he took on the difficult task of Minister for Food in those years of rationing.

The Montrose Burghs constituency was rather more settled in terms of its MPs during the inter-war years, but the fact that all of them were elected on a ticket with the word "Liberal" in it masks a rather hard swing to the right. Robert Harcourt stood down in 1918 and his seat was easily retained by new Liberal candidate and newspaperman John Sturrock. Sturrock was re-elected rather more narrowly in 1922 and 1923, contesting the first of those elections on the National Liberal ticket.

In 1924 John Sturrock stood down and was replaced as Liberal MP for Montrose Burghs by Sir Robert Hutchison, who had come to politics from a glittering career as an Army officer which went all the way back to the Boer War: Hutchison retired from the Army in 1923 with the rank of major-general and a DSO, and he was mentioned in despatches six times. He then went straight into politics as MP for the Kirkcaldy Burghs in 1922, but lost that seat in 1923 to Labour's Tom Kennedy; the Montrose Burghs proved to be a safer berth. For most of his time as MP here Hutchison was in the whips office, serving from 1926 to 1930 as Liberal chief whip. He then defected to the National Liberals, the breakaway Liberal group which supported the National Government in 1931, and he was re-elected that year on their ticket.

Sir Robert Hutchison was elevated to the peerage in 1932, as the first and only Lord Hutchison of Montrose. The resulting Montrose Burghs by-election of 28th June 1932 was contested by the former Kirkcaldy Burghs Labour MP Tom Kennedy, who came close to winning but ultimately lost by 933 votes to the new Liberal National candidate Charles Kerr, who was very much in the same mould as Hutchison. Kerr was a former Horse Guards officer with a DSO and a Military Cross to his name, and he ended up as the National Liberals' chief whip.

Charles Kerr was was elevated to the peerage in 1940, as the first Lord Teviot, and the resulting Montrose Burghs by-election of 5th July 1940 was won without a contest (thanks to the wartime political truce) by the new National Liberal candidate John Maclay, who had been selected to stand for the party here in the 1939 or 1940 general election that never took place. Maclay had been on the winning Cambridge team in the 1927 Boat Race, defeating Sir Douglas Thomson who we met in the Aberdeen South Preview above. He was safely re-elected in 1945 and spent most of the 1945-50 Parliament leading the National Liberal group in the Commons.

There were major boundary changes here in 1950, as the two-seat burgh of Dundee was divided into two single-seat constituencies and the Montrose Burghs constituency was abolished. Broughty Ferry was now located within the single-member constituency of Dundee East, while Arbroath, Carnoustie and Monifieth became part of the new seat of South Angus - reflecting the county's name change from Forfarshire. This seat pattern remained little changed from 1950 until 2005, although the rural seat was renamed as East Angus in 1983, before becoming simply Angus in 1997.

These two constituencies had very different political persuasions, reflecting their urban and rural natures. The urban Dundee East seat started off by re-electing Labour's Thomas Cook in 1950 and 1951 with relatively narrow majorities over the National Liberal candidate. Which posed a potential problem in 1952, when Cook died in a car crash on the road between Dundee and Arbroath: the car which he was driving left the road, hit a tree, went over a low wall and ended up in a field. Cook, who was 43 years old, was killed instantly.

The resulting Dundee East by-election of 17th July 1952 marked the entry to elections here of the Scottish National Party, although their candidate Donald Stewart (who would later serve for many years as the MP for the Western Isles) finished in third place with 7.5% and lost his deposit. The poll proved to be a comfortable win for the new Labour candidate George Thomson, who held the seat with an increased majority over the National Liberals.

This set up a long political career for George Thomson, who was brought up in Monifieth just outside the constituency and had been educated at Broughty Ferry. Thomson had left school at 16 to join the Dundee publishers DC Thomson (no relation) and briefly served as editor of The Dandy when he was 18, before joining the Royal Air Force in 1940. He was unable to fly due to sight problems and spent the Second World War as ground crew in Fighter Command; after the war he went back to journalism and became editor of the Scottish socialist newspaper Forward.

George Thomson's 1952 by-election majority of 8,126 proved to be his best result in Dundee East and the seat went back to marginal status thereafter, but he was ultimately elected for six terms of office: the National Liberals were in second place until 1964, after which they merged with the Conservatives. In 1966 Thomson made it into Harold Wilson's Cabinet as Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster; ultimately he had four Cabinet roles between 1966 to 1970, including serving as the last Commonwealth secretary before the Commonwealth Office was merged with the Foreign Office in 1968.

In 1973 the United Kingdom joined the European Economic Community, and George Thomson left the Commons to serve as one of the UK's two representatives on the European Commission: he took the regional policy portfolio under president François-Xavier Ortoli. The resulting Dundee East by-election of 1st March 1973 changed the game of Dundee politics with an unexpectedly strong showing for the Scottish National Party, who surged into second place and came within 1,200 votes of winning. The Conservatives' William Fitzgerald, who was the Lord Provost of Dundee, finished third with 25%; the SNP's Gordon Wilson, a solicitor who had once controlled a pirate radio station, polled 30%; and the winner on just 33% of the vote was the Labour candidate George Machin. Machin was an Englishman from Sheffield who had risen to the Commons through the trade union movement, and his victory speech after the declaration was disrupted by hecklers with chants of "go back to Yorkshire".

Fifty-two weeks later Labour's Machin and the SNP's Gordon Wilson faced off again in the snap general election of February 1974. By this point, as mentioned in the Aberdeen South Preview, the North Sea oil industry was starting to get going in earnest, and Wilson - who had by now become depute leader of the SNP - had a coined a pithy slogan to convert this geological good fortune into Nationalist votes: "It's Scotland's Oil". It worked, and Wilson defeated Machin in Dundee East by 2,966 votes to become one of seven Scottish National Party MPs in the House of Commons - the party's first big electoral breakthrough.

This boom was followed by bust, and in 1979 the SNP held just two seats: Donald Stewart in the Western Isles, and Gordon Wilson in Dundee East. Wilson may well have been helped in that year by another controversial selection by the Dundee East branch of Labour: they went for the Red Clydeside union leader Jimmy Reid, who had been a Communist candidate in both 1974 general elections and didn't have the length of party membership which was normally expected for Labour parliamentary candidates at the time. Wilson defeated Reid by 2,519 votes, and later that year he was elected as leader of the Scottish National Party - a post which he would hold until 1990, when Alex Salmond took over for the first time.

By that point Gordon Wilson was out of the Commons, as he had lost his seat back to Labour in 1987. This set up John McAllion, who defeated Wilson by 1,015 votes, for a similarly long tenure in Dundee East. McAllion had been the convenor of Tayside regional council before his election, and he had worked as a teacher before going into politics. He went on to be re-elected in 1992 and 1997.

John McAllion was a supporter of home rule for Scotland, and when the Scottish Parliament was re-established in 1999 he became the first MSP for Dundee East. McAllion then stood down from Westminster at the 2001 general election, and he passed his seat on to Labour MP Iain Luke. Like McAllion, Luke had come up from local politics: he was a Dundee city councillor before entering the Commons.

As stated, during the period from 1950 to 2005 Arbroath, Carnoustie and the rural parts of the modern Arbroath and Broughty Ferry seat were included in various constituencies with the name "Angus" in the title. The first of these was South Angus, which in the 1950s was a safe seat for the National Liberals' James Duncan. Duncan was making a return to Parliament, having previously represented the London seat of Kensington North from 1931 until he lost his seat in 1945. He was made a baronet in 1957.

Sir James Duncan retired at the 1964 general election and passed South Angus on to Jock Bruce-Gardyne, who was from a family with extensive landholdings in the area. There is still a Gardyne Castle in this seat, a privately-owned tower house not far from the village of Friockheim.

Bruce-Gardyne continued to enjoy large majorities right up until October 1974, when he was swept away in a landslide gain for the SNP. This was the start of the long political career of Andrew Welsh, a Glasgow schoolteacher who had just turned 30. Welsh defeated Bruce-Gardyne by 1,824 votes in October 1974. For the 1979 general election Bruce-Gardyne found a safer berth south of the Border in Knutsford, and the Conservatives selected Peter Fraser who defeated Welsh by the even narrower margin of 963 votes.

Peter Fraser had been born in Zambia and came to Scotland at the age of 12, after the Prime Minister Anthony Eden had pulled strings to get Fraser a scholarship to Loretto School in Musselburgh. Fraser made his career in the law, and in 1982 he was appointed to the post of Solicitor General for Scotland and received the title of QC to go with it.

The 1983 boundary changes redrew Fraser's constituency as East Angus, moving Forfar out of the seat and Brechin and Montrose in. The new seat was considerably more Conservative than the old Angus South, and had it not been for the redistribution Peter Fraser would probably have lost his seat back to the SNP's Andrew Welsh in 1983. As it was, the third and final round of Fraser v Welsh in 1987 was a win for Welsh, who returned to the Commons after eight years away. In the interim Welsh had served as provost of Angus and as leader of Angus district council.

Andrew Welsh never lost an election again after that, whether in East Angus or in the Angus seat that replaced it in the 1997 redistribution. As with John McAllion in neighbouring Dundee East, Welsh was elected to the Scottish Parliament in 1999 and stood down from Westminster in 2001, successfully passing the seat on to his party colleague - in this case, Mike Weir. Weir, who had been the SNP candidate for Aberdeen South in 1987, was a solicitor and an Angus councillor before his election to the House of Commons.

The advent of the Scottish Parliament, and the subsequent reduction in the number of Scotland's parliamentary seats to match, forced a major redistribution here in 2005. The two Dundee constituencies expanded outwards into their rural hinterlands, with Dundee East gaining Monifieth and Carnoustie from the Angus constituency. This made the Dundee East seat less urban, and that might well have made the difference in the 2005 general election when the Scottish National Party's Stewart Hosie gained Dundee East from Labour's Iain Luke. Hosie, who had worked for many years in IT before his election, had previously finished as runner-up here in 2001.

Stewart Hosie would go on to hold Dundee East for 19 years, and for much of that time he was one of the SNP's most high-profile figures. In 2014 he became the party's deputy leader, succeeding Nicola Sturgeon after she ascended to the top job. Hosie didn't seek re-election to that position in 2016 following media scrutiny over his private life: he had had an affair with a Westminster journalist, which had led to the breakup of his marriage to Shona Robison - who, awkwardly, was the SNP MSP for the corresponding Holyrood seat of Dundee City East. Hosie and Robison subsequently divorced, and he married said Westminster journalist.

The heavily-redrawn Angus constituency turned in close results between the SNP and the Conservatives in 2005 and 2010, with Mike Weir holding his seat on both occasions. But he eventually went down to defeat in the good Conservative year of 2017, when the Angus seat returned the Conservative candidate Kirstene Hair who overturned a majority of over 11,000 votes. Hair, who had been the party's Holyrood candidate for Angus South in 2016, was - like George Thomson who we met earlier - a former employee of the DC Thomson publishing empire.

The Scottish Conservatives went backwards in 2019, and Kirstene Hair proved to be a one-term MP. She lost her seat back to the SNP, a result which brought Dave Doogan into the House of Commons. Doogan had given up a job as an MoD aircraft engineer in order to enter politics, and he had sat on Perth and Kinross council for seven years before he won the Angus constituency. He quickly made it to the party's frontbench, being appointed as the SNP's Westminster spokesman on agriculture and rural affairs within a month of the election. Following the 2024 redistribution Doogan was re-elected for the successor seat of Angus and Perthshire Glens, and last month he took over from the departing Stephen Flynn as the leader of the SNP's Westminster group - a group which is much smaller now than it was when Doogan was first elected in 2019.

The Arbroath and Broughty Ferry constituency was created for the 2024 general election, essentially as the successor to Dundee East. Compared to the old seat, the east end of Dundee moved out and was replaced by Arbroath and its hinterland. The seat continues to cover a series of peripheral Dundee estates, mostly in the city's North East ward, as well as affluent Broughty Ferry.

Stewart Hosie retired at the 2024 election, and the SNP managed to hold his former seat - no mean achievement in an election where Labour ran rampant across the Central Belt and SNP seats fell like ninepins. The Labour charge fell 859 votes short here, with Stephen Gethins of the SNP winning on 35% of the vote to 33% for Labour and 16% for the Conservatives.

Stephen Gethins had been an MP before, representing North East Fife from 2015 to 2019. His time there included being on the right side of one of the closest Westminster election results of modern times: in the 2017 general election he was re-elected with 13,743 votes to 13,741 for the Liberal Democrat candidate, a majority of two votes after three recounts. He had been the only SNP MP to lose his seat in 2019. Before entering politics Gethins had been working on embedding peace and democracy in the Caucasus and the Balkans; after his 2019 defeat he joined the ranks of academia, becoming a professor of practice in international relations at St Andrews.

The Angus wards of this seat have the distinction of being part of an SNP-held constituency at all seven Holyrood elections to date. Andrew Welsh held the Angus Holyrood constituency until he retired in 2011; at this point his seat was broken up in boundary changes with Arbroath, Carnoustie and so on transferring into a recreated Angus South constituency. This was won in 2011 by Graeme Dey, yet another journalist from the DC Thomson stable, who served for 15 years and was a Scottish Government junior minister for much of the period from 2018 to 2026; his final portfolio was minister for Parliamentary business, which he still held up to his retirement from Holyrood this year. Angus South was left unchanged in this year's Scottish Parliament redistribution, and it was held for the SNP by Lloyd Melville who is an Angus councillor: he represents Monifieth and Sidlaw ward, and he is entitled to remain as a councillor until his term expires next year. Melville won Angus South last month with 42% of the vote, against 21% for the Conservatives and 18% for Reform UK; vote shares here in the North East Scotland regional ballot, with a wider choice of parties, were 32% for the SNP, 19% for the Conservatives, 18% for Reform UK, and 10% each for the Scottish Greens and Labour.

Meanwhile the Dundee portion of the Arbroath and Broughty Ferry constituency is nearly all in the Dundee City East Holyrood constituency (a small corner is in Dundee City West). Dundee City East has been in SNP hands since 2003, when Shona Robison gained the predecessor seat of Dundee East from Labour's John McAllion. Robison, who had been a regional MSP for North East Scotland in the first Holyrood term, was a regular fixture of the Scottish Government for much of the last 20 years: she made it to Cabinet level in 2014 with the sprawling portfolio of the Glasgow Commonwealth Games, sport, equalities and pensioners' rights, and her career peaked in 2023-24 when she was Humza Yousaf's deputy as First Minister of Scotland. Robison was still the Scottish cabinet secretary for finance when she retired from Holyrood in 2026 after six full terms of office.

Stephen Gethins won the 2026 SNP nomination for Dundee City East to succeed Robison, and he was easily elected in May with 49% of the vote against 18% for Labour and 16% for Reform UK, whose candidate Mark Simpson was elected to Holyrood at the top of the Reform UK list for North East Scotland. The regional vote here gave 35% to the SNP, 16% each to Labour and Reform UK, and 14% to the Scottish Greens. There is no exemption from the double-jobbing rules for Gethins, and he was obliged to resign from Westminster upon his election to the Scottish Parliament.

The ward and constituency boundaries here don't match up, which is not uncommon in Scotland where the use of PR for local government results in wards of 3 or 4 councillors with relatively large headcounts. If we take as a best fit for this seat the four Angus wards wholly or partly in the constituency plus the Dundee wards of The Ferry and North East, then first preference votes here in 2022 were 30% for the SNP, 28% for Labour who didn't stand in Carnoustie and District ward, 18% for the Conservatives and 10% for the Liberal Democrats (the vast majority of which came out of The Ferry ward covering Broughty Ferry, where they topped the poll). Councillor totals for the four Angus wards in the seat were six SNP, four independents, three Conservatives and one Labour, while the two Dundee wards returned three SNP councillors, two Labour and one each for the Conservatives and Lib Dems. The independent Angus councillor for Arbroath West, Letham and Friockheim ward resigned in 2024, and the resulting by-election was gained by the Conservatives.

The Scottish National Party have only ever had to defend two Westminster by-elections, both of them in the 2019-24 Parliament, and they have a 50% retention rate to date. Holding this seat and Aberdeen South would increase that to 75%. The one previous SNP by-election hold was Airdrie and Shotts in 2021, which was for the same reason as these two: the incumbent Westminster MP had gone to Holyrood.

Defending for the SNP is Lara Bird, a qualified barrister from Broughty Ferry who is currently studying for a PhD at King's College London. Bird is also an experienced long-distance runner, and she completed this year's London Marathon in a time of 3 hours, 12 minutes and 58 seconds to raise funds for the RNLI. She is a first-time election candidate.

The Labour candidate this time is Heather Doran, who works for Dundee University and is an Angus councillor for Monifieth and District ward. In May Doran was the Labour candidate for Angus South, and she was in second place on the party's list for North East Scotland.

Also straight back on the campaign trail is the Conservative candidate Jack Cruickshanks, who won the 2024 Angus council by-election in Arbroath West, Letham and Friockheim ward; he was then a 24-year-old student reading financial economics at Dundee University. In May Cruickshanks was the Conservative candidate for Dundee City East and he was in an unwinnable eighth place on the party's list for North East Scotland.

Fourth place here in 2024 went to Reform UK who polled 8.6%; their candidate this time is Bill Reid, who contested Angus South in May and was ranked in ninth and last place on the Reform UK list for North East Scotland. Last time round the Lib Dems polled 5.1% and saved their deposit by 43 votes; their candidate is Tanvir Ahmad, who stood in Dundee City East in May and was placed third on their North East Scotland list. In 2024 Arbroath and Broughty Ferry also had candidates from Alba and a pro-independence group called Sovereignty, both of whom lost their deposits; Alba are now defunct and Sovereignty are not standing again.

Nobody else has come forward so this by-election has a field of just five candidates, the shortest ballot paper for a Westminster by-election since West Tyrone in 2018. But if you want weird and wonderful candidates, you'll be well catered for in the final part of this week's Parliamentary Special as we head south of the Border...

Moorside

Bury council, Greater Manchester; postponed from 7th May following the death of Reform UK candidate Victor Hagan.

Today's three Parliamentary Specials in Aberdeen South, Arbroath and Broughty Ferry, and Makerfield form the Main Event in this week's elections, but there is also a substantial undercard to cover. Enough time has now passed since May's elections for us to start covering the fallout and other matters arising from those polls, which includes those wards where a candidate in the election died during the election campaign. The procedure here is that the poll is called off, nominations are reopened to allow new candidates to stand (previously-nominated candidates automatically stay on the ballot), and then a new polling date is set within 35 working days of the original. This year the Grim Reaper called for six of the around 25,000 candidates from May in the weeks between nominations and polling. Four of the resulting postponed polls have been rescheduled for today, with the other two to follow next week.

We're going to start in a district of Greater Manchester which was one of the brighter spots for Labour in this year's local elections. Labour retained overall control of Bury council with a net loss of just one seat, with losses of Redvales ward to the Workers Party and Unsworth ward to Reform UK partly balanced by a surprise gain of the affluent North Manor ward from the Conservatives: this left Labour on 30 seats against 7 for Radcliffe First, 6 Reform UK, 3 Conservatives, 2 councillors in the Conservative splinter group Together for Bury, 1 independent and 1 Workers Party councillor, with this result to come.

The 2026 Bury council election will conclude today with the poll for Moorside ward, which covers the north of Bury town and is centred on Clarence Park. Here we have a mix of Victorian terraced housing leading off Walmersley Road, industrial areas close to the River Irwell, and a large chunk of the deprived Dicky Bird estate in eastern Bury. This was laid out in the 1930s with a strip of open land running through the middle of it for a future road connecting Manchester with Rossendale, but that road was eventually built in the late 1970s as a motorway - the M66 - which thunders through the estate at rooftop level on an embankment. There is not much actual moorland in Moorside ward, with the open space on higher ground to the east mostly covered by Clarence Park or a pair of golf courses.

This area is part of the Bury North parliamentary constituency, which has voted for the government candidate in every election since its 1983 creation with the exception of a Labour win in 2017. The winning candidate on that occasion was James Frith, who lost his seat in 2019 by just 105 votes - the second-closest result in the 2019 general election - but easily returned to office in 2024. Frith joined the government ladder in March this year as a junior Cabinet Office minister, with responsibility for digital ID.

The last seven of today's polls take place in Wales, with all but one of them being a direct result of a recent law change by the Senedd. Welsh local government law has a general principle that you can only wear one political hat at once, so if you're a councillor in Wales and you are elected to Westminster or to Senedd Cymru, then you are expected to resign your council position. There used to be an exemption from this rule for councillors who were in the last 53 weeks of their term, who would be allowed to see out their last year of office but would then be required to stand down at the following ordinary election. Since local elections in Wales take place a year after Senedd elections, this exemption saved quite a lot of hassle for newly-elected MSs. But the last Senedd abolished that exemption from May 2026 onwards, and we are now reaping the result of that change in the law. Nineteen of the 96 MSs elected in May were local councillors, and that means that 19 by-elections have now been triggered to add to the burden on electoral administrators, electors and this column. Six of those polls take place today, with the other 13 to follow over the coming weeks.

The first of those 19 new MSs we will feature here is Francesca O'Brien, who was top of the Reform UK list for the Gŵyr Abertawe constituency, which covers most of the Swansea council area, at the 2026 Senedd election. Reform UK won two of the six seats in Gŵyr Abertawe at that election, finishing in second place with 27% of the vote: Plaid Cymru topped the poll with 32% and won three seats, Labour won one seat with 14%, and the Conservatives were best of the rest on 10%. Gŵyr Abertawe covers both of the Swansea wards holding elections today.

O'Brien is a fairly recent recruit to Reform UK. She was elected to Swansea council in 2022 on the Conservative ticket, as a councillor for Mumbles ward. The headland known as the Mumbles is a beauty spot which lies at the western end of Swansea Bay: the land behind it is fairly heavily built up, with the villages of Langland, Newton and Oystermouth having fused together into a single urban area. Oystermouth is dominated by the impressive remains of a 13th-century castle, which is now in the care of Swansea council, while the beaches of western Swansea Bay and Langland Bay are a draw for day-trippers and holidaymakers on those occasions when the Welsh summer weather behaves itself.

Oystermouth is the final resting place of a man who died in 1825 but whose name lives on today in the English language. Thomas Bowdler has a chess attack named after him (1. e4 c5 2. Bc4, a reply by White to the Sicilian Defence which is now rarely seen at the top level), but he is mostly remembered as the publisher of The Family Shakespeare, "in which nothing is added to the original text; but those words and expressions are omitted which cannot with propriety be read aloud in a family". What this meant in practice was the compilation of a censored edition of Shakespeare's plays, with anything deemed too sexy, blasphemous or otherwise unsuitable for Georgian women and children to hear ruthlessly cut out. Ironically this censorship extended to the work's authorship, as although Thomas Bowdler was credited on its title page the first edition of The Family Shakespeare was in fact edited by his sister Harriet. Its commercial success led to Thomas spending the rest of his years compiling "Bowdlerised" editions of other works, including a family-friendly version of Gibbons' Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire which was unfinished when he died and was completed by his nephew. Bowdler set off a whole genre of expurgated literary works which were popular and generally seen as a Good Thing in the nineteenth century, and it wasn't until the First World War years that the term "bowdlerisation" started to take on connotations of unwelcome censorship. But in these days of mooted social media bans for children, maybe Thomas Bowdler's legacy is starting to come fashionable again?

The Mumbles area is still populated by the sort of affluent middle-class families who would have enjoyed Bowdler's work back in the day. Rather different in character is Morriston, a planned industrial village in northern Swansea which was built for and named after the industrialist Sir John Morris in the late eighteenth century. This is the core of the Morriston electoral ward, which extends along the west back of the River Tawe to take in the villages of Cwmrhydyceirw and Ynystawe. The ward's largest employer is Morriston Hospital, where many of Swansea's residents begin and end their lives. Although the Swansea offices of the DVLA are often described as being in Morriston, the DVLA complex is in fact located to the west of here in Mynydd-bach ward.

Morriston is a very large electoral ward which elects five Swansea councillors by first-past-the-post - something which would not be tolerated in England, where the Local Government Boundary Commission caps the size of wards at three councillors. In the 2022 Swansea council election the Labour slate here was guaranteed one of the five seats because only four opposition candidates stood, and Labour went on to elect all five councillors very easily with a 61-15 lead over the Conservatives. This places Morriston within the Labour majority on Swansea council.

At Westminster Morriston is part of the Swansea West constituency, which has only had three MPs - all Labour - in the last 60 years. Alan Williams retired in 2010 as Father of the House, with continuous service from 1964; his successor Geraint Davies represented the seat as a Labour MP for 13 years before being suspended from the party in 2023 over sexual harassment allegations. The Labour party's investigation into this never seems to have reached a conclusion, and Davies was effectively forced into retirement as he was still outside the fold when the 2024 general election was called. His successor is a Labour Party high-flyer: Torsten Bell came to the Commons in 2024 from nine years running the Resolution Foundation economic thinktank, and he has brought that work into government as a junior Treasury and DWP minister.

The Mumbles ward was created in 2022 as a merger of the previous single-member Newton and Oystermouth wards, plus territory transferred from West Cross ward to the north, with an increase in the overall number of councillors from two to three. Newton and Oystermouth had both returned Conservative coucillors for some years previously, and Mumbles continued in that vein in 2022 by giving 42% to the winning Conservative slate, 29% to Labour and 13% to the Greens.

This ward is part of the Gower Westminster constituency, which has been represented since 2017 by the chair of the Northern Ireland Affairs committee Tonia Antoniazzi. In 2019 she narrowly defeated Conservative candidate Francesca O'Brien, who was subsequently elected in 2022 as a Conservative councillor for Mumbles ward before defecting to Reform UK in 2025. O'Brien has now left Swansea council after her election to the Senedd last month, and she has slotted straight onto the Reform UK frontbench in Cardiff Bay as shadow cabinet member for local government, housing and planning.

Today's other by-election is to replace Swansea's longest-serving local councillor. Robert Francis-Davies was first elected all the way back in 1983, giving 43 years of unbroken service as a councillor for Morriston. He was Lord Mayor of the city in 2001-02, and he was active in local government right up to the end as the Swansea cabinet member for investment, regeneration, tourism and events. It was a job Francis-Davies had clearly excelled at over many years, with the council leader's tribute noting his contribution to the planning of the Swansea.com Stadium (home of Swansea City FC) and the National Waterfront Museum, together with bringing events such as Proms in the Park to Swansea. Robert Francis-Davies died in May at the age of 78, with the coroner's inquest attributing his death to a combination of renal cancer and a fall from a mobility scooter.

The defending Labour candidate in the by-election to replace Robert Francis-Davies is his daughter Rebecca, an HR consultant who was in fourth place on the Labour list for Gŵyr Abertawe last month; in the 2022 Swansea elections she contested Uplands ward. Another unsuccessful Senedd candidate who is straight back on the campaign trail is the Conservatives' Idin Ghotbi, who made up the numbers in sixth and last place on the Conservative list for Gŵyr Abertawe. The Lib Dem candidate is Hayden Lewis, Reform UK have selected Graham Ashby, Gareth Schofield is the Green candidate and Ioan Warlow stands for Plaid Cymru.

In Mumbles ward Reform UK will want to convert their defection gain into a by-election gain, and hoping to do that is their candidate Emilie Cox: she was an unsuccessful Conservative candidate in the 2022 Swansea elections, finishing as runner-up behind Labour in the neighbouring West Cross ward. The Conservatives will want to recover their loss and they have selected Hannah Williams. Standing for Labour is David Lewis, who sits on Mumbles community council and runs a B&B in the ward. The Green candidate Rich Williams has had a long career in the commercial and charity sector, and he currently runs a Swansea youth work charity. Also on the Mumbles ballot are Plaid Cymru's Tricia Sanderson who was an unsuccessful candidate here in 2022, the Lib Dems' Jim Hehir who stood for the Senedd last month in Afan Ogwr Rhondda, and independent candidate Dorian Davies who stood in Oystermouth ward back in 2017 and lists current membership of Reform UK on his nomination papers. Intending Conservative and Green voters should check carefully which Williams they are voting for.

Wrexham council, North Wales; caused respectively by the resignations of Plaid Cymru councillors Becca Martin, Marc Jones and Carrie Harper.

We now come to the largest settlement in North Wales, the city of Wrexham, where three by-elections are taking place to replace Plaid Cymru councillors who have all been elected to Senedd Cymru. In May Carrie Harper and Marc Jones were the top two candidates on the Plaid list for the Fflint Wrecsam constituency which covers Wrexham, where the main nationalist party polled 26% of the vote and won two out of six seats; while Becca Martin was elected in second place on the Plaid Cymru list for Clwyd, which scored 28% and won two seats. Reform UK were first in both constituencies, and in the first-past-the-post context of these by-elections that's all-important.

The three outgoing Plaid councillors all represented urban wards in Wrexham proper. Carrie Harper's ward was Queensway, which is in the east of the city and is one of the five wards covering the massive Caia Park housing estate. Its main feature is the Queensway Stadium, which is mainly used for athletics but is also home to the local non-league football team, FC Queens Park, who play in the Welsh third tier. Harper was first elected for the ward in 2017, and she won a second term of office in 2022 with a thumping 75-22 lead over Labour.

Also first elected in 2017 was Marc Jones, who was re-elected in 2022 in Grosvenor ward with a 63-26 lead over Labour. Most of this ward's residents live in the Rhosddu area immediately to the north of the city centre; the name of Grosvenor ward is a reference to the Dukes of Westminster, who we met in last week's Preview. This ward covers a large chunk of Wrexham city centre, including the Guildhall which is the home of Wrexham council, and it extends north-westwards to take in Wrexham General railway station, the main campus of Wrexham University, and the Racecourse Ground football stadium.

The Racecourse Ground is the home of Wrexham AFC, who have gained a new fanbase and success in recent years thanks to their ownership by US actors Rob McElhinney and Ryan Reynolds. The stadium also claims to be the oldest international football ground in the world. The Welsh national team played their first home match here in 1877, losing 2-0 to Scotland, and the Racecourse Ground has hosted more Wales matches through the years than any other venue. But these days the Cardiff City Stadium is the national football team's permanent home, and Wales' last match at the Racecourse was a low-profile friendly against Gibraltar in 2023.

The third Wrexham councillor departing for Cardiff Bay is Becca Martin, who last appeared in this column when she won a March 2021 by-election for Maesydre ward which lies immediately north-east of the city centre. It's fair to say that I didn't see that result coming: Plaid had not previously stood a candidate in Maesydre ward this century. Boundary changes for the 2022 election merged Maesydre with Acton ward to the north, creating a two-seat ward of Acton and Maesydre which split its representation between Plaid and Labour: Plaid Cymru polled 37% to 26% for Labour and 22% for an independent candidate.

The northern end of Acton and Maesydre ward is covered by the open space of Acton Park, which was once the grounds of Acton Hall before its demolition in the 1950s. Acton Hall was the birthplace of George Jeffreys, the notorious hanging judge who was James II's Lord Chancellor and presided over the "Bloody Assizes" of 1685. The park includes Britain's Tree of the Year for 2023, a sweet chestnut tree which is nearly 500 years old; also here is a stone circle, commemorating the visit of the National Eisteddfod to Wrexham in 1977.

Harper, Jones and Martin were a large chunk of the Plaid Cymru group on Wrexham council, which has a plurality of independent councillors. The 2022 elections here returned 23 independents, 14 Labour, 9 Conservatives, 9 Plaid and a Lib Dem councillor; the main independent group heads a coalition which also includes the Conservatives and the Lib Dem. All this is rather different to the 2024 Westminster election in the Wrexham constituency, which was a rather easy gain for Labour: the town's MP is Andrew Ranger, who has spent most of his career working in hospitality.

On the basis of the previous results Plaid Cymru might be most worried about Acton and Maesydre. Their defending candidate in that ward is Richard Everall, a long-serving former fireman who was also a Fire Brigades Union rep. Labour have selected Salli Edwards, who runs the Flintshire branch of Citizens Advice. We have an independent candidacy from Alex Jones, who was an independent candidate for the city's Erddig ward in 2017 and 2022. The Conservatives have selected Will Martin, Natasha Morris stands for the Greens, Reform UK's nominee is Jan Davey, and the Lib Dems' Beatrice Williams is last on the Acton and Maesydre ballot paper.

The Grosvenor by-election is defended for Plaid by first-time candidate Jonathan Billington. Here the Labour candidate is Brendan Murray who is a driving instructor. Beth Vaughan stands in Grosvenor ward for the Cosnervatives, Graham Bannister-Kelly for the Lib Dems, Gerald Craddock for the Lib Dems, former community councillor Philip Penk for Reform UK, Sam Regan - who owns and runs a hotel in the city - is standing here as an independent candidate, and Martine Richards makes a bit of electoral history as the first-ever council by-election candidate for Your Party.

For their defence of the Queensway by-election Plaid Cymru have decided to ignore the usual stereotypes by selecting Pol Wong, a 33rd-generation Shaolin monk who makes a living by teaching kung fu. Wong, whose father Jummy opened and ran the first Chinese restaurant in North Wales, was last seen on a ballot paper in 2017 when he stood in Ruabon ward. The Labour candidate Morgan Peters is a Caia Park community councillor who stood on the party's list in Gwynedd Maldwyn at the recent Senedd election. Also contesting Queensway ward are Geoffrey Sutton for the Conservatives, Robbie Green for Reform UK and Lucy Sutton for the Greens. Intending Conservative and Green voters should check carefully which Sutton they are voting for.

Gogarth Mostyn; and
Tudno

Conwy council, North Wales; caused respectively by the resignations of Louise Emery and Thomas Montgomery.

We'll finish for the week on the seaside, in the Costa Geriatrica of North Wales. Roughly at the centre of the North Wales coast we find the town of Llandudno, which lies on low ground between the twin headlands of the Little Orme and the Great Orme. The town has two beaches, one facing north into the Irish Sea and the other facing west into the mouth of the River Conwy.

Orme is a Norse name, an unusual feature in North Wales, which refers to a sea serpent and is from the same root which gave us the English word "worm". The craggy limestone of the Great Orme rises to a summit of 207 metres and it is classed as a Marilyn, a British peak which rises at least 150 metres above the surrounding landscape. Its highest point is occupied by a visitor centre, which was once a semaphore station passing messages between Holyhead and Liverpool. This is an excellent viewpoint for the Irish Sea, with views extending on a good day to the Isle of Man and the Lake District; theoretically the summit of Slieve Donard, 103 miles away in Northern Ireland, is also visible from here.

The summit complex is adjacent to the terminus of the Great Orme Tramway, which runs down the steep slope into the town centre. This is operated as two separate funicular railways, meaning that all passengers must change trams at the Halfway station. The lower section of the Tramway runs along public streets to reach its lower terminus.

Not far away from here are the remains of copper mines on the Great Orme which go all the way back to the Bronze Age: metallurgical analysis has demonstrated that during the period 1600 to 1400 BC the Great Orme was the main source of copper in Britain. Also up here on the headland we find a small church dedicated to St Tudno, an obscure saint who lived as a hermit on the Great Orme in the sixth century. This is the church of St Tudno which gives its name to the town of Llandudno below.

Llandudno as we know it today is a creation of the Victorians, who turned it from a copper-mining centre into a seaside resort, and the North Promenade in particular is wall-to-wall Victorian hotels. Behind those hotels we find the town centre, whose main shopping street is Mostyn Street which runs parallel to the North Promenade: this is named after Lord Mostyn, who still owns large chunks of Llandudno to this day.

Holidaymakers here back in the day included Alice Pleasance Liddell, the inspiration for Lewis Carroll's Alice novels. To mark this connection a series of statues of characters from Alice's Adventures in Wonderland and Through the Looking Glass have been erected in the town - this trend started with a depiction of the White Rabbit on Mostyn Street, which was unveiled by Llandudno's then MP David Lloyd George all the way back in 1933. The annual Victorian Extravaganza, which is normally held over the May bank holiday weekend so you've missed it for this year, is quite the experience.

The Gogarth Mostyn ward of Conwy covers Llandudno's town centre, the western shore and the Great Orme. This ward also includes the town's railway station, which lies at the end of a branch off the North Wales Coast line.

Immediately to the east of here is Tudno ward, where we find the theatre and conference centre of Venue Cymru on the North Promenade. This is the regular location for the Welsh Open snooker tournament, and it also hosts politicians from time to time. The Welsh Conservative conference was held at Venue Cymru in February, and Plaid Cymru have pencilled Llandudno in as the location for their next party conference in October.

The boundary between Tudno and Gogarth Mostyn wards runs along Vaughan Street, and the T-junction of Vaughan Street and the North Promenade marks the northern end of the A470 road which winds its merry way down the length of Wales to the Senedd building at Cardiff Bay. Today's by-elections in both of those wards, which between them cover nearly all of Llandudno town, are both caused by Conwy councillors being elected to Senedd Cymru in May. Louise Emery and Thomas Montgomery were respectively ranked second and third on the Reform UK list for the Clwyd constituency, which polled 32% of the vote and won three out of six seats.

Emery and Montgomery are both relatively recent recruits to Reform UK, and they were originally elected to Conwy council on the Conservative ticket. Louise Emery was first elected in 2017 for Gogarth ward which then covered the Great Orme and the west shore, gaining her seat from an independent councillor. Boundary changes for 2022 then merged Gogarth with the town-centre Mostyn ward, and the combined ward turned in a close result with 36% for Labour, 34% for the Conservatives and 30% for independent candidates; the three seats split one to Labour and two to the Conservatives. Thomas Montgomery was in his first term as a councillor for Tudno ward, which in 2022 voted 31% for Labour, 29% for the Conservatives, 25% for independent candidates and 16% for the Liberal Democrats; Labour had only stood one candidate, leaving Montgomery to win the second seat on the Conservative ticket. These results are unusually party political for Conwy council, where independent councillors are the largest group and lead the administration: the 2022 Conwy election returned 22 independent councillors, 11 Labour, 10 Conservatives, 7 Plaid Cymru (most of whom were elected from wards in the interior), 4 Lib Dems and a single Green councillor. These are the first by-elections of the present Conwy council term.

Llandudno is one of the major towns in the Westminster seat of Bangor Aberconwy, which was won in 2024 by Labour candidate Claire Hughes. For Senedd purposes that seat is combined with the Isle of Anglesey to form a rather undersized constituency of Bangor Conwy Môn: in May this constituency gave 45% and three seats to Plaid Cymru, 28% and two seats to Reform UK, and 12% and one seat to the Conservatives. Top of the Plaid Cymru list here was their party leader, and now the First Minister of Wales, Rhun ap Iorwerth. Local elections in Wales are not a particularly reliable indicator of party support, but it's fair to say that Plaid Cymru's strength in this constituency lies outside Llandudno. Neither of these by-elections has a Plaid candidate.

Whether this means that Reform UK can convert their defection gains into by-election gains remains to be seen. In Gogarth Mostyn the new Reform UK candidate is Gareth Probert, a Conwy town councillor who has recently defected to the party from the Conservatives. The Tories have selected Greg Robbins, who is trying to get back on Conwy council after losing his seat here in 2022: Robbins had represented the former Mostyn ward from 2017 to 2022, and he still sits on Llandudno town council and has served as mayor of the town more than once. He previously appeared in this column as the losing Conservative candidate for a Mostyn ward by-election in 2016. Labour's David Hawkins is a Llandudno town councillor for Mostyn ward, and he is married to this year's mayor Mandy Hawkins. There are no independent candidates this time, but Dave Coffin completes an all-male ballot for the Lib Dems.

Finally for this week, we come to the Tudno ward by-election where the Reform UK candidate is Alan Davies. The Tories will want their seat back and they have selected Michael Hall. Standing for Labour is Joanna Stallard, who was top of the Labour list for Bangor Conwy Môn in May; she also stood in the 2024 general election in Dwyfor Meirionnydd. Again there is no independent challenge this time. The Lib Dems are back with James Pethica, who gives an address in Rhos-on-Sea and runs a business selling climbing-related jewellery, and this time we also have an intervention from the Greens whose candidate is Joshua Morris.
