There are eighteen elections taking place on 18th June 2026, of which four (for a total of seven seats) are polls which were postponed from 7th May after a candidate died during the election campaign; eleven are ordinary by-elections, some of which deal with matters arising from May's local elections; and three are Parliamentary Specials, one of which might have an impact far larger than the usual filling of a casual vacancy. But we're not going to start with that one. In fulfilment of Andrew's Previews' promise to cover all the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order, let's go north of the Border:

Aberdeen South

House of Commons; caused by the resignation of Scottish National Party MP Stephen Flynn. In 1969, British Petroleum were drilling for gas in the North Sea, about 135 miles east of Aberdeen. Gas was already being exploited in British and Dutch waters of the North Sea to the south, and BP wanted to test a theory that the gas field extended further north. Their geologists were astonished when the well struck oil. This original discovery is now the Montrose oil field, and it is still going strong. Over the next few years many more oilfields were discovered under the harsh waters of the North Sea - Forties, Brent, Piper and so on - and the oil shocks of 1973 and 1979 made it commercially viable to develop them. To this day the Brent oilfield gives its name to the Brent Crude price for North Sea oil, although Brent itself is now exhausted and buying a barrel of Brent Crude will instead give you a blend of crude oil from other British and Norwegian fields. North Sea oilfields in British waters were developed by private enterprise, and their extraction turned the old city of Aberdeen into an economic boom town, the Oil Capital of Europe. Aberdeen has the largest and busiest seaport in north-east Scotland, and its airport is the main base for helicopters serving the oil rigs. But Aberdeen goes back a long way. The Port of Aberdeen has been here since at least 1136 when David I gave the city a royal charter, and Aberdeen had a floushing fishing and shipbuilding industry for centuries. Aberdeen harbour is still the embarkation point for the main ferry service to Shetland, with overnight sailings departing every evening. The nearby city centre is filled with 19th and 20th century buildings in grey granite, and Marischal College is the second-largest granite building in the world after the Escorial Palace in Madrid. The building is now occupied by the city council, but Marischal College was a university founded in 1593: it merged with King's College in 1860 to form the modern Aberdeen University. Aberdeen's second, more modern university is Robert Gordon which obtained its university status in 1992: its main campus is a few miles up the river Dee at Garthdee. The eponymous Robert Gordon had graduated from Marischal College before making his fortune as a merchant in the Baltic city of Danzig (modern-day Gdańsk), and on his death in 1731 he left his fortune to found a private school in the city from which the university eventually spun off. Two current MPs were pupils of the original Robert Gordon's College, including the former Labour Party chairman Dame Anneliese Dodds and the present Aberdeen North SNP MP Kirsty Blackman. The city of Aberdeen was promoted from one MP to two by the Third Reform Act in 1885, and the Aberdeen South constituency has existed ever since then. It has been subject to frequent boundary changes over the years, and only the areas of Ferryhill, Broomhill, Ashley and Seafield have been in the Aberdeen South constituency continuously since 1885. One major factor in boundary-drawing here over the decades has been the River Dee, which was the boundary between Aberdeenshire and Kincardineshire: the Torry district, despite being not far to the south of the city centre, was in a different county. So, as originally drawn, the Aberdeen South constituency of 1885 to 1950 (with minor boundary changes in 1918) lay entirely to the north of the Dee. It was centred on the city's West End, with the eastern boundary being the arrow-street George Street. In other words, this was Aberdeen's middle-class seat - and the comparison to Aberdeen North, which has only voted Consrvative once in its similarly long history (in 1935) is stark. Some of what are now the built-up western suburbs of the city such as Hazlehead, Airyhall and Garthdee were still open space at this time, and they had not yet been incorporated into Aberdeen: these areas were part of the seat of West Aberdeenshire until 1918 and Central Aberdeenshire thereafter. In 1885 the Kincardineshire parts of the present Aberdeen South - Kincorth, Torry and Cove - were part of the Kincardineshire constituency; in 1918 Torry was transferred to Aberdeen North, while Kincorth and Cove moved into a new seat of Kincardine and Aberdeenshire West. In this form Aberdeen South was a safe Liberal seat from 1885 to 1918, including in the 1886 general election when the seat was uncontested. For most of this period its MP was James Bryce, a noted scholar of history and law who held the Regius Chair of Civil Law at Oxford University from 1870 to 1893; in 1876 he was one of the first people since Noah to reach the summit of Mount Ararat. He had entered politics in 1880 as a Liberal MP for the Tower Hamlets constituency, transferring here in 1885 as a result of the boundary changes. In 1892 Bryce joined Gladstone's Cabinet as Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster, which under the rules of the time meant that he had to seek re-election to the House of Commons in a by-election: at the resulting first Aberdeen South by-election of 23rd August 1892 he was returned unopposed. Bryce subsequently served as president of the Board of Trade from 1894 until the Liberal government was defeated in 1895; when the Liberals returned to power under Campbell-Bannerman in 1905, Bryce rejoined the Cabinet as chief secretary for Ireland. James Bryce resigned from the Commons in 1907 to become British ambassador to the USA. The resulting second Aberdeen South by-election of 20th February 1907 was a much closer affair than previous elections here thanks to the intervention of the future TUC general secretary Fred Bramley as the candiate of the Aberdeen Labour Representation Committee. The Conservatives' Ronald McNeill, a barrister who had previously edited the London evening newspaper St James's Gazette and was also contributing to the Encyclopædia Britannica at the time, eventually lost by 367 votes to the new Liberal candidate George Esslemont. A rematch in January 1910 saw Esslemont increase his majority to 2,316; McNeill's subsequent government career (he was Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster from 1927 to 1929) came from seats elsewhere. George Esslemont resigned from the Commons in 1917, a few months before his early death at the age of 57. The resulting third Aberdeen South by-election of 3rd April 1917 was not contested by the Conservatives thanks to the wartime electoral truce, but that didn't stop the Liberals bickering over their candidate. They eventually settled on Sir John Fleming, a timber businessman who had been Lord Provost of Aberdeen in 1898-1902 and was the first holder of that title to act as Lord-Lieutenant of Aberdeen when the city was made a county of itself. Sir John was a younger brother of Robert Fleming, who went into merchant banking and became an extremely wealthy man, and he was a great-uncle of the author Ian Fleming. He won the by-election easily against competition from two independent candidates: second place went to James Watson, a chemistry professor at Anderson's College in Glasgow who had previously been associated with the Independent Labour Party, while the women's suffrage campaigner Frederick Petrick-Lawrence (who was very briefly Leader of the Opposition during the Second World War coalition government) performed very badly on a platform of "peace by negotiation". With peace won instead through the surrender of Germany, the 1918 general election marked a step-change in Aberdeen South's politics. Sir John Fleming stood for re-election, but he didn't get the coalition government's coupon which instead went to the Unionist candidate Frederick Thomson. Thomson was a barrister from the Leith family which had founded the Ben Line shipping company, which is still in operation today as a shipping agency based in Singapore; he had fought in the First World War in Egypt and in Salonika, where he had been severely wounded. He defeated Fleming by over 7,000 votes, with independent James Watson standing again and saving his deposit. Frederick Thomson went on to successfully navigate the turbulent political years of the 1920s as the Unionist MP for what quickly became a safe seat, particularly after Labour took over second place in Aberdeen South in 1922. In April 1923 he was appointed as Solicitor General for Scotland and got the title of KC to go with it, but from 1924 onwards he was a Conservative whip. In 1929 Thomson was created a baronet. Sir Frederick Thomson died in 1935 at the age of 59. For the resulting fourth Aberdeen South by-election of 21st May 1935 the Unionists selected his son Sir Douglas Thomson, 2nd Baronet, as their defending candidate, and he romped to victory by almost a 2 to 1 margin over Labour's Joseph Duncan who was secretary of the Scottish Farm Servants' Union. Douglas Thomson had previously rowed for Oxford in the 1926 and 1927 Boat Races, finishing on the losing side on both occasions. The Unionist majority over Labour here was cut to 1,816 votes by the Attlee landslide of 1945, so when Sir Douglas Thomson resigned the following year the fifth Aberdeen South by-election, scheduled for 26th November 1946, proved to be a close contest. The Labour challenger was Arthur Irvine, a barrister who had made several previous attempts to get into Parliament: he would eventually win a by-election in Liverpool the following year and served as Solicitor General in the second Wilson government. The Unionist defence was led by Aberdeen South's first female candidate. Priscilla Grant was only 31 but she was already a widow after her first husband, Grenadier Guards officer Sir Arthur Grant, had been killed in action in 1944. Lady Grant had stood in Aberdeen North in the 1945 general election and got nowhere; but Aberdeen South proved to be a much easier proposition, and Grant defeated Irvine by 3,839 votes. In 1948 Priscilla Grant remarried. Her second husband was Johnnie Buchan, who was the son of the author John Buchan and had inherited the title of Lord Tweedsmuir - and a seat in the House of Lords - from him. Baroness Tweedsmuir went on to have a long parliamentary career, which included winning what was dubbed the "Battle of the Housewives" in 1955: in a rare all-female candidate list, that year her Labour opponent was a young Judith Hart. Her tenure in Aberdeen South also took in an expansion of the seat in 1950, when boundary changes brought Torry, Kincorth and some new western suburbs of Aberdeen into the seat while moving most of the city centre into Aberdeen North. It took one of the titans of Scottish political history to finally lever Priscilla Buchan out of office. Donald Dewar had first contested Aberdeen South in the 1964 general election, just after he had finished his law degree at Glasgow University. He cut Lady Tweedsmuir's majority to 3,898 votes on that occasion, and Dewar - then aged 29 - finished the job in the 1966 Wilson landslide to become the first Labour MP for Aberdeen South with a majority of 1,799. This small majority proved to be a false start for Donald Dewar's parliamentary career: he held Aberdeen South for only one term and didn't get back into the Commons until 1978. In the 1970 general election Aberdeen South reverted to the Conservatives, with Iain Sproat defeating Dewar by 1,089 votes. Sproat would go on to win four terms of office here, often on knife-edge majorities: his re-elections in October 1974 and 1979 (when the Labour candidate was future Greenock MP Norman Godman) were both with under 1,000 votes. The 1983 redistribution saw major changes to the Aberdeen South constituency. In came the city centre and Pittodrie from Aberdeen North; out went the suburbs of Kincorth and Garthdee, which were transferred into a rural seat called Kincardine and Deeside. The BBC/ITN estimates were that this reduced the Conservative majority in Aberdeen South from 772 votes to 541. Iain Sproat decided to do the chicken run and he sought re-election in Roxburgh and Berwickshire, which proved to be a disastrous decision for him: not only did Sproat lose in his new seat, but Aberdeen South returned the new Conservative candidate. Gerry Malone was elected to Parliament at his fifth attempt with an increased Conservative majority of 3,581 votes thanks to an even vote split between Labour and the SDP. Malone's four previous failed candidates had including losing the 1982 Glasgow Hillhead by-election to Roy Jenkins. (Sproat, meanwhile, did not return to the Commons until 1992 when he was elected for Harwich.) Gerry Malone was appointed to the Whips office in 1986 towards the end of what proved to be his only term as MP for Aberdeen South. In 1987 he lost his seat to Labour by 1,198 votes, and he subsequently turned up as MP for Winchester from 1992 to 1997. Malone lost his seat in Winchester in May 1997 by just two votes, went to the Election Court which voided the result for uncertainty and ordered a re-run, and he then lost the resulting Winchester by-election in November 1997 by 21,556 votes. The electorate tend not to like sore losers. Malone's successor as MP for Aberdeen South was Labour's Frank Doran, a solicitor who only served one term in the seat after his election in 1987. In 1992 Aberdeen South was the only seat (excluding by-election regains) which the Conservatives gained from Labour against the national trend, with former teacher Raymond Robertson defeating Doran by 1,517 votes. The 1997 redistribution reflected the oil boom and population growth in Aberdeen by granting three seats to the city for the first time. The new seat was Aberdeen Central, which took the city centre and the West End from Aberdeen South; it was projected to be a Labour seat, and Frank Doran successfully returned to the Commons there. In compensation Aberdeen South regained Garthdee and also extended up the Dee valley to take in the villages of Milltimber and Peterculter from the Kincardine and Deeside seat. The Kincardine and Deeside had been gained by the Liberal Democrats' Nicol Stephen in a 1991 by-election before the Tories took it back in 1992, and partly because of this the Rallings and Thrasher estimates had the Lib Dems in second place in the redrawn Aberdeen South. Which is what happened in 1997 - except they were in second place behind Labour. The outgoing Conservative MP Raymond Robertson polled 26% and finished in third place; Nicol Stephen placed second with 28%, and the winner with 35% of the vote was Labour's Anne Begg. As in many other places across the UK, 1997 was a result which broke the mould in this seat. Dame Anne Begg, as she became in 2011, went on to win four terms of office as MP for Aberdeen South, becoming by far the longest-serving of the seat's three Labour MP. And Begg didn't let her physical disability stop her from pursuing elected politics or her previous teaching career. She suffers from Gaucher's disease, a genetic degenerative condition, and she has been a wheelchair user since 1984. We can see from elections to the Scottish Parliament that there was nothing inevitable about Begg's wins: the Aberdeen South constituency there was represented by the Lib Dems' Nicol Stephen from 1999 to 2011. The establishment of the Scottish Parliament led to a redistribution of Scotland's Westminster seats in 2005. Before devolution Scotland had been over-represented in the House of Commons, and the 2005 boundary changes brought the size of its constituencies into line with those in England by cutting the number of Scottish MPs from 72 to 59. This led to the abolition of the Aberdeen Central constituency after only eight years, and some of its territory returned to Aberdeen South. It took the SNP landslide of 2015 to sweep Dame Anne Begg away. She was defeated in 2015 by the Scottish National Party's Callum McCaig, who jumped from fourth place at the previous election to win the seat by over 7,000 votes. McCaig had started his political career early, being elected as an Aberdeen city councillor for Kincorth/Loirston ward in 2007 and then taking over as leader of the city council in 2011. He was 26 at the time. McCaig resigned his seat on Aberdeen council after the election, and the resulting by-election was held by the SNP's Stephen Flynn - who I described at the time as "McCaig's parliamentary assistant" - with a massive 61% of the first-preference vote. But if Callum McCaig thought he was in for a long political career, he was wrong. The snap general election in June 2017 saw the Conservatives make gains in Scotland, and one of them was Aberdeen South. McCaig lost his seat to the Conservatives' Ross Thomson by 4,752 votes. He is now the SNP's chief executive, trying to pick up the shattered pieces left behind by the embezzlement conviction of the party's long-serving former chief executive Peter Murrell. Ross Thomson had also started his political career early. He first stood for election at the age of 19 as the Conservative candidate for Coatbridge and Chryston in the 2007 Holyrood elections, and in 2012 he was elected to Aberdeen city council as a councillor for Hazlehead/Ashley/Queens Cross ward. In 2016 Thomson was elected to the Scottish Parliament as a regional MSP for the North East region, and he stood down from Aberdeen council at the May 2017 election. He then resigned his Holyrood seat shortly afterwards upon being elected to Westminster. During his time in Holyrood Ross Thomson was one of the few MSPs who supported a Leave vote in the 2016 EU membership referendum (which was ten years ago next week). He was a strong supporter of Brexit in a Parliament which was dominated by that single issue, and he supported Boris Johnson in the 2019 leadership election. But if Ross Thomson thought he was in for a long political career, he was wrong. His time at Westminster ended with his constituency party refusing to support him when allegations of sexual misconduct in Parliament's Strangers Bar were made: this effectively forced Thomson into a last-minute retirement when the 2019 general election was called. Ross Thomson subsequently joined Reform UK and there was some speculation that he might be their candidate in this by-election, but the latest news is that he has flounced out of the party after failing to get their nomination for an Aberdeenshire council by-election in Peterhead next month. The beneficiary of Ross Thomson's fall from grace in December 2019 was the SNP's Stephen Flynn, who as previously stated had been office manager for and had taken over the Aberdeen council seat vacated by Callum McCaig. He defeated the replacement Conservative candicate Douglas Lumsden, the co-leader of Aberdeen council, by 3,990 votes. Flynn then resigned his council seat in Kincorth/Nigg/Cove ward and a by-election to replace him was scheduled for May 2020, but this had to be called off as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic: his seat eventually remained vacant until November 2020, when the SNP easily held the resulting by-election. Stephen Flynn took over in 2022 as leader of the SNP's Westminster group, giving him the speaking rights afforded to the leader of the third-largest party in the Commons. In July 2024 Stephen Flynn lost those rights to the Lib Dem leader Sir Ed Davey but was still easily re-elected in his own seat, with a redrawn Aberdeen South constituency - now again including the city centre and Pittodrie - giving him 33% of the vote to 25% for Labour and 24% for the Conservatives. This was a numerical majority of 3,758 votes over Labour. The relationship between Westminster and Holyrood constituencies was decoupled by the 2005 Westminster redistribution, and the former Aberdeen South Holyrood seat has taken a different turn. As stated above, it was held by the Lib Dems' Nicol Stephen from 1999 until he retired in 2011. In that year the Holyrood seat was redrawn with the new name of Aberdeen South and North Kincardine, and it has had SNP representation since then. Maureen Watt (who is the daughter and mother of former SNP MPs) took the seat over in 2011 after five years as a regional MSP for North East Scotland. She passed it on in 2021 to Audrey Nicoll, who had won a November 2019 Aberdeen council by-election in Torry/Ferryhill ward; I described Nicoll then as "a part-time lecturer and retired police officer". Her husband Alex is leader of the SNP group on Aberdeen council. Nicoll stood down at last month's Holyrood election and the SNP nomination in a renamed seat of Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine went to then-MP Stephen Flynn. Flynn was elected fairly narrowly, polling 34% against 31% for the former Scottish Conservative deputy leader Liam Kerr and 18% for Reform UK's Duncan Massey; Kerr and Massey were both returned as regional MSPs for North East Scotland, at the top of their respective party lists. Under the terms of the Scotland Act 1998 as amended, once Stephen Flynn was elected to Holyrood he was obliged to resign his Westminster seat within 49 days or be disqualified from the Scottish Parliament. The pro-independence majority in Holyrood is not a reflection of the votes which were cast last month, and is entirely due to the SNP's overhang in the constituency seats. Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine is one seat where the Scottish Greens' withdrawal from the constituency vote might have made a crucial difference, because the Conservatives carried that seat in the Scottish Parliament regional vote with 26%; the SNP had 25%, Reform UK 19% and Labour 9%. The 2,671 votes or 8% which went to the Greens in the regional vote is comfortably more than Stephen Flynn's constituency vote majority of 1,244. Mind, the Conservatives probably shouldn't look on that result from Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine and conclude that they are about to win this Westminster by-election. The Westminster seat does not include the North Kincardine ward of Aberdeenshire, where the Conservatives are strong, and it takes in large parts of the Aberdeen Central Holyrood seat where the Tories were fifth in the regional vote this year. The last local elections in Scotland were back in May 2022. If we add together the votes cast then for the six wards wholly in this constituency, we see that the SNP topped the poll with 29% of the first preferences, the Conservatives polled 23%, the Lib Dems and Labour scored 16% each, and 10% of the votes were cast for independent candidates. This translated into eight SNP councillors, five Conservatives, four Labour, three Lib Dems and two independents. The Aberdeen South constituency also includes part of Midstocket/Rosemount ward, which the SNP carried in 2022. There is currently one vacant seat in the city-centre George St/Harbour ward where the Lib Dem councillor has resigned, and the Aberdeen returning officer had already scheduled a by-election to fill that seat for 25th June before Stephen Flynn's resignation came through. Westminster elections and Scottish local elections can't be combined because of the voter ID rules which apply to Westminster elections, so the electors of George St/Harbour ward will have to come back and do this all over again next week. And I'm going to have to write something different about the place. Before then we have a Westminster by-election to deal with. Defending for the SNP from the bottom of the alphabetical ballot paper is Richard Thomson, who is hoping to return to elected politics after losing his Westminster seat in 2024. Thomson had represented the Gordon constituency in the 2019-24 Parliament, narrowly losing the successor seat of Gordon and Buchan to the Conservatives in 2024; before then he was an Aberdeenshire councillor from 2012 to 2020, representing Ellon and District ward and serving as leader of Aberdeenshire council from 2015 to 2017. Since 2024 Thomson had been working in public affairs. He had applied without success for the SNP nomination for Angus North and Mearns in the recent Holyrood election, but maybe this will be a consolation prize? Starting in second place and hoping for more is the Labour candidate Nurul Ali, who is an Aberdeen city councillor for Bridge of Don ward (which is not part of this constituency). Ali was the Labour candidate for Gordon and Buchan in the 2024 Westminster election. The Conservatives' Douglas Lumsden is straight back on the campaign trail after securing re-election to the Scottish Parliament in May. Lumsden was previously the Conservative candidate for this seat in 2019; in 2021 he was elected as a North East Scotland regional MSP, and he retained that seat last month after narrowly missing out on a constituency seat in Aberdeenshire East. Two other parties saved their deposits here in 2024. Reform UK, who finished fourth here in 2024 with 6.9%, have selected Jo Hart: she stood in May in the Aberdeenshire West constituency and was in fourth place on the Reform UK list for North East Scotland. The Lib Dems' Mel Sullivan is also straight back on the campaign trail after her fourth-place finish in Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine last month, which is better than the fifth-place 6.3% the party managed in 2024. Sullivan, who was also ranked fourth on the Lib Dem list for North East Scotland, is an Aberdeenshire councillor for North Kincardine ward which borders this constituency. Of the three parties or candidates who lost their deposit here in 2024, one is back for another go: the Scottish Greens' Jorg Shelton-Eckstein is hoping to improve on his party's 3.5% and sixth place from two years ago. And one new party has entered the fray: David Ballantine, who gives an address in Edinburgh and was the Brexit Party candidate for Edinbrugh South West in 2019, is standing on the ticket of the Alliance for Democracy and Freedom. He completes a ballot paper of seven candidates. The list of polling stations for this by-election includes St Mary's Cathedral (Roman Catholic), St Andrew's Cathedral (Scottish Episcopal Church) and, for those whose religion is football, Pittodrie Stadium where electors from the Pittodrie North polling district are invited to cast their vote in the Sir Alex Ferguson Lounge. The returning officer has successfully managed to avoid this by-election clashing with Scotland's World Cup games, and once the polls close the challenge will be on to see whether we can get a result earlier in the night than Haiti v Scotland last weekend. This is a Westminster by-election, so all voters are reminded to bring photo ID with them to the polling station. Arbroath and Broughty Ferry

House of Commons; caused by the resignation of Scottish National Party MP Stephen Gethins. Our second Scottish National Party defence of the week occurs in the ground zero for Scottish nationalism. The date is 6th April 1320, the scene is the abbey at Aberbrothock on the east coast of Scotland. King Robert the Bruce is still fighting the English invaders under Edward II, and he needs to keep his friends outwith Scotland on side. Which is a problem, because the world's most powerful political figure is an septuagenarian who wants the war to end and is prepared to make dire threats to the leader of the smaller country in order to bring peace about. (Nothing like that could happen today, of course.) And 76-year-old Pope John XXII has recently made good on that threat by excommunicating King Robert. In response to this, the Scottish Parliament met at Aberbrothock and put together three letters to be sent to the Pope in Avignon: one from Robert himself, one from the bishops, and one from the nobles. The last letter has survived. Composed (probably) by Abbot Bernard of Kilwinning and written in Latin, as was normal for learned people of the day, the Declaration of Arbroath made the case that Scotland was a proud, independent kingdom which occupied not only the literal but also the moral high ground. It asserted that the English had committed atrocities and their attacks on Scotland were unjust. There was also a hint that Scotland, if supported, might help out the Pope in a war which he wanted to wage against Muslim states in the Middle East. And there needed to be a defence of King Robert's previous behaviour in coming to the Scottish throne by deposing King John Balliol and then murdering John Comyn III, so the Declaration included text to the effect that the independence of Scotland was the prerogative of the Scottish people and that Balliol had broken his contract with them by failing to defend the country against the English. This was probably not intended as a statement of popular sovereignty, which did not exist in the mediaeval period when European politics was a literal game of thrones. We can't be sure what influence this argument had on John XXII, but the Declaration of Arbroath as a whole found favour: Robert the Bruce's excommunication was lifted. Seven centuries on, the De Arbroath Abbey now lies in ruins, but the town itself continues to thrive. Arbroath has been a royal burgh since 1599, and the former Town House on the High Street which was home to the burgh council still has legal significance: genuine Arbroath Smokies can only be produced within an 8-kilometre radius of the Town House. Smokies, which originated in the tiny fishing village of Auchmithie to the east of Arbroath, are salted haddock cooked over a very hot and smoky fire: there are several firms in the harbour area which still produce this local delicacy. The town's economy was traditionally based on the harbour, together with textiles: there were a number of jute mills here, and Arbroath was once a major manufacturer of sailcloth. Jute, together with jam and journalism, were the traditional industries of the city of Dundee further along the coast. The profits from the jute mills accrued to a number of "jute barons", who showed off their wealth by building massive houses in the fishing village of Broughty Ferry not far to the east of Dundee. By the turn of the 20th century, Broughty Ferry ranked among the richest suburbs in Europe. It became an independent burgh in 1864, and maintained that status until it was annexed by Dundee in 1913. Despite the name, the ferry (which connected the town with Tayport, on the Fife side of the Firth of Tay) no longer runs: its business was taken long ago by the Tay Bridges to the west. In between Arbroath and Broughty Ferry we find this constituency's third town. Carnoustie is another old burgh based on the textile industry, but there ie another side to its economy: tourism, particularly that driven by the sport of golf. Carnoustie Golf Links is one of the courses on the Open Championship rota and has hosted the tournament eight times, most recently in 2018 when Francesco Molinari was the winner. The course is noted for its difficulty, and the events of the 1999 Open Championship - when the French golfer Jean van de Velde notoriously blew a three-shot lead on the final hole, and subsequently lost a playoff to local favourite Paul Lawrie - still live long in the memory. Golf is not yet on the Commonwealth Games programme, but Carnoustie was nonetheless involved in the 2014 Glasgow Commonwealth Games - it hosted the shooting events. These were held at the Barry Buddon training area, a Ministry of Defence rifle range which occupies most of the extensive system of sand dunes between Carnoustie and Monifieth. The military also have a presence at Arbroath, which hosts the Condor training area for the Royal Marines. All of these towns are linked together by the A92 dual carriageway and by the railway line from Dundee to Aberdeen. Arbroath is the main railhead for the area, with express trains to England as well as the usual ScotRail services; the Aberdeen portion of the Highland Sleeper to and from London calls both there and at Carnoustie. Broughty Ferry is recognised by ScotRail as Scotland's oldest railway station still in operation, having opened in October 1838. Many of the intermediate stops are sparsely served, and Golf Street and Barry Links stations (which lie to the west of Carnoustie) see just three trains a day and regularly feature near the top of the annual list of the UK's least-used railway stations. The towns and the large rural hinterland within the Arbroath and Broughty Ferry constituency were all within the traditional county of Angus, which takes its name from an 8th-century king of the Picts. But the county of Angus wasn't always called that. The name dates in law from the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1947, which consolidated Scottish local government law of the time; the county council had previously resolved to use the name Angus in 1928. Before then, the county of Angus - and the parliamentary seat based on it, which went back at least as far the Union of 1707 - was known as Forfarshire. The First Reform Act of 1832 took the Forfarshire towns of Arbroath, Brechin, Forfar and Montrose out of the Forfarshire seat: those four burghs, plus the Kincardineshire burgh of Inverbervie, then formed the disconnected Montrose Burghs constituency. The Montrose Burghs was a strongly Liberal constituency almost throughout its existence, and the Conservatives rarely even contested it. MPs of some strain of Liberalism held the seat continously from 1859 to 1930. In fact, in the 1885 general election - held on a reformed and expanded franchise - the Liberal vote here was so strong that the top two candidates in the final reckoning were a Liberal and an Independent Liberal. The official Liberal John Shiress Will, a barrister, was the winner with a shade under 50% of the vote, and he was easily re-elected in 1886, 1892 and 1895 against opposition from the Liberal Unionists. In 1896 John Shiress Will resigned his seat in the House of Commons to allow a return to the Commons for John Morley, a former Cabinet minister who had served in the third and fourth Gladstone administrations as Chief Secretary for Ireland until he lost his seat in Newcastle upon Tyne at the 1895 general election. Morley increased the Liberal majority at the resulting Montrose Burghs by-election of 22nd February 1895, and in time he was back on the Cabinet frontline: when the Liberals returned to power in 1905 he became India secretary. In between, Morley was one of the first 12 people appointed to the Order of Merit on its creation in 1902. The Order of Merit is, of course, unique in the British honours system in that it is awarded for merit. When Asquith came to power in 1908 he kept Morley in post as India secretary but elevated him to the House of Lords, as the first and only Viscount Morley of Blackburn. The resulting Montrose Burghs by-election of 12th May 1908 was won by Robert Harcourt, the son of the former Liberal Party leader Sir William Harbourt, who had previously worked in the Foreign Office and in journalism; second place in that by-election went to an Independent Labour Party candidate. The more rural Forfarshire seat - which at this time took in Carnoustie and the fast-growing Broughty Ferry - was also Liberal-inclined during the period 1885 to 1918. It had been held since an 1872 by-election by James Barclay, a large landowner and farmer in the county who was living proof of the old adage that where there's muck there's brass: he was a manure merchant. He won his fourth term of office very easily in 1885, but when the Liberal Party split a year later over the issue of Irish home rule Barclay joined the breakaway Liberal Unionists. He was narrowly re-elected on that ticket in 1886 with a majority of 407 votes, before losing his seat back to the official Liberals by 866 votes in 1892. The winning Liberal candidate on that occasion was John Rigby, a noted lawyer who was making a return to the Commons after some years away: he had represented Wisbech from 1885 to 1886. Gladstone immediately appointed Rigby to the frontbench as Solicitor General for England and Wales, with a knighthood to go with it: this meant he had to seek re-election to the Commons, and nobody opposed him in the resulting Forfarshire by-election of 24th August 1892. Sir John Rigby's legal career then blossomed: in 1894 he was promoted to Attorney General, and later that year he resigned from the Commons to become a Court of Appeal judge. The resulting Forfarshire by-election of 17th November 1894 saw a poor choice of candidate from the defending Liberals, who selected London-based stockbroker Henry Robson over a well-known local candidate. This created an opening for the Conservative candidate Charles Ramsay, the youngest son of the 12th Earl of Dalhousie, who had inherited a large amount of land in the county. In an upset result, Ramsay gained the seat with a majority of 286 votes; the large agricultural labourers' vote in the county was thought to have been decisive in this result. For the 1895 general election the Forfarshire Liberals reversed their mistake and selected the well-known local candidate who had been pasesd over for the 1894 by-election. Martin White, who defated Ramsay by 441 votes, was a Dundee businessman whose wealth enabled him to pursue his interests. As well as politics, White was a skilled player of the pipe organ; he funded academic work in the relatively new discipline of sociology; and installed he Scotland's first domestic hydropower plant to power his baronial castle at Balruddery, north-west of Dundee. White resigned his seat in the Commons in November 1896 in rather murky circumstances. Officially he fell out with his constituency party after a story falsely reporting his resignation had appeared in the Dundee Advertiser; the backstory is that he had had an affair for some time with a woman called Helen Grant and had got her pregnant, and she was suing him for breaching a promise to marry her. They eventually settled the case out of court without the public or the press noticing. The resulting Forfarshire by-election of 30th January 1897 provided a route back into the Commons for John Sinclair, a former Army officer who had served in the Gordon relief expedition in Sudan in the 1880s. Sinclair had previously been the Liberal MP for Dunbartonshire from 1892 until he lost his seat in 1895, and he had been a London county councillor before that. In the 1897 Forfarshire by-election he defeated the Conservatives' Charles Ramsay by 458 votes, with no swing at all from the 1895 result. Ramsay then went off to the Yukon Territory to look for gold in the Klondike, presumably without success: he was back for another go in the 1900 general election, but narrowly lost to Sinclair again. John Sinclair had worked closely in Parliament with the Liberal leader Sir Henry Campbell-Bannerman, and when the Liberals came to power in 1905 Campbell-Bannerman appointed him to the Cabinet as Scottish Secretary. Sinclair used this position to push through a number of reforms, including allowing women to stand in Scottish local government elections for the first time: in November 1907 Lavinia Malcolm became Scotland's first female councillor in Dollar, Clackmannenshire. In 1909 Sinclair was promoted to the House of Lords, taking the hereditary title of Lord Pentland; he then left these shores in 1912 to become Governor of Madras. The resulting Forfarshire by-election of 27th February 1909 pitted two lawyers against each other, with the Conservatives' Robert Blackburn (who would go on to become a Court of Session judge) being easily defeated by the new Liberal candidate James Falconer. Falconer went on to be easily re-elected in both 1910 elections. Once the First World War was over the the Forfarshire constituency was reduced in size at the 1918 general election, reflecting that Broughty Ferry had by now been annexed by Dundee. This might have been important in the Unionist gain of Forfarshire that year by William Shaw, a stockbroker who had served in the Royal Army Service Corps during the war; there were also rumours that Shaw had received the coalition government's coupon, although this was not in fact the case. James Falconer got his seat back in 1922 and round 3 of Falconer v Shaw was also a Liberal win in 1923. But when Falconer lost his seat in 1924 that pretty much ended the Liberal challenge in Forfarshire. The 1924 election here was a big win for the Unionist candidate Sir Harry Hope, a farmer and agriculturalist who had previously sat for Buteshire from January 1910 to 1918 and then for West Stirlingshire from 1918 to 1922. Hope retired at the 1931 election and William Shaw then retured as the MP for Forfarshire, winning the 1931 and 1935 elections with large majorities. William Shaw finally retired from the Commons in 1945 and assed the Forfarshire seat on to the new Unionist candidate Simon Ramsay, who had served in the Black Watch during the Second World War: he had fought in the Allied invasion of Sicily and won the Military Cross. Ramsay spent much of the 1945-50 Parliament as a Conservative whip. He retired from the Commons in 1950, shortly before he inherited the title of Earl of Dalhousie from his brother. Meanwhile the Dundee constituency, which included Broughty Ferry from 1918, was a two-seat borough during this period. In 1918 it easily re-elected its incumbent MPs, Labour's Alexander Wilkie and the National Liberal candidate Winston Churchill. Yes, that Winston Churchill: at this point he was Minister of Munitions and he was in the National Liberal faction associated with the Prime Minister David Lloyd George. Churchill had received the Coalition government's coupon, and both Churchill and Wilkie were endorsed by the Dundee branch of the Unionist Party which had stood down in their favour. In the 1918-22 Parliament Churchill returned to the Cabinet, first as War and Air Secretary, then as Colonial Secretary from 1921. Winston Churchill leaves our story in 1922, when the Dundee constituency returned a unique combination of candidates. Top of the poll was Edwin Scrymgeour, the only MP ever elected for the single-issue Scottish Prohibition Party; while the Labour seat was retained by E D Morel, a journalist who had spent most of his career exposing human rights abuses in the Belgian Congo; Churchill finished in fourth place, behind his National Liberal running-mate David Macdonald, and lost his seat. Morel suffered a fatal heart attack in 1924, and the resulting Dundee by-election of 22nd December 1924 was held for Labour by former MP Tom Johnston who had lost his seat in West Stirlingshire at the previous general election. In 1929 Johnston went back to West Stirlingshire, and Dundee's Labour seat was retained by solicitor and Edinburgh councillor Michael Marcus. Marcus and Edwin Scrymgeour were both ejected by the voters of Dundee in the 1931 National Government landslide. Again Dundee split its two seats, but this time between the Liberals and the Unionists. The Liberal MP was a very young Dingle Foot, who had recently been called to the bar and who joined his father Isaac Foot (re-elected for Bodmin) in the Commmon; the Unionist MP was Florence Horsburgh MBE (as she was then) who became Dundee's first ever female politician, as no women had ever been elected to the city council before then. Horsburgh holds the distinction of being the first MP ever to be interviewed for television, in 1936. The unusual double act of Foot and Horsburgh represented Dundee until 1945, when they lost thair seats to Labour who won both of Dundee's seats in that year for the first time. Top of the poll here in 1945 was Thomas Cook, a Lanarkshire man who had got into politics via the co-operative movement; his running-mate was John Strachey, a journalist who had previously served as MP for Birmingham Aston in the 1929-31 Parliament and who had been closely associated at that time with Oswald Mosley, before Mosley went fascist. Strachey had spent much of the war in the RAF as a public relations officer, and he immediately joined the government ranks as under-secretary of state for war; in 1946 he took on the difficult task of Minister for Food in those years of rationing. The Montrose Burghs constituency was rather more settled in terms of its MPs during the inter-war years, but the fact that all of them were elected on a ticket with the word "Liberal" in it masks a rather hard swing to the right. Robert Harcourt stood down in 1918 and his seat was easliy retained by new Liberal candidate and newspaperman John Sturrock. Sturrock was re-elected rather more narrowly in 1922 and 1923, contesting the first of those elections on the National Liberal ticket. In 1924 John Sturrock stood down and was replaced as Liberal MP for Montrose Burghs by Sir Robert Hutchison, who had come to politics from a glittering career as an Army officer which went all the way back to the Boer War: Hutchison retired from the Army in 1923 with the rank of major-general and a DSO, and he was mentioned in despatches six times. He then went straight into politics as MP for the Kirkcaldy Burghs in 1922, but lost that seat in 1923 to Labour's Tom Kennedy; the Montrose Burghs proved to be a safer berth. For most of his time as MP here Hutchison was in the whips office, serving from 1926 to 1930 as Liberal chief whip. He then defected to the National Liberals, the breakaway Liberal group which supported the National Government in 1931, and he was re-elected that year on their ticket. Sir Robert Hutchison was elevated to the peerage in 1932, as the first and only Lord Hutchison of Montrose. The resulting Montrose Burghs by-election of 28th June 1932 was contested by the former Kirkcaldy Burghs Labour MP Tom Kennedy, who came close to winning but ultimately lost by 933 votes to the new Liberal National candidate Charles Kerr, who was very much in the same mould as Hutchison. Kerr was a former Horse Guards officer with a DSO and a Military Cross to his name, and he ended up as the National Liberals' chief whip. Charles Kerr was was elevated to the peerage in 1940, as the first Lord Teviot, and the resulting Montrose Burghs by-election of 5th July 1940 was won without a contest (thanks to the wartime political truce) by the new National Liberal candidate John Maclay, who had been selected to stand for the party here in the 1939 or 1940 general election that never took place. Maclay had been on the winning Cambridge team in the 1927 Boat Race - defeating Sir Douglas Thomson, who we met in the Aberdeen South Preview above. He was safely re-elected in 1945 and spent most of the 1945-50 Parliament leading the National Liberal group in the Commons. There were major boundary changes here in 1950, as the two-seat burgh of Dundee was divided into two constituencies and the Montrose Burghs constitueny was abolished. Broughty Ferry was now located within the single-member constituency of Dundee East, while Arbroath, Carnoustie and Monifieth became part of the new seat of Angus South - reflecting the county's name change from Forfarshire. This seat pattern remained little changed until 2005, although the rural seat was renamed as Angus East in 1983, before becoming simply Angus in 1997. Hillingdon West

Hillingdon council, London; postponed from 7th May following the death of Reform UK candidate Sean Cooling. We now move into Greater London with the fourth postponed poll of the week, which is very much in Outer London. Hillingdon West ward lies to the east of Uxbridge town centre and is centred on the open space of Dowding Park, whose name is a clue to the area's history. It's a reference to Air Chief Marshal Hugh Dowding, who was the commander of RAF Fighter Command during the 1940 Battle of Britain. Dowding Park lies on the site occupied until 2010 by RAF Uxbridge, which during the war years was the home of 11 Group RAF who were responsible for the aerial defence of London. The main operations room was underground, and it is now open to the public as a museum: the Battle of Britain Bunker has been restored in recent years with funding from central government and from Hillingdon council, which runs the place. Not far away from the bunker is the 19th-century mansion of Hillingdon House, whose grounds all this once were. Redevelopment work continues around the area, and Hillingdon council have plans to turn some derelict former RAF housing on the western side of the park into an extension of Uxbridge town centre. Hillingdon council is run by the Conservatives, who have successfully defended their majority in this election. The 2022 result here was quite close with 30 Conservatives and 23 Labour councillors; this time round the Tories have won 30 seats against 16 Labour, 4 Reform UK and 1 Green, with the two seats in Hillingdon West ward to come. The election here was postponed after Sean Cooling, who was to have been a Reform UK candidate for the ward, died during the election campaign. The Conservatives look well set to increase their council majority in the final reckoning. Hillingdon West was a safe Conservative ward when it was created for the 2022 election, with the Tories defeating Labour here on that occasion by 55-34. Before 2022 this area was mostly part of the larger Uxbridge North ward, which always voted Conservative in local elections. Nonetheless Labour might want to see how much of a fight they can put up here, because this ward is part of the Uxbridge and South Ruislip parliamentary seat whose Labour MP Danny Beales has a majority of just 587 votes from July 2024: Beales had been on the wrong side of a similar margin when he lost the parliamentary by-election here the previous year. Steve Tuckwell, the Conservative MP who he defeated in 2024, is now the Hillingdon council leader. The two outgoing Conservative councillors for the ward are both seeking re-election. In the case of Reeta Chamdal the prize for winning is a big one: she was elected last month as the mayor of Hillingdon for 2026-27, a post which is presumably conditional on her getting back to the council in this poll. Her running-mate Adam Bennett has worked for over 20 years for British Airways, where he is currently head of Global Online Travel Agents. Bennett and Chamdal were both first elected in 2022. The Labour slate here consists of Tiffany Beales and Imran Razak, who are both first-time candidates. The only other party to stand here in 2022 was the Greens, who have taken advantage of the postponement to nominate a full slate with Alicia Windsor from the cancelled election joined by Ruhi Patel; on the Reform UK side Margaret Forrest remains from the original candidate list while Justin Anderson (who gives an address in faraway Portsmouth) has been nominated to replace the late Shaun Cooling; and the Lib Dem candidates of Muhammad Iqbal and Gautam Sabarwal complete a ballot paper of ten candidates. There are two seats available in this poll, and electors here have two votes. Crofton Park

Lewisham council, London; caused by the election of Green Party councillor Liam Shrivastava as mayor of the borough. Having got London's postponed poll out of the way, we now travel south of the river to the first in a short series of London by-elections defended by newly-elected Green Party councillors who were unable or unwilling to serve. In this case it's a result of Liam Shrivastava being too successful at the polls in May and winning two jobs at once: he became the fourth elected mayor of Lewisham, and he was also elected to Lewisham council as an ordinary councillor. Mayor Shrivastava can't do both jobs at once, so his councillor seat has been automatically vacated. Shrivastava was elected in 2022 as a Labour councillor for Lewisham's New Cross ward before defecting to the Greens, but he didn't seek re-election there. Instead we're going to move two stops down the Windrush line to Honor Oak Park station, which lies on the western boundary of Lewisham borough and serves the western part of Crofton Park ward. The name Crofton Park was an invention of the London, Chatham and Dover Railway which opened Crofton Park station in 1892 on its Catford Loop line; before then the Crofton Park area in the north of this ward was considered to be part of Brockley. The largest open space in the ward is Blythe Hill Fields, a high point which has good views of London. Crofton Park ward was once the home of the Irish political activist Jim Connell, who wrote the socialist anthem The Red Flag while on a train home to Honor Oak Park in 1889. Connell might well be pleased to note that his old stomping ground is a left-wing area in local elections, and - in common with Lewisham as a whole - Crofton Park ward had a full slate of Labour councillors from 2010 to 2026. But this year the Labour redwash in Lewisham was broken in spectacular fashion, with the Green Party winning the borough's mayoralty and an overall majority on the council, with 40 seats to Labour's 14. Dspite the fact that all three Green candidates were at the bottom of the ballot paper, Crofton Park ward followed suit with the Green slate defeating Labour by 52-31 - a swing of 18½% from Labour's 49-33 win here four years ago. This might well be causing some concern to this area's Labour MP, although it would take a bigger Labour to Green swing to oust her. Crofton Park is part of the Lewisham West and East Dulwich constituency, which also takes in territory from Southwark borough to the west. In 2024 it was won by Ellie Reeves, who had represented the predcessor seat of Lewisham West and Penge since 2017. Reeves is a barrister who holds the post of Solicitor General, but she is not the most senior member of her family serving in government: she is the younger sister of Rachel Reeves, the Chancellor of the Exchequer. As stated above, Liam Shrivastava's election as mayor and councillor last month has opened up a vacancy on Lewisham council. Defending the resulting by-election for the Greens is Esther Lie, who currently works as a community organiser. Labour's Alex Brooks was on their unsuccessful slate here in May and he is back for another go. Also standing are Richard Elliott for the Lib Dems, Caitlin Pugh for the Conservatives, Paul Newman for Reform UK and indepndent candidate Roger Mighton, who finished in eighth and last place in the Lewisham mayoral election last month.
