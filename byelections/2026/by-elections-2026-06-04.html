Two by-elections on 4th June 2026:

Coleman Street

City of London Corporation; caused by the election of Common Councilman Sushil Saluja as an Alderman of the City.

Hawcoat and Newbarns

Westmorland and Furness council, Cumbria; caused by the resignation of Conservative councillor Niyall Phillips. For our only partisan by-election of the week we are off to a town that's not often visited. Barrow-in-Furness is very much out on a limb, located at the end of the Furness peninsula at the south-western corner of what is now Cumbria. The main road and railway line to Barrow have to take a very indirect route around Morecambe Bay, with the railway crossing some of the estuaries feeding the bay on impressive viaducts. The railway line into Barrow town passes the ruins of a twelfth-century structure which once dominated the local economy. Furness Abbey was founded in 1123 by the future King Stephen, and it was originally a house of the short-lived Savignac order of monks before they merged with the Cistercians in 1147. By the 15th century it was the Cistercians' second-richest abbey in England, after Fountains, thanks to the monks discovering iron ore deposits. The income from this enabled to Furness Abbey to control large landholdings both in Cumbria and over the sea on the Isle of Man. This gave the monks of Furness Abbey a stake in the Kingdom of Mann and the Isles, a Norse-Gaelic polity which controlled the Isle of Man until the kingdom was absorbed into Scotland in 1265. The modern Church of England diocese of Sodor and Man is descended from that kingdom. One of the old kings of Mann and the Isles was Rǫgnvaldr Guðrøðarson, who reigned from the death of his father in 1187 until he was deposed by his younger brother Óláfr in 1226. Rǫgnvaldr died in 1229 on the Isle of Man, and his body was transported over the sea for burial at Furness Abbey. The modern economy of Barrow-in-Furness started to get going in the 1850s when the iron ore deposits were rediscovered, and Barrow quickly developed as a major port and steelworking centre. The town declared independence from the parish of Dalton-in-Furness in 1867 to become a borough of its own: in 1885 it was enfranchised by the Third Reform Act, and in 1889 Barrow-in-Furness became a county borough within Lanacshire. By 1897 Barrow's largest employer was the shipyard, which was taken over in that year by Vickers. It was the profits from this which led to the development of Abbey House, which looks suitably old and lies close to the ruins of Furness Abbey but has no historical connection to it. Abbey House, which is now a hotel and where I once attended a wedding reception, is in fact a Lutyens design from 1914, built to provide accommodation for company bigwigs visiting the Vickers shipyard in Barrow-in-Furness. Nearby Hawcoat, which was a village here before Barrow's growth got going in the 19th century, is where most Barrovians start and end their lives as the location of Furness General Hospital; much of the housing in Hawcoat dates from the 1960s, when Vickers expanded its operations in Barrow to build the Resolution class nuclear submarines for the Royal Navy. The Vickers shipyard is still in business today, now operated by BAE Systems, and it is still busily building new nuclear submarines - currently of the Astute and Dreadnought classes. It continues to underpin the economy of this entire corner of Cumbria. Hawcoat and Newbarns ward, which covers Barrow's norh-eastern suburbs and is the most middle-class of the town's six electoral wards, makes the top 40 wards in England and Wales for residents working in the manufacturing sector at 20.25% of the workforce. This ward also ranks second of all the wards in England and Wales for residents with apprenticeship qualifications (11.05%), behind only nearby Walney Island: in fact all of the top four wards for this statistic are in Barrow-in-Furness, with the neighbouring Dalton South ward coming in at number 5. And it's not just defence which drives all of this. The seas around Barrow are filled with wind turbines, and when the Walney Extension windfarm was completed in 2018 it was the largest offshore windfarm in the world. It's nowhere near being the largest now, such has been the development of renewable energy in the last eight years. Hawcoat and Newbarns ward's middle-class nature is reflected in its local elections. The former Hawcoat ward of Barrow-in-Furness normally returned Conservative local councillors, while Newbarns ward was more marginal between Labour (which normally ran the former borough council) and the Conservatives. This was reflected in the first election result on these boundaries in 2022, when Hawcoat and Newbarns narrowly returned the Conservative slate with 40% of the vote to Labour's 34%. Third place on 20% went to an independent slate headed by former long-serving Conservative councillor (Denise) Hazel Edwards, who was mayor of Barrow in 1993-94 and is a former leader of the Conservative group on the old Barrow council; that slate also included long-serving Conservative Barrow councillor Wendy McClure, so the right-wing position here might be better than it looks from this result. In 2023 Cumbria's local government was reorganised, a decision which has not served Barrow well. The successor local authority of Westmorland and Furness administers the town from over 30 miles away in Kendal, and it is run by the Liberal Democrats who have never had any sort of local presence in Barrow. The Lib Dems last won a council seat in the town in 1983, and they rarely even stood a candidate here when local election time came round. The Westmorland and Furness councillors elected in 2022 were elected for a five-year term (including 11 months as shadow councillors before the old régime disappeared in April 2023), so the next local elections here are due in 2027. This means that the last poll here was the general election in the Barrow and Furness constituency, which has existed since 1983 to take in Barrow town and its Furness hinterland (including Ulverston). This has historically been a Labour-inclined marginal, although the Conservatives are capable of winning in good years or when Labour have a leader who wants to cancel the UK's nuclear deterrent. In 2024 the Barrow and Furness seat was gained for Labour by Michelle Scrogham, a former mayor of Ulverston who ran a fashion boutique in that town before entering politics. The Conservatives are the third-largest group on Westmorland and Furness council, and they are on the defensive in this by-election to replace Niyall Phillips following his resignation last month. Phillips, a project manager who was one of the many Barrow residents working for BAE Systems, has been transferred by his employer to Adelaide where he is working on the development of the forthcoming Aukus-class submarines. He was in his first term as a councillor. The Barrow Tories' selection committee clearly subscribe to the maxim that if you're good enough, you're old enough: their defending candidate Marco Fawcett is an 18-year-old who is currently taking his A-levels at Ulverston Victoria high school. He's less than a quarter of the age of Labour's Mike Leach, a Unite figure who worked for many years at the gas terminal in Barrow which serves the Irish Sea gas fields; Leach was also on the Labour slate here in 2022. Also returning from that election is Hazel Edwards, who was top of the independent slate here last time and served for many years as a Conservative member of the former Barrow-in-Furness council, being first elected all the way back in 1979: this time Edwards has the Reform UK nomination. The Lib Dems' Stephen Pickthall and the Greens' Rebekah Atkinson complete a ballot paper of five candidates.
