"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

Two by-elections on 4th June 2026:

Coleman Street

City of London Corporation; caused by the election of Common Councilman Sushil Saluja as an Alderman of the City.

The coming tidal wave of by-elections arising from the 7th May polls has not yet hit us. There are only two by-elections taking place this week, both of which arise well before 7th May - and only one of them will tell us anything about the current state of party politics. The other is a Quaint British Tradition.

The City of London Corporation has been running local government in the Square Mile since - well, nobody really knows how long but certainly since time immemorial. The first Lord Mayor of London, Henry fitz Ailwin, served in that office from 1189 until his death in 1212; and even before that he had been involved in London's governance as an Alderman of the city. Since 1200 London has had 25 Aldermen, one for each of the City's ancient wards; this Court of Aldermen was quickly supplemented by the larger Court of Common Council, which currently has 100 members.

One of the few surviving clauses of Magna Carta still in force provides that "the City of London shall have all the old Liberties and Customs which it hath been used to have". Partly because of this, and partly because the City's system of local governance has always had an unusually wide franchise, the City Corporation has escaped unscathed from every local government reorganisation which has taken place over the last two centuries.

This means that many of the old mediaeval traditions still survive, including the concept of the wardmote - a public meeting which is hardwired into the City's electoral process. The wardmote takes place on the day before polling and in modern times it functions as a hustings for the election: the meeting is then adjourned for the poll to take place, and it will be reconvened tonight for the declaration of the result.

City election rules require all of this action to take place within the ward boundary, a rule which is just about satisfied in this election. Today's polling station is the church of St Margaret Lothbury, located directly opposite the Bank of England, which actually straddles the boundary between this ward and the neighbouring Broad Street ward. This is one of the many churches in the City which were rebuilt by Sir Christopher Wren after the Great Fire of 1666. Not all of the seventeenth-century Wren churches still exist, and over the years St Margaret Lothbury has amasseed a large quantity of original woodwork and other knickknacks from City churches which are now gone.

Coleman Street ward runs north from Lothbury and Gresham Street near the Bank to the northern boundary of the City. The eponymous Coleman Street - which takes its name from mediaeval charcoal-burners who were located here - would once have been a major artery, but it has been superseded by the busy modern thoroughfare of Moorgate.

The original Moorgate was a minor gate in the Roman Wall which surrounded the City, taking its name from the open space of Moorfields to the north. The Moorfields was a marshy area, partly because this is where the Wall obstructed the Walbrook river which once flowed through the City from north to south. The Walbrook's course is marked by the modern Blomfield Street which is Coleman Street ward's eastern boundary, and which hides a macabre secret. In 1838 workmen building a sewer along Blomfield Street discovered hundreds of human skulls on the old course of the Walbrook, which appear to date from the Roman period; further ancient skeletons have turned up here in later excavations.

The modern equivalent of the Moorfields is Finsbury Circus, the largest public green space in the City. Nonetheless the builders were here for much of the 21st century so far, as part of construction work for the Elizabeth Line whose Liverpool Street station lies under the Circus.

The Crossrail station also links up to Moorgate underground station, where the east-west Metropolitan and Circle lines cross the north-south City branch of the Northern Line. This is also a mainline London Terminal, with suburban trains running north from here through old Underground tunnels towards Finsbury Park and beyond.

Back in their Underground days, the mainline platforms at Moorgate station were the scene of the Tube's worst peacetime accident on 28th February 1975, when an approaching train crashed at full speed into the end of the tunnel killing the driver - whose failure to apply the brake has never been satisfactorily explained - and 42 passengers. As a result of this tragedy, a safety system called Moorgate control was installed at all London Underground terminal platforms to prevent this happening again. The railway line from Moorgate to Finsbury Park has recently been converted to use digital signalling, providing a further margin of safety.

The City of London is known for its tall buildings and Coleman Street is no exception to this. Around the corner from Moorgate station, and diagonally opposite the Armourers' Hall, is a building of sexily-curved steel and glass called Moor House which dwarfs a new statue of the poet John Keats in the plaza outside. Keats was born in a pub a few yards away which is now called the Globe and is attached to Moorgate station.

Not far to the north is Citypoint, a 35-storey skyscraper which opened in 1967 as the corporate headquarters of BP. It was the first building in the Square Mile to exceed the height of St Paul's Cathedral, but it's now only the 13th-highest building in the City. Round the corner from there is a curious salient of this ward along Silk Street and Chiswell Street, opposite the Barbican theatre and concert hall. The Jugged Hare pub, which is convenient for those spending an evening at the Barbican, lies at the north-west corner of this ward.

The reason for this curious salient appears to be to ensure that the boundaries of Coleman Street ward do not include the Heron, a 35-storey skyscraper on the corner of Silk Street and Moor Lane which is partly used by the Guildhall School of Music and Drama (there are a couple of theatres and a 608-seat concert hall lurking inside the buliding) but is mostly given over to residential apartments. Having the Heron here would disturb the nature of Coleman Street ward as a ward dominated by business.

I mentioned earlier that the City Corporation has always had an unusually wide franchise, and that remains the case today. Within the Square Mile, the right to vote is vested not just in people who live in the City but also in people who work here: sole traders get votes as of right, while businesses based here can nominate a number of their employees as electors on a sliding scale depending on their headcount. There was another poll in Coleman Street last February at which the Magic Circle law firm Linklaters, whose head office is on Silk Street opposite the Barbican, got to nominate 48 electors; while Deutsche Bank, who occupy a large and very new office building at 21 Moorfields which was named as The City Building of the Year 2025, could register 70 of its employees to vote. By contrast, the number of residential electors in Coleman Street ward was, it appeared, one. This election is very much not a case of one man, one vote: the Ward List for this by-election lists an electorate of 630.

That election in February was to fill an open seat in the City's Court of Aldermen, and it was won by Sushil Saluja. He had previously been one of the four Common Councilmen for Coleman Street ward, the other three being Shahnan Bakth, Philip Kelvin and Dawn Wright. All of them were elected last year without a contest as independent candidates, because City elections are normally run on a non-partisan basis. In recent years Labour have normally stood a few official candidates in the wards covering the Barbican estate where most of the City's residents live, but essentially the Common Council and the Court of Aldermen have an independent majority which will continue for the foreseeable future.

Saluja's election as Alderman for Coleman Street has now created a vacancy for the ward in the Court of Common Council, and there is a lot of interest in it with eight candidates standing. None of the defeated Aldermanic candidates in February have returned for another go, so this is a completely new state.

Top of the ballot paper is Randall Anderson, who is one of three candidates who have served on the Common Council before: Anderson was a long-serving councilman for Aldersgate ward, covering part of the Barbican estate where he lives, until 2025 when he sought re-election in Bassishaw ward and was defeated. Philip Baldwin hasn't been seen on a public ballot paper since 2018, when he was a Conservative candidate for Tower Hamlets council, but he is an elected member of the General Synod of the Church of England who represents the Diocese of London in the House of Laity. Baldwin is apparently notable enough for Wikipedia, which describes him as a human rights activist who campaigns on LGBT and HIV awareness (he is HIV positive). Ronnie Barker (no, not that one) stood for Castle Baynard ward last year, finishing 18th out of 19 candidates. Richard Fenner is a senior director at the London branch of Euroclear, a Brussels-based financial services firm which acts as a central securities depository. John Griffiths is a former Common Councilman for Castle Baynard ward who served from 2017 until he lost his seat in 2025; he is the founding director of a consulting firm called Rocket Science UK Ltd, but he might find that this election is not rocket science. Suyi Opebiyi describes himself on LinkedIn as a financial services professional, because that's the best way to stand out in the City. The only woman on this ballot is Alpa Raja, who specialises in business turnaround and restructuring: Raja is a frequent candidate in City elections (she stood in a previous by-election here in July 2019) who will be looking to turn around her own political career after she lost her Common Council seat in Castle Baynard ward last year. And finally we have Andrew Tyler, who commutes into the city from a village near Chelmsford: he is a partner in the property and estate agents firm Knight Frank. Polls will be open in this election from 8am to 8pm today.

Hawcoat and Newbarns

Westmorland and Furness council, Cumbria; caused by the resignation of Conservative councillor Niyall Phillips.

For our only partisan by-election of the week we are off to a town that's not often visited. Barrow-in-Furness is very much out on a limb, located at the end of the Furness peninsula at the south-western corner of what is now Cumbria. The main road and railway line to Barrow have to take a very indirect route around Morecambe Bay, with the railway crossing some of the estuaries feeding the bay on impressive viaducts.

The railway line into Barrow town passes the ruins of a twelfth-century structure which once dominated the local economy. Furness Abbey was founded in 1123 by the future King Stephen, and it was originally a house of the short-lived Savignac order of monks before they merged with the Cistercians in 1147. By the 15th century it was the Cistercians' second-richest abbey in England, after Fountains, thanks to the monks discovering iron ore deposits. The income from this enabled to Furness Abbey to control large landholdings both in Cumbria and over the sea on the Isle of Man.

This gave the monks of Furness Abbey a stake in the Kingdom of Mann and the Isles, a Norse-Gaelic polity which controlled the Isle of Man until the kingdom was absorbed into Scotland in 1265. The modern Church of England diocese of Sodor and Man is descended from that kingdom. One of the old kings of Mann and the Isles was Rǫgnvaldr Guðrøðarson, who reigned from the death of his father in 1187 until he was deposed by his younger brother Óláfr in 1226. Rǫgnvaldr died in 1229 on the Isle of Man, and his body was transported over the sea for burial at Furness Abbey.

The modern economy of Barrow-in-Furness started to get going in the 1850s when the iron ore deposits were rediscovered, and Barrow quickly developed as a major port and steelworking centre. The town declared independence from the parish of Dalton-in-Furness in 1867 to become a borough of its own: in 1885 it was enfranchised by the Third Reform Act, and in 1889 Barrow-in-Furness became a county borough within Lanacshire.

By 1897 Barrow's largest employer was the shipyard, which was taken over in that year by Vickers. It was the profits from this which led to the development of Abbey House, which looks suitably old and lies close to the ruins of Furness Abbey but has no historical connection to it. Abbey House, which is now a hotel and where I once attended a wedding reception, is in fact a Lutyens design from 1914, built to provide accommodation for company bigwigs visiting the Vickers shipyard in Barrow-in-Furness. Nearby Hawcoat, which was a village here before Barrow's growth got going in the 19th century, is where most Barrovians start and end their lives as the location of Furness General Hospital; much of the housing in Hawcoat dates from the 1960s, when Vickers expanded its operations in Barrow to build the Resolution class nuclear submarines for the Royal Navy.

The Vickers shipyard is still in business today, now operated by BAE Systems, and it is still busily building new nuclear submarines - currently of the Astute and Dreadnought classes. It continues to underpin the economy of this entire corner of Cumbria. Hawcoat and Newbarns ward, which covers Barrow's norh-eastern suburbs and is the most middle-class of the town's six electoral wards, makes the top 40 wards in England and Wales for residents working in the manufacturing sector at 20.25% of the workforce. This ward also ranks second of all the wards in England and Wales for residents with apprenticeship qualifications (11.05%), behind only nearby Walney Island: in fact all of the top four wards for this statistic are in Barrow-in-Furness, with the neighbouring Dalton South ward coming in at number 5.

And it's not just defence which drives all of this. The seas around Barrow are filled with wind turbines, and when the Walney Extension windfarm was completed in 2018 it was the largest offshore windfarm in the world. It's nowhere near being the largest now, such has been the development of renewable energy in the last eight years.

Hawcoat and Newbarns ward's middle-class nature is reflected in its local elections. The former Hawcoat ward of Barrow-in-Furness normally returned Conservative local councillors, while Newbarns ward was more marginal between Labour (which normally ran the former borough council) and the Conservatives. This was reflected in the first election result on these boundaries in 2022, when Hawcoat and Newbarns narrowly returned the Conservative slate with 40% of the vote to Labour's 34%. Third place on 20% went to an independent slate headed by former long-serving Conservative councillor (Denise) Hazel Edwards, who was mayor of Barrow in 1993-94 and is a former leader of the Conservative group on the old Barrow council; that slate also included long-serving Conservative Barrow councillor Wendy McClure, so the right-wing position here might be better than it looks from this result.

In 2023 Cumbria's local government was reorganised, a decision which has not served Barrow well. The successor local authority of Westmorland and Furness administers the town from over 30 miles away in Kendal, and it is run by the Liberal Democrats who have never had any sort of local presence in Barrow. The Lib Dems last won a council seat in the town in 1983, and they rarely even stood a candidate here when local election time came round. The Westmorland and Furness councillors elected in 2022 were elected for a five-year term (including 11 months as shadow councillors before the old régime disappeared in April 2023), so the next local elections here are due in 2027.

This means that the last poll here was the general election in the Barrow and Furness constituency, which has existed since 1983 to take in Barrow town and its Furness hinterland (including Ulverston). This has historically been a Labour-inclined marginal, although the Conservatives are capable of winning in good years or when Labour have a leader who wants to cancel the UK's nuclear deterrent. In 2024 the Barrow and Furness seat was gained for Labour by Michelle Scrogham, a former mayor of Ulverston who ran a fashion boutique in that town before entering politics.

The Conservatives are the third-largest group on Westmorland and Furness council. They have already lost one by-election to the Lib Dems in this term, and they are on the defensive again here in this poll to replace Niyall Phillips following his resignation last month. Phillips, a project manager who was one of the many Barrow residents working for BAE Systems, has been transferred by his employer to the Australian city of Adelaide where he is working on the development of the forthcoming Aukus-class submarines. He was in his first term as a councillor.

The Barrow Tories' selection committee clearly subscribe to the maxim that if you're good enough, you're old enough: their defending candidate Marco Fawcett is an 18-year-old who is currently taking his A-levels at Ulverston Victoria high school. He's less than a quarter of the age of Labour's Mike Leach, a Unite figure who worked for many years at the gas terminal in Barrow which serves the Irish Sea gas fields; Leach was also on the Labour slate here in 2022. Also returning from that election is Hazel Edwards, who was top of the independent slate here last time and served for many years as a Conservative member of the former Barrow-in-Furness council, being first elected all the way back in 1979: this time Edwards has the Reform UK nomination. The Lib Dems' Stephen Pickthall and the Greens' Rebekah Atkinson complete a ballot paper of five candidates.
