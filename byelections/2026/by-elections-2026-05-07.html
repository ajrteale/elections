"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

Welcome to the annual Andrew's Previews megapreview, covering everything that goes on in the ordinary local elections in Great Britain on 1st May 2025.

Introduction

Spring has well and truly sprung. The trees are full of the new season's blossom. The roads are full of the last season's potholes. And the bins are full of leaflets, straining the backs of the binmen who are the first step in turning this season's election publicity into next season's recycled paper.

A huge amount of what you see in the picture above has been influenced by the local council. The lamppost in the background at the right, partly hidden behind a tree, was designed to look good given its location in a town centre. The lamppost in the foreground has a speed limit sign attached to it; its counterpart on the other side of the post reads "20", instructing drivers to keep their speed down through a residential area - the council signed that off. The bookmakers on the left edge of the picture went through the council planning process when it opened, many years ago. The health centre and library is a new building which opened in 2022, built, paid for and operated out of my council tax. And the potholes in the picture - which have been fixed since this photograph was taken four years ago, but that hasn't stopped new ones appearing elsewhere - are an illustration of the parlous state of council finances.

It's the council who get to have the final say on important matters like how often the bins are emptied, how often the roads are resurfaced, and how much their employees get paid for doing that. It's the council who get to have the final say on important matters like where shops can be opened, how our libraries are run, where our children's schools are located, where new housing is to be built, whether new housing is to be built. It's the council that registers our hatchings, matchings and dispatchings, and that supports its more vulnerable residents in between through one of the largest parts of the local government budget - social services. To do all this our nearly 400 local councils, between them, employ around two million people - a figure that has reduced by a third since 2010 as central government, who stump up the bill for much of this, have slowly turned the financial thumbscrews.

In the UK, we ensure that the public have oversight of all this through regular local government elections. Every year, on the first Thursday in May, new councillors are elected while old councillors retire.

For electors in England, which form the majority of those voting today, the council elections take centre stage. The 2026 English local elections see just over 5,000 seats up for election in ordinary course across 136 councils, including two brand-new councils in Surrey. Five of those districts will also elect or re-elect their mayor. In Scotland and Wales, this year's polls form the seventh general election for the Scottish Parliament and for the democratic institution now known as Senedd Cymru. There will also be 20 standalone local by-elections today for a total of 22 seats. It's the biggest electoral test of the year. Let's look at what we might expect.

Setting the scene

Local elections relate to local issues, but they take place against the backdrop of the national political scene. A rising tide lifts all boats, whereas a falling tide can leave some vessels high and dry. Many councillors each year find that their jobs are won and lost based on what's happening outside their district.

Like most local election years in the UK there is patchy coverage this year, with some districts going to the polls while others don't. Everybody in Scotland and Wales will have something to vote for, but in England this year's local elections are restricted to Greater London, a few county councils whose elections were postponed from last year for no particularly good reason, those councils in England which return a third or a half of their councillors at each election, and a few other special cases which have non-standard electoral cycles. As a general rule, the areas up for election today are the most urban districts and they include nearly all of England's metropolitan areas. If you live in rural England, chances are that you'll be sitting this year's elections out.

This urban bias translates into a Labour-voting bias. Most of the seats up this year were last contested in 2022, and the Local Elections Archive Project database shows that that year (in England only) 39.5% of the votes were cast for Labour, 28.9% for the Conservatives, 15.8% for the Liberal Democrats, 9.5% for the Green Party, 2.3% for independent candidates, 0.1% for Reform UK (which ran very few candidates) and the remaining 3.9% for localists and other parties. In terms of council seats, Labour won 2,210, the Conservatives 982, the Liberal Democrats 606, the Greens 103, independents 63, Reform UK 0 and localists and the rest won 137. The biggest contributor to the latter score was the 24 seats won by Aspire, the Lutfur Rahmanite party which controls Tower Hamlets council, which stood only in that borough but polled enough votes there to finish in sixth place across the country.

These figures will not be directly comparable with this year's elections. In the case of the councils whose polls were postponed from last year the comparison is with May 2021, when the Conservatives were riding high in the national polls on the back of the COVID vaccination programme. The local elections reflected this: the projected shares came out at 36% for the Conservatives, 29% for Labour and 17% for the Liberal Democrats. In a few areas, boundary changes since 2022 mean that the seats up for election were last filled in 2023 or 2024. And the lineup of councils holding polls today is not the same as it was four years ago, partly because of the postponements.

To allow consistent comparisons to be made across local elections from year to year, the BBC make an estimate of how the vote would have gone across the whole country based on vote changes in a representative sample of wards. Their Projected National Share for the 2022 local elections put Labour on 35%, the Conservatives on 30% and the Liberal Democrats on 19%. We can see from the differences between these and the figures above that the wards holding elections then tend to be stronger for Labour, and weaker for the Conservatives and the Lib Dems, than the GB average.

Since May 2022 we have had a change of government in Westminster. The Conservative government, which had been discredited by failing to solve the UK's economic stagnation and high inflation, by a series of personal scandals from the Prime Ministers downwards and by a general impression of incompetence in government, was annihilated at the 2024 general election. The new Labour bosses have similarly seen their polling position evaporate in the face of pretty much the same economic and societal issues that the Conservatives failed to handle.

But, as I noted in last year's megapreview, the Conservatives have not recovered in the polls and they have indeed fallen further. Instead, the beneficiary so far from the fall of Labour has been Reform UK. They won the 2025 local elections which a projected national share of 30%, against 20% for Labour, 17% for the Liberal Democrats, 15% for the Conservatives and 11% for the Green Party. In my debrief piece posted over the weekend after those polls took place, I suggested "that the Conservative slide in local government may now have hit the bottom, while if Labour can't get their act together then they will be in deep trouble in the May 2026 local elections, when a large number of their seats will be up for election".

Labour have not got their act together. When this piece was drafted over the Easter weekend, Reform UK had led in all but one national opinion poll held since then. As of 15th April, the Britain Elects poll of polls had 26% for Reform UK, 18% for Labour, 18% for the Conservatives, 17% for the Green Party and 12% for the Lib Dems. These figures are not too dissimilar to the projected national shares from a year ago: we still have five parties over 10% of the vote. Electoral fragmentation remains the order of the day.

The 2025 local elections coincided with Reform UK's first parliamentary by-election win, as they very narrowly defeated Labour in Runcorn and Helsby. Since then Labour have had to defend by-elections in all three of the UK's Parliaments. In June they held the Holyrood seat of Hamilton, Larkhall and Stonehouse; in October they lost the Seneedd Cymru Caerphilly constituency to Plaid Cymru; and most recently in February they lost the Westminster seat of Gorton and Denton to the Green Party.

Reform UK have had more success on the local by-election front, and local by-elections over the last year have seen a lot of volatility with a large proportion of seats changing hands. Out of 231 seats contested in Great Brtiain between last May and Easter, Reform UK have won 80 (a net gain of 68), the Liberal Democrats 64 (a net gain of 20), the Conservatives 28 (a net loss of 23), the Green Party 19 (a net gain of 4), Labour 17 (a net loss of 53), Plaid Cymru 5 (a net gain of 1), the Scottish National Party 4 (a net loss of 1), and independents and localist parties 14 (a net loss of 16). Of the 74 seats which Reform UK have gained, 30 came from Labour, 21 from the Conservatives, 18 from independents and localist parties, 4 from the Lib Dems and 1 from Plaid Cymru. Most of the 54 seats which Labour have lost since May went to Reform UK as above; of the remainder, 8 went to the Greens, 6 to independents and localist parties, 5 to the Lib Dems, 2 to the Conservatives, 2 to Plaid Cymru and one to the SNP. Labour have only gained one seat in a local by-election in the last 12 months.

All of this suggests that we are in for massive Labour seat losses in this year's local elections. The Labour party are defending more than half of the seats up for election this year, and a large proportion of this set of elections is Greater London where Labour performed particularly well at the last local elections in 2022. Outside the capital, an unusually large proportion of the metropolitan boroughs - 16 out of the 32 holding elections - will have all-up elections, in which a new ward map is brought in and all the councillors are up for election rather than the usual third. All but one of these areas were last re-warded in 2004 when a general ward boundary review was brought in for the metropolitan boroughs, and the Local Government Boundary Commission has reasonably taken the view that 22 years of the same ward map is quite enough. Meanwhile, in the large county councils of East Anglia, Hampshire and Sussex whose elections were postponed from last year, the Cosnervatives will struggle on current polling to replicate their 2021 result.

For electoral fragmentatiion to happen you need to have lots of parties standing, which has the more welcome effect of expanding voter choice. And in this year's English local elections there is a lot of voter choice, with a total of 25,046 cndidates standing for the 5,066 seats up for election: a very high average of 4.94 candidates for each seat. (For comparison, the average local election over the period 2002-25 had around 3.2 candidates per seat.) Not a single ward is uncontested this year, and only one ward in the whole of England is a two-candidate fight. All five of the Westminster parties are contesting 86% or more of the wards up for election, with Reform UK having the greatest coverage: they are absent from only two of the 2,972 ballots. (Those are King George's and Sunray ward in Kingston upon Thames, where rumour has it the Reform UK nomination was handed in after the deadline, and Holywell ward in central Oxford.) Labour have the highest total number of candidates, contesting 96.7% of the seats up for election.

Every council has its own idiosyncracies of course, but you'll see this theme developing in the detailed previews which now follow. Links given are to results from the Local Elections Archive Project, while the maps are from 2022 or (if different) the year when these seats were last contested. Votes will be counted on Friday unless otherwise stated. Without further ado, let's get out into the country:

North East

The North East region will see whole-council elections in Gateshead, Newcastle upon Tyne, South Tyneside and Sunderland, and elections for a third of the council in Hartlepool and North Tyneside.

The only one of these which is counting overnight is Hartlepool, where a recent defection to Reform UK has reduced the Labour majority to 21 out of 36 seats: in the opposition are 6 independents, 5 Reform UK councillors (two Conservative defectors, one Labour defector and two by-election gains from Labour in Throston ward) and 4 Conservatives. One of the Reform UK by-election winners in Throston is up for re-election this year; that leaves Labour defending six seats, and they need to hold four of them to preseve their council majority.

Normally we start in the North East with Sunderland, whose electoral services team has a reputation for very fast counting, but this year they will not be first. The boundary changes mean that three seats are up per ward rather than the usual one, and the returning officer has decided on this occasion that being accurate is better than being first: Sunderland, as with all the Tyne and Wear boroughs, will count its votes on Friday.

The last composition here has a fairly large Labour majority, with 50 Labour seats (one of which is vacant) against 12 Lib Dems, 9 Conservatives, 3 independents and a Green councillor. But a quick look over the county boundary to what happened in Durham last year suggests that Sunderland Labour, after riding their luck somewhat in the last few years as the seats in Sunderland proper started to fragment, may now be in serious trouble. Reform UK will have this council in their sights.

We can say similar things about Gateshead and South Tyneside, where Labour have recently lost their majority following a defection loss and some appalling local election results in recent years: Labour have 27 seats, half of the council, against an opposition which presently consists of independent and Green councillors. Gateshead, which has been a two-party state of Labour and Lib Dems for decades, might finally give me something interesting to write about.

Newcastle upon Tyne is the all-up metropolitan borough which has already had one boundary review since 2004: this was based on botched population projections, and the new map creates a new ward in the fast-growing outer suburbs while abolishing South Jesmond ward in the inner city. That ward was gained by the Green Party in an August 2025 by-election, putting more pressure on a Labour administration that has lost control of the city as a result of defection losses: the last composition has 36 Labour seats (two of which are vacant) against 22 Lib Dems, 16 independents of various stripes, 4 Greens and a Conservative.

North Tyneside has a Labour elected mayor and the Labour majority on the council is impregnable, but Reform UK were a strong second in the mayoral election last year and they have since got onto the council by gaining a by-election in Killingworth ward. They will be looking for more.

North West

There are no local elections in Cumbria this year so we'll start our tour of the north-west in Lancashire, where there are elections this year for one-third of Pendle, Burrnley, Hyndburn, Blackburn with Darwen, Chorley, West Lancashire and Preston councils. Rossendale moved over to whole council elections in 2024 and so is not up for election this year.

The East Lancashire boroughs are areas where the Labour vote has been heavily impacted by the Gaza Independent phenomenon. The most extreme example of this is now Pendle council which, despite the map above, is a Labour-free zone after the entire Labour group quit the party and went independent in 2024. The current composition has 12 independent councillors, 11 Conservatives, 9 Lib Dems and a Reform UK councillor, who won a by-election last May in Vivary Bridge ward. The ex-Labour independent councillors run the council in coalition with the Lib Dems and they won both of the county divisions covering Nelson in last year's Lancashire county elections.

Ex-Labour Gaza independents also lead the ruling independent/Lib Den/Green coalition in Burrnley, which stands at 13 Labour councillors, 11 independents, 7 Conservatives, 7 Lib Dems, 5 Greens and 2 Reform UK. Last year the independents won the Central East and North East divisions where the town's Muslim population is concentrated, and two by-elections in this area in November were both won by independent candidates. The rest of the borough was swept by Reform UK in 2025, and it is noticeable that councillor Alan Hosker - who was first elected here for UKIP before essentially pulling off a reverse takeover of the Burrnley Conervatives - is now in the Reform UK camp.

In Hyndburn the Labour group has remained relatively united and the beneficiaries of the Gaza vote phenomenon have been the Green Party, who won Accrington's Central wward (which is not up for election this year) in 2024; in 2025 the Greens won Accrington West and Oswaltwistle Central division, with Reform UK sweeping the rest of the district. A further defection from Labour to Green has left Labour with 20 out of 35 seats, and they need to hold 3 of the 5 seats they are defending to preserve their majority.

Blackburn with Darwen is ouside the Lancashire county council area and hasn't seen any polls since the 2024 general election, when the Blackburn constituency - which covers most of this district - was narrowly gained from Labour by independent candidate Adnan Hussain. This was after the May 2024 local elections had seen independent candidates sweep the predominantly Muslim wards in Blackburn, and a repeat of that result this time would see Labour lose six seats net and control of the council. There are currently 27 Labour councillors here against 15 independents and 9 Conservatives.

In Preston Labour are defending ten of the 17 seats up for election following a by-election loss to the Lib Dems in Ashton ward. Currently Labour hold 26 seats against 14 Lib Dems, 5 Conservatives, 2 independents (both elected as Labour) and 1 Reform UK (elected as Conservative). None of this has much resemblance to the 2025 county elections here, which returned three independent councillors, 3 Lib Dems, 2 Reform UK, one Labour and no Conservatives. We can probably expect big seat changes here.

The only Lancashire council Labour are guaranteed to keep control of this year is Chorley, the town represented in Parliament by the Speaker Sir Lindsay Hoyle, where Labour hold 36 seats out of a possible 42 and are defending 12. Also up this year is a Reform UK by-election gain from Labour in Chorley East ward. This was the district where Labour performed best last year in the Lancashire county elections. Chorley is the only Lancashire district which will count its votes overnight.

The Labour adminstration in West Lancashire district, which is based on Ormskirk and Skelmersdale, has fallen into a minority over the last two years: the current score is 21 Labour councillors against 14 Conservatives, 7 councillors for the localist party Our West Lancashire, 2 independents and a defector from Labour to Your Party. (Your Party, incidentally, are not running many candidates themselves in these elections: instead, they have settled on the interesting tactic of endorsing a large number of left-wing localist groups and independent candidates.) The seats up here date from a full-council election in 2023, and Labour are defending eight of them incluing Aughton and Holborn ward which was gained by Our West Lancashire in a by-election last year. The Your Party defector is also up. Labour were wiped out here in the county elections, with the district's eight seats splitting five to Reform UK (including all three Skelmersdale divisions), two to Our West Lancashire and one to the Conservatives.

In Merseyside we have a whole-council in St Helens, which has an unusual electoral cycle. The 2022 elections here returned 29 Labour councillors against 7 independents, 6 seats for the Green Party, 4 Lib Dems and 2 Conservatives; Reform UK have since won two by-elections, in Blackbrook ward (gaining from Labour) and Sutton South East ward (from the Lib Dems), and they have picked up a defector from the Green Party. It takes all sorts to make a world. St Helens Town Centre ward will not poll on 7th May, because one of the Reform UK candidates for the ward has died: the election here will be rescheduled for a later date.

Reform UK will also be eyeing up Sefton, which is all-up this year on new boundaries, and they might particularly fancy the Southport end of the borough where seats are often won or lost on low vote shaes. Sefton currently has a large Labour majority with 51 out of 66 seats.

The days when Knowsley could return a full slate of Labour councillors are over, but the party does still have a large majority with 30 out of 45 seats and should be favoured to hold this, given the borough's antipathy to right-wing parties. The opposition consists of 8 Green seats (one of which is vacant), 4 independents and 3 Lib Dems.

The only Cheshire borough holding elections this year is Halton, which includes most of the Runcorn and Helsby parliamentary seat that Reform UK gained in a by-election last year. Reform UK will want to get group status on the council after picking up a defection gain, but council control will have to wait for at least another year: the Labour majority on Halton council, which currently stands at 47 seats out of a possible 54, is impregnable. This will be an overnight count.

Greater Manchester is the only metropolitan county this year which is not affected by boundary changes. There are currently three hung councils here, including Bolton where the seats up for election are from 2023. For some time now Bolton has had significant votes for localist parties in the small towns orbiting Bolton proper, with the current council having 25 Labour councillors running a minority administration against 15 Conservatives, 6 Lib Dems, 5 seats for Farnworth and Kearsley First, 3 seats for Horwich and Blackrod First, 3 ex-Horwich and Blackrod First councillors who are now in the Communities First group with the Green Party councillor, and an independent. The Green and the independent were both elected in Muslim wards in 2024 (respectively Halliwell and Rumworth) off the back of the Gaza vote phenomenon. Labour are only defending seven seats this year (the Conservatives are defending in Bradshaw and the Lib Dems in Westhoughton South), but none of them look particularly safe. In recent years Reform UK have nominated joint candidates in this borough with another localist party called Bolton for Change, but this time Bolton for Change are nowehere to be seen. It will be interesting to see if Reform UK can wipe out the other localist groups this time after several previous failed attmepts: Kearsley in particular has recorded very high UKIP scores in the past, while the Horwich and Blackrod localists have split and there are now two competing localist slates there. We shouldn't have too long to wait, because Bolton is counting its votes overnight.

The Conservatives also have seven seats to defend in Bolton, which include some of Greater Manchester's most affluent and true-blue areas. So a recent by-election loss to Reform UK in the affluent and true-blue Tottington ward of Bury may well be causing consternation in the local Tory ranks. Mind, the Bury Conservatives have been suffering from infighting as we can see from the council composition: there are 32 Labour councillors against 7 seats for Radcliffe First, 5 Conservatives, 3 seats for Reform UK (the by-election winner, a Radcliffe First defector and a Conservative defector), 3 seats for Conservative splinter group Together for Bury, and an independent. Labour need to hold 3 of the 9 seats they are defending to preserve their majority, and the party most vulnerable to Reform UK fire here mught well end up being Radcliffe First. We will be back in Bury again very shortly after the election, because the election in Moorside ward has been postponed after the Reform UK candidate died.

Greater Manchester's other hung council at present is Oldham, where the seats up for election are from an all-out election in 2023. Here the minority Labour administration is in serious trouble: there are 26 Labour councillors against 16 independents (a mixture of Gaza-type independents in Oldham town and localists for Failsworth, Royton and Shaw), 9 Lib Dems, 6 Conservatives and 3 Reform UK defectors. This year Labour (or at least councillors elected for Labour) are defending ten seats, five of which returned independent candidates in 2024. Whatever council emerges out of this overnight count is likely to be a messy one. The candidate list includes at least figure who is well-known enough outside of politics for Wikipedia: Vic Flowers, one of the ringleaders of England cricket's Barmy Army, is the Reform UK candidate for Medlock Vale ward.

We have another overnight count in Tameside. A Labour loss here would be more of a shock, but the Trigger Me Timbers WhatsApp group scandal which brought down Andrew Gwynne MP and led to Labour's loss in the Gorton and Denton by-election has also affected the local party: most of the Tameside councillors for Denton were members of that group. This and other ructions has reduced the Labour group to 39 seats (one of which is vacant) against 10 ex-Labour independents, 7 Conservatives and 1 Reform UK by-election winner in Longdendale ward. Reform UK must have had a big lead in the three Denton wards at the Gorton and Denton by-election, and they will be looking for similar here.

The Tameside seats up for election are from 2023. This is also the case in Stockport, which will declare its results ovenight and where the Liberal Democrats are two seats short of an overall majority: they hold 30 seats against 19 Labour councillors, 10 independents (4 elected as Labour, 3 Heald Green Ratepayers and 3 for the Edgeley Community Association), 3 Greens and a single Conservative, who gained a 2024 by-election from the Lib Dems in Brahall South and Woodford ward. The Lib Dems are defending ten seats here and will also be looking to reverse a defection loss in Marple South and High Lane ward, where the defending councillor has walked off to Labour; to get a majority, the Lib Dems will also need to gain Offerton ward from Labour.

The Labour majorities in Manchester, Rochdale, Salford, Trafford and Wigan are impregnable or nearly so, but there is the potential for significant Reform UK gains in Wigan in particular. Of these, Salford and Wigan are counting overnight.

Yorkshire

Five of Yorkshire's metropolitan boroughs have whole coucil elections in new ward maps. Most of these are minimum-change efforts, but in one case the Local Government Boundary Commission have increased the size of the council by creating a new ward in Calderdale. This currently has a Labour majority of one seat (26 seats against 8 Conservatices, 6 Lib Dems, 3 Greens, 3 Reform UK, 2 independents, 1 Workers Party of Britain and 2 vacant seats following the recent deaths of Conservative councillors). The new ward is in southern Halifax and takes territory from the present Skircoat ward, which Reform UK won in a by-election last year.

The biggest single English council election this year is Bradford, where all 90 councillors are up for election on new ward boundaries. Again, this has the narrowest possible Labour majority of 46 seats, against 16 independents of various types, 13 Conservatives (who successfully defended a by-election in Worth Valley ward in February), 10 Greens and 5 Lib Dems. Bradford looks likely to go into No Overall Control, and independents can expect to do well - particularly in the Muslim wards. Results here will take a long time to come through, because the count is being staggered over two days: half of the wards will be counted on Friday and the other half on Saturday. The exception to this is Idle and Thackley ward, which won't go to the polls until June after one of the outgoing Lib Dem councillors died during the election campaign.

In Kirklees the Labour group has fallen apart over the last two years, and the minority Labour administration now has just 23 councillors - a third of the 69 seats. The opposition consists of 16 Conservatives, 16 independent councillors (a mixture of Muslims in the Dewsbury and Batley area and localists in the Pennine valleys), 9 Lib Dems, 4 Greens, and 1 vacant seat following the sudden death of Lib Dem councillor Cahal Burke, who passed away just a few days after he had been suspended from the party in March: it has since been revealed that Burke had been arrested on suspicion of an online sex offence. We can probably expect a similarly messy council to emerge from this election, with Reform UK additionally in the mix.

Reform UK will be looking to do particularly well in the two boroughs which cover the coalfield area between Leeds and Sheffield. Wakefield already has two Reform UK councillors thanks to defection, but Reform UK underperformed in a December 2024 by-election in Featherstone ward and will they need to do better than they managed there to make significant gains.

In Barnsley the Lib Dems have established themselves as the main opposition to Labour on the council by taking over the former Conservative vote in the Penistone area, but there is plenty of potential for Reform UK in Barnsley town and the coalfield wards to the east of it. Barnsley is not going back to thirds elections after this year, so the next scheduled council election here will be in 2030.

That leaves three big cities in Yorkshire for which a third of the council is up. The one of these with a Labour majority is Leeds, where the current composition has 58 Labour seats (two of which are vacant) against 14 Conservative seats (one of which is vacant), 6 Lib Dems, 5 Greens, 4 seats for the Morley Borough Independents (one of which is vacant), 3 seats for the Garforth and Swillington Independents, 3 seats for the SDP, 3 independents and 2 seats for Reform UK, who gained a by-election from the Morley Borough Independents last year in Morley South ward. To preserve their majority, Labour need to hold 11 of the 20 seats they are defending this year.

Sheffield has been a messy hung council for some years now. Labour (36 seats, one of which is vacant), the Lib Dems (28 seats, one of which is vacant) and the Greens (14 seats) are the main players in the committee system running the city, with a Labour splinter group of four seats, an independent and a recent Reform UK by-election winner for Stocksbridge and Upper Don ward in opposition. Another by-election since the 2024 election has seen the Lib Dems gain Woodhouse ward from Labour; the Lib Dem by-election winner Willis Marshall, who was 19 at the time, has recently been reinstated to the party after a complaint against him was dismissed, and he is due to seek re-election this year.

The Lib Dems currently have an overall majority in the city of Hull, although only just: there are presently 29 Lib Dem councillors against 23 Labour and 5 independents (3 ex-Labour, 2 ex-Lib Dem). The Lib Dems will need to improve on their 2024 performance to keep control, while Reform UK will be stalking round the more deprived areas of the city. Hull is the only Yorkshire council where votes will be counted overnight.

East Midlands

This year is a quiet part of the electoral cycle in the East Midlands, with just two councils holding ordinary elections - in both cases for a third of the council. Both of these are overnight counts.

In North East Lincolnshire the Conservatives lost their majority in 2024 after a landslide result in 2021 was largely reversed. They still run a minority administration with 18 seats against 15 Labour councillors, 5 independents, 3 Lib Dems and a defector from the Conservatives to Reform UK, who is up for re-election this year in Croft Baker ward in Cleethorpes. Reform UK will be looking for significant gains here, with the Coservatives having the most to lose: the Tories are defending eight seats, meaning that nearly half of their council group are up for re-election.

The other East Midlands council holding ordinary elections today is the city of Lincoln, which currently has a Labour majority with 20 seats against 6 Lib Dems, 5 Conservatives and two ex-Labour independents; the Lib Dems gained a by-election from Labour in Park ward in April 2025. In the 2025 Lincolnshire county elections the city returned 3 Labour county councillors, 3 for Reform UK and 2 Lib Dems; the Reform UK county divisions generally correspond to Conservative-held wards on the southern edge of the city.

There are two standalone by-elections taking place in this region, one of which is for two seats.

There are no local elections at all this year in Leicestershire, Nottinghamshire or Rutland.

West Midlands

In the West Midlands region the big prize is the UK's largest and most controversial single-tier local authority. The current term of Birmingham city council started in 2022 with the Commonwealth Games; but it has ended in the council's bankruptcy, a massive payout for equal pay liabilities and a damaging strike by the city's binmen, which has now been going on for more than a year, over proposed pay and job cuts prompted in part by the equal pay decision. There are various inter-union issues playing out here as well: the equal pay issues generally affected Unison and GMB members, whereas the binmen are in Unite which is having a hissy fit at the government over its failure to support their members' position.

The 2022 elections here returned 65 Labour councillors against 22 Conservatives, 12 Lib Dems and 2 Greens. One seat has changed hands in a by-election since then, with the Lib Dems gaining Moseley ward in October 2025; in the meantime ten Labour councillors have gone independent. Subsequent election results - not least the election of an independent MP for Perry Barr in the 2024 general election - suggest that the anti-incumbent mood is real and anti-Labour votes could go anywhere.

Anti-Labour votes in the Black Country, meanwhile, are more likely to go to the right as we saw when the Conservatives won both West Bromwich constituencies at the 2019 general election. But the Tories never managed to get much more than a foothold on Sandwell council, which currently has 61 Labour seats (one of which is vacant) against 4 Conservatives, 3 independents, 2 Greens and one defector to Advance UK. Sandwell council gets new boundaries this year, and Reform UK might fancy their chances of significant gains.

Meanwhile neighbouring Dudley council is on a knife-edge after an all-out election in 2024 returned a tie with 34 Conservative and 34 Labour councillors: 3 Lib Dems and an independent held the balance of power. The Conservatives have since gained a by-election from Labour in Brockmoor and Pensnett ward, and the Labour group appears to have fallen apart a bit: the latest score has 33 Conservatives against 24 Labour seats (one of which has recently been vacatd by the Stourbridge MP Cat Eccles), 6 defectors to the Black Country Party, 5 Lib Dems, 3 defectors to Reform UK and the aforementioned independent. Notionally the seats up for election this year are 13 Labour, 9 Conservative, 1 Lib Dem and 1 independent, so one suspects that Conservative control of Dudley will continue in some form for the next year. This is an overnight count.

Reform UK won a 2024 by-election in Bilston North ward, and that seat is due for re-election this year. The seats up here are from an all-out election in 2023.

Walsall, which has a whole-council election this year to introduce new ward boundaries, is a different story. The council has been under Conservative control since 2018, for most of that time with a majority; but the Conservative majority has recently disappeared following defection losses and a by-election loss to Reform UK in Pelsall ward last year.

But the latest council composition frankly doesn't bear much resemblance to that: there are now 29 Conservative coucillors, 12 in the Walsall Indepndent Group which appears to be the local Your Party franchise, 11 Labour, 5 councillors in the independent group (including the Reform UK by-election winner), 1 councillor each from Advance UK and Restore Britain, and 1 ungrouped councillor. It's all a bit of a mess, and it's not clear whether the electors will resolve that or leave us with a messy hung council.

The only Conservative-controlled metrpoolitan borough at present is Solihull, where the party's majority has been cut to one seat by a recent defection to Reform UK. That puts the Tories on 27 seats against 8 Greens, 8 Lib Dems, 4 Reform UK defectors, 3 independents and a single Labour councillor. Solihull gets new ward boundaries this year with an all-out election. The same is true of our final metropolitan borough, Coventry, which has a large Labour majority at present: Labour also comfortably won the last poll here, an October 2024 by-election in the city-centre St Michael's ward.

Things are not presently so clear-cut in Nuneaton and Bedworth, which had a whole-council election in 2024 that returned a small Labour majority: 20 seats against 16 Conservatives and 2 Greens. The large number of Labour and Conservative councillors who are up for re-election here this year - Nuneaton and Bedworth turns over half of its councillors every two years - are either seriously committed to democracy or confident that they can reverse the 2025 Warwickshire county results: last year the borough returned 11 Reform UK county councillors (half of the total for the whole of Warwickshire), 1 Green and 1 independent.

To the east of Coventry, Rugby district has a balanced council with 17 Conservative seats (one of which is vacant), 15 Labour and 10 Lib Dems; Labour run a minority administration with Lib Dem support. Last year Rugby district returned 4 Conservatives and 2 councillors each from Labour, the Lib Dems and Reform UK to the county council, so we can probably expect the hung council to continue. In 2025 Labour only won one seat outside Rugby in the whole of Warwickshire: that was Warwick West.

In Worcestershire, Worcester has come off the thirds cycle following an all-out election in 2024 which leaves Redditch as the only council in that county holding elections. After averting wipeout here in 2022, Labour won a large majority on Redditch council at all-out elections in 2024 with 21 seats against 5 Conservatives and 1 Green; three of the Labour councillors and one Conservative have since gone independent. This year Labour are notionally defending seven seats and the Conservatives and the Greens one each; last year Redditch was a clean sweep for Reform UK which won all eight of the borough's Worcestershire county council seats. Redditch will declare its results overnight.

In fact this should have been an unloseable election for Tamworth Labour, as if you add up the results since 2022 you get 18 Labour seats, 10 Conservatives, 1 independent and 1 Reform UK gain from Labour at a March 2026 by-election: Labour only won two seats in 2022, and if they lost the lot that would still leave 16 out of 30 and an overall majority. We will only get results for nine of Tamworth's ten wards overnight, as the poll in Wilnecote ward had to be postponed when the Labour candidate died.

Reform UK will also be heopful of big gains in Cannock Chase, where an all-out election in 2024 returned 21 Labour councillors against 10 Conservatives and 5 Greens. Reform UK have since gained two by-elections from Labour - one in Norton Canes ward, the other in Hednesford Green Heath - and they had a clean sweep here in the Staffordshire county elections by winning all seven seats. This year Labour are defending eight seats, the Conservatives three and the Green Party one.

The final West Midlands council up for election this year is Newcastle-under-Lyme, which holds whole-council elections on an unusual cycle. This was a two-party state in 2022 with the Conseratives defeating Labour by 25-19. Two subsequent by-elections in Knutton ward resulted in a Labour hold in September 2023 and a Reform UK gain in May 2025; the Reform UK councillor has also now resigned but thankfully there hasn't been time to organise a third by-election. Reform UK failed to sweep the board here in the 2025 Staffordshire county elections, as they won eight seats to one (Westlands, Thistleberry and Keele) for the Conservatives. Newcastle-under-Lyme is staggering its count, with the borough votes due to be declared on Friday but parish council elections are not counting until Monday: as a result, this is likely to be the last council to finalise its results.

South West

The South West is a relatively quiet region this year, with just four districts holding elections. We'll start in Gloucestershire, where half of Cheltenham's councillors will be renewed. Cheltenham had a whole council election in 2024 at which the Conservatives were wiped out: the Lib Dems won 36 seats against 3 for the Green Party and 1 for the People Against Bureaucracy. There was a similar story in the 2025 Gloucestershire county elections, when Cheltenham returned 9 Lib Dems and 1 Green. The Lib Dems successfully defended a Cheltenham council by-election last year in Charlton Kings ward and it would be a brave person who bet against another majority for them; but Cheltenham was the only Lib Dem district to request that its 2026 election be postponed, and it's always possible there might be a backlash from that.

The Cheltenham council election will be combined with a by-election to Gloucestershire county council in St Mark's and St Peter's division.

Over in Wiltshire, there will be a whole-council election in Swindon on new ward boundaries and Swindon will not go back to the thirds electoral cycle after this year. Swindon council currently has a Labour majority, although this has been dented a bit by defection losses to the Green Party and a Conservative by-election gain in Rodbourne Chaney ward in October 2024. The current composition has 34 Labour councillors against 16 Conservatives, 4 defectors from Labour to the Green Party, 2 ex-Labour independent councillors and one Lib Dems. Council elections here will be declared on Friday, with the count for parish elections to follow on Saturday.

Last year's Devon county councils in Exeter were a fragmented mess, with Labour managing the impressive feat of topping the poll across the city but failing to win a single seat on the county council: the city's Devon county council seats ended up going four to Reform UK, 3 to the Green Party, and 1 each to the Conservatives and Lib Dems, with Labour finishing less than 100 votes behind the winner in 5 divisions. Exeter will declare its results overnight.

The current council has 39 Labour seats against 8 independents (two ex-Labour, one ex-Conservative), 7 Conservatives, 2 Greens and a Lib Dem. Plymouth has a reputation for being politically volatile and Reform UK will fancy their chances in a large number of wards, but Labour did so well here in 2023 and 2024 that their majority this time is impregnable: they could lose all ten seats they are defending and still have a council majority. The Conservatives, on the other hand, are defending five of their remaining seven seats.

There is one other standalone by-election in this region, which is in the Mendip Hills ward of Somerset.

Eastern

We now move to the Eastern region, starting in Cambridgeshire. This is a particularly busy area this year, with one-third of Cambridge and Peterborough councils up together with the whole of Huntingdonshire and South Cambridgeshire districts. Only the east side of the county (East Cambridgeshire and Fenland districts) misses out on the fun.

Peterborough has been under no overall control since 2019, with Labour forming a minority administration after the Conservatives lost a large number of seats here in 2024. And it's a very precarious minority administration: following a large number of defections the Labour group of 14 seats (one of which is vacant) is opposed by 12 independent councillors, 10 Conservatives, 10 councillors in the breakaway/localist group Peterborough First which won the most seats here in the 2024 election, 8 Lib Dems, 5 Greens and a recent Reform UK by-election winner in Fletton and Woodston ward. The independent total includes the former Labour council leader Dennis Jones, who was forced out of that role last year after referring to grooming gang victims as "white trash from Rotherham"; he is up for re-election this year in Dogsthorpe ward. Labour won six seats in 2022 but defection losses mean they are only defending three; mind, in a fragmented council like this one any seat loss (or gain) could be crucial. Peterborough is outside the Cambridgeshire county council area, so no hints are available from that source; but we shouldn't have too long to wait for the results because this is an overnight count.

South of Peterborough we come to Huntingdonshire, where the Conservatives lost their majority in 2022 and the council is run by a coalition of everybody else under a Lib Dem leader. The 2022 result was 22 Conservatives, 11 independents, 10 Lib Dems, 4 Labour, 4 councillors in the St Neots Independent Group (which is now defunct) and one Green. The Conservatives have lost two further seats in 2024 by-elections, Great Paxton to the Lib Dems and St Neots Eatons to an independent candidate, and the Lib Dems have also picked up a number of defectors to trim the Conservative lead to 18-16. However Huntingdonshire was the district where the Conservatives performed best in the 2025 Cambridgeshire county elections, with the district's representation having 6 Conservative councillors out of a total of 10 across the county, 5 Lib Dems, 3 Reform UK, 2 independents, and a fluke Labour win with just 23% of the vote in the unlikely territory of St Ives South and Needingworth. In these days of five or more party politics, results like this are getting more and more common.

The Lib Dems have a large majority on South Cambridgeshire council where they swept the board in 2025. This year Cambridge offers more of interest after an unusually good Liberal Democrat performance in the county elections last year: the city returned 5 Lib Dem county councillors, 4 Labour and 3 Greens. The Labour majority on Cambridge council currently stands at 23 seats against 10 Lib Dems, 6 Greens, 1 Conservative and 1 independent; Labour are defending 10 of the 14 seats up for election this year, including all five of the wards which currently have split political representation (Castle, King's Hedges, Newnham and the two Chesterton wards).

The Eastern region includes four of the nine councils which had their elections postponed in 2025 for no particularly good reason. We now come to the first of these, Norfolk county council, which finally gets a much-needed new division map this year: these new boundaries were originally supposed to come in for the last election in 2021 to replace a map from 2005, but the boundary review failed to report in time after being blown off course by the pandemic. The 2021 elections, held in a good Conservative year, returned 58 Conservatives against 12 Labour councillors, 8 Lib Dems, 3 Greens and 3 independents; in by-elections over the last five years the Lib Dems have gained 2 seats nett, Reform UK have gained 2 seats, the Greens have gained 1, 1 has been gained by an independent, Labour have lost 1 and the Conservatives have lost 5. The Tories performed very poorly in Norfolk at the 2024 general election, finishing with just three seats against three for Labour (who famously unseated Liz Truss in South West Norfolk), one for the Lib Dems, one for Reform UK and half a seat for the Green Party, whose Waveney Valley constituency straddles the Norfolk-Suffolk boundary. This poll is Reform UK's first major chance in the county.

The county elections are combined with an ordinary election for one-third of Norwich council, which might well be the last poll on the city's present anachronistic boundaries. Yes, the city boundary really does look like that: many of Norwich's outer suburbs are in other districts, while the long thin string of Thorpe Hamlet ward to the east reflects that Norwich city council has harbour rights over a long stretch of the River Wensum. Norwich is currently run by a minority Labour administration, with 19 seats against 16 Green seats (one of which is vacant); the balance of power is held by 3 Lib Dems and an ex-Labour independent. This year Labour are defending seven of the thirteen wards, including Mile Cross and Wensum which both voted Green in 2024; a repeat of that result would see the Greens become the largest party.

There is also a by-election taking place to Great Yarmouth council in Caister South ward.

Similar themes to Norfolk apply with the postponed election to Suffolk county council, including the delayed boundary review. Here the 2021 elections returned 55 Conservatives against 9 Green Party county councillors, 5 Labour, 4 Lib Dems, 1 independent and 1 candidate nominated by the West Suffolk Independents. The Conservatives have lost four seats in by-elections since then, two to Reform UK (who have also picked up four other defectors) and one each to Labour and the Lib Dems. Labour actually won the most seats here in the 2024 general election, with four (including the unlikely gains of Bury St Edmunds and Suffolk Coastal) to three for the Conservatives and one-half for the Green Party; that was a while ago, though. There is also an election for one-third of Ipswich council, which has an impregnable Labour majority; Ipswich will count both its borough and county results overnight. Long-term by-election watchers might want to take particular attention of results in the Central Suffolk and North Ipswich constituency, whose Conservative MP Patrick Spencer is scheduled to stand trial this summer on sexual assault charges; he has had the Conservative whip withdrawn pending the outcome of the trial.

Essex saw some of the most bizarre decisions over postponements of the 2026 elections, as several districts here requested postponements but the county council wanted to go ahead with its poll that had already been postponed once from 2025. Farage's seat might well find its local government administered from England's oldest and newest city, Colchester, in the fairly near future. Southend's count will take place overnight.

Reform UK won two by-elections in this district last year, gaining one seat from the Wickford Independents and the other from Labour; both by-election winners will need to seek re-election this year alongside five Labour councillors, four Conservatives, two independents and one Wickford Independent.

Brentwood district, at the eastern end of the Elizabeth Line, is run by a Lib Dem-Labour coalition after all-out elections in 2024 returned 19 Conservatives, 17 Lib Dems and 3 Labour councillors. The Brentwood returning officer has decided to stagger the count: the district results will be declared overnight with the county results to follow on Friday.

All-out elections in Epping Forest in 2024 returned a narrow Conservative majority, with 29 councllors against 13 for the Loughton Residents Association, 7 Lib Dems, 3 independents, 1 Labour and 1 Green; but some recent defections have tipped the Conservative administration into a minority with 26 seats. One of the defectors is up for re-election this year in Waltham Abbey West, while the Conservatives are defending five of the seven wards which returned split representation in 2024.

Last year Reform UK gained a by-election from Labour in Mark Hall ward; there have been no defections to report, which makes a refreshing change. The only ward which is split Conservative-Labour is Latton Bush and Stewards, which the Conservatives are defending this year. Harlow will declare both its district and county results overnight.

A Conservative party in a better polling position might have hoped to strike back here - the Tories are only defending four seats - but a poor outing here in 2025 will give them pause for thought. In the 2025 Hertfordshire county elections Welwyn Hatfield elected three Lib Dems, two Conservatives, two Reform UK and one Labour county councillor.

We still have an election for a third of Stevenage council which has been in Labour hands continuously since the 1974 reorganisation. A whole-council election in 2024 returned 32 Labour seats against 6 Lib Dems and 1 Conservative. Reform UK have since gained two seats from Labour, one in a by-election in Roebuck ward and one by defection, and in the 2025 Hertfordshire county elections Labour were wiped out in Stevenage as Reform UK won five seats to one for the Lib Dems. We move into the South East region by way of the New City of Milton Keynes, which gets new ward boundaries this year. Labour gained overall control here in May 2024 after many years of a hung council, and three by-elections later that year saw no change. The Labour majority is small - they have 30 seats against 18 Lib Dems and 9 Conservatives - and with a whole council election it won't take much for the council to fall back into no overall control.

In Oxfordshire, Cherwell council is quite balanced with 17 Lib Dem councillors, 12 Conservatives, 11 Labour seats (one of which is vacant), 4 Greens and 4 independent seats (one of which is vacant). The Lib Dems and Greens run a minority coalition. In last year's Oxfordshire county results Cherwell returned ten Lib Dem county councillors, three Labour, two Conservatives and a Green. This year the Lib Dems are only defending four of the eighteen seats up for election, and while overall control might be a stretch too far this time (it would probably involve winning seats in Banbury, where the party are weak) we can expect the Lib Dem/Green coalition to reach majority status.

West Oxfordshire is another balanced council, with scores on the doors currently at 21 Lib Dems, 13 Conservatives, 10 Labour, 4 Greens and 1 Reform UK; the Lib Dems lead a traffic-light coalition. This is the last example of a council which elects by thirds but which does not have a ward map with predominantly three councillors per ward: most West Oxfordshire wards only have one or two councillors, and so several wards are not up for election at each year's annual elections. The pattern is fairly random, as we can see from the 2022 map.

Even though they polled fewer votes across the district than the Conservatives in 2022, the Lib Dems won eight out of 16 wards to the Conservatives' four. The eight seats the Lib Dems are defending this year are not the same eight that they won in 2022: the councillor for Carterton North West is now in Reform UK, while the Lib Dems have gained Chipping Norton from Labour in a by-election. In 2025, when all of the district was up for election, the Conservatives won five county council seats to four for the Lib Dems and two for Labour.

Oxford city council has turned into a bit of mess in this decade. The Labour group of 21 runs the city as a minority against 9 Greens, 9 Lib Dems, 4 seats for the Independent Oxford Alliance which opposes Low Traffic Neighbourhoods and other traffic reduction measures, 3 independent councillors who were elected on the Labour ticket and quit the party over Gaza, and 2 independent councillors who were elected as such in Temple Cowley ward. Reading council, which will count its votes overnight, has a Labour majority of 32 seats (one of which is vacant) out of 48. The 16 opposition councillors are split 8 for the Green Party, 3 Conservatives, 3 Lib Dems and one Reform UK councillor: that's the former BBC newsreader and journalist Clarence Mitchell, who was leader of the Conservative group before he jumped ship in January. Mitchell is up for re-election this year in Emmer Green ward, and to secure re-election he'll have to do better than his old party did in the 2024 Reading election when they failed to win a single seat.

Reading council is another one whose boundaries are anachronistic in that it doesn't cover the whole of the Reading urban area. Some of the town's western suburbs are in West Berkshire where there are no elections this year, while much of the eastern side of the built-up area is covered by the bizarrely-drawn Wokingham district. This district had an all-out election in 2024 on new ward boundaries, with the Liberal Democrats coming out with 27 seats - half of the council - against 19 Conservatives and 8 Labour. A subsequent by-election gain from the Conservatives in Maiden Erlegh and Whitegates ward gave the Lib Dems a majority on Wokingham council which they will now seek to defend. The postponed 2025 election to Hampshire county council is taking place now, and all of Hampshire's county results will be counted overnight. Like many counties this was a big Conservative win in 2021, with 56 seats against 17 Lib Dems, 3 Labour, 1 independent and 1 councillor from the Whitehill and Bordon Community Party. In by-elections since then the Conservatives have lost two seats in Winchester district, one to the Greens (Meon Valley) and the other to the Lib Dems (Bishops Waltham). In July 2024 the Conservatives won 9½ seats here (counting Farnham and Bordon, which straddles the Surrey boundary, as the half) to three for the Lib Dems and two for Labour.

Located in the north-east corner of the county, Rushmoor covers the military towns of Farnborough and Aldershot, which have suddenly swung a long way to the left in the last few years. Rushmoor's count is being staggered, with the county results being declared overnight and the borough votes to follow on Friday.

The neighbouring Hart district, which is based on Fleet and nearby towns, is hung and will remain so. The Liberal Democrats are the largest party with 12 seats and they have a longstanding coalition agreement with the Community Campaign (Hart) who have 11 seats; in opposition are 9 Conservatives and an independent. However, the longstanding Lib Dem and Community Campaign electoral pact has broken down, and the Liberal Democrats are standing this year in three of the wards being defended by the Community Campaign. The exception is Fleet Central ward, which throws up the intriguing possibility that the Liberal Democrats have instead entered into an electoral pact with .. the Official Monster Raving Loony Party, whose leader Alan Hope lives in Fleet Central ward and is standing there in the latest of his many, many election campaigns. Both the district and county votes in Hart will be counted overnight.

Current proposals for reorganisation in Hampshire involve amalgamating the previous two districts with Basingstoke and Deane. This is a very messy hung council. The ruling coalition is made up of 12 Basingstoke and Deane Independents, 11 Lib Dems, 4 other independents, 2 Greens and one councillor who was originally elected for the now-defunct Women's Equality Party and has now formed the All In Party. In opposition are 14 Conservatives (including two vacancies), 10 Labour councillors and a defector to Reform UK. Of the councillors elected in 2022, the Labour councillor for Brookvale and Kings Furlong is now in the Greens; the Conservative councillor for Hatch Warren and Beggarwood is now an independent; and the Labour councillor for Popley is now in the Basingstoke and Deane Indepndents. Confused? You will be. And there's plenty of time to be confused, because Basingstoke and Deane will count its district votes on Friday but its county votes overnight.

Further down the M3 corridor, the Lib Dems have impregnable majorities in Winchester and Eastleigh districts. Southampton council is run by Labour, who currently have 31 councillors against 9 Conservatives, 7 Lib Dems, 2 Greens, 1 Reform UK and 1 independent. The seats up here are from all-out elections in 2023. There are four changes to the above map: this year the Conservatives are defendng Peartree ward, while the Labour councillor due up in Coxford has defected to the Lib Dems, the Labour councillor for Redbridge has gone independent and the Conservative councillor for Sholing has defected to Reform UK. As such Labour are defending 9 seats, including Shirley ward where the Liberal Democrats have won two by-elections in the last two years, and they need to hold 4 of them to keep control of the council. This is an overnight count.

Further down the Solent, the Conservatives have a large majority in Fareham where a whole-council election in 2024 returned 22 Conservatives, 8 Lib Dems, 1 Labour and 1 independent. This might be Reform UK-proof. Gosport, which like Fareham is counting overnight and re-elects half of its council every two years, is another matter. This council has a minority Lib Dem administration, with 13 seats, facing 11 Conservatives, 2 Labour, 1 independent and 1 Reform UK councillor, who gained Bridgemary ward from the Lib Dems in a by-election last January. Reform UK will fancy their chances in a few other wards here.

On the far side of the harbour is the city of Portsmouth. Again, this is a minority Lib Dem administration. Unusually for a council which didn't go to the polls in 2025, Portsmouth already has a sizeable Reform UK group: they have essentially absorbed the Portsmouth Independent Party, who won a significant number of seats here over the last electoral cycle. A by-election in Paulsgrove ward last year was a Reform UK gain from PIP. The current composition has 18 Lib Dem councillors against 10 Reform UK, 9 Labour, 4 Conservatives and an independent. More than half of the Labour group is up for re-election this year and the party would appear to be in serious trouble: Labour are defending five seats, the Lib Dems four, Reform UK three (including Hilsea ward, where the outgoing councillor has defected from the Lib Dems) and the Conservatives two. Portsmouth's results are being declared overnight.

Portsmouth is a major embarkation point for the Isle of Wight, which had its 2025 election postponed pending reorganisation in a genuinely baffling decision. There is very little you can realistically do to change the structure of Wight's local government, as central government eventually conceded when they proposed that reorganisation of Hampshire's local authorities leave the island's governance as it is.

The 2021 elections here returned a hung council with 18 Conservatives, 13 independents, 2 Greens, 2 councillors from the Independent Network and one each for Labour, the Liberal Democrats, "Our Island" and the Vectis Party. The presence of three separate localist parties doesn't inspire confidence in the ability of the island's politicians to play nicely with each other, and this has been borne out in Wight being run for the last five years by a rather precarious independent minority administration. In by-elections over the last five years the Lib Dems and Reform UK have gained two seats each, the Independent Network have gained one, independents have lost one, the Vectis Party lost their only seat and the Conservatives are down three. Reform UK actually won three by-elections rather than two, but one of them was to replace the winner of a previous by-election who resigned after just a few months. In the 2024 general election Isle of Wight West voted Labour and Isle of Wight East voted Conservative - previously the island had been a single, oversized, constituency. The candidate list for this election includes the only two-candidate fight in the country: Parkhurst and Hunnyhill ward is a straight contest between the defending Lib Dems and Reform UK.

Our last Hampshire local authority is Havant council, where an all-out election in 2024 coincided with a Conservative meltdown to produce a rather balanced council. The Conservatives remained the largest party with 13 seats, but a traffic-light coalition of Labour (10), Lib Dem (7) and Green (4) councillors was formed to run the borough. The other two seats went to Reform UK, which was the first occasion Reform UK had ever won seats in an ordinary council election in their own right. Both Reform UK councillors face re-election this year (in the two Leigh Park wards) with the other seats up being 5 Conservative, 3 Labour and 2 Greens; for the Lib Dems, who have nothing to defend this year, the only way is up. Interestingly the three caolition partners have formed an electoral pact and have divided Havant's twelve wards equally between them, meaning that every ward has one Conservative, one Reform UK and ond administration candidate: this means there will be a guaranteed seat change, because Labour are not defending their seat in Hayling East ward where the administration candidate is a Green. Havant is an overnight count for both borough and county.

The West Sussex county council election postponed from 2025 is going ahead now amid a complete lack of consensus as to future local government for Sussex. In 2021 this area returned 48 Conservative county councillors against 10 Lib Dems, 9 Labour, 1 Green, 1 independent and a Local Alliance county councillor from Selsey. The Conservatives have lost three seats in by-elections since then, two to the Lib Dems and the other to an independent, and they were in third place here at the 2024 general election: the Lib Dems and Labour won three seats each in West Sussex, while the Conservatives won two-and-a-half (counting East Grinstead and Uckfield as the half). Moreover, the Conservatives have completely collapsed in the second-tier districts across both halves of Sussex.

We can see this in the two West Sussex coastal authorities up for election, Adur and Worthing. Worthing had never elected a Labour councillor before 2017; nine years on, Labour have an overall majority with 21 seats aganst 9 Conservatives, 3 members of the Worthing Community Independents who are a Labour splinter group, 2 Greens and 2 defectors from the Conservatives to Reform UK. Both of the Reform UK defectors are up for re-election this year and one of them took his seat in Heene ward off Labour in a by-election; this means Labour are defending seven out of 13 wards this year, and they need to hold five to preserve their majority.

Worthing council shares a returning officer and back-office staff with Adur council, which lies between Worthing and Brighton and is based on Shoreham-by-Sea. This council surprisingly returned a Labour majority in 2024 after the Conservative vote here went into meltdown, and what's left of the Conservative group has fallen apart so spectacularly that the Tories, who are supposed to be defending seven seats based on the 2022 result, only have two councillors left. The full composition at present is 17 Labour against 8 independents, 2 Greens and 2 Conservatives; Labour need to hold three of the five seats they are defending to preserve their majority.

The Conservative situation in Crawley is also rather dire. This is a two-party state with Labour presently leading the Conservatives 25-11; one of the Conservative seats is presently vacant. There have been no defections here (stands back in amazement). This is effectively an impregnable lead, because Labour are only defending seven seats from the 2022 result: if Labour lost the lot, they would still have half of the council and the mayoral casting vote.

This majority has since disappeared as a result of three Conservative by-election losses, one each to the Greens, the Lib Dems and Reform UK, and a couple of other defections have reduced the minority Conservative administation to 22 seats out of 51. Fourteen Labour councillors were returned here in the 2022 and 2024 elections, but the council composition now has 12 Greens, 9 Labour seats (one of which is vacant), 6 independents (all ex-Labour and affiliated with Your Party) and 5 Conservatives. All six independent/Your Party councillors are due for re-election this year, and the Green Party might reasonably see the resulting disarray in Labour ranks as a good opportunity to take overall control.

The Lib Dems successfully defended three by-elections last year and also did well in Tunbridge Wells district at the 2025 Kent county elections, winning 5 seats to just 1 (Cranbrook) for the Conservatives; this was the only Kent district where Reform UK, who control Kent county council, drew a blank. A continued Lib Dem majority looks like a reasonable shout.

We'll finish this tour of England outside London in the suburban territory of Surrey, where local government reorganisation is now being finalised. The scheduled elections to Surrey county council (postponed from 2025) and to Elmbridge, Mole Valley, Reigate and Banstead, Runnymede, Tandridge and Woking councils have been cancelled, with councillors' terms extended or further extended to abolition in April 2027. Instead we have two brand-new districts holding elections today.

East Surrey council is the successor to five present districts, those being Tandridge, Reigate and Banstead, Epsom and Ewell, Mole Valley and Elmbridge. This covers a number of outer commuter towns to the south of London, including the two Surrey districts (Epsom and Ewell, and Tandridge) which are dominated by Residents and/or independent councillors. Mole Valley council has a large Liberal Democrat majority, Elmbridge council is run by a Lib Dem and Residents coalition, while Reigate and Banstead has a minority Conservative administration with the Green Party as the main opposition. In the 2024 general election the Liberal Democrats won the constituencies of Dorking and Horley, Epsom and Ewell, and Esher and Walton with the Conservatives holding on to East Surrey and Reigate.

In the 2021 Surrey county council elections, which were held before the Conservative collapse in Surrey really started to set in, the Conservatives won 24 seats across the East Surrey area to 8 Residents, 3 Lib Dems, 2 Greens and an independent. By-elections since then have seen the Lib Dems gain two seats from the Conservatives. The ward boundaries for the new council will be based on the 36 divisions which were originally drawn up here for the cancelled 2025 Surrey county council election, but with two councillors per ward for a total of 72 councillors. The Lib Dems might fancy a go at this one given their strength in some of the districts, but no overall control might be the most likely outcome for the new East Surrey council.

The cancellation of the Surrey county council elections unlocks a by-election to the county council in Warlingham division.

There are also two by-elections to Surrey district council. Reigate and Banstead, Earlswood and Whitebushes; Guildford, Onslow.

Meanwhile West Surrey is the successor to six previous districts, specifically Waverley, Guildford, Surrey Heath, Woking, Runnymede and Spelthorne. Given the debts which have been built up by Woking and Spelthorne, this new council might end up being bankrupt from day one. None of the outgoing districts are under Conservative leadership: Waverley is run by a coalition of Lib Dems and independents, we have already discussed Guildford above, Surrey Heath and Woking have Lib Dem majorities, Runnymede is run by a five-way coalition of Labour, Lib Dem, Residents, Green and independent councillors, and Spelthorne district has a minority coalition of Lib Dem and independent councillors under an independent leader. It will be noted that the Lib Dems run all six of these districts in some form or another, and on current polling they should be favourites for an overall majority in West Surrey.

Looking at other recent elections in West Surrey, the 2024 general election saw the Lib Dems win three seats here (Guildford, Surrey Heath and Woking) and the Conservatives three-and-a-half (Spelthorne, Runnymede and Weybridge, Godalming and Ash, and the Farnham half of Farnham and Bordon). In the 2021 Surrey county council elections, the divisions in this area returned 25 Conservatives, 11 Lib Dems, 5 Residents, 2 Labour and 2 independents; in by-elections since then the Lib Dems have gained two seats from the Conservatives and one from Residents, while the Conservatives have lost a further seat to Reform UK. Again, the lines drawn for the cancelled Surrey county council elections will be drawn with two councillors per ward, for a total of 90 councillors.

London

We now turn to Greater London, where all the councillors in all 32 London boroughs are up for election at once. As such, the capital will form a major part of this year's local elections. Also up for election here are four local authority mayors.

The 2022 London borough elections saw the Labour party put in a very good performance. They won overall majorities in 21 boroughs, compared to five Conservative and three Lib Dem councils, an overall majority for Aspire in Tower Hamlets, and two councils under no overall control. One of those is Havering, which in 2022 returned 23 Conservatives, 23 councillors from various Residents Associations, and 9 Labour councillors holding the balance of power; the Residents and Labour formed a coalition. There has been one by-election since, a hold for the Residents in Upminster ward. Havering is the eastern end of Greater London, taking in Romford, Upminster and a number of former GLC overspill estates which used to have a significant populist right vote and may well do so again this year. In many ways this is the least metropolitan of the London boroughs, and all other things being equal we might expect it to behave more like the Essex borough of Thurrock which it borders; but Thurrock doesn't have organised Residents Associations, which introduce an extra level of uncertainty here. Another wrinkle is that Andrew Rosindell, the long-serving Conservative MP for Romford, is now in Reform UK. Havering will count its votes overnight.

It's now nearly 18 years since anybody other than Labour won an election in Barking and Dagenham, and at the last London borough elections in 2022 a massive 69% of the votes were cast for Labour candidates. That doesn't mean the borough isn't in play. There have been strong votes here for the radical right before, most notably in 2006 when the British National Party won 12 seats despite only contesting seven of the borough's then seventeen wards. Barking and Dagenham has seen a lot of demographic change in the two decades since then and its population is much less white and more diverse now, but some of that vote will still be there. Meanwhile Labour have some competition on the left here for once. In 2022 many wards in Barking and Dagenham only had Labour and Conservative candidates, and Labour were guaranteed 14 seats before a vote had been cast due to insufficient opposition candidates standing: this year every ward has a full slate of Labour, Conservative and Reform UK candidates, the Lib Dems are standing one candidate in every ward and the Greens (who have polled decently in a couple of recent by-elections here) have their targets. More choice for the electors is a good thing; as to whether this translates into a more politically diverse council, we shall have to wait and see.

The neighbouring Redbridge council has also seen major demographic change in this century, resulting in it swinging a mile to the left politically. Redbridge had a Conservative majority continuously until 1994 and again from 2002 to 2009, but Labour won a majority here for the first time in 2014 and increased their lead over the Conservatives to 58-5 at the 2022 elections. The council leader Jas Athwal was elected in 2024 as the Labour MP for Ilford South and resigned his council seat; the resulting by-election in Mayfield ward returned Ilford Independents candidate Noor Begum who had come a strong second to Athwal in the general election. Meanwhile Wes Streeting was run very close in Ilford North at the same election by another independent candidate, Leanne Mohamad. Despite the large Labour majority here, the party could be in trouble against an organised independent challenge.

One of the most unexpected results of the 2024 general election was the re-election of long-serving Conservative MP Iain Duncan Smith in the Chingford and Woodford Green constituency, after the prospective Labour candidate Faiza Shaheen was deselected at the last minute and ran a spoiler campaign which split the Labour vote. Mind, perhaps we shouldn't have been too surprised given the 2022 results in Waltham Forest, in which Labour won 47 seats to 13 Conservatives who are strongly concentrated in wards within Duncan Smith's constituency. Three of the Conservative councilors are now in Reform UK, while three Labour councillors have gone independent. It's results in Leyton and Walthamstow which will determine whether Labour are re-elected here.

Newham council has a similar dynamic to Redbridge in that Labour have been challenged here in recent years by a Newham Independents slate, who have won three of the eight council by-elections which have taken place in this borough since 2022. This includes an easy independent win in the most recent by-election, in Plaistow South ward last September. The Green Party also got a foothold here in 2022 by gaining the brand-new (in every sense) Stratford Olympic Park ward. But it's the Newham mayoralty which is important for political control here, and Labour are defending a large majority: in 2022 Rokhsana Fiaz was re-elected for her second term in the first round with 56% of the vote, with the Conservatives best of the rest on 12%.

Fiaz is standing down and the defending Labour candidate is Forhad Hussain, who is seeking to return to elected politics after some years away: Hussain, who is an engineer by trade and is currently working on the Lower Thames Crossing project, previously served as a councillor for Plaistow North ward from 2010 to 2018 and served in the cabinet of former mayor Sir Robin Wales. One of the candidates for Plaistow North this time round is the Conservatives' mayoral candidate Terri Bloore, who is standing for election for the first time. The Green Party have selected Areeq Chowdhury, a resarcher in the technology sector who was elected in 2022 as a Labour councillor for Canning Town North ward before defecting to the Greens; another councillor on the ballot is the Newham Independents' Mehmood Mirza, who finished in fourth place with 8.5% at the last Newham mayoral election as an independent candidate and subsequently won a 2023 by-election in Boleyn ward, where he is also seeking re-election; the Lib Dem candidate Laura Willoughby, who is also standing for the council in Stratford Olympic Park ward, is a former Islington councillor with an MBE to her name who runs a "mindful drinking" movement promoting low- and non-alcoholic drinks; the Christian Peoples Alliance, who had Newham councillors many years ago, have nominated pastor Bharath Swamy who is also standing for the council in Wall End ward; Reform UK's Clive Furness has 21 years of previous service as a Newham Labour councillor, latterly representing Canning Town North ward from 2002 to 2018; and Kamran Malik, who stood in the 2010 and 2014 Newham mayoral elections before serving two spells in prison for providing immigration advice and services which he was not qualified to provide, is now eligible to stand as a councillor again (it's more than five years since his last imprisonment) and he is back on the Newham mayoral ballot for his Communities United Party.

Crossing the Lea from Newham we come to the 21st Century Rotten Borough of Tower Hamlets, which looks set to be just as rotten this time round. In 2022 the ele/cted mayoralty was recovered by the monstrously corrupt Lutfur Rahman, who had served his five-year disqualification after being ejected by the Election Court in 2015 for the most fraudulent UK election campaign of modern times. Rahman defeated the outgoing Labour mayor John Biggs by 47-33 in the first round, a margin which closed to 55-45 in the runoff. His Aspire party also won an overall majority on Tower Hamlets council, with 24 councillors against 19 Labour, 1 Green and 1 Conservative. There has been one by-election since then, in Bow East ward in September 2024, which had no Aspire candidate - Aspire's support is concentrated in the borough's Bangladeshi community, and Bow East has a low Bangladeshi population - and which Labour held with the Greens in second place. In this election Bow East ties with Lansbury and Whitechapel wards for the distinction of this year's longest ballot paper: all three of those wards have 22 candidates chasing three seats.

Lutfur Rahman is standing for re-election on the Aspire ticket for his fourth term of office (including the voided 2014 election), and he is bottom of the alphabetical ballot paper. This time Labour have selected veteran councillor Sirajul Islam, who is leader of the council's Labour group where he represents Bethnal Green East ward; Islam is not seeking re-election to the council after 24 years in office, so this election is very much win or bust for him. The Liberal Democrats are seeking to defend third place from last time with their new candidate Mohammed Abdul Hannan, a telecom network engineer who is also standing for the council in Whitechapel ward; the Conservatives' Dominic Nolan, who is also a council candidate in Canary Wharf ward, is an actuary who has previous local government experience, serving from 2017 to 2021 as a Fife councillor for St Andrews before he moved to London for work; Hugo Pierre of the Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition, who is also on the council ballot in Whitechapel, is back for another go after standing unsuccessfully for mayor in 2018 and 2022; the Tower Hamlets Independents, a new party who have impressively fielded a near-full slate of council candidates, haev nominated barrister Zami Ali; the Reform UK candidate is John Bullard who is also standing for the council in Bromley North ward; the Greens have entered the fray with their candidate Hirra Khan Adeogun, who co-leads an environmental charity and is also a council candidate in Bromley North; and independent candidate Terence McGrenera, a journalist who campaigns on housing matters, last came to the attention of this column when he stood in a 2019 by-election for Lansbury ward for his then political party - The House Party.

Tower Hamlets will count the mayoral votes on Friday, with the council elections to declare on Saturday. Hopefully there won't be a repeat of the chaos we saw at the 2014 count, when Bromley South ward didn't finish counting until 8:30pm on the Monday after the election.

To the north of Tower Hamlets we have a third mayoral election in Hackney, another very safe Labour council but this time with little history of recent electoral fraud. The 2022 council elections here returned 50 Labour councillors, 5 Conservatives, who have a base in the Hasidic enclave of Stamford Hill, and 2 Greens.

At the same time the Labour mayor Philip Glanville was re-elected for his third term of office in the first round with a 59-17 lead over the Greens' Zoë Garbett. Glanville was then forced to resign in 2023 over his association with Tom Dewey, who had been elected in May 2022 as a Labour councillor for De Beauvoir ward but resigned almost immediately: the reason for this resignation was not disclosed at the time, but Dewey was subsequently prosecuted for possessing indecent images of children. The mayoral by-election to replace Dewey took place in November 2023 and was won by Labour's Caroline Woodley with 50% of the vote against 24% for the Greens' Garbett and 14% for the Conservatives. Labour then embarrasingly lost the by-election to fill Woodley's previous council seat in Cazenove ward to the Conservatives, while a more recent by-election in Stoke Newington ward in September 2024 was gained by the Green Party. That put the Greens up to four seats on the council, while four other Labour councillors have walked out and three of them have formed the Hackney Independent Socialist council group. Hackney is staggering its count, with the mayoral votes to be counted overnight followed by the council election on Friday.

Neighbouring Islington might be one to watch at this election. This council has also had issues with a Labour councillor who resigned after a very short term in mysterious circumstances which have subsequently become less mysterious. Ollie Steadman, who won a May 2024 by-election in Hillrise ward, lasted just two months before handing in his notice and a second by-election had to be held in August 2024 to replace him; Steadman is now awaiting trial on blackmail and communications offence charges arising from the so-called Westminster honeytrap scandal. In between those two by-elections the former Labour party leader Jeremy Corbyn was re-elected in his Islington North constituency as an independent MP. Corbyn is now the leader of Your Party, who haven't got their act together enough to actually stand official candidates in this year's Islington election: they have endorsed three independent candidates, but that's not quite te same thing. Labour are defending a large majority in Islington from 2022, when they defeated the Greens by 48-3. Four Labour councillors have since quit the party and joined forces with the Greens to form an Independent and Green group on the council; they have also formed the Islington Community Independents candidate, which is standing nine candidates. This relative lack of left-wing independent activity suggests that the Greens might be the main challengers here.

One council where the Greens have recently gained a foothold is Haringey, which includes the Tottenham constituency of the justice secretary David Lammy. Labour led the Lib Dems here in 2022 by 50-7 and have held five out of six by-elections since then; but the most recent poll here was a Green Party gain in St Ann's ward in April 2025. The Green Party have also picked up a defector from Labour here, and they have joined forces with two other ex-Labour councillors in a Green Socialist Alliance group on the council.

Further up the Lea Valley, Labour won all three parliamentary seats for Enfield in 2024 but they lost ground here in the 2022 borough elections, with the Conservatives cutting the Labour majority to 38-25. Three Labour councillors have since gone independent. It will be interesting to see what happens here this time, particularly given the announcement of a potential new town at Crews Hill - which would be located within the present Whitewebbs and Ridgeway wards, and might well trigger a boundary review here in the near future.

Mind, for the Conservatives in outer north London Barnet forms a more iconic target. This council voted Labour in 2022 for the first time with a 41-22 lead over the Conservatives; since then both parties have successfully defended three by-elections each. Defections mean that there is now a Green, an independent and a Reform UK councillor, while the former Conservative council leader Daniel Thomas has recently resigned his seat in Finchley Church End ward and then turned up shortly afterwards as the Welsh leader of Reform UK. There has not been time to hold a by-election for Thomas' council seat, which remains vacant going into this election. Labour performed well here in the 2024 general election, winning all three of Barnet's parliamentary seats.

Next to Barnet lies another closely-fought outer London council: the borough of Harrow, which was an against-the-trend Conservative gain in the 2022 elections: the Conservative lead on the council is 31-24. Harrow is the only London borough where there have been no by-elections in this council term so no further information is available from that source, but in July 2024 the Harrow East Conservative MP Bob Blackman was not only re-elected but was the only Conservative MP to poll more than 50% of the vote, gaining a 4% swing in his favour from Labour in a result completely out of kilter with everything else that was going on. This is clearly an artefact of the swing to the Conservatives among British Hindus in recent years, and it will be interesting to see if this persists now that Rishi Sunak is no longer running the show.

Next to Harrow we have Hillingdon borough, which combines right-wing areas of outer London like Uxbridge with more working-class areas like Hayes and with the daily chaos that is Heathrow Airport. This borough was quite close in 2022, but the Conservatives were re-elected with a 30-23 lead over Labour; the Tories then held the Uxbridge and South Ruislip parliamentary by-election the following year following the final disgrace of Boris Johnson, but Labour then gained the seat at the 2024 general election. There has only been one Hillingdon council by-election in this term, a Conservative hold in Hillingdon East ward. The Tories might well be worried about the Reform UK threat here, particularly in the rather isolated Harefield Village ward at Greater London's north-east corner; Labour might be worried about the threat from the Hayes Independent Party, a Labour splinter group which currently has five councillors. In the event of a close result we might have to wait until June for certainty as to who will be running the show, because the election in Hillingdon West ward has been postponed following the death of a Reform UK candidate.

Crossing to the other side of Heathrow Airport brings us to Hounslow, where Labour are defending a large majority of 52-10 over the Conservatives. This has now fallen to 41-10: Labour lost a by-election in Syon and Brentford Lock ward last year to an independent, six Labour councillors have quit the party with five going independent and one ending up in the Grens, and three Labour councillors have recently died and one more has resigned leaving four vacant seats. As can be seen from the map, the Conservative vote in this borough is strongly concentrated in Chiswick; Labour might instead see Reform UK as their main threat, particularly in the Feltham area. Hounslow is now represented in the London Assembly by a Liberal Democrat, as part of the South West constituency, but the Lib Dem strength in south-west London lies elsewhere. Sadly we won't get the chance to vote for Umesh Kumar, who put in nomination papers to stand simultaneously in Heston East ward for the Lib Dems and in Heston West ward for Reform UK: unfortunately, standing in simultaneous elections to the same council is against the rules, and once Kumar was rumbled the returning officer disqualified him in both wards.

Labour may be struggling with Hindu voters at the moment, but they retain stronger support in London's Sikh community and this contributes to the large Labour majority in Ealing. The 2022 result here gave 59 Labour councillors against 6 Lib Dems and 5 Conservatives; the Lib Dems have since picked up a Conservative seat in a by-election in Hanger Hill ward plus a defection gain from Labour. Ealing will declare its results overnight.

Three-party politics in Brent - in 2022 Labour won 49 seats against 5 Conservatives and 3 Lib Dems - has now become four-party politics after five Labour councillors crossed the floor to the Green Party in December. Labour has also lost defectors here to independent, to Your Party and even to the Conservatives. What the voters of Wembley and Willesden make of all this is yet to be seen.

Next to Brent is the borough of Camden. Labour did well here in 2022, winning 47 seats agianst 4 Lib Dems, 3 Conservatives and one Green Party councillor - that was Siân Berry in Highgate ward, who is now the MP for Brighton Pavilion. By-elections since 2022 have seen wins for all four parties, with the Lib Dems taking seats off Labour in Hampstead Town and West Hampstead wards. There are no defections at all to report here, whch makes a refreshing change. Camden is the borough which which contains the Holborn and St Pancras constituency of the Prime Minister, Sir Keir Starmer, so any Labour losses here would be particularly embarrassing for the party.

The Prime Minister's central London constituency borders the city of Westminster, which returned a Labour majority in 2022 for the very first time. It was a fairly close result, with Labour defeating the Conservatives by 31-23. The Tories have since taken two by-elections off Labour in West End and Vincent Square wards, while Reform UK have taken a defector off both parties. With 29 councillors remaining out of a possible 54, Labour cannot afford any further losses here.

Westminster's elections often feature notable candidates. This year's Conservative slate includes the former Bedfordshire Police and Crime Commissioner (and losing Conservative candidate in the Mid Bedfordshire by-election) Festus Akinbusoye in Abbey Road ward, the former Shrewsbury and Atcham MP Daniel Kawczynski in Maida Vale ward, and the former Evening Standard editor (and sister-in-law of David Cameron) Emily Sheffield in Bayswater ward. Not to be outdone, the Green Party have nominated Ted "Hapax legomenon" Loveday, who was the star player on the Gonville and Caius, Cambridge team which won University Challenge in 2015, on their slate in Little Venice ward. Votes here will be counted overnight.

Also declaring its results overnight is the one London borough where the Conservatives can probably feel absolutely secure in their majority. This is Kensington and Chelsea, which is surely far too affluent to vote Reform UK; the borough might now have two Labour MPs, but that was in landslide conditions for the Labour Party. Things were more normal here in 2022 when the Tories won 35 seats against 13 Labour and 2 Lib Dem councillors. The Labour group has since fallen apart, and bizarrely one of their councillors has ended up in the very right-wing outfit Advance UK.

The ruling Labour group has managed to remain relatively united in our last North London borough Hammersmith and Fulham, reducing from a 40-10 lead over the Conservatives to 36-10 thanks to two defections to the Greens, a third councillor going independent and the recent resignation of a fourth councillor which has left a vacant seat in Hammersmith Broadway ward. Three by-elections since 2022 have resulted in two Labour holds and one Conservative hold. This is an overnight count.

Over the river, via whichever Thames bridge is open this week, we come to Wandsworth which until 2022 was the land where Normal Rules Did Not Apply. After decades of the local Conservatives defying political gravity here, Labour took overall control of Wandsworth in 2022 with 35 seats against 22 Conservatives and independent councillor Malcolm Grimston of West Hill ward. The Conservatives have got one seat back since then, in a 2024 by-election in the marginal West Putney ward; there are quite a lot of other marginal wards in Wandsworth, and it wouldn't take much for the council to swing back. Wandsworth is counting overnight, and its elections team have a reputation for delivering swift and efficient results.

In London Assembly elections Wandsworth is paired with Merton, based on Morden and Wimbledon, which has a small Labour majority: the 2018 election returned 31 Labour councillors, 17 Lib Dems, 7 Conservatives and 2 residents' councillors for Merton Park ward. The Conservatives actually polled more votes than the Liberal Democrats across the borough, but they were in second place nearly everywhere which is fatal in a first-post-the-post context: the only ward the Tories carried was Village, the super-affluent area around the All England tennis club. The Labour councillors are concentrated in the Mitcham and Morden parliamentary seat, while the Wimbledon constituency was a Lib Dem gain in the 2024 general election; the only two by-elections in the borough since 2022 were on the undercard of that poll, with Labour holding seats in Figge's Marsh and St Helier wards. Merton counts its votes overnight.

There will be another overnight count in the neighbouring borough of Richmond upon Thames, which is famously the only London borough to include territory from both sides of the river. The Liberal Democrats swept the board here in 2022, topping the poll in all 18 wards; the council seats split 48 to the Lib Dems, 5 to the Green Party, and one to veteran Conservative councillor Godfrey Samuel. He died in 2023, and the Liberal Democrats then gained the resulting by-election in Hampton North ward to leave Richmond and Twickenham as a Tory-free zone. All five of the Green Party councillors won without Lib Dem opposition as part of an electoral pact, which has now broken down: this has prompted the Greens to stand a full slate of 54 candidates this time, but an error by the election agent means that 39 of them will appear on the ballot paper with a Green Party emblem against their name but no description. Confused? You might be.

The neighbouring borough of Kingston upon Thames is mostly the constituency of the Lib Dem party leader Sir Ed Davey, so any Lib Dem backslide here could be embarrassing - and there will be quite a turnover of councillors at this election with 19 Lib Dems standing down. The 2022 results here gave 44 seats to the Lib Dems against 3 Conservatives and 1 councillor from the Kingston Independent Residents Group: that's James Giles in Green Lane and St James ward, who has since thrown his lot in with a number of left-wing and pro-Palestine MPs: he managed George Galloway's win in the 2024 Rochdale by-election before becoming one of the people involved in setting up Your Party. The Kingston Independent Residents Group have since gained a by-election from the Lib Dems in Giles' ward and picked up a defector to become the official opposition here, but time will tell whether campaigning on Gaza works as well in Richmond as it did in Rochdale.

Next-door Sutton is one of the longest-standing Lib Dem councils in the country with continuous majority control since 1990. Despite this, and despite the Lib Dem gain of both of the borough's Parliamentary seats in 2024, Sutton's demographic is an increasingly poor fit for the Liberal Democrat vote: it's much less affluent than Kingston and Richmond. Although, to be fair, that's not difficult. The Conservatives have been talking up their chances in Sutton for years without ever managing to get over the line: in 2022, which was the closest borough election here for years, the Lib Dems just held their majority with 29 seats against 20 Conservatives, 3 Labour councillors and 3 independents in Beddington ward. The Lib Dems rather easily defended two seats in by-elections last year, while a 2024 by-election in St Helier West ward saw the defending Labour vote collapse and a gain for the Conservatives. The St Helier estate is another area of outer London where Reform UK might fancy their chances this year. Sutton will count its votes overnight

Croydon in 2022 became the latest (and probably the last) council to go over to the elected mayoral system, following a well-publicised bankruptcy in the 2018-22 term. That happened on Labour's watch, and the 2022 mayoral election turned in a very close result. In the first round the Conservatives' Jason Perry led with 35% of the vote against 33% for Labour and 10% for the Lib Dems; in the runoff Perry defeated the Labour candidate Val Shawcross by 50.4% to 49.6%, a majority of 589 votes out of nearly 77,000. Mayor Perry has had to work not only with the Commissioners but also with a hung Croydon council, as the Conservatives won 33 seats to 34 for Labour, 2 Greens and a Lib Dem. Four by-elections since then - including one for the council seat won simultaneously by Perry - have resulted in no change, and this is one of the few councils which has seen no defections in the lsst four years. Croydon council has very few marginal wards, but the closeness of the mayoral result last time makes the borough's second mayoral election hard to call. The count here is being staggered, with the mayoral election to be declared on Friday and the council elections on Saturday. At least this is a scheduled delay, whereas the 2022 Croydon count ran so late that it had to be adjourned twice and the final results didn't trickle out until Sunday evening.

The Conservatives are resting on stronger majorities in the two Kentish outer London boroughs. Despite this, Bromley council is home to the only Reform UK local councillor elected as such in London to date: that's Alan Cook, who gained a by-election from the Conservatives last year in Bromley Common and Holwood ward. The Tories still have a strong majority after winning 36 seats in 2022 (against 12 Labour, 5 Lib Dems, 3 Chislehurst Matters councillors and 2 independents), but Reform UK have picked up two more seats here by defection and will be looking for more at this election.

Similar comments apply in our final Outer London boruogh of Bexley, if not more so. Bexley borough is dominated by lower middle-class voters, and it has similar demographics to neighbouring Dartford where Reform UK swept the board in last year's Kent county elections. In local elections Bexley has been a two-party state throughout this century, with the Conservatives leading Labour by 33-12 in the 2022 election; but the Tories' lead in votes then (47-41) was much less than might be assumed from the seat totals, and it wouldn't take much for a Reform UK intervention to cause some chaos here. Votes here will be counted overnight. The ballot paper in Sidcup ward includes a man who has been on quite the political journey: Laurence Williams, who since 1994 has previously stood for election to Bexley council as an independent (twice) and on the tickets of the Official Monster Raving Loony Party, the UK Independence Party, the English Democrats, the Christian Peoples Alliance, the continuing Liberal Party and the continuing SDP, and who has contested other elections for the Christian Party, the Welsh nationalist group Gwlad and Rejoin EU, is now standing in his home Sidcup ward - on the Green Party slate, making a total of ten political parties in 32 years. Who will take him on next?

Moving back into Inner London, Greenwich looks like being a fascinating contest at this election. The 2022 elections had a very large Labour lead, with the party carrying every ward and winning 52 seats to 3 for the Conservatives, but Labour have suffered some embarrassing by-election losses since then: in 2024 the Conservatives defended a seat in Mottingham and gained a seat from Labour in Eltham Town, while a by-election last year in Shooters Hill ward was lost to the Green Party. The Eltham wards also look like good territory for Reform UK. That area used to be the political stomping ground of Liz Truss, whose first election win came in the former Eltham South ward in 2006; this time round her husband Hugh O'Leary is making up the numbers as part of the Conservative slate for Woolwich Arsenal ward.

On paper, Labour start from an even stronger position in the final mayoral election in the capital, which takes place in Lewisham. It's now 12 years since Labour lost an election of any kind in Lewisham, and the borough's elected mayoralty has been continuously in Labour hands since it was established in 2002. The 2022 winner here was Damian Egan, who won in the first round with a 58-16 lead over the Green Party; Egan then resigned in 2024 to stand for Parliament in the Kingswood by-election, which he won, and his former deputy Brenda Dacres won the resulting mayoral by-election in March 2024 with 52% of the vote against 16% for the Greens and 12% for the Lib Dems. Four of the 54 Labour councillors elected here in 2022 have since defected to the Green Party, who have enjoyed a significant vote here in the past and will be eyeing this borough up.

Similar comments apply in Southwark, but this borough also has the complicating factor of the Liberal Democrats whose strong local vote in the Bermondsey area - dating back to the Simon Hughes years - has not yet faded away entirely. The Dulwich end of the borough is also much more middle-class than anything to be found in Lewisham, although that hasn't stopped the former Conservative vote here from collapsing; while the Greens have previously won a council seat in Peckham although that was a very long time ago. Labour are defending 52 seats from the 2022 results, the Lib Dems 11.

We'll finish our spiral tour of London in a borough which was once synonymous with the "loony left" - although its current Labour administration is a much more sober affair. Lambeth returned 58 Labour councillors in 2022 against 3 Lib Dems and 2 Greens - who have now drawn level with the Lib Dems after a spectacular by-election gain from Labour last year in Herne Hill and Loughborough Junction ward. The Lambeth Greens will be hoping for more results like that.

Wales

Here's a dirty little secret that I've not been telling you in this piece so far. Nearly all council majorities in England and Wales are based on a minority of the actual votes cast: that's one of the perceived advantages of England's first-past-the-post system. But proportional representation applies to the devolved administrations in Wales, Scotland, Northern Ireland and the London Assembly.

This year we see the seventh general election to Senedd Cymru, the Welsh Parliament, which gets a rather new look this year. The Senedd is being expanded from the current 60 members to 96, reflecting the extra responsibilities it has taken on over the last 27 years; it is also going back to 4-year terms, so the next elections after this will be in 2030 rather than 2031.

The expansion of the Senedd is also being accompanied by electoral reform, in which the previous Additional Member top-up system has been abolished. Previously 40 of the 60 MSs were elected from single-member constituencies and the remaining 20 seats were allocated to correct the resulting disproportionality in the constituency results; but in practice there weren't enough top-up seats to fully compensate for Labour's strong lead in the constituencies at the last six elections. In the 2021 election Labour won 30 seats out of 60 from 36% of the top-up vote, having won 27 constituencies and 3 top-up seats; the Conservatives polled 25% and won 16 seats, Plaid Cymru had 21% and 13 seats, and the remaining seat went to the Lib Dems in the Mid and West Wales region. The "overhang", where Labour won more constituencies than they would have been entitled to from the top-up vote, was worth four seats to Labour.

The new electoral arrangements are the result of a compromise between Labour and Plaid Cymru. The old 40 single-member constituencies and 5 regions have been cleared away. In their place Wales has been divided into 16 Senedd constituencies, constructed by pairing off the 32 Westminster seats, and each of the new Senedd constituencies will each return six MSs by proportional representation. Each of the constituencies has a Welsh-only name. The original idea was for each party to submit a list alternating between male and female candidates; this failed to make it through the Senedd, but Plaid Cymru have adopted the idea - somewhat to the detriment of some of their outgoing MSs including the former party leader Adam Price, who is in third place on the party's list for Sir Gaerfyrddin.

There are no seat overhangs in the new system, but nonetheless the largest parties do still have an advantage thanks to the relatively small number of MSs per constituency. Parties (or independent candidates) will need to poll one-seventh of the vote (14.3%) in any constituency to guarantee a seat, although a percentage point or two less might well be enough depending on how fragmented the result is. Notional results prepared by Pete Whitehead suggest that if the present system had been in use in 2021, then Labour would have won 44 seats against 28 Conservatives, 24 for Plaid Cymru and nothing for the Liberal Democrats, who had enough votes for one seat out of 12 in the old Mid and West region but not enough votes for one seat out of 6 in any of the successor constituencies. If we plug the rather different votes cast in the 2024 Westminster election into the formula, then Labour would have won 47 seats - two short of a majority - against 19 for the Conservatives, 16 for Reform UK, 12 Plaid Cymru and 2 Lib Dems.

We can probably expect something different again in this election. Opinion polling in Wales tends to be infrequent and this is a difficult place to poll well, but the surveys which have been carried out this year suggest that the Labour vote is running at around half or less than half of the 36% they got in 2021; instead Plaid Cymru have moved into first place, with Reform UK either level with them or close behind in second. This being a PR election, we can expect vote percentages to translate into seats much more cleanly and predictably then in England's first-past-the-post system, so a small Plaid Cymru lead in votes should be reflected in a small Plaid Cymru lead in seats.

This picture is also borne out by by-elections in Wales over the last couple of years, notably the stunning Plaid Cymru gain at the Senedd by-election in Caerphilly last year. This was not a flash in the pan. Local by-election results in Wales are an unreliable indicator, but since January 2025 Labour have had thirteen seats to defend and have only managed to hold on to two of them: seven Labour seats were gained by Reform UK, two by Plaid Cymru and one each went to the Greens and the Lib Dems. Plaid Cymru have fared much better, holding five of the six by-elections they were defending: there have only been two local by-elections for Welsh Conservative seats since the start of last year, of which one was held and the other was gained by an independent.

In terms of where the party leaders are standing, the First Minister Eluned Morgan is top of the Labour list in Ceredigion Penfro; the Conservatives' Darren Millar is seeking re-election in Clwyd; Plaid Cymru's Rhun ap Iorwerth heads their list in Bangor Conwy Môn; Jane Dodds of the Liberal Democrats contests Brycheiniog Tawe Nedd; the Greens' Antony Slaughter is standing in Caerdydd Penarth; and Reform UK's Dan Thomas, who we mentioned earlier in his previous role as a Conservative Barnet councillor, is number 1 on the RUK list for Casnewydd Islwyn. The Caerphilly by-election winner Lindsay Whittle will get back if Plaid Cymru win multiple seats in Blaenau Gwent Caerffili Rhymni: 29% of the vote would guarantee that. Of the MSs who have changed party allegiance during this term, Rhys ab Owen (who was thrown out of Plaid Cymru following a sexual harassment scandal) is standing as an independent in Caerdydd Penarth, while Conservative to Reform UK defectors James Evans and Laura Anne Jones are respectively seeking re-election under their new colours in Brycheiniog Tawe Nedd and Sir Fynwy Torfaen. A total of 14 Labour MSs are standing down, including the former First Ministers Mark Drakeford and Vaughan Gething. Of the four remaining members who have continuous service since the first Assembly election in 1999, two are standing again: the Llywydd Elin Jones is top of the Plaid Cymru list in Ceredigion Penfro, and Labour's Lynne Neagle is her party's number 1 candidate for Sir Fynwy Torfaen.

There are two local by-elections on the undercard of the Senedd election, one in Powys council for Llandrindod South and the other to Newport council in Rogerstone North.

All votes in Wales are being counted on Friday.

Scotland

In Scotland there are no local by-elections on the undercard of today's poll, for the excellent reason that there were no vacancies in Scottish local government at the cutoff date. So it's all eyes on the 7th election for the 129 members of the Scottish Parliament.

Unlike in Wales, Scotland has retained its two-tier electoral system in which 73 MSPs are elected from single-member constituencies and the other 56 are regional top-up seats allocated to correct the disproportionality in the constituency result. For the latter purpose Scotland is divided into eight electoral regions, which are constructed by aggregating together between eight and ten constituencies: each region elects seven additional members.

There are boundary changes this year, which at the regional level mostly affect the Central, Lothians and South regions. In rough terms, West Lothian transfers from the Lothians region to the Central region (which is renamed as "Central Scotland and Lothians West"); East Lothian transfers from the South region to the Lothians region (which is renamed "Edinburgh and Lothians East"); and Hamilton transfers from the Central region to the South region. There is also more minor tinkering around the edges going on elsewhere, one effect of which is that the Glasgow region no longer includes the whole of Glasgow: the city's Cardonald ward has been tranferred into the Renfrewshire North and Cardonald constituency, which is part of the West region.

The seven additional members per region are not always enough to prevent parties from having an overhang when they sweep the board in the constituencies - something which often happens in the Central Belt, which contains the bulk of Scotland's population and is relatively homogeneous. Originally this was to the benefit of the Labour Party, but since 2011 the Scottish National Party has won the bulk of the constituencies. At the last Holyrood electon in 2021 the SNP won 62 constituencies to 5 for the Conservatives, 4 for the Liberal Democrats and 2 for Labour; once the top-ups had been made, the final result was 64 SNP MSPs, 31 Conservatives, 22 Labour, 8 Greens and 4 Lib Dems. This was based on a regional vote of 40.3% for the SNP, 23.5% for the Conservatives, 17.9% for Labour, 8.1% for the Greens and 5.1% for the Lib Dems. The SNP's constituency performance meant that they had an overhang of four seats.

All this left the Scottish National Party, then led by Nicola Sturgeon, one seat short of an overall majority. Initially they entered into a coalition with the Scottish Green Party, but this collapsed in April 2024; the Scottish Greens then pulled the plug on First Minister Humza Yousaf, who had replaced Sturgeon in 2023, and he was forced to resign. John Swinney then took over as First Minister and he has run an SNP minority administration since 2024.

The first few months of the Swinney administration were marked by a sudden revival for Scottish Labour, who made sweeping gains in the 2024 Westminster election, but since then the Labour vote in Scotland has dissipated as suddenly as it had arrived. The SNP are back in first place, but their vote has slipped since 2021: polling in this Holyrood election campaign has normally had the Nationalists in the high 20s, with the other four Holyrood parties plus Reform UK all jockeying for position in the range 8-20%. If the voters want fragmentation, then most PR systems would deliver that: but most seat projections for this election still have the SNP close to an overall majority, which is pretty good going for a government that has been in office continuously since 2007. This is mostly thanks to the SNP's continued dominance in the constituency vote; not only are they leading in the Central Belt, but also the Scottish Green Party are running very few constituency candidates which means that in most areas the SNP are the default pro-independence option.

Looking at local by-election results in Scotland tends to present a mixed picture, partly because the SNP performance at the last local elections in 2022 was nothing to write home about and partly because the use of PR for Scottish local government means that seat changes in by-elections are not necessarily a reliable indicator. There have been 24 by-elections in Scotland for 25 council seats since the start of 2025, with the SNP winning 10 seats, independents 5, the Lib Dems 4, Labour 3, the Conservatives 2 and Reform UK 1; the biggest movement of seats is from Labour to the SNP, with five by-elections going that way over the period. Labour have only gained one seat from the SNP since January 2025, but it was a big one: Davy Russell came out on top of a close three-way split in the Hamilton, Larkhall and Stonehouse Holyrood by-election last June.

In 2021 the Holyrood election count was staggered over two days due to COVID restrictions reducing the number of people able to work in the counting halls, and the returning officer for Airdrie and Shotts refused a request to combine a Westmisnter by-election with the Scottish Parliament poll partly for that reason. COVID considerations are now a thing of the past, but the Electoral Management Board for Scotland have decided not to go back to overnight counts: instead the Holyrood votes will be counted in daylight on Friday after the election staff who are responsible for delivering the result have had a good night's sleep. It's better to conduct a correct vote count than a quick one.

First Minster John Swinney is one of three MSPs with continuous service since the re-establishment of the Scottish Parliament in 1999 who are seeking re-election for a seventh term of office. (The other two are Labour's Jackie Baillie, who is seeking another term as the constituency MSP for Dumbarton and is top of the Labour list in Wset Scotland, and Fergus Ewing who is standing as an independent candidate for his Inverness and Nairn constituency after falling out with the SNP.) Swinney is the SNP candidate for Perthshire North and is also top of the party's list for the Mid Scotland and Fife region. The West Scotland regional ballot is one to watch with three of the Scottish party leaders standing there at the top of their respective party lists: they are Russell Findlay of the Conservatives, the Scottish Greens co-leader Ross Greer and Malcolm Offord (who is also contesting the Inverclyde constituency) for Reform UK. Labour's Anas Sarwar is standing in the Glasgow Cathcart and Pollok constituency and in first place on the Labour list for Glasgow; the other Scottish Green co-leader Gillian Mackay is top of her party's list in the Central Scotland and Lothians West region; and the Lib Dems' Alex Cole-Hamilton is standing only in the constituency ballot, where he is seeking re-election in a redrawn seat of Edinburgh North Western.

Five MSPs who have changed party allegiance since 2021 are standing again. Fergus Ewing has already been mentioned above, Ash Regan (who was elected for the SNP, defected to Alba and then went independent once Alba was wound up) and Jeremy Balfour (who fell out with the Conservatives over Findlay's leadership) are both seeking re-election as independent candidates for the Edinburgh and Lothians East region. Conservative to Lib Dem defector Jamie Greene is the Lib Dem candidate for Inverclyde and number 2 on his party's West Scotland list, and Conservative to Reform UK defector Graham Simpson is standing in Airdrie and is number 1 on the RUK list for Central Scotland and Lothians West.

Finally, we should note that the SNP slate for these elections includes two current Westminster MPs. The party's House of Commons leader Stephen Flynn is standing in Aberdeen Deeside and North Kincardine and is top of the SNP list for North East Scotland, while Stephen Gethins is the SNP candidate for Dundee City East. If either Flynn or Gethins win, then they would have to resign their Westminster seats and by-elections would follow. But those would be Previews for another time.

Concluding remarks

Results for these elections will trickle through slowly, with most seats not declaring until Friday and relatively little action overnight. There will be plenty of time for result followers, so pace yourself.

When the results come through, be prepared for shocks because elections are always like that. Once all the votes are in I will name one result which made my jaw drop when I saw it, so if you're a candidate in these elections and you think that I've unfairly written off your chances of winning, the best way to counter that is to get out there on the campaign trail, prove me wrong and make the case for me to give you that award. It will be much more satisfying for all of us if you send me a smug message afterwards rather than a whinging message now - and your win will give me material for future columns. And the prize for winning is a worthwhile one: the chance to improve your local community, to fix those potholes rather than point at them.

Those results will be the outcome of a lot of hard work by around 150 Returning Officers and their tens of thousands of poll and count staff, who do a thankless job that doesn't just last for one day a year. There is a fantastic amount of behind-the-scenes work that's needed to put this show on for your benefit: polling day is just the tip of the administrative iceberg, representing the culmination of months of planning. Your council election teams have no margin for error: it all has to go right, every single time. This column sends its best wishes to everybody working on this election for a smooth and trouble-free poll and count.

Shoutouts are also due to the more than 25,000 candidates across England, Scotland and Wales in this most brutal form of job application. No, I am not listing you all individually; but your local candidates can be found by entering your postcode into whocanivotefor.co.uk. For the winners, perhaps: the chance to do something about those potholes rather than point glumly at them, the chance to improve their local community. For the losers: well, nobody remembers who comes second, with the exception of this column. Your columnist's other electoral hobby, the Local Elections Archive Project, will eventually get around to recording your result for posterity.

But the most important people in today's festival of democracy are you, the voters. You are fortunate that you do not live in a place where democracy is not free, not fair or not existent. The people of unoccupied Ukraine are literally fighting for their right to live in a country that holds elections rather than stages election-type events. Democracy will only last for as long as there are people prepared to support it, prepared to participate in it, prepared to defend it, prepared to fight for it.

The best way you can defend Britain's local democracy is to take part in it. Your vote will be free, it will be fair, it will be secret, and it will count in the same way as my vote and everybody else's vote. But if you don't vote, your voice won't be heard when the ballot papers tumble out of the box onto the counting table, to decide your councillors' future - and yours. If you have a postal vote you should have received it by now; if you intend to vote at a polling station, don't forget to go there between 7am and 10pm on Thursday. If you are voting in England, take your photo ID with you. You will be most welcome. You can find your designated polling station on your polling card, or by going to wherecanivote.co.uk.

This column doesn't just come out at election time: Andrew's Previews is here for you all year round. We will be taking a break next week to try and process what the hell just happened, but the wheel of democracy continues to turn and preparations are already underway for four local by-elections on 21st May. Stay tuned.
