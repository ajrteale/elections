"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

One by-election on Tuesday 17th March 2026:

Milford: Hakin

Pembrokeshire council, West Wales; caused by the death of independent councillor Mike Stoddart.

There will be no Thursday edition of Andrew's Previews this week. We're now on the glidepath down to the May local and devolved elections, and not that many local by-elections are taking place at the moment: for the moment, many local parties are keeping their powder dry in advance of the main event. No local by-elections have been scheduled for 19th March. But there is no peace for the wicked nor rest for the righteous, so this column is going to make up for that by travelling to a council area where the returning officer is deliberately trying to create a tradition of Tuesday by-elections.

The scene for today's by-election is Milford Haven, which lies on the north bank of the drowned estuary of the two Cleddau rivers: its Welsh-language name, Aberdaugleddau, refers to that location. But many locations on the Pembrokeshire coast have Viking names, and Milford is one of them: it comes from Old Norse words melr, referring to sand, and fjorthr which indicates an inlet or fjord. Under English influence, "fjord" turned into "ford"; the tautological suffix "Haven" was then added by the railways in the 19th century, to disambiguate the town from other Milfords. The English-language influence was important here because Milford Haven lies in the Little England Beyond Wales, an area covering most of modern Pembrokeshire which was aggressively settled by English Flemings in the 12th century. Even in modern-day census returns, use of the Welsh language drops off dramatically at the Landsker line, the traditional linguistic boundary of that area.

The original Flemish settlement was facilitated by the fact that Milford Haven is an excellent harbour. The modern town of that name was established in 1793 by Sir William Hamilton, who served for 36 years as the British ambassador to the Kingdom of Naples but is now probably best known to history as the husband of Admiral Nelson's mistress Emma Hamilton. Hamilton came from aristocratic stock and he was a wealthy man: while in Italy he had bought the decorated Roman glass vase now known as the Portland Vase and brought it to England. Much of this wealth derived from Hamilton’s estates in Wales, where he owned land and saw the potential for a significant port. He persuaded Parliament to pass the Hubberston and Pill, Pembroke, Docks and Piers Act 1790, and work began on a new town on the eastern side of a bay on the estuary's north side.

Milford Haven has been one of the UK's busiest ports ever since, and the growth of the town has swallowed up several nearby villages. This includes Hakin, which faces the town centre across the old harbour: it stretches down to Hakin Point, which originally marked the western end of the bay. Again, Hakin Point is a tautological name: "Hakin" is an Old English name which is cognate with the modern German word Ecken, meaning a point of land. In the mid-19th century Hakin was a major boatbuilding centre; now it's a Milford suburb. A causeway now runs across the harbour mouth from Hakin Point, with dock gates turning the original harbour into a marina.

The old harbour is far too small for the big ships of today, on which Milford's economy now depends. In the 2021 census Hakin ward ranked 6th in England and Wales for residents employed in semi-routine occupations, at 20.7% of adults, and it had the second-highest figure for apprenticeship qualifications (8.5%) of any ward in Wales. These figures are underpinned by the fossil fuel industry. Just to the west of Milford is the South Hook liquified natural gas terminal, which is the largest such facility in Europe; to the east of the town is the smaller Dragon LNG terminal. Both of these took their first gas deliveries in 2009, and between them they can handle 25% of the UK's gas requirements on the occasions when gas tankers actually turn up here from the Middle East. The South Hook and Dragon sites were both used for oil refineries before being redeveloped for their current purpose; visitors looking out from Hakin ward's coast can gaze across the estuary to the Pembroke oil refinery, which is very much still in operation.

Milford: Hakin is now one of Wales' most working-class wards, and appropriately enough it has a Labour MP. Henry Tufnell, who was elected in 2024 for the Mid and South Pembrokeshire constituency which roughly corresponds to the Little England Beyond Wales, is a barrister and trade union representative who comes from a noted political and landowning family: a number of his ancestors were Conservative MPs in the early 20th century, and the London district of Tufnell Park is named after the family. Tufnell defeated the former Conservative Cabinet minister Stephen Crabb, who stood here after his Preseli Pembrokeshire constituency was broken up by boundary changes.

The old Preseli Pembrokeshire constituency still exists at Senedd level for the time being, although electoral reform means that its days are numbered. Since 2007 this seat has been represented in Cardiff Bay by Paul Davies, who was leader of the Welsh Conservatives from 2008 to 2021. The Senedd's electoral reform for May will place Milford Haven within the Ceredigion Penfro constituency, which will return six MSs by proportional representation. On the votes cast in 2024 Ceredigion Penfro would have returned two MSs each for Plaid Cymru and Labour and one each for the Conservatives and Reform UK. Four outgoing MSs are to seek re-election in this constituency in May: Plaid Cymru's list is headed by the outgoing Ceredigion MS and Llywydd Elin Jones, top of the Labour list will be the First Minister Eluned Morgan, while Davies is number 1 on the Conservative list with the outgoing Carmarthen West and South Pembrokeshire MS Sam Kurtz at number 2.

Andrew's Previews was last in Pembrokeshire a month ago, covering a council by-election in Fishguard which ended in a Plaid Cymru gain from Labour. This doesn't necessarily mean much given that Welsh local elections are fought in a very different context to Westminster and Senedd Cymru contests. Pembrokeshire county council is a bastion of independent councillors, who have held a majority continuously since the creation of the present council in 1995; and Hakin ward is very much in that tradition.

Since 2004 Milford: Hakin ward's independent councillor had been Mike Stoddart, whose outspoken local government career went back a long way. He was a Cumberland man who came to Pembrokeshire in the 1970s and turned his talent for entrepreneurship into running a dairy farm, founding a building construction firm and becoming a sports reporter for the Western Telegraph. In 1991 he was elected to Preseli Pembrokeshire council as an independent councillor for Milford North and West ward, but he gave that seat up shortly afterwards in favour of founding a local newspaper, the Milford Mercury. This became the first home of Stoddart's long-running Old Grumpy column, which he turned into the newfangled technology of a blog - oldgrumpy.co.uk - after selling the newspaper in 1999.

In 2004 Stoddart returned to local politics by defeating the outgoing independent councillor for Milford: Hakin ward, which he had represented ever since. His final re-election in 2022 saw Stoddart win with 49% of the vote against 30% for another independent and 21% for the Conservative candidate. He kept the Old Grumpy blog going throughout his council career, and his final post in September 2025 was a critical review of the memoirs of his old Cumbrian schoolfriend Melvyn Bragg. Mike Stoddart passed away in January at the age of 86, but the Stoddart family hasn't disappeared from Pembrokeshire county council yet: his wife Viv (who represents the next-door ward of Milford: Hubberston) and his daughter Tessa Hodgson are also county councillors.

We have Stoddart's hard work in 2013-14 to thank for uncovering a scandal. This centred on EU grant money which had been awarded for restoration work on historic commercial property in Pembroke Dock town centre. Following a year of investigative work by Stoddart irregularities were discovered relating to the tender for work on a former bank; the Welsh Government then suspended funding for the scheme and Pembrokeshire council were forced to call in Dyfed-Powys police, but no charges were ever brought. Last November the council was finally forced to publish its legal advice relating to the scandal, which stated that fraud had been committed which was "clear”, “unsophisticated” and “supported by evidence", but that the county council's own failings meant that there was no realistic prospect of securing a conviction.

Whoever succeeds Mike Stoddart in this by-election - and there are large number of candidates who want to do so - has a lot to live up to. Three new independent candidates have come forward, including both of the people who Stoddart beat in 2022: local resident Duncan Edwards was runner-up here four years ago, while Milford Haven town councillor Lee Bridges had the Conservative nomination here last time but is now an independent. Another independent town councillor on the ballot is Derrick Abbott. The official Conservative candidate this time is Brian Taylor. A long candidate list is completed by Sam Booth for the Greens, Labour's Nicola Harteveld who has withdrawn from the campaign trail over abuse received by her family, Reform UK's Scott Thorley who is hoping to improve on his fourth-place finish in a by-election in Haverfordwest last year, and Sam Warden for the Lib Dems.
