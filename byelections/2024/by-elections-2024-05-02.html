"All the right votes, but not necessarily in the right order"

Welcome to the annual Andrew's Previews megapreview, covering everything that goes on in the ordinary local elections in England and Wales on 2nd May 2024. This year marks the 50th anniversary of most of the councils up for election in England this year, which first took up their responsibilities on 1st April 1974 after having initial elections in the spring of 1973. It remains to be seen whether the whole shebang can last another 50 years.

Because of its size, this Preview has been split into four parts. Part I will cover the introduction, Wales and London. Part II will cover the English North and Midlands, with the exception of the Lancashire police area. Part III will cover the South of England outside London. Finally, Part IV will be the Parliamentary Special for Blackpool South, other elections in Lancashire, and concluding remarks including the annual In memoriam section where we pay tribute to councillors who have passed away over the last year.

Introduction

In seat terms, this is the quietest local election year since 2017 with the only councils holding elections being those cities, boroughs and districts in England which return one-third or one-half of their councillors at each election. Since most of these are big cities and large towns outside London, this year's council elections have a rather urban slant. Only by-elections take place to the London borough councils; there are no council elections in Wales this year, and Scotland misses out on the fun entirely - unless the current troubles for the First Minister end up provoking an early election to Holyrood.

But in voter terms, this is not a quiet year at all because of more recent constitutional innovations of the 21st century. The London Mayor and Assembly is up for election, together with all the Police and Crime Commissioners and some of the regional mayors. Between those categories, the whole of England and Wales has something to vote for.

The reason that this is the quietest local election year since 2017 is the COVID-19 pandemic of 2020-21, which led to the May 2020 local elections being postponed by a year and merged into the May 2021 local elections. The Coronavirus Act 2020, the legal basis for this postponement, provided that once the pandemic was over council elections should go back to their previous cycle, so some of the seats up for election in May 2021 were for four-year terms (mostly the county councils) and some were for three-year terms ending in May 2024. It's the latter category of seats that are up for re-election this year.

The May 2021 local elections were a very important return to normality as the COVID vaccination programme continued its work. They also marked a very good performance for the ruling Conservative Party, which was partly ascribed to a "vaccine bounce" and which in many cases seemed to confirm the Brexit-related realignment of politics that had been seen in the December 2019 general election. The BBC's projected national share of the vote in May 2021 gave 36% to the Conservatives, 29% to Labour and 17% to the Liberal Democrats.

The intervening three years have seen the Conservative vote evaporate on a national level in the face of economic stagnation, high inflation, a series of personal scandals from the Prime Ministers downwards and a general impression of incompetence in government. The Labour Party is recording double-digit leads in the national opinion polls and has been doing so consistently for a long time.

Really this is the same national picture as we saw at last year's local elections in May 2023, when the projected national share was 35% for Labour, 26% for the Conservatives and 20% for the Liberal Democrats - a swing of 8% to Labour since 2021. This was widely interpreted at first as the sort of result which presaged a hung parliament at the next general election, but the detail of the results suggests that the Brexit realignment of 2019-21 has unwound, hard. There aren't that many Conservatives left in urban areas now, and in rural areas the party is often under threat from the Lib Dems, the Greens (who have got the knack of campaigning effectively in local elections) or both. By contrast the newish kids on the block Reform UK are not much of a threat to the Conservatives in these local elections, mainly because they don't have much of an on-the-ground campaign infrastructure; Reform UK are only contesting around an eighth of the seats up for election this year. Meanwhile it's not all plain sailing for Labour: in particular, there are a few areas where the war in Gaza has hit the Labour party hard and led to mass defections.

The Conservative Party's in-house psephologist Lord Hayward has done his usual pre-election briefing for journalists in which he suggested that the Conservatives stood to lose upwards of 400 seats - roughly half of what they are defending in these local elections. In terms of what the 8% swing to Labour implied by last year's results means for a particular district, this column has gone for its usual metric of a "par score" - what would happen if last year's results were repeated. If, when the results come in, you see one party doing consistently better or worse than the par score then you can reasonably conclude that their performance is better or (as the case may be) worse than last year.

The national political scene affects the local one in the sense that a rising tide lifts all boats, but that doesn’t mean that the tide rises to the same level in all harbours. Local elections are local, and this column is here to describe the local scene. In the rest of this piece I’ll go through England in detail, painting a more local and more varied picture for you. Links given are to results from the Local Elections Archive Project; while the maps are from 2021 or (if different) the year when these seats were last contested, and as such might not reflect the lines on which the election will actually be drawn if there are new ward boundaries this year. Council compositions were correct when this piece was drafted over the Easter weekend. Without further ado, let’s get out into the country:

Wales

Let's start with the easy bit. With Senedd Cymru next up for election in 2026 and the Welsh local government term expiring in 2027, the only polls in Wales on 2nd May 2024 are for its four Police and Crime Commissioners. Welsh returning officers have scrupulously avoided scheduling any local by-elections for today because of the different rules for PCC and Welsh local elections: most notably, PCC elections require Voter ID and Welsh local elections don't. The Welsh local election franchise is also wider than the PCC franchise.

At the last elections Wales saw two instances of the now-abolished Supplementary Vote system resulting in a come-from-behind win for the candidate who started in second. Both of these involved close three-way results with low vote shares for the first-preference leader, so the old system was doing its job in seeing through vote splits and ensuring that the winning candidate had the broadest possible support.

We can see this very clearly in North Wales where there was a close three-way fight in the first round in 2021. The Conservatives led with just 32%, Labour polled 29% and Plaid Cymru (who were defending the position) 28%; Labour went on to win on transfers from Plaid and minor parties. Labour PPC Andy Dunbobbin is seeking a second term of office. The Conservative candidate is Brian Jones, who appeared in this column last year when he won the Rhyl Tŷ Newydd by-election to Denbighshire council. Plaid Cymru have reselected their 2021 candidate Ann Griffith. Richard Marbrow completes the North Wales ballot paper for the Lib Dems; there is no sign of any nomination from Mrs Trellis.

A similar freak vote split gave the Conservatives a tiny first-round lead in Dyfed-Powys, with 34.0% against 33.6% for Plaid Cymru and 24% for Labour. Plaid's Dafydd Llywelyn was the incumbent, and he went on to win the runoff quite easily. Llywelyn is seeking a third term of office against Powys councillor Ian Harrison for the Conservatives, Labour candidate Philippa Thompson who fought Preseli Pembrokeshire at the last two Westminster elections, and Lib Dem Justin Griffiths.

The two PCC positions in industrial south Wales are both safely Labour. Last time out the first-round shares in Gwent were 42% for Labour, 29% for the Conservatives and 16% for Plaid Cymru. Labour PCC Jeff Cuthbert is retiring after two terms and the new Labour candidate is Jane Mudd, the leader of Newport council; she takes on Army veteran Hannah Jarvis for the Conservatives, Caerphilly councillor Donna Cushing for Plaid and Mike Hamilton for the Lib Dems.

We also have a retirement in South Wales where octogenarian political veteran Alun Michael is standing down after three terms as Labour PCC. He wasn't seriously challenged for his final election in 2021, polling 41% of the first preferences against 24% for the Conservatives and 19% for Plaid. Michael's deputy Emma Wools has been selected as the new Labour candidate for South Wales PCC. Again, this is a four-way ballot paper with the Conservatives standing George Carroll who leads the party's group on Vale of Glamorgan council, Barry town councillor Dennis Clarke being the Plaid Cymru candidate and Sam Bennett standing for the Lib Dems.

Last time out all of these elections were combined with the 2021 Senedd election. For that reason, and perhaps also because this is the first Welsh election which requires Voter ID, we can expect a massive drop in turnout across the board. Expect that to be a big story of the Welsh PCC elections, whatever result they come up with. All these posts will count on Friday.

London

Mayor and Assembly

We now turn to the biggest single election in the UK, the sixth poll for the post of Mayor of London. This is defended by Labour mayor Sadiq Khan, who in 2021 led the Conservatives' Shaun Bailey by the rather close level of 40-35 in the first round before winning the runoff decisively. This time there will be no runoff, so we may see a mayor elected on a minority vote for the first time.

Khan, who won more than a million first preferences last time, is seeking re-election for a third term of office. The Conservative candidate is Susan Hall, who has sat on the London Assembly since 2017: she originally took over the Londonwide seat left behind by Kemi Badenoch after Badenoch's election to Parliament. Hall is also a Harrow councillor representing Hatch End ward, and she was briefly leader of that council in 2013-14 after Harrow's then Labour administration fell apart.

The Green Party (who were third in 2021 with 7.8%) have selected Zoë Garbett, who appeared in this column last year when she was the Green candidate in the November 2023 Hackney mayoral by-election which we shall return to presently; Garbett is a Hackney councillor representing Dalston ward. The Lib Dem candidate (4th, 4.4%) is Rob Blackie, who works in digital marketing and has recently completed a course of treatment for cancer. There are a number of anti-ULEZ candidates on the ballot including podcaster Brian Rose, who placed 7th here in 2021 for his London Real Party. Defending ninth place is Count Binface, who needs no introduction. Twelfth last time were the Animal Welfare Party who have secured top spot on the ballot paper by selecting former Afghan refugee Femy Amin: she was fourth on the party's list in 2021, and she fought Ruislip, Northwood and Pinner in December 2019. Independent candidate Farah London, who placed 14th here in 2021, is back for another go; she will improve on that position, as will the SDP's Amy Gallagher whose party starts 17th, because this time there are only 13 candidates for the electors of London to choose from.

Of the candidates and parties who didn't stand last time, if the national polls are any guide then the one to watch is Howard Cox. He is a longtime campaigner against fuel duty increases standing on a stridently anti-ULEZ and pro-motorist ticket, and he is the candidate of Reform UK. In 2021 Reform stood on the Londonwide list (finishing 10th out of 18 parties with 1.0%) but didn't stand a candidate for mayor, instead endorsing the Reclaim candidate Laurence Fox who finished sixth in that poll with 1.9%. Fox himself applied to stand in this election as an independent candidate, but he was one of two candidates (along with YouTuber Niko Omilana, who was 5th last time with 2.0%) who had their nominations rejected: to stand for Mayor of London you need to get the paperwork signed by ten electors from each borough plus ten electors from the City of London, and when the returning officer checked the forms there were issues with the signatures which Fox and Omilana couldn't sort out before the deadline. That leaves three independents on the ballot: Natalie Campbell, the chancellor of the University of Westminster, is a businesswoman running a bottled water firm who applied for the Conservative nomination and didn't get it; Tarun Ghulati is an investment banker standing on an anti-ULEZ ticket; and Andreas Michli is a businessman who got into legal trouble for keeping his gym open during lockdown: he is also anti-ULEZ, and he wants Metropolitan Police officers to be trained to blue-belt level in Brazilian jiu-jitsu. Last on the mayoral ballot is Nick Scanlon for Britain First.

The Greater London Assembly is the only English elected body to use proportional representation. Last time around Labour polled 38% of the vote on the Londonwide list ballot and won 11 seats; the Conservatives polled 31% of the vote and won 9 seats; the Greens polled 12% and won 3 seats; and the Liberal Democrats won 2 seats with 7%, with no other parties coming close to the 5% threshold which applies to the list vote. Other parties hoping to win representation this time are (in ballot paper order) the Animal Welfare Party, Nick Scanlon for Britain First, the Christian Peoples Alliance, the Communist Party of Britain, the Heritage Party, Reform UK, Rejoin EU, the Social Democratic Party and independent candidates Laurence Fox, Farah London and Gabe Romualdo. To date no party has ever held the 13 seats required for an overall majority in the Assembly: to do so Labour will need to improve their list vote.

Results for the Mayor and Assembly will come through on Saturday.

By-elections

The London mayoral and assembly elections fall halfway through the term of the 32 London borough councils, which provides a good opportunity for mid-term by-elections to be held for relatively little cost and inconvenience. Of the 48 local by-elections being held on 2nd May, fifteen are in Greater London.

We'll start our tour of the Great Wen with two polls in Hackney including the second by-election of this term in De Beauvoir ward. This safely returned two Labour councillors in May 2022. One of them - Tom Dewey - then resigned almost immediately, and the resulting by-election in July 2022 was very close between Labour and the Green Party with Labour holding the seat by just 27 votes.

At the time of that by-election it was not publicly known that Dewey had been arrested during the May 2022 election campaign on suspicion of possessing indecent images of children. This eventually came to light in 2023 when Dewey pleaded guilty (he received a 12-month suspended prison sentence), and the resulting scandal led to the resignation of Hackney's elected mayor Philip Glanville who had been Dewey's housemate. The mayoral by-election in November 2023 was won easily by Labour councillor Caroline Woodley; the consequential by-election for Woodley's council seat in Cazenove ward saw a farcical Labour campaign with their candidate first disendorsed and then re-endorsed, and the end result was that the seat was lost to the Conservatives on a massive swing.

So Hackney Labour will need to get their act together to hold the highly marginal De Beauvoir ward at this second by-election, which follows the resignation of the ward's other councillor Polly Billington. She has been selected as the party's prospective parliamentary candidate for East Thanet. The defending Labour candidate is Jasmine Martins, Antoinette Fernandez will hope to gain for the Green Party, and the Lib Dems' Thrusie Maurseth Cahill and the Conservatives' Tareke Gregg also stand.

Another Hackney Labour councillor who has Parliamentary ambitions is Steve Race, who has been selected to stand in the safe Labour seat of Exeter at the next general election and has resigned his seat in the trendy Hoxton East and Shoreditch ward to concentrate on his campaign in Devon. Race had first been elected in 2018 on a Labour slate that also included Feryal Demirci, as she was then; Feryal Clark is now the MP for Enfield North. She resigned her seat on Enfield council in July 2020 but no by-election could be held for nearly a year became of the pandemic; the eventual poll in May 2021, and the subsequent Hackney council elections in 2022, both had a large Labour lead in Hoxton East and Shoreditch. Faruk Tinaz will defend this seat for Labour against competition from Liam Davis for the Green Party, Becket McGrath for the Lib Dems and Samuel Adele for the Conservatives.

In neighbouring Islington we have a by-election in Hillrise ward, which is at the northern end of the borough and runs from the Archway area to Hornsey Rise and Crouch Hill station. This is part of the Islington North parliamentary seat represented by Labour's former leader and persona non grata Jeremy Corbyn; he enjoyed a big majority, and so did Hillside ward's Labour slate in 2022. There's no Corbynite independent candidacy in this by-election; instead the unusual feature of this Hillrise ballot paper is that the Islington Conservatives either haven't found a candidate or missed the nomination deadline. Ollie Steadman defends a safe Labour ward against competition from Alex Nettle for the Greens and Rebecca Taylor for the Lib Dems.

Perhaps the local Conservatives are concentrating on trying to defend one of their three remaining seats on Camden council. The party's Camden group leader Gio Spinella resigned his seat in March for personal reasons, although a history of outspoken comments about Boris Johnson, Liz Truss and Brexit suggests that he is also politically out of step with what the Conservatives have become. This forces a by-election in Frognal ward, which runs from the Finchley Road uphill uphill nearly to Hampstead underground station and is the only remaining safe Conservative ward in Camden. Steve Adams will try to defend this seat against competition from Simon Lickert for Labour, Charles Harris for the Greens and Sarah Hoyle for the the Lib Dems.

Also in the dwindling number of safe Conservative wards in inner London is Norland ward, which covers the Victorian Norland estate in Kensington: Norland Square and the Royal Crescent are part of the ward, with Holland Park underground station on its southern boundary. This is part of the Conservative majority on Kensington and Chelsea council, and the party's defending candidate Stephanie Petit will be challenged by Monica Press for Labour, Heloise Hunter for the Greens and Finlay Dargan for the Lib Dems.

The last Conservative by-election defence in North London is way out in Hillingdon East ward, which runs from Hillingdon underground station south to the Uxbridge Road. It's part of the Uxbridge and South Ruislip parliamentary seat which went to the polls in a famous parliamentary by-election last summer: the Conservatives held the seat thanks to a combination of anti-ULEZ rhetoric and a boost from not having as their candidate Boris Johnson, who clearly had a strongly negative personal vote in the seat. Hillingdon East is safely Conservative in Hillingdon council elections, as this column previously discussed in February 2020. The defending Conservative candidate Kelly Martin faces opposition from Labour's Steve Garelick, Lib Dem Tom Cottew, independent candidate Geoff Courtenay and the Green Party's Sarah Green. The Local Elections Archive Project database, which goes back 21 years, does show that people with the surname "Green" are more likely to be Green Party candidates; maybe there's something in this nominative determinism thing.

Turning south we come to a Labour defence in Brentford West ward of Hounslow borough, which is centred on the western end of the M4 Chiswick flypass; the ward includes the global headquarters of the pharmaceutical company GSK next to the floyover, although GSK are intending to move out later this year in favour of a central London location. Brentford railway station (on the Hounslow Loop) and Boston Manor underground station lie at opposite ends of the ward. Brentford West ward is safely part of the Labour majority on Hounslow council; the defending Labour candidate is Emma Yates, and standing against her are Freya Summersgill for the Green Party, Michael Denniss for the Conservatives, independent candidate Theo Dennison, William Francis for the Lib Dems and John Viner for the Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition.

Taking the train from Brentford station towards Waterloo we come to Putney station, which is located at the northeast corner of West Putney ward. This is part of the London Borough of Wandsworth, where Normal Rules Did Not Apply until Labour gained a majority on the council in 2022 after 48 years of flagship Conservative rule. West Putney ward takes in some council or ex-council estates including part of Roehampton; it was very close in 2022 and its three seats split two to Labour and one to the Conservatives. Labour are defending this by-election with their candidate Jane Briginshaw, the Conservatives have selected Nick Austin, and also standing are Fergal McEntee for the Green Party and Mark Lejman for the Lib Dems.

Another marginal ward which Labour have to defend in South London is St Helier West. The large St Helier estate - named after former London County Council alderman Baroness St Helier - is split between the boroughs of Merton and Sutton; we're in the southern, Sutton part of the estate here. Sutton council has been under Liberal Democrat control since 1990 and for most of that period Labour have drawn a blank in local elections here, but the Labour party returned to the council in 2022 by gaining three seats. One of those was in St Helier West ward, which is centred on St Helier Hospital; this ward was very close three ways in 2022, with Labour winning one seat on 32%, the Conservatives two seats on 31% and the Lib Dems close behind on 28%. Labour's candidate Margaret Thomas has the task of defending this seat; the Conservatives have selected Catherine Gray; the Lib Dems candidate is Noor Sumun; and two independent candidates stand, "Hamilton Activist Kingsley" aka Kingsley Hamilton who was an anti-ULEZ candidate in last year's Uxbridge and South Ruislip by-election, and Renos Costi.

In bankrupt Croydon, the Conservatives' attempts to sort out the financial mess left behind by the borough's previous Labour administration will not be helped by the borough's electoral services team having to organise two by-elections. These are one defence each for the Conservatives and Labour, who are currently tied for first place on the council at 33 seats plus one vacancy each. However, control of the council is not at stake because Croydon moved in 2022 to the elected mayoral system and its Conservative mayor will serve until at least 2026.

For the second time in three years the London Mayor and Assembly elections are combined with a by-election in Park Hill and Whitgift, a small ward to the east of Croydon town centre which is served by Sandilands and Lloyd Park tram stops. This is safely Conservative. Defending candidate Andrew Price, a former Croydon councillor for the neighbouring Addiscombe area, will take on Labour's Melanie Felten, the Green Party's James Cork, the Lib Dems' Andrew Pelling (who has previously represented this area as a Conservative MP and London Assembly member and has also served on Croydon council for both the Conservative and Labour parties in the past), the Trade and Socialist Coalition's Ben Goldstone and independent candidate Mark Samuel, a regular feature of Croydon local by-elections who sometimes reaches the dizzy heights of ten votes.

Another Croydon ward which featured in the May 2021 megapreview and is having another by-election now is Woodside, which lies in the north-east of the borough; it is served by Norwood Junction railway station and by a number of tram stops on the lines to Elmers End and Beckenham. This is a safe Labour ward with a long ballot paper: defending Labour candidate Jessica Rich takes on Titilope Adeoye for the Conservatives, Nicholas Burman-Vince for the Greens, Jahir Hussain for the Lib Dems, Shane Sobers for the Taking the Initiative Party of Britain, and Michelle Wall of the Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition.

Labour have two further seats to defend in Lambeth borough. In Knight's Hill ward, which covers the West Norwood area, outgoing councillor Sonia Winifred has fallen out with the party. Her former ward is safely in the Labour column; defending Labour candidate Emma Nye takes on the Greens' Torla Evans, the Conservatives' Leila Yaasen, the Lib Dems' Nicholas Sanders and independent candidate Janet Gayle.

Meanwhile in Streatham Common and Vale Tom Rutland has resigned from another safe Labour ward to focus on a Parliamentary campaign in Sussex: he will stand as the Labour candidate for East Worthing and Shoreham at the next general election. Here the Labour defence is led by Sarah Cole, while the opposition candidates are Duncan Eastoe for the Green Party, Promise Phillips for the Conservatives and Nicholas Davidson for the Lib Dems.

Moving over the border into what was once Kent, the electors of Lewisham's Deptford ward are being called out for their third by-election in six months following a previous council by-election in November and the Lewisham mayoral by-election in May. This is the latest in a chain of polls which was set off by the Conservative government introducing the Offshore Petroleum Licensing Bill to Parliament in January: that led to the resignation of Conservative MP Chris Skidmore, the resulting February by-election in the Kingswood parliamentary seat was gained by Labour candidate Damian Egan who had previously been Lewisham's elected mayor, and the mayoralty which he left behind was won in the resulting March by-election by his former deputy, Labour candidate Brenda Dacres. Dacres had previously been a councillor for the safe-Labour Deptford ward, and when she won the Lewisham mayoralty her Lewisham council seat was automatically vacated. David Walker leads the Labour defence this time against the Greens' Adam Pugh, the Conservatives Hugh Rees-Beaumont, Lib Dem Jean Branch and independent Tan Bui.

For our final London by-election of 2nd May it's back to the suburbs as we travel to the Bromley ward of Shortlands and Park Langley. This is safely Conservative based on the 2022 results but looks likely to be closely fought nonetheless: most of the seat is within the future Beckenham and Penge parliamentary seat which is transformed by the boundary changes from safe Conservative to knife-edge marginal, so everybody will be looking to make a good impression here. Departing councillor Aisha Cuthbert has her own parliamentary ambitions as she will be the next Conservative candidate for Sittingbourne and Sheppey, further down the Chatham railway line. Gemma Turrell is seeking to defend Cuthbert's Bromley council seat against challenges from Labour's Charlotte Grievson, the Lib Dems' Gita Bapat, Edward Apostolides of Reform UK and independent candidate Brendan Donegan.

North East

Mayors

We'll now commence our tour of England outside London by starting in the North East, where everybody has at least two votes: one for a regional mayor, one for a Police and Crime Commissioner, and possibly a local council election on top. Because the mayoral and police boundaries don't match up, I'll take this region as a single section.

The two mayoral contests in this region are a large one and a small one. The large one is for the North East Mayoral Combined Authority, a new body which is taking over from the former North of Tyne Combined Authority and the former North East Combined Authority. The new body covers the metropolitan county of Tyne and Wear together with the Durham and Northumberland council areas.

The presence here of the strongly Labour-voting county of Tyne and Wear means that this post should be Labour's to lose. If we add together the 2021 results in the Northumbria and Durham police areas while deducting votes cast in Darlington (which is not part of this mayoral area), then we get a Labour lead of 46-33 over the Conservatives. In the 2019 general election the constituencies covering this area returned 15 Labour MPs and six Conservatives, with the latter tally including the Sedgefield constituency which straddles the Durham-Darlington council boundary.

This new mayoral post supersedes the North of Tyne mayoral position which went to the polls in 2021. That was won by Labour candidate Jamie Driscoll, who was very much on the left of the party and was blocked from the Labour nomination for the new mayoral post. He is standing as an independent. The official Labour candidate is Kim McGuinness, who is currently the Northumbria police and crime commissioner. The Conservative candidate is Guy Renner-Thompson, who is a Northumberland councillor for Bamburgh division; he sits on the Northumberland council cabinet, with the children's and education portfolio. Also standing (in ballot paper order) are Paul Donaghy for Reform UK, Andrew Gray for the Green Party and Aidan King for the Lib Dems. One opinion poll in the last week of the campaign has shown a close fight between Labour and Driscoll.

The smaller mayoral position in this region is the Tees Valley mayoralty, which covers the five boroughs of Darlington, Hartlepool, Middlesbrough, Redcar and Cleveland, and Stockton-on-Tees. This has been held since its establishment in 2017 by Lord Houchen of High Leven, a Conservative mayor who was re-elected for a second term in 2021 with a crushing 72-28 over Labour in a straight fight; despite this, Houchen has never been far away from the pages of Private Eye, and local election results in the five boroughs last year were not encouraging for him. Houchen is seeking a third term of office against Labour candidate Chris McEwan, who is deputy leader of Darlington council; this time there will not be a straight fight, with the Lib Dems' Simon Thorley intervening. An opinion poll during the campaign, published by Redfield and Wilton with fieldwork from 17-19 April, put Houchen and McEwan neck-and-neck on 47% each.

Counting for both of these mayoral positions will be on Friday.

Police and Crime Commissioners

As stated, the current Northumbria PCC Kim McGuinness is seeking election as the first North East mayor. She leaves behind a safe PCC position in the Northumbria police area, covering both the Tyne and Wear county and Northumberland, where in 2021 she polled 47% against 31% for the Conservatives and 12% for an independent candidate. Hoping to take over for Labour is Susan Dungworth, whose recent electoral record has been accident-prone: she was the Labour candidate who lost the safe seat of Blyth Valley in the 2019 general election, and she went on to lose her Northumberland council seat in Hartley ward in 2021 on the returning officer's drawing of lots after tying for first place with the Conservative candidate. This time her Conservative opposition comes from Ros Munro, a communications professional who has previously worked in the criminal justice system. Independent candidate Musataque Rahman and Lib Dem John Appleby also stand.

The Durham PCC position, which covers the Durham council area and Darlington, was extremely close in 2021: new Labour candidate Joy Allen won the runoff by just 51-49 over the much-travelled Conservative candidate George Jabbour, who has since finally achieved elected office as a North Yorkshire councillor. The margin on first preferences was even closer, at 43.8-43.0 to Labour. Allen is seeking a second term of office against the Conservatives' Rob Potts, who is a Durham county councillor and former police officer, and the Lib Dems' Nigel Boddy.

The Conservatives won everything up for election in the Tees Valley boroughs in 2021, including the Cleveland PCC: Steve Turner gained the position from Labour by the wide margin of 54-29. Turner is seeking a second term; he is opposed in a straight fight by Labour candidate and Middlesbrough councillor Matt Storey.

Counting for all of these PCC positions will be on Friday.

Local elections

There are elections this year for all councillors in North Tyneside on new boundaries (but not the mayor, whose term runs to 2025), and for one-third of councillors in Gateshead, Newcastle upon Tyne, South Tyneside, Sunderland and Hartlepool. All of these count overnight except for North Tyneside, where results will be announced on Saturday.

It's traditional to start in Sunderland, which makes a point of trying to be first to declare its council results - although, with PCC and mayoral votes to be verified before the local count can begin, that might not be the case this year. The danger for Sunderland Labour in recent years now appears to have passed, and the council currently has a large Labour majority with 47 seats against 13 Conservatives, 12 Lib Dems, 3 independents and 1 for Reform UK. The 2021 results saw big gains for the Conservatives and Lib Dems in Sunderland proper, but the par score is for Labour to gain five seats.

Instead the one to watch in this region is Hartlepool, which was left on a knife-edge by the 2023 elections: Labour have 18 seats, half of the council, but they are shut out of power by 12 Conservatives, 6 independents and the mayoral casting vote. This year Labour are only defending Victoria ward and a by-election gain from the independents in Foggy Furze ward . They only need one gain to take control of the council; last year's elections saw a Labour landslide here, and this column sets a par score of seven Labour gains in Hartlepool at this election.

The other four Tyne and Wear boroughs have large Labour majorities which are not in serious danger. The outgoing localist councillor for Lemington ward in Newcastle resigned shortly before the election and there hasn't been time to hold a by-election to replace him. This means that readers of this column didn't get to see my photograph of the surviving section of Hadrian's Wall that runs through Lemington ward, right next to the A69 arterial road. Let's rectify that.

There definitely had been time for a by-election in the Cullercoats ward of North Tyneside, where a seat has been vacant for nearly eight months following the death of Conservative councillor Linda Arkley. She was a high-profile political veteran, having been first elected to the council in 1991 for Tynemouth ward. In June 2003 there was a by-election for Mayor of North Tyneside after Conservative mayor Christopher Morgan was forced to resign over a child pornography scandal; he was found not guilty on all charges at the time, but was successfully prosecuted for similar offences many years later. Arkley held the mayoralty for the Conservatives in the 2003 by-election, lost it to Labour in 2005, got it back in 2009 but lost re-election again in 2013. In 2018 Linda Arkley was appointed OBE for political service in the north-east of England; after unsuccessfully applying for the Conservative nomination for the 2021 North of Tyne mayoral election, she returned to North Tyneside council in 2021 as councillor for Cullercoats ward. Arkley passed away in October at the age of 71, and nobody ever called a by-election for her vacant seat.

In the rest of the North East mayoral area we have a by-election to Durham council in Horden ward, which lies on the Durham coast immediately to the east of the New Town of Peterlee and is now part of Peterlee's urban area. Greater Manchester

Mayors

Possibly the most powerful of all England's regional mayoralties is that in Greater Manchester, where Andy Burnham has built up a nice little empire. The Greater Manchester Combined Authority has control over (among other things) Greater Manchester Police, the county's health and social care budget, and most prominently the buses.

Having successfully seen off legal challenges from bus companies, Burnham is presently in the middle of a high-profile and very visible reform of Greater Manchester's bus network. Greater Manchester was the first part of England to cap bus single fares at £2 (which represented a massive fare cut), and buses across the north of the county are now fully regulated and franchised under the banner of the Bee Network - with the south of the county to follow at the start of next year. Yellow Bee Network buses are now a familiar sight on Greater Manchester's roads. Mayor Burnham has achieved all this without resorting to road user charging: plans for a Clean Air Zone - in the vein of London's ULEZ - have apparently been seen off in favour of investment in electric buses and taxis to reduce pollution.

All this, together with visibly sticking up for the county and indeed the region during the COVID-19 pandemic, has made Labour's Andy Burnham one of the most popular politicians in the country. In 2021 he won a second term of office as mayor with 67% of the first preference vote, with only the Conservatives (20%) providing any sort of opposition. A ward-level breakdown of that vote was published, and Burnham carried not just every borough but every ward in Greater Manchester - including bastions of the middle class like Bramhall, Hale Barns and Bromley Cross.

Nothing has happened since to suggest that Burnham will struggle for re-election, and an opinion poll in the last week of the campaign gave him another massive lead. The Conservatives had to reselect at the last minute after their original candidate Dan Barker walked off to join Reform UK, and they have ended up with former Trafford councillor Laura Evans who was their candidate last time; also standing are Hannah Spencer for the Green Party, Jake Austin for the Lib Dems, the aforementioned Dan Barker for Reform UK, and independent candidate Nick Buckley who was the Reform UK candidate three years ago. Counting for this post will be on Saturday.

Andy Burnham's designated deputy mayor is Labour's Paul Dennett, who is himself up for re-election this year as the elected mayor of Salford. Neither of his two election wins to date went to transfers: in 2021 Dennett won a second term of office with a 59-23 lead over the Conservatives. He is seeking a third term against opposition from the Conservatives' Jillian Collinson (councillor for Boothstown and Ellenbrook ward), David Jones of the Green Party and Sally Griffiths of the Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition. Results for the Salford mayoral election are expected on Saturday evening.

Further up the Trent we come to Melbourne - the original Derbyshire town of Melbourne, from which the nineteenth-century British prime minister Viscount Melbourne and (via him) the Australian city of Melbourne took their names. Melbourne town is a backwater now, but in times past this was an important place because Melbourne lay close to the southern end of Swarkestone Bridge. There was a small battle there in the English Civil War, and Swarkestone Bridge marked the point where Bonnie Prince Charlie's forces turned back during the 1745 Jacobite rebellion. Melbourne is normally a safely Conservative ward, so it says something about how far the party shrunk last year in South Derbyshire district that the ward's two seats split between the Conservatives and Labour in 2023. Police and Crime Commissioner

One thing which the new mayor of the West Midlands will not be responsible for is West Midlands Police. That's not for want of trying. Andy Street has for some years been trying to merge the mayor and PCC positions, as is being done in South Yorkshire this year; but he had been stymied by opposition from the West Midlands Combined Authority, made up of himself and council leaders from the area, which decided in 2019 not to bring a merger proposal forward. The police and crime commissioner himself was also against the idea of a merger - and it didn't help that the mayor and PCC were from different parties. The West Midlands police and crime commissioner, Simon Foster, is a Labour politician who was elected in 2021 with a 46-39 lead over the Conservatives, at the same time and on the same boundaries as Andy Street's re-election. Foster's first-preference score of 276,743 votes was the highest for any PCC candidate in 2021, beating the 267,404 votes scored by the Conservatives in the Thames Valley police area into second place.

In October last year a change in the law removed the requirement for the combined authority to consent to a plan for merging the mayoral and PCC position. Street now saw his chance, and he formally proposed a merger to the Home Secretary who has to sign off the paperwork for this change. James Cleverly duly wrote to the PCC and the mayor on 6th December 2023 indicating that he would draw up the appropriate legal order under section 107F of the Local Democracy, Economic Development and Construction Act 2009 and lay it before Parliament for approval.

However, Street and the Home Secretary had been caught out by another change in the law made in October: orders under section 107F are now subject to section 113 of the 2009 Act, which says that the Secretary of State can make such an order only if three conditions are satisfied. First, the Secretary of State must consider that to do so is likely to improve the economic, social, and environmental well-being of some or all of the people who live or work in the area; second, the Secretary of State must consider that to do is appropriate having regard to the need to secure effective and convenient local government, and to reflect the identities and interests of local communities; and finally, a public consultation has to be carried out.

PCC Foster's solicitors wrote to the Home Secretary pointing all this out, and as a result a public consultation was rather hastily set up which closed at the end of Janaury. Having considered the submissions, the Home Secretary made a decision on 6th February to merge the Mayor and PCC for the West Midlands. A draft statutory instrument was drawn up to effect this.

Simon Foster now took the Home Secretary to judicial review. On 18th March the High Court ruled that the public consultation was inadequate: the consultation document had not given the public enough information to allow for a fair opportunity to participate with the consultation. Mr Justice Swift accordingly quashed the merger decision. With only eight days left before notice of election had to be given, the Home Secretary immediately appealed the decision; the Court of Appeal heard the case within the week, but on 22nd March they upheld the High Court's decision. Notice of election was duly published on 26th March.

So we have a PCC election as well as a mayoral election. A hastily-assembled candidate list sees a straight fight between the Conservatives and Labour: Simon Foster is seeking re-election from the red corner, former West Midlands policeman Tom Byrne challenges from the blue corner. Counting for both this and the mayoral election will be on Saturday.

Wiltshire

Police and Crime Commissioner

The Wiltshire PCC election of May 2021 was, in terms of the number of voters inconvenienced, the biggest electoral mess of that year. It was won easily by the new Conservative candidate Jonathan Seed, who polled 41% of the vote against 17% each for the Lib Dems and Labour and 15% for independent candidate Mike Rees.

However, at the time of polling it was not widely known that Seed was disqualified from being a police and crime commissioner due to his criminal record. He had spent convictions from 1993 for drink-driving and failure to stop at the scene of an accident, for which he was fined £500 and disqualified from driving for 18 months by Kennet magistrates. The maximum penalty for those offences is now six months' imprisonment and an unlimited fine, and these were also imprisonable offences in 1993. Which meant Seed was ineligible to be a police and crime commissioner: being convicted of any imprisonable offence is a disqualification for that post, whether you actually went to prison or not.

Full marks are due to the investigative team at ITV News for digging up Seed's past, but they didn't get the story out until the interval between polls closing and the votes being counted. By the time Seed had been declared as the winner by the returning officer, it was clear that he could not take up the post of PCC. The job was declared vacant when he failed to sign his declaration of acceptance of office within the two-month deadline after the poll. Jonathan Seed was subsequently charged with making a false statement (that he was not disqualified from office) on his nomination papers; he pleaded not guilty at Oxford Crown Court in November 2021, and the charge was subsequently dropped due to insufficient evidence.

The by-election resulting from all this took place at the awkward time of mid-August 2021, and on the predictable awful turnout the Conservatives' Philip Wilkinson became Wiltshire PCC with 36% of the vote, against 28% for independent Mike Rees and 20% for the Lib Dems.

Philip Wilkinson is seeking a second term of office for the Conservatives. Mike Rees is also having another go after his second-place finish last time; he spent 30 years in Wiltshire police before starting a second career in business a decade ago. The Conservatives selected as their defending candidate for that by-election Peter Dickenson, and he went on to win. Which was a problem, because at the time he worked for the council as a lollipop man. I wrote at the time:

The problem with this is that Dickenson's employer is Dorset council - which means he's not eligible to be a Dorset councillor. Local government law says you can't stand for election to a council you're drawing a wage from. Unfortunately, by the time this was realised it was too late to take Dickenson off the ballot paper and he has gone forward to the election as the official Conservative candidate.

If Dickenson wins this by-election, then he will be unable to take up his seat, Dorset council will remain a man down until May, the returning officer will have spent a five-figure sum in council taxpayers' money organising this by-election with nothing to show for it, and Dorset Police (who have already received a complaint about the matter) will have to investigate why Dickenson signed the declaration on his nomination papers that he was not disqualified from office. I was criticised at the time for apparently calling for the imprisonment of a popular local lollipop man. Dickenson went on to resign his employment as a school crossing attendant, and he was then sworn in as a Dorset councillor on 1st March and is duly listed as such on the council website despite being returned in the January by-election while ineligible. There was no council meeting for him to attend before May - April's full council was cancelled due to lack of business - and press reports indicated that Dickenson would not take his allowance before the May election, which is probably for the best on his part given that he is not legally a councillor and he should not be using or profiting from that title. There are elections this year for the whole of Tandridge council on new ward boundaries and for one-third of councillors in Elmbridge, Mole Valley, Reigate and Banstead, Runnymede and Woking. All of these are Friday counts.

It says a lot about how far the Conservative brand has sunk that they no longer have majority control of any district in Surrey. The last domino to fall was Reigate and Banstead, which recently fell into a Conservative minority when councillor Zelanie Cooper quit the party; she is seeking re-election as an independent candidate in Lower Kingswood, Tadworth and Walton ward. The current composition has 22 Conservatives, 11 Greens, 6 Residents (including a vacant seat), 3 Lib Dems, 2 independents and one Labour. A repeat of 2023 would see the Conservatives lose one seat nett to the Greens and also lose Redhill West and Wray Common ward to Labour.

In recent years the Liberal Democrats have built up a very large majority in Mole Valley district, while last year they gained control of bankrupt Woking council which now stands at 20 Lib Dem seats (one of which is vacant), 4 Conservatives, 4 independents and 2 Labour. All four remaining Tory councillors are up for re-election this year and the Woking Conservatives are facing the prospect of wipeout - which rather reflects what they did to the borough's finances before the recent Lib Dem takeover.

Runnymede district went into No Overall Control last year, with a Conservative minority administration of 19 seats (one of which is vacant) facing six councillors from the Runnymede Independent Residents Group, 5 independents, 4 each from Labour and the Lib Dems, 2 Greens and one ex-Tory councillor who has walked off to join Reform UK. He was the outgoing councillor for Addlestone North ward, which voted Green last year, but is seeking re-election in Englefield Green West. A repeat of last year's results would see the Conservatives lose three seats, with Labour gains in Egham Hythe and Englefield Green West wards plus a Lib Dem gain in New Haw ward.

Further into the Surrey commuter belt we have the Elmbridge district, which is one of a number of Surrey councils where Residents Associations are a significant factor. The Residents currently have a coalition agreement with the Lib Dems, who have rather usurped them in recent years to become the largest party on a hung council: there are currently 20 Lib Dems, 16 Residents and 12 Conservatives. The Liberal Democrats did very well in the 2023 elections here, and a repeat of that would see them confirm a by-election gain from the Residents in Molesey East and make five other gains (four from the Conservatives, one from the Residents): that would give 25 Lib Dem seats and an overall majority.

We have an all-out election to Tandridge council which covers the rural eastern end of Surrey. This has been subject to an independent/Residents takeover in recent years; the council is currently run by the Residents Alliance of 10 independents and eight councillors from the Oxted and Limpsfield Residents Group, who face-off against 11 Lib Dems, 9 Conservatives and four other independents. There's not much sign from last year's elections that this will significantly change.

There is one by-election to Waverley council for the Witley and Milford ward, which covers villages in the Surrey countryside to the south-west of Godalming. This ward split its three seats three ways in 2023, between independent councillor Maxine Gale who had won a February 2020 by-election for the former Milford ward; the Lib Dems' Andrew Law; and the Conservatives' Phoebe Sullivan, who defeated her running-mate Gary Hudson for the final seat by just one vote. Andrew Law died in January, so after some delay the Lib Dems are defending a by-election which is hard to call. Their defending candidate is Laura Cavaliere, who takes on independent Tony Sollars and Gary Hudson (again) for the Conservatives.

Sussex

Police and Crime Commissioner

Katy Bourne of the Conservatives has been the Sussex police and crime commissioner since the post was set up in 2012. In 2021 she was re-elected for a third term of office with 47% of the vote, against 19% for Labour and 14% for the Lib Dems. Bourne is seeking a fourth term of office against Labour's Paul Richards, who runs a communications firm; the Lib Dems' Jamie Bennett who is a hotel manager and former Arun councillor; and the Green Party's Jonathan Kent. This will be one of the last results to come through, with counting not due to start until Sunday.

Local elections

There are elections this year for one-half of councillors in Adur and Hastings and for one-third of councillors in Crawley and Worthing. All of these will count their votes on Friday.

We'll start with Crawley, a two-party state where Labour currently have a 20-16 council majority over the Conservatives. The Tories' situation looks like it's about to get worse, as a repeat of last year's performance would result in three net gains for Labour.

Following the recent Labour takeover in Worthing and massive Tory losses elsewhere in the county in 2023, the last-remaining Conservative-majority district in Sussex is Adur - which wasn't up for election in 2023. Here the Conservative overall majority is just one seat: they have 15 councillors against 9 Labour seats (one of which is vacant), 3 independents and 2 Greens. Labour actually polled the most votes here in 2022, but that was mainly because the Conservatives didn't oppose the independent councillor for Marine ward. A repeat of the 2022 results would see Labour gain Eastbrook ward from the Conservatives, tipping Adur council into No Overall Control.

Hastings, on the other hand, has turned into a very messy hung council. Labour lost control of this council in 2022 after losing seats to the Greens; a Labour minority administration resulted. The Labour group then split down the middle last December with eight of its 14 members - including the entire cabinet - walking off to form a new Hastings Independents group. The Hastings Independents now run the council in coalition with the Green Party - the council composition is 10 Conservatives, 8 Hastings Independents, 6 members of the rump Labour group, 4 Greens, 3 other independents and one councillor who left the Conservatives to join Reform UK. Goodness knows what the electors will make of this - but if the last elections in 2022 are anything to go by, the Greens should be well-placed to make gains.

Our final two local by-elections are in adjoining wards in the city of Brighton and Hove. Kemptown and Queen's Park wards lie to the east of Brighton city centre, with Kemptown being a long and thin ward running along the seafront while Queen's Park ward lies inland. Eastern Road is the boundary between the wards. Brighton and Hove was taken over by Labour at the 2023 local elections and both of these wards are safely part of the Labour majority.

These by-elections both arise as the result of a scandal involving Kemptown councillor Bharti Gajjar and her daughter Chandni Mistry, who is the youngest member of the council and represented Queen's Park ward. Both of them were thrown out of the Labour party in December over allegations that they do not live in the city: Mistry is a student reading medicine at Lancaster University, while a Companies House filing for Gajjar gives a correspondence address in Leicester. The pair have also become embroiled in legal action in India. Labour have a good opportunity to take these seats back in the by-elections, with their defending candidates being Théresa Mackey in Kemptown and Milla Gauge in Queen's Park. The other candidates for Kemptown ward are Ricky Perrin for the Greens, Gary Farmer for the Brighton and Hove Independents, Josephine O'Carroll for the Conservatives, Robert Brown for the Lib Dems and independent candidate Jamie Gillespie; while Queen's Park is additionally contested by the Greens' Luke Walker, the Brighton and Hove Independents' Adrian Hart, the Conservatives' Sunny Choudhury and the Liberal Democrats' Dominique Hall.

Kent

Police and Crime Commissioner

Our last PCC election this year comes in one of the strongest counties for the Conservatives in 2021. Matthew Scott was re-elected for a second term three years ago with a 58-25 lead over Labour. He is seeking a third term now against Labour candidate Lenny Rolles, who is a Gravesham councillor, and Lib Dem Graham Colley. Again, this will not be counted until Sunday making Kent PCC one of the last results to declare.

Local elections

There are elections this year for the whole of Maidstone council, which is moving off the thirds electoral cycle, and for the whole of Tunbridge Wells council on new ward boundaries. Both of these are Friday counts.

Maidstone has been one of the brighter spots in Kent local government for the Conservatives in recent years, but they are currently short of a majority and run the council as a minority. There are currently 25 Conservative councillors, 12 Lib Dems, 11 independents of varying stripes, 4 Labour and 3 Greens. The way the borough's previous wards came up for election makes it difficult to construct a par score, but the Conservatives performed well here in 2021 and defending all of those seats could be difficult.

Finally we come to the spiritual home of disgust, Tunbridge Wells, where the voters have made their disgust with the Conservatives abundantly clear in recent years. The council currently has 17 Lib Dem councillors, 11 Conservatives, 9 seats for the Tunbridge Wells Alliance, 7 Labour and 4 independents. The Lib Dems, Tunbridge Wells Alliance and Labour are in coalition. In each of the last two years the Conservatives have won just two wards (behind Labour's three) despite still topping the poll across the district, which shows just how badly their brand has sunk here. Based on the 2023 results, we can probably expect the current coalition to continue.
