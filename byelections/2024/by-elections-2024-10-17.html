Tirymynach

Ceredigion council, West Wales; caused by the death of Liberal Democrat councillor Paul Hinge. There are two by-elections taking place today in rural Wales. We'll start on the west coast with the Tirymynach ward of Ceredigion, which covers a number of villages just to the north of Aberystwyth. The largest settlement in the ward is Bow Street on the main road and railway line towards Machynlleth; Bow Street railway station was reopened in 2021 to serve the village. Bow Street is unusual in Ceredigion in having an English-language name, which appears to have been lifted directly from the central London thoroughfare of that name. The ward also takes the smaller settlement of Llangorwen to the west and the coastal village of Clarach, which has been more or less swallowed up by large caravan parks for holidaymakers. Clarach Bay has a sandy beach, which is crossed by the small Afon Clarach on its way to the ocean. Further up the coast is the curious feature of Sarn Gynfelyn, a natural causeway which extends 11 kilometres into the sea. Geographers will tell you that this is the remains of a glacial moraine from the last Ice Age. Legends, on the other hand, have it that this causeway is a relic of the kingdom of Cantre'r Gwaelod, which now lies beneath the waters of Cardigan Bay. Ceredigion is one of the four Welsh local government districts which are controlled by Plaid Cymru, all of which are predominantly rural areas with significant numbers of Welsh speakers. Plaid also represent the Ceredigion Senedd constituency which has same boundaries as the county, although the MS Elin Jones has had a non-partisan role since 2016 as the Senedd's Llywydd, or presiding officer. Llanberis

Gwynedd council, North Wales; caused by the resignation of Plaid Cymru councillor Kimberley Jones. Our other Welsh by-election today takes in place in the heart of Parc Cenedlaethol Eryri, or as English speakers know it, the Snowdonia National Park. The village of Llanberis lies just under five miles north of Yr Wyddfa, and it is the most common starting point for those who want to climb the mountain - either by foot on the Llanberis Path, or by rail via the Snowdon Mountain Railway. The Llanberis electoral ward doesn't extend to the summit of Yr Wyddfa, but it does take in most of the Llanberis Path and some summits over 1,000 metres: Snowdon's lower summit of Garnedd Ugain is on the ward boundary, as is the highest point of Glyder Fawr on the north side of the Pass of Llanberis. This valley runs down to the village of Nant Peris, the ruins of Dolbadarn Castle which was built in the 12th century by Llywelyn the Great, and to the lakes of Llyn Peris and Llyn Padarn either side of Llanberis village. Llyn Peris, the higher and smaller of the two lakes, is the lower reservoir feeding the massive Dinorwig hydroelectric power plant, and its water level varies a lot depending on the time of day: water flows into Llyn Peris from a higher reservoir at times of peak power demand, and is then pumped back up the mountain when there is excess power in the National Grid. Llanberis is a strongly Welsh-speaking village. Indeed the website of Llanberis and Nant Peris community council apologises that "not all the information on this website is available in English at present", which is a refreshing change. It thus should be no surprise that it is represented at all three levels of government by Plaid Cymru. In the Senedd, Plaid's former co-deputy leader Siân Gwenllian MS has one of the safest seats in Wales: her Arfon constituency is based on Bangor and Caernarfon. At Westminster elections, Llanberis was added in 2024 to the Dwyfor Meirionnydd constituency which is represented by Plaid's Westminster group leader Liz Saville Roberts. Plaid Cymru have a large overall majority on Gwynedd council, and since 2022 Llanberis has been part of that majority: outgoing councillor Kim Jones was elected here that year with 60% of the vote against 32% for an independent candidate. Before 2022, Llanberis had voted for independent councillors this century. Defending this by-election for Plaid Cymru is Gwilym Evans, who is a Llanberis and Nant Peris community councillor. There is one independent candidate standing, Martin Jones, who lives in the village of Cwm y Glo further down the valley: he was a Labour candidate for Cwm y Glo ward in the 2017 Gwynedd elections. Also standing are Dewi Evans who has the Welsh-only ballot paper description "Plaid Werdd Cymru" (for the uninitiated, that's the Green Party of Wales), and local resident Anne Williams for the Liberal Democrats. Holme Valley South

Kirklees council, West Yorkshire; caused by the resignation of Labour councillor Paul Davies. We now commence a tour of some of the picturesque parts of northern England by climbing up into the spine of the Pennines and specifically to Black Hill, the highest point of West Yorkshire at an elevation of 1,909 feet. The name is an apt one: the summit of Black Hill consists of peat with no vegetation, and its ascent - an early climb on the Pennine Way - was notorious for its boggy conditions until flagstones were laid. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FAAQzA8Fpec Black Hill lies on the boundary between West Yorkshire and Derbyshire, and its northern and eastern slopes descend into the slopes and villages of the Holme Valley. This is the countryside which came into our living rooms for decades as the filming location for the sitcom Last of the Summer Wine, a massive 31 series of which were broadcast between 1973 and 2010. No official final episode of the show was ever made, so we might see a 32nd series yet. Fourteen years after Last of the Summer Wine ended, the picture-postcard town of Holmfirth still attracts tourists. Sid's Café, in the town centre, remains very much in business. Nora Batty's house is now a holiday let (self-catering). The main road from Holmfirth to the summit of Holme Moss is known in the cycling community as one of England's most difficult ascents - 7 kilometres at an average gradient of 5.6% - and it was a Category 2 climb in the 2014 Tour de France. All this is part of the Holme Valley South ward, which includes Holmfirth, a lot of moorland, and a large number of scattered villages with names like Netherthong, Upperthong, Thongsbridge and Hole Bottom. (Stop laughing at the back.) Also here is Longley Farm, whose dairy products are exported to Yorkshire and beyond. Longley Farm was the site of the UK's first commercial wind turbine, which started making electricity for the farm in 1986; the original turbine was dismantled in 2015 and replaced by a more powerful model. Despite the rural nature of this area the local authority here is the Metropolitan Borough of Kirklees, which gets its metropolitan nature from the towns of Huddersfield, Dewsbury and Batley. Kirklees council is often hung; Labour did have a majority here from 2022 to 2024, but in May's council elections they suffered major losses to independent candidates in Dewsbury and Batley - which were followed by that parliamentary seat returning an independent candidate in July. Dewsbury and Batley are strongly Muslim areas, which gives them a very different electoral dynamic to Holme Valley South ward which has no Muslim population to speak of: Holmfirth and its surrounding villages are generally affluent areas with a strong middle-class demographic. Indeed Holme Valley South ward was safely Conservative not all that long ago. Holmfirth was once on the Parliamentary map. Yorkshire was the biggest winner from the 1885 redistribution that more or less created the system of single-member Parliamentary constituencies, and that redistribution created a Holmfirth parliamentary seat covering the Pennine countryside south of Huddersfield and west of Barnsley. This seat returned Liberal MPs at every election from 1885 to a 1912 by-election. It was broken up in 1918, with the Holme Valley transferring into the Colne Valley constituency where it has resided ever since. The Colne Valley constituency has prven to be much more exciting over the decades and its list of previous MPs includes several famous names. A 1907 by-election was won by the mysterious Victor Grayson as an independent Labour candidate; he lost his seat in January 1910 to the Liberals' Charles Leach, who in 1916 gained the dubious distinction of being the only MP ever to be deprived of his seat for lunacy. Philip Snowden, the first Labour Chancellor of the Exchequer, represented Colne Valley from 1922 to 1931. A later Labour MP for the seat, who won a by-election in 1963, was Sir Patrick Duffy who, now aged 104, is Britain's oldest surviving former MP. If Sir Patrick makes it into the New Year he would become the longest-lived MP since births were registered: that record is currently held by Ronald Atkins, who died in 2020 at the age of 104 years and 200 days. Colne Valley had a significant Liberal and later Liberal Democrat vote all the way up to the formation of the Coalition, since when the seat has been a tight Labour-Conservative marginal. It has changed hands at all of the last three general elections, with Labour candidate Paul Davies defeating the Conservatives' Jason McCartney in July by 4,963 votes. Davies had started his parliamentary career right here in Holme Valley South ward in 2019, when he gained for Labour what had previously been a safe Conservative ward. He was re-elected for a second term in 2023, and Labour now hold all three of the ward's council seats following narrow wins over the Conservatives in 2022-24. The Tories lost their final seat here in May, with Labour winning by 45-41. Given Labour's generally poor performance in council by-elections since July, the Conservatives will have this one in their sights after Paul Davies resigned from Kirklees council to concentrate on his Westminter duties. Defending for Labour is Phillip Lucitt, who contested Batley East ward at the other end of the borough in May and lost what had previously been a safe Labour ward to an independent candidate, extremely badly. The Conservatives have renominated Damian Brook who was their losing candidate here in the last three local elections: Brook is a Holme Valley parish councillor and works as an enforcement officer for the Environment Agency. Also standing are Toby Cooper for the Green Party, Howard Cohen for the Lib Dems, independent candidate Miri Finch who has stood in other wards at the last three Kirklees elections (in 2022 with the Freedom Alliance nomination) to very little effect, and Susan Laird for Reform UK. Grange and Cartmel

Westmorland and Furness council, Cumbria; caused by the resignation of Liberal Democrat councillor Sue Sanderson. Continuing the theme of nice-looking countryside in the North, we now come to the Lonsdale part of the Westmorland and Lonsdale district - Lancashire beyond the sands, as it was once was. Today Grange-over-Sands is a Victorian seaside resort on the south coast of Cumbria, located on the west bank of the Kent estuary at the point where it flows into the flat and dangerous sandbanks, mudflats and saltmarsh of Morecambe Bay. The Furness railway line, which runs along the promenade, is the main link to the outside world: the railway viaducts on the lines to Lancaster and Ulverston take miles off the roads around the Kent and Leven estuaries. Grange-over-Sands' promenade is interrupted by the forlorn remains of the Grange Lido: this is an Art Deco open-air swimming pool which closed in 1992 and which the council are now finally doing something about. Grange-over-Sands is the main town on the Cartmel peninsula, which lies between the two estuaries. Historically the major centre here was Cartmel Priory, founded in 1190 by the great twelfth-century soldier William the Marshal; its church survives as the local parish church. Appropriately for a ward which is in the top 100 in England and Wales for those employed in accommodation and food service activities, Cartmel is also noted for its food: the village's restaurant L'Enclume is the only restaurant in northern England with three Michelin stars, and Cartmel sticky toffee pudding is a local delicacy. This pudding is given to all winning connections following races at Cartmel racecourse, whose meetings are normally well-attended; Cartmel's main three-day meeting in May actually takes place over five days, giving visitors time to go off and visit the Lake District countryside in between race days. Cartmel's isolation was a key ingredient in one of the more audacious betting coups in recent years, involving two horses who were purpotedly both called Gay Future. The imposter was presented by his trainer, Anthony Collins, as poorly performing in order to lengthen the odds at the bookmakers, who were then caught off guard when the real Gay Future turned up at Cartmel on the 1974 August bank holiday and won his race by fifteen lengths at a starting price of 10-1. Those in on the scam had staked doubles and trebles on Gay Future and two other horses trained by Collins, which were never intended to run and never even left their stables; when those horses became non-runners, those wagers suddenly turned into single bets on Gay Future. The betting firms had twigged that something was going on by the time the horses went under starter's orders, but they were unable to alert the on-course bookies who set the starting prices because Cartmel Racecourse only had one telephone line at the time; Ladbrokes went so far as to send an employee to the course on a motorbike with a bag full of cash, but Bank Holiday traffic meant he arrived too late. Collins was subsequently prosecuted for conspiracy to defraud, but a sympathetic judge only imposed a fine of £1,000 and a suspended prison sentence while the Jockey Club merely warned him off for ten years. Grange and Cartmel are part of the Westmorland and Lonsdale parliamentary seat, which is the fiefdom of the former Liberal Democrat leader Tim Farron who has represented that seat since 2005. The local authority is Westmorland and Furness council, whose only elections to date were in 2022. On that occasion the Liberal Democrats won every single seat within Farron's constituency as it was then drawn, which was crucial to them winning overall control of the new council. Grange and Cartmel ward returned the Lib Dem slate easily with 47% of the vote, against 26% for an independent candidate and 19% for the Conservatives. Lib Dem councillor Pete Endsor resigned earlier this year due to family ill-health; the resulting by-election was held in May alongside the Cumbria police and crime commissioner elections, and the Liberal Democrats held the seat with a massive 77% of the vote. This second by-election of the year in Grange and Cartmel ward follows the resignation on health grounds of Sue Sanderson, who was the council's cabinet member for education, inclusion and skills; Sanderson had also hed cabinet positions in the post on Cumbria county council and South Lakeland council, which she served as deputy leader of. Defending for the Lib Dems this time is Tim Bloomer, who is opposed in a straight fight by Tor McLaren for the Conservatives. Keswick has a strong claim to being one of England's most picturesque wards, as it covers a large chunk of the Lake District National Park. As well as Keswick town, the ward boundaries include all of Borrowdale and the Thirlmere reservoir, which supplies the far-off city of Manchester with water via a 96-mile aqeduct. Falkirk South

Falkirk council, Scotland; caused by the resignation of Labour councillor Euan Stainbank.

We finish north of the border in Scotland's most beautiful town, according a 2011 poll for the STV television station. Falkirk might not have seemed so beautiful back in the day to the Romans: in the second century AD this was the northern frontier of the Roman empire, with the Antonine Wall guarding the high ground overlooking the Forth estuary.

The Romans recorded the site as Varia Capella; this, the Celtic Ecclesbrith, the Gaelic An Eaglais Bhreac and the Scots Fawkirk all refer to a "speckled" church on the site of the present Falkirk Trinity Church. Falkirk's location next to the Forth and with easy access to local ironstone resulted in it becoming an early centre of the Industrial Revolution, with communication links to match. The Forth and Clyde Canal opened in 1790, the Union Canal in 1822 and the railways came in the 1830s, giving easy access to Edinburgh and Glasgow. All of them run through this ward. The two canals were rejoined in the twenty-first century by the Falkirk Wheel, a unique rotating boat lift which draws large numbers of tourists to the area. Including your columnist, who took this picture in April 2019.

The Falkirk Wheel lies on the boundary of Falkirk South ward, which takes in the town centre and points south of it including a small rural hinterland. Both of the town's main railway stations (High and Grahamston) are here, as is the mansion of Callendar House which serves as the main museum and heritage centre for Falkirk.

This ward was originally drawn up for the May 2007 elections when Scotland introduced proportional representation to its local elections. In both 2007 and 2012 Labour won two seats, the SNP and Conservatives one each. Boundary changes for the 2017 cut the ward down from four councillors to three, and it was Labour who lost from this: the SNP took over first place with 36% of the first preferences, the Conservatives were second with 32% and Labour tell to third on 27%. All three of those parties won one seat each; in fact, all of the winning candidates (Lorna Binnie for the SNP, John Patrick for the Conservatives and Pat Reid for Labour) got over the 25% required for election on first preferences alone, so there was no need to go to transfers. The ward's Labour councillor Pat Reid then resigned in 2021, seeking to retire from public life at the age of 79. This left a difficult seat for Labour to defend from third place at the resulting by-election in October 2021, and in the end they finished in the resulting by-election which went to the SNP's Emma Russell. Both SNP coucillors sought re-election in May 2022, but Labour got their seat back in the ordinary election from Russell: vote shares were 42% for the SNP, 29% for the Conservatives and 22% for 22-year-old Labour candidate Euan Stainbank, who picked up transfers from the Conservative surplus and the Greens to win the final seat. Stainbank, who had finished his law studies at Stirling the previous year, became deputy leader of Falkirk council's Labour group. Now aged 24, Euan Stainbank has become Scotland's youngest MP after he pulled off an enormous swing of 26% to pick up the Falkirk constituency left open by the retirement of SNP MP John McNally, who enjoyed the largest majority of any Scottish MP in 2019. Labour had actually finished in third place in Falkirk that year, although this may have been a false position after their parliamentary candidate that year was disendorsed partway through the campaign for antisemitism. At Holyrood level most of this ward is part of the Falkirk West constituency, whose MSP Michael Matheson has been in the Scottish Parliament cotinuously since it was reconvened in 1999: he was originally a regional MSP for Central Scotland before being first elected for Falkirk West in 2007. Matheson was a Scottish Cabinet secretary from 2014 to 2024, latterly holding the NHS recovery, health and social care portfolio. He resigned that post in February following a scandal over £11,000 of roaming charges which he had run up on a Parliamentary iPad during a family holiday. The Conservative candidate for Falkirk West in 2021 was Stephen Kerr, who had previously been MP for Stirling from 2017 to 2019; Kerr finished third behind Matheson and Labour, but did get into Holyrood as a regional MSP for Central Scotland. The rural fringes of Falkirk South ward are in the Falkirk East constituency represented by another former MP: the SNP's Michelle Thomson had a scandal-ridden term for Edinburgh West in 2015-17, but no evidence of wrongdoing was eventually found and she returned to frontline politics as an MSP in 2021. With Euan Stainbank off to Westminster Labour will need to perform very well to hold this Falkirk South by-election. In the previous by-election three years ago the Labour candidate was eliminated in third place and the SNP beat the Conservatives in the final count by 51-49; rerunning the 2022 count for one seat gives an increased SNP lead over the Conservatives of 57-43, with Labour again eliminated in third place. The defending Labour candidate in this by-election does have the advantage of top spot on the alphabetical ballot paper. Claire Aitken is based in the village of Hallglen just to the south of the town, where she does a lot of community volunteer work. The SNP have seleceted Carol Beattie, who was chief executive of neighbouring Stirling council until May. Standing for the Conservatives is David Grant, who is hoping to return to Falkirk council after losing his seat last year in the neighbouring Bonnybridge and Larbert ward. Completing the ballot paper are Tom McLaughlin for the Greens, the founder of Falkirk District Action Group Sharron McKean as an independent candidate, Stuart Martin for Reform UK and Sean McCay for the Lib Dems.
